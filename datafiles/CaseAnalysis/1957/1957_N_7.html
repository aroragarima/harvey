Â Niemla Textile Finishing. Mills Ltd. v The 2Nd Punjab Industrial Tribunal(With Connected Appeals An

Supreme Court of India

10 January 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 52; AIR 1957 SC 329; 1957 (1) LLJ 460; 1957 SCJ 275; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 335

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Textiles, Indian Contract Act

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 10 -

- Dispute - Jurisdiction - Validity - Appellants were engaged in manufacture and production of textiles - There were disputes between them and their workmen - Respondent No. 2, referred disputes for adjudication to Tribunal respondent No. 1, who entered upon references and issued notices to appellants to file their written statements - Appellants in appeal filed written statement without prejudice to their contentions that respondent No. 2 was not competent to refer disputes for adjudication by respondent No. 1 and that respondent No. 1 had no jurisdiction to entertain reference - Appellants in all three appeals filed petitions in HC u/art. 226 of Constitution against respondents Nos. 1 and 2 - DB of HC dismissed petitions - DB of HC had admitted petition based on very same grounds and had granted stay of proceedings before respondent No. 1 - Appellants filed applications before HC to appeal to this Court and for stay of further proceedings before respondent No. 1 - Notices were issued by HC to respondents - Appellants filed petitions for review of orders dismissing their writ petitions in limine - In these petitions for review appellants, alleged that their counsel had inadvertently failed to raise contention that s. 10 of the Act was ultra vires the Constitution and petitions for review were dismissed - Hence, instant appeals - Whether order passed by HC was maintainable - Held, Tribunals had put extended construction on term 'industry' was no reason for holding that definition itself was bad or ultra vires - Wrong application of definition to cases which were not strictly covered by it could not vitiate definition if otherwise it was not open to challenge - According to preamble, the Act was enacted not only for settlement of industrial disputes but for other purposes also - In instant case concerns or undertakings were all industrial concerns and fell squarely within definition of term ' industry ' strictly so- called and it was not open to pursuers, situated as they were, to challenge same - Appeals dismissed.
