Sharma and Associates Contractors Private Limited v Progressive Constructions Limited

Supreme Court of India

10 February 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 159; (2017) 5 SCC 743; AIR 2017 SC 847; JT 2017 (3) SC 256; 2017(2) SCALE 326

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Arbitration Act, 1940

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940, s. 20 - Sub-contract work - Arbitral award - Validity - Tenderor to whom the contract was awarded by State govt. entered into contract with respondent permitting sub-contracting - Respondent sub-contracted work to appellant - Disputes arose between appellant and respondent in respect of execution of work - Appellant filed petition u/s. 20 of the Act before HC - Single Judge allowed petition and upheld award passed by Arbitrator - On appeal, DB allowed appeal holding that no money was payable by respondent to appellant - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the principle of incorporation would enter into and extend to such sub-contract in absence of any clause of back to back contract that was signed between appellant and respondent.

Held, it was found that claim was entertained by Arbitrator on the basis of provisions in the contract entered into between tenderor and respondent and said provisions were not made applicable in contract which was entered between appellant and respondent. Approach of Arbitrator was clearly perverse in justifying the claim on the basis of provisions which were not applicable. Whether contract entered into between tenderor and respondent governed the relationship between appellant and respondent was a fundamental and jurisdictional issue and such an exercise was permissible by Court while examining validity of an award. Further, DB was on the principle that Arbitrator was a creature of contract between the parties and if he ignores the specific term of contract, it would be a question of jurisdictional error which could be corrected by Court. Though respondent was able to get the benefit of enhanced rate and was able to retain the same thereby depriving appellant to get that benefit. However, in a matter of contract where parties have to stick to govern by the provisions of contract entered into between them, equity has no role to play. Insofar as contract between appellant and respondent was concerned, appellant was satisfied with "escalation" clause. Respondent, while entering into contract with tenderor ensured that enhancement of rates by principal employer in favour of tenderor would enure to the benefit of respondent as well. However, appellant could not successfully negotiate such aspect with respondent in the absence of any such clause/arrangement in contract entered into between appellant and respondent. As the contract between appellant and respondent deals only with escalation, appellant has to be satisfied with the same. Appeal dismissed.
