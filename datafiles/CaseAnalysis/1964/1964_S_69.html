Â Sri Venkata Seetaramanjaneya Rice and Oil Mills and Others v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

25 March 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, S.M. Sikri, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 404; AIR 1964 SC 1781; 1965 (1) MLJ 31; 1965 (1) MLJ(SC) 31; [1964] 7 S.C.R. 456

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Trade

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Electricity & Energy - Trade - T.N. Essential Articles Control and Requisitioning Act (Temporary Powers), 1949, s. 3 - A. P. Essential Articles Control and Requisitioning (Temporary Powers) Act, 1955, s. 3 - (A) Whether State is bound by statute or can claim benefit of a provision in statute?- Held, state can claim benefit but it depends on intention of legislature in framing statute - It would still be necessary to ascertain whether intention of legislature was to make relevant provisions applicable to State - State is not bound by a statute unless it is so provided in express terms or by necessary implication - After reading all relevant provisions of statute, court is satisfied that by necessary implication obligation imposed by statute should be enforced against State, that conclusion must be adopted - It is necessary also to enquire whether conclusion that State is not bound by specific provision of a given statute, would hamper working of statute, or would lead to anomalous position that statute may lose its efficacy - Court must adopt usual rule of construction, it must not read s. 3 in isolation, but must consider it in its proper setting and must have due regard for other provisions of Act, and its general scheme and purpose - (B) Whether state can issue orders u/s. 3(1) of T. N. Essential Articles Control and Requisitioning Act (Temporary Powers), 1949 ?- Further held, it can - It cannot be contended that state has no power to issue orders u/s. 3(1) when state itself is supplier of essential articles viz electrical energy - If object which s. 3 (1) has in mind is equitable distribution and availability at fair prices of essential articles that object would still continue to attract provisions of s. 3 (1) even though essential article may be produced by State and may be supplied by it to consumers - Words, used in s. 3 (1) are so clear, unambiguous and wide that it would be unreasonable to limit their scope artificially on ground that by giving effect to wide language of section, court might reach a result which is not completely harmonious or consistent with assumed object and purpose of Act - The argument that s. 3(1) does not confer on State power to modify terms of agreements between it and consumers cannot be accepted as correct - When State wants to purchase essential articles it can regulate prices in that behalf by means of a notified order issued u/s. 3(1) and that shows that in cases of both sale and purchase of essential articles by State, s. 3(2)(b) r/w s. 3( 1) would clothe State with power to issue relevant notified order - (C) Whether power to regulate u/s 3(1) includes power to enhance rate ?- Further held, power to regulate u/s 3(1) includes power to enhance rate - Word regulate is wide enough to confer power on State to regulate either by increasing rate or decreasing rate, test being what is it that is necessary or expedient to be done to maintain, increase, or secure supply of essential articles in question and to arrange for its equitable distribution and its availability at fair prices - Challenge to validity of orders increasing agreed tariff rate on ground that they are outside purview of s. 3(1) cannot be sustained - (D) Whether s. 3(1) of T. N. Essential Articles Control and Requisitioning Act (Temporary Powers) contravenes art. 19 of Constitution?- Further held, order u/s. 3(1), by state Govt. increasing agreed tariff rate for supply of electricity to its consumers is valid, reasonable and in interests of general public - Orders of state Govt. increasing agreed rate of tariff for supply of electricity to its consumers have been notified by virtue of powers conferred on state by s. 3(1) of Madras Essential Articles Control and Requisitioning (Temporary Powers) Act - Orders are reasonable and in interest of public and cannot be challenged as contravening arts. 19(1)(f) and 19(1)(g) of Constitution - Appeal dismissed.
