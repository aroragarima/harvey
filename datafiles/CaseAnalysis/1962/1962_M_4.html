Â M. G. Agarwal v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

24 April 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 520; AIR 1963 SC 200; 1963 CRLJ 235; [1963] 2 S.C.R. 405

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Board Of Revenue Act, 1913

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Board of Revenue Act, 1913 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898, s. 423(1)(a) - Constitution of India, 1950, art.136 - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 3 and 60 - (A) Whether before reversing a judgment of acquittal, High Court must necessarily consider findings recorded therein? - Held, not necessarily - In dealing with such appeals, High Court bears in mind presumption of innocence in favour of an accused person and has been given benefit of a reasonable doubt - HC is slow in disturbing finding of fact recorded by trial Court, particularly based on an appreciation of oral evidence because trial Court has advantage of watching demeanor of witnesses who have given evidence - But HC is undoubtedly entitled to reach its own conclusion upon evidence adduced by prosecution in respect of guilt or innocence of accused - (B) What is question which Supreme Court has to ask itself, in appeals against conviction by HC when trial court has acquitted? - Further held, whether on material produced by prosecution, HC was justified in reaching conclusion that prosecution case against appellants had been proved beyond a reasonable doubt and that contrary view taken by trial Court was erroneous - Supreme Court should consider salient and broad features of evidence in order to appreciate grievance made by appellants against conclusions of HC - But u/art. 136 Supreme Court would ordinarily be reluctant to interfere with findings of fact recorded by HC particularly where said findings are based on appreciation of oral evidence - (C) Whether circumstantial evidence can be reasonably made basis of an accused persons conviction? - Further held, yes, it can be if it is of such a character that it is wholly inconsistent with innocence of accused and is consistent only with his guilt - If circumstances consistent either with innocence of accused or with his guilt, then accused is entitled to benefit of doubt - Appeal dismissed.
