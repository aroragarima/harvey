M/S. Usha Stud And Agricultural Farms Private Limited And Others v State Of Haryana And Others

Supreme Court of India

02 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, Kurian Joseph, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 179; (2013) 4 SCC 210; AIR 2013 SC 1282; 2013 (3) AWC 2177; 2013 (3) Bom.C.R. 1; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 122; 2013 (3) CTC 449; JT 2013 (4) SC 426; 2013 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1860; 2013 (4) MahLJ 489; 2013 (3) MPLJ 325; 2013 (2) RCR(Civil) 788; 2013(5) SCALE 118; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 645

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Punjab Scheduled Roads and Controlled Areas (Restriction of Unregulated Development) Act, 1963, Violation of provisions of the Act, Discrimination in releasing of land

Summary: (A)Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss.5A and 6(1) - Acquisition - Legality - Whether the acquisition of the appellants' land was vitiated due to violation of ss.5A and 6(1) of Act -

Held ratio of one judgment of SC was that s.5A(2) of Act, which represented statutory embodiment of the rule of audi alteram partem, gave an opportunity to the objector to make an endeavour to convince the Collector that his land was not required for the public purpose specified in the notification issued u/s. 4(1) of Act or that there were other valid reasons for not acquiring the same - Kamal Trading (P) Ltd. v. State of West Bengal 2011 Indlaw SC 803, relied on - That section also made it obligatory for the Collector to submit report(s) to the appropriate Govt. containing his recommendations on the objections, together with the record of the proceedings held by him so that the Govt. might take appropriate decision on the objections - S. 6(1) of Act provided that if the appropriate Govt. was satisfied, after considering the report, if any, made by the Collector u/s.5A(2) of Act that particular land was needed for the specified public purpose then a declaration should be made - That necessarily implied that the State Govt. was required to apply mind to the report of the Collector and take final decision on the objections filed by the landowners and other interested persons - Then and then only, a declaration could be made u/s. 6(1) of Act - Appeals allowed.

(B) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss.5A and 6(1) - Acquisition - Legality - Whether the State Govt. resorted to discrimination in the matter of release of the acquired land -

Held, , it was not in dispute that the lands owned by the appellants and four others were acquired for one and the same purpose i.e. the development of Sectors 21, 22, 23 and 23A of Gurgaon - Therefore, once the State Govt. took a conscious decision to release the lands of appellant and four others, albeit by executing agreements with them, there could be no justification whatsoever for not according similar treatment to the appellants - As would be seen, the solitary reason put forward by the respondents for not releasing the appellants' land, namely, that most of it was lying vacant was ex-facie erroneous - In that context, it would be apposite to take cognizance of the notings recorded by the officers and the Special Secretary to the Chief Minister of the State on the objections filed by the appellants - Reading of the notings made it clear that while the Additional Director and the Director, Urban Estates Department had treated the appellants' case as similar to M/s. R and others, the Chief Minister ordered the issue of notification u/s. 6(1) of Act in respect of the land of appellant No.1 by assuming that major portion of it was lying vacant. Of course, he also ordered that the vacant lands belonging to M/s. R might also be notified for acquisition - It was a different thing that in the second round also the lands owned by M/s. R and four others were released during the pendency of the writ petitions and the civil appeal filed by them - It was intriguing that while ordering the issue of notification u/s. 6(1) of Act, the Chief Minister did not even advert to the objections filed by the appellants and the report made by the Land Acquisition Collector u/s.5A(2) of Act - Appellant was totally oblivious of the fact that the appellants had already utilised substantial portion of their land for establishing Stud Farm and for other activities, like, animal husbandry, agriculture, horticulture, nursery and dairy farming and had also constructed a large number of buildings by spending crores of rupees and planted 5,000 trees - Be that as it may, the direction given by the Chief Minister for the issue of notification u/s. 6(1) of Act without considering the objections of the appellants and other relevant factors should be held as vitiated due to non application of mind - What was most surprising is that not only the Chief Minister, but the High Court also overlooked the fact that after the Chief Minister had ordered acquisition of vacant land belonging to M/s. R and others and notification dated 11.9.1990 was issued, the State Govt. and/or HUDA executed agreement with them and released the acquired land leaving out the appellants' land and in this manner they were subjected to hostile discrimination - Appeals allowed.
