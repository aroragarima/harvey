State of Kerala and others v B. Surendra Das etc.

Supreme Court of India

05 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 755; AIR 2014 SC 2762; [2014] 3 S.C.R. 1054

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution

Keywords: Kerala Abkari Act, Comptroller and Auditor-General's (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971, Haryana Panchayati Raj Act, 1994, Foreign Liquor Rules

Summary: Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 47 - Foreign Liquor Rules, r. 13 - Liquor policy - Amendment - Legality - Two amendments were introduced by the appellant-State of Kerala, in pursuance of its Abkari Policy framed in 2011- 2012, in the Foreign Liquor Rules - Firstly, the words 'three star' were omitted from r. 13(3) of these rules by Government of Kerala - Consequently, after this amendment, three star hotels not already having a licence, will not be eligible to get a bar licence for retail sale of liquor in the hotels - Secondly, no new bar hotels of any classification would be permitted to be opened (a) if they are situated within a distance of 3 kms. from existing bar hotels in a panchayat area, and (b) within a distance of 1 km. from existing bar hotels in a municipal area - Object of this Abkari Policy was to curb the rampant alcoholism in the State of Kerala - DB of the HC struck down the two amendments as unconstitutional - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, State Govt. is empowered to bring about the necessary reform, by modifying the rules, but it has got to be justified on the touchstone of the correlation between the provision and the objective to be achieved. If that correlation is not established, surely the rule will suffer from the vice of arbitrariness, and therefore will be hit by art. 14 of the Constitution. The consequences of the amendment of 2012 will be that four star and five star hotels would not be permitted to have FL-3 licences only on the ground that they are within the prohibited distance from such hotels which have poor hygiene standards, and which are not following norms laid down by the State Govt. It will surely be counter-productive to the objective of r.13(3), which is to promote tourism, as well as to the State's avowed policy of improving the health and nutrition standards of its citizens. If it is not possible for the Govt. to reduce the existing FL-1 shops, with respect to which it enjoys a monopoly, it is of no use for it to direct the private sector alone to function in a particular manner. The Govt. must as well behave in conformity with the mandate of art. 47 of the Constitution. The judgment rendered by the HC set aside to the extent it interferes with the amendment brought in the year 2011. The deletion of three star hotels from the category of hotels eligible for FL3 licenses u/r. 13(3) is held valid. Amendment brought in 2012 introducing the distance rule by way of addition of r. (3E) in r. 13(3) is held to be bad in law. Appeals partly allowed.
