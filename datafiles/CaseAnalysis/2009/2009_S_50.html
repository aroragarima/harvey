Biswanath Agarwalla v Sabitri Bera and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 August 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Deepak Verma

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1087; (2009) 15 SCC 693; 2009 (77) ALR 183; 2009 (3) AWC 2911B; 2011 (1) CLT(SC) 206; JT 2009 (10) SC 538; 2009 (4) RCR(Civil) 1; 2009 (2) RCR(Rent) 277; 2010 (109) RD 200; 2009(11) SCALE 12; [2009] 12 S.C.R. 459

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Review Application, Licensor And Licensee, Rent Receipt, Trespasser, Appeal4 allowed

Summary: Civil Procedure - Civil Procedure Code, 1908 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s.106 - Suit for eviction - Filed by plaintiff/respondent no. 1 against defendant/appellant - Defendant/appellant denied and disputed tenancyship and claimed title and possession over suit premises on basis of agreement for sale of year 1970 entered into with one AWM - Case of plaintiffs/respondents was that they had purchased suit premises in 1980 from one SR son of AWM and that defendant/appellant was a tenant under SR, which tenancyship was expired in 1990 - Before filing suit plaintiff/respondent no.1 had served notice u/s. 106 of TP Act - Trial Court denied the decree on ground that there was no landlord-tenant relationship, but held plaintiffs/respondents to be owner of suit premises - In appeal, 1st appellate Court decreed the suit on basis of general title/ownership of plaintiffs/respondents - High Court in 2nd appeal upheld decision of 1st appellate Court - HC said that appellant/defendant had denied tenancy and thus at most he can be a licensee, and if he is a licensee he cannot challenge title of plaintiffs/respondents as per s.116 of Evidence Act - HC further said that as tenancy was unproved and appellant/defendant had never claimed to be licensee, possibility was that, that he was a permitted occupant and if he was a permitted occupant he cannot be regarded as having adverse possessory right; on this premise HC gave finding that only remaining possibility was that that appellant/defendant was a trespasser, and it is far to say that a trespasser can challenge title of landlord - (A) Whether a Civil Court can pass a decree on ground that defendant is a trespasser in a simple suit for eviction? - Held, landlord if is unable to prove relationship of landlord and tenant can still obtain a decree if he proves his general title; but in such situation a defendant is entitled to raise contention that he had acquired an indefeasible title by adverse possession - (B) Finding of HC as to trespasser and decision thereupon, whether proper? - Held, no - Issue as to whether defendant was a trespasser or not, was required to be framed - Non-framing of such issue had prejudiced case of defendant/ appellant - Courts below have decided against defendant/appellant that he was not put in possession by predecessor-in-interest of plaintiffs/respondents in terms of agreement for sale; if he was not able to prove agreement, he could have taken other plea - Court exercised jurisdiction u/art. 142, Constitution of India and issued directions inter alia for framing of appropriate issues - Directions issued - Appeal allowed.
