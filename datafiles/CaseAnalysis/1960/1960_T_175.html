Supreme Court of India

03 October 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 143; AIR 1961 SC 264; 1961 CRLJ 326; 1983 ECR 1935; 1983 (13) E.L.T. 1321; 1962 (2) SCJ 549; [1961] 1 S.C.R. 933

Case Digest

Subject: Customs

Keywords: Evidence Act, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, Land Customs Act, 1924, Sea Customs Act 1878, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949

Summary: Customs - Bombay Prohibition Act, 1949 - Sea Customs Act, 1878, ss. 167(8), 168 and 171A - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898 - Land Customs Act, ss. 5 and 7 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Land Customs Act, 1924, ss. 5 and 7 - Constitution of India, 1950 - The appellant is at present a resident of Barmer in State of Rajasthan - Deputy Superintendent, Land Customs Station, Barmer, conducted a search of appellant's house and recovered articles worth Rs.46, 500 - Asst. Collector, Ajmer, gave notice' to appellant to show cause and explain why goods seized from him should not be confiscated u/s. 167(8) of Sea Customs Act, 1878 and s. 7 of Land Customs Act - Appellant in his reply stated that items No. 5 supra were brought by appellant from Pakistan after partition of country in 1947 and that items 6 to 10 were purchased by him bonafide for value in Barmer - Collector of Central Excise held, that appellant had failed to establish that items 1 to 5 had been brought by him to India in year 1947 and he also did not accept plea of appellant in regard to items 6 to 10 that he was a bonafide purchaser of them - All goods were imported into India in contravention of : (i) s. 3 of Import Export Control Act r/w ss. 19 and 167(8) of Sea Customs Act, 1878; (ii) ss. 4 and 5 of Land Customs Act r/w s. 7 - Order of confiscation of said articles under s. 167(8) of Sea Customs Act, 1878 and s. 7 of Land Customs Act - Aggrieved by said order, appellant preferred an appeal to Central Board of Revenue - It rejected the plea of appellant mainly on basis of a statement alleged to have been made by him at time of seizure of said articles - Revision filed by appellant to Central Govt. was also dismissed - Thereafter, appellant filed a writ petition under art. 226 of Constitution of India, 1950 in High Court of Punjab but it was dismissed by a Division Bench of HC - (A) Whether burden of proof is on customs authorities to bring home, guilt of person alleged to have committed a particular offence under said Acts by adducing satisfactory evidence? - (B) Whether s. 106 of Evidence Act, 1872 apply to a proceeding under said Acts? - Held, u/s. 106, when any fact is especially within knowledge of any person, burden of proving that fact is upon him - This Court in Shambu Nath Mehra v. State of Ajmer ([1956] S C R 1991956 SCR 199 ), after considering earlier Privy Council decisions on interpretation of s. 106 of Evidence Act, 1872 it was held that section cannot be used to undermine well established rule of law, save in a very exceptional class of case, burden is on prosecution and never shifts, if s. 106 of Evidence Act, 1872 is applied, then, by analogy, fundamental principles of criminal jurisprudence must equally be invoked - If so, it follows that onus to prove case against appellant is on customs authorities and they failed to discharge that burden in respect of items 1 to 5 - Confiscation was bad in respect of items 1 to 5, amount of penalty of Rs.25,000 should proportionately be reduced - But it cannot reduce amount, as under s. 183 of Sea Customs Act, 1878 amount has to be fixed by concerned officer as he thinks fit - But as basis of order partially disappears, the Court has given liberty to appellant to apply to customs authorities for giving him an option to redeem confiscated goods on payment of a lesser amount, having regard to changed circumstances - Order of confiscation relating to items 1 to 5 is set aside - Appeal partly allowed.
