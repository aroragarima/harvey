Vohra Sadikbhai Rajakbhai and others v State of Gujarat and others

Supreme Court of India

10 May 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 371; AIR 2016 SC 2429; 2016 (117) ALR 889; 2016 (3) AWC 3137; 2016 (2) CLT(SC) 314; 2016 (4) G.L.R. 3510; JT 2016 (5) SC 174; 2016 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 2797; 2016 (5) MLJ 10; 2016 (3) RCR(Civil) 107; 2016(5) SCALE 534; [2016] 2 S.C.R. 772; 2016 (3) WLN(SC) 11

Case Digest

Subject: Tort; Water

Keywords: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Administrative

Summary: Tort - Water - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 80 - Negligence - Principle of Strict Liability - Water released from dam flooded land of appellants and destroyed plantation therein - Aggrieved, appellants sent legal notice to respondents claiming compensation for damage done to standing trees on said land and later filed civil suit in 1998 against respondents for compensation of Rs.21,50,000 - However, Trial Court dismissed suit holding inter alia that appellants could not prove that they had suffered damage and loss due to negligence on part of respondents - Aggrieved, appellants preferred appeal before HC but the same was also dismissed vide impugned judgment dt.27-6-2011 holding that it was force majeure circumstance and hence, appellants were not entitled to any compensation - Hence, instant appeal - Whether damage to the property of appellants was result of inevitable accident.

Held, it was incumbent upon respondents to demonstrate, by adequate evidence, that the water in the dam was kept at reasonable and proper level to take care of normal rains; the rains in the said monsoon season were more than the ordinary rains which could not be foreseen; and that the public purpose was served in taking the decision to release the water which prevented larger catastrophe. Further, respondents have not refuted averment of appellants that there was negligence on part of respondents in not taking care of forthcoming monsoon season and keeping the water level in the dam at sufficiently low level to absorb the rainfall which was going to rise the water level in the dam. Merely saying that level of water in dam increased because of monsoon rains and that water was released in public interest could not be treated as discharging the burden on the part of respondents in warding off the allegation of negligence. Further, in view of principle laid down in Rylands v. Fletcher, onus was on respondents to discharge such a burden, and it has miserably failed to discharge the same. Hence, there is negligence on part of respondents which caused damage to fields of appellants. Further, loss is not only on account of rain, though a part thereof could be attributed to the nature, but also due to negligence on part of respondent authorities in not taking due precautions in time which could have avoided some loss if not entirely. Hence, damages to tune of Rs.5,00,000 were awarded to appellants. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - An act of God is that which is a direct, violent, sudden and irresistible act of nature as could not, by any amount of ability, have been foreseen, or if foreseen, could not by any amount of human care and skill have been resisted.
