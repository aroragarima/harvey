Â Seth Nand Lal and Another v State of Haryana and Others

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

V.D. Tulzapurkar, Y.V. Chandrachud, P.N. Bhagwati, V.R. Krishna Iyer, A.P. Sen

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 461; (1980) Supp SCC 574; AIR 1980 SC 2097; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1181

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Northern India Canal and Drainage Act, 1873, Punjab State Tubewell Act, 1954

Summary: Land & Property - Haryana Ceiling on Land Holdings Act, 1972 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31 A, 245 and 246 - Haryana Ceiling on Land Holdings Rules, 1973, r. 5(2) - (A) Constitutionality of - Held, Statute enacted by a Legislature falling within its competence which does not offend any Fundamental Rights - Does not contravene any other provision of Constitution - Not ultra vires either on ground that its provisions were vague, or uncertain or ambiguous or mutually inconsistent - It is duty of Court to examine language of law, context in which it was made, to discover intention of Legislature and to interpret law to make effective and not to frustrate legislative intent - Court could always call in aid well-known canons of interpretation and even where provisions of a statute appeared to be mutually inconsistent there were several well-known rules of interpretation to guide Court in giving a proper meaning to such provisions - (B) Amendments of the Act - Immunity from arts. 14, 19 and 31 - Held, Act with its amendments squarely falls within art. 31 A and is immune from attack on ground of violation of arts. 14, 19 and 31 - On basis of justificatory material produced by State Govt. provisions laying down artificial definition of family in s. 3(f) and double standard for fixing permissible area in s. 4 could not be said to be violative of art. 14 - (C) Whether ss. 8 and 9 are violative of art. 14? - Held, no - No question of any discrimination resulting to wife would arise - If husband's behaviour is guided by self-interest, as it would normally be, then he will not transfer his land in contravention of s. 9(1) because he would be doing so at his own peril for he would not only be putting his own land into jeopardy of litigation but also lose his wife's land which will become surplus and vest in State Govt. - (D) Scope of r. 5(2) - Held, r. 5(2) of Haryana Ceiling on Land Holdings Rules, 1973 only prescribes basis of evaluation - Effective ceiling is prescribed by s. 4 - R. 5(2) is not ultra vires s. 4(1) - (E) Illusory nature of s. 16 - Held, rates of compensation payable in respect of surplus land are based on actual quality of soil and its yield and cannot be said to be illusory - (F) Maintainability of Revision - Held, challenge to s. 18(7) imposing a condition of making a deposit of a sum equal to 30 times land holdings tax payable in respect of disputed area before any appeal or revision is entertained by appellate or revisional authority must fail - First, object of imposing condition is prevent frivolous appeals and revision that impede implementation of ceiling policy - Secondly, u/ss. 18(8) and (9) cash deposit or bank guarantee is not by way of any exaction but in nature of securing mesne profits from person who is ultimately found to be in unlawful possession of land - Thirdly, deposit or guarantee is correlated to land holdings tax and varies in Haryana around a paltry amount of Rs. 8 per acre annually - Fourthly, deposit to be made or bank guarantee to be furnished is confined to land holdings tax payable in respect of area or part thereof which is declared surplus after leaving permissible area to appellant or petitioner - Appeals, Writ Petitions and SLPs dismissed.
