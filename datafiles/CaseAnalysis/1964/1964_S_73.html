Â Hari Charan Kurmi and Jogia Hajam v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

03 February 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, K.C. Das Gupta, J.C. Shah, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 114; AIR 1964 SC 1184; 1964 CRLJ 344; [1964] 52 ITR 443; 1964 (2) SCJ 454; [1964] 6 S.C.R. 623

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 30 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 396 - Appellants along with four other persons charged u/s. 396 for committing dacoity and during course they committed murder - Whether High Court erred in law in treating confession made by co-accused substantive evidence against appellants? - It is recognised principle of administration of criminal law that confession of co-accused person cannot be treated as substantive evidence and can be used only when court is inclined to accept other evidence and feels necessity of seeking for an assurance in support of its conclusion deducible, from said evidence - In criminal cases where other evidence adduced against accused person is wholly unsatisfactory and prosecution seeks to rely on confession of a co-accused person, presumption of innocence which is basis of criminal jurisprudence assists accused person and compels Court to render verdict that charge is not proved against him, and so, he is entitled to benefit of doubt - Held, orders of conviction and sentence passed against two appellants are set aside - Appeals allowed.
