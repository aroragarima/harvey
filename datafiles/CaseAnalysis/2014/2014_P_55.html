Madhya Pradesh State Legal Services Authority v Prateek Jain and another

Supreme Court of India

10 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 606; (2014) 10 SCC 690; 2014 (4) BC 392; 2014 (5) Bom.C.R. 756; 2014 (3) JabLJ 243; JT 2014 (10) SC 413; 2014 (3) KLT 1102; 2015 (2) MahLJ 506; 2014 (8) MLJ 232; 2015 (2) MPLJ 104; 2014 (4) RCR(Criminal) 178; 2014(10) SCALE 407; [2014] 7 S.C.R. 743

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Dishonor of cheque

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 320 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 147 - Dishonor of Cheque - Settlement - Compoundability - Respondent no. 1 filed a complaint u/s. 138 of the Act against respondent no. 2 - Matter was settled between parties - Lok Adalat did not give imprimatur to said settlement and passed order accordingly - Respondent no. 2 filed petition before HC and the same was dismissed - Hence instant appeal -

Held, It is thus manifestly the framing of 'Guidelines' in judgment was also to achieve a particular public purpose. Here comes the issue for consideration as to whether these guidelines are to be given a go by when a case is decided/settled in Lok Adalat, Our answer is that it may not be necessarily so and a proper balance can be struck taking care of both the situations. It would be for parties, particularly accused person, to make out plausible case for the waiver/reduction of costs and to convince concerned Court about the same. This course of actionwould strike balance between two competing but equally important interests, namely, achieving the objectives delineated on the one hand and public interest which is sought to be achieved by encouraging settlements/resolution of case through Lok Adalats. Parties had already settled matter and purpose of going to Lok Adalat was only to have a rubber stamp of Lok Adalat in form of its imprimatur thereto, No error was found in impugned judgment passed by HC. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio: S. 147 of the Act did not carry any guidance on how to proceed with compounding of the offences under the Act and s. 320 of CrPC could not be followed in strict sense in respect of offences pertaining to s. 138 of the Act , there was a legislative vacuum which prompted the Court to frame guidelines.
