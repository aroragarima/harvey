Â Raja Sri Sailendra Narayanbhanja Deo v State of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

03 February 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

Sudhi Ranjan Das, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, B. Jagannadhadas, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 15; AIR 1956 SC 346; 1956 (22) CutLT 251; 1956 SCJ 449; [1956] 1 S.C.R. 72

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Fishing rights

Summary: Land & Property - Registration Act, 1908 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Orissa Estates Abolition Act, 1952 - Land Registration Act, 1876 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Bengal Land Registration Act, 1876 - Supreme Court Rules, 1950 - (A) Whether plaintiff's land is an 'estate' within meaning of Orissa Estates Abolition Act? - Held, in order to be an 'estate', land must be held by an 'intermediary' and must be included under one entry in any of general registers of revenue-paying lands and revenue-free lands and such general registers must be prepared and maintained under law for time being in force - If name of Killa Kanika was borne on revenue-roll of district, Collector would be bound to enter same in Part I of general register prepared and kept up by him u/s 4 - S. 20 of the Act provides that until registers by that Act directed to be prepared were so prepared, existing registers then kept up in office of every Collector should be deemed to be registers kept up under Bengal Land Registration Act, 1876 - Prior to 1876, land registers used to be maintained under Bengal Regulation XLVIII of 1793 as amended by Bengal Regulation VII of 1800 - Existing registers mentioned in s. 20 of Bengal Land Registration Act, 1876, clearly refer to registers kept under those Regulations and s. 20 gives a statutory validity to registers kept under those Regulations - (B) Whether plaintiff is estopped by compromise decree passed by High Court on 02-05-1945 from contending that his land is not an 'estate' within meaning of Abolition Act? - Held, plea of estoppel was pointedly raised and made subject matter of an issue before High Court and was elaborately dealt with by High Court in its judgment under appeal - In circumstances we do not consider it proper to shut out this plea of estoppel - Compromise decree precludes plaintiff from re-asserting title, which had been negatived by compromise decree although it related only to his claim to a part of lands, namely beds of rivers therein mentioned - One of main considerations for compromise, was clear admission on part of plaintiff in that case that his status in respect of Killa Kanika was nothing more than that of a proprietor of a permanently settled estate liable to pay land revenue - Appeal dismissed.
