Mahamadkhan Nathekhan v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

10 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

C. Nagappan, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 675; (2014) 14 SCC 589; JT 2015 (5) SC 225; 2014(10) SCALE 480; [2014] 7 S.C.R. 777

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Conviction

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 120(B), 201 r/w s. 34 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25(A), 25(1)(a) - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) were charged for offence u/ss. 302, 120(B) and 201 of IPC and s. 25(A) of the Act - Trial Court acquitted appellants from all the charges - On appeal, HC found appellant nos. 1 and 3 were guilty of offence u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC and appellant no. 2 found guilty for offence u/s. 302 r/w s. 114 of IPC and u/s. 25(1)(a) of the Act and sentenced them to undergo RI for life - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contended that HC elaborately considered the evidence on record and concluded that prosecution has the guilt of the accused persons and conviction and sentence imposed on appellants are sustainable.

Held, relationship deceased had with A1 and 3 was very cordial. It is in evidence that A3 would frequently visit the shop of deceased and was helping deceased in improving his Tyre business. In fact, A3 was reluctant to go to 'S' in the night on 20-2-1984 on account of cold climate and suggested to leave the next day morning. Deceased having come fully prepared for the trip with his suitcase, compelled A1 and 3 to leave the same night by car to 'S'. Much reliance was placed by HC on the information of the FSL expert about the presence of nitrate in the hand-wash of accused no.1. A1 was seated in the back seat along with deceased when the gun fired. PW7, Ballistic expert has testified that on the back seat of the car and on the doors of the left side glass pane nitrite was found. Having seated in the back seat it is but natural that nitrate was found in the hands of A1 but from that no inference can be drawn that he used the gun and was responsible for injury. The HC treated the statement of A3 given u/s. 313 of CrPC as evidence against him. This is clearly impermissible in law. Deceased died of gunshot injury is established by the medical evidence. The Trial Court elaborately considered the evidence and held that there was possibility of accidental death rather than homicidal death. The homicidal death has not been established in the facts of the case. There was no motive for the occurrence. Though large amounts are said to be due from the customers at 'S', there is no evidence of record in the form of books of accounts. Practically, there is no evidence on this aspect. HC misread the material evidence and reversed the decision of Trial Court by convicting the accused. The conviction and sentence are liable to be set aside. Appeals allowed.

Ratio - When the prosecution case rests purely on circumstantial evidence, motive plays an important part in order to tilt the scale against the accused.
