Union of India and another v Cipla Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

21 October 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 808; (2017) 5 SCC 262; AIR 2016 SC 5025; 2017 (1) AWC 705; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 891; 2016(10) SCALE 255; [2016] 7 S.C.R. 523

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Health & Drug

Keywords: Conversion Cost, Bulk Drug, Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940, Essential Commodities Act, 1955, Formulations, Analysis, Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1987, Prescription, Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1995, Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1979, Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1970

Summary: (A) Health & Drug - Administrative - Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1995 - Notifications -Packaging material costs - Challenged - Appellants filed instant appeals challenging impugned order of HC wherein HC held that notifications issued by appellant no. 1/UOI on 12-7-2000, 12-7-2001, 12-7-2002 and 11-7-2003 re-notifying the norms prescribed by notification dt. 13-7-1999 issued under para 7 of the 1995 Order on the basis of which the retail price of formulations was fixed under para 8 of the 1995 Order were issued mechanically and without any application of mind - Whether notification dt. 13-7-1999 issued by UOI under para 7 of the 1995 Order prescribing norms for conversion cost, packing charges and process loss of raw materials (other than packing materials in conversion) and packing and process loss of packing materials in packaging was issued mechanically and without any application of mind or is it valid in law.

Held, perusal of Report of Masood and Jharwal Committees clearly brings out that UOI initiated a detailed exercise for prescribing the norms but unfortunately the drug industry did not extend its full cooperation in the exercise and declined to provide necessary information and data despite requests and reminders. Therefore, UOI was left with no alternative but to notify the norms in compliance with the provisions of the 1995 Order on the basis of materials already available. However, Masood Committee did some useful work by recommending the norms for cost of packing material but that could not be acted upon by UOI in isolation and in the absence of norms on process loss. A significant observation of Masood Committee related to stipulation for a yearly notification of norms in terms of the 1995 Order. Those recommendations were then examined and considered by UOI and on the basis of expert conclusions arrived at, the norms were prescribed by a notification dt. 13-7-1999 issued under para 7 of the 1995 Order. It cannot be said that notification dt. 13-7-1999 was based on no material or was issued without any application of mind. Fixing the price of any commodity is not only difficult but also tricky. Once that was appreciated, it would be realized that the task before UOI in prescribing the norms was not easy. SC disagree with HC and hold that various notifications issued under para 7 of the 1995 Order in 1999 and thereafter prescribing the norms for conversion cost, packing charges and process loss of raw materials (other than packing materials in conversion) and packing and process loss of packing materials in packaging were issued after due application of mind and based on available material duly examined by an expert body. The notifications were not arbitrarily issued nor were they discriminatory in any manner at all nor were they issued mechanically nor could it be said that they were issued without any application of mind. Appeals disposed of.

(B) Health & Drug - Administrative - Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1995 - Norms re-determined - Permissibility - Whether notifications issued by UOI on 12-7-2000, 12-7-2001, 12-7-2002 and 11-7-2003 under para 7 of the 1995 Order re-notifying the norms prescribed on 13-7-1999 were issued mechanically, without any application of mind and without re-determining the norms every year as required by the 1995 Order were valid in law.

Held, concept or principle of re-notification was not unheard of and there was no illegality per se in re-issuing the norms without any change, but the reasons for re-notification ought to exist and re-notification should not be a short-cut method to be routinely employed. On the one hand, there was virtual non-cooperation from drug industry in providing information to UOI despite repeated requests and reminders even by expert Committees constituted for the purpose. On the other there was a perceived statutory obligation on UOI to notify the norms every year and that responsibility could not be effectively discharged without the cooperation of the drug industry. Therefore, UOI faced with an extra-ordinary situation and a stalemate putting the consumers of an essential commodity at the mercy of the drug industry, had no option but to re-notify the existing norms in public interest on the basis of the available material. The overall attitude of drug industry appears to be one of profit making or preserving commercial interests, while the concern should really be of promoting consumer interest. Faced with those competing interests, UOI sided with consumer and cannot be faulted for it. Thus, re-notification of prescribed norms in the period 2000 to 2003 was not mechanical or without any application of mind. The materials were before UOI and there was no change in the content of materials. If there was, drug industry failed to effectively point it out as a result of their non-cooperative attitude. SC hold that re-notification of prescribed norms were per se not impermissible and in instant case it was justified in the circumstances. Appeals disposed of.

(C) Health & Drug - Administrative - Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1995 - Ceiling price formulations - Applicability - Whether various notifications issued by UOI fixing the retail price or ceiling price of formulations under paras 8 and 9 of the 1995 Order without determining the norm for cost of packing material as required by para 7 of the 1995 Order were valid in law.

Held, norms fixed by UOI were of general application, they were not intended to benefit or harm any particular manufacturer or formulator and indeed no manufacturer or formulator was required to be heard (or was heard) in determination they were notified in Official Gazette for the information of general public and in arriving at the norms the general attributes of legislative activity were attended to by UOI for the benefit of consumers. The notification of the norms has the character of legislative activity. The statutory criterion for price fixing was the formula given in para 7 of the 1995 Order. Whether such formula was operated as it should be was subject to judicial review. Therefore, while operating the formula, if UOI did not take conversion cost into consideration (for example) or took into consideration some factor not in the formula then, Court could certainly strike down the retail price or the ceiling price so fixed by UOI on the ground that relevant factors were ignored or irrelevant factors were taken into consideration. No such allegation was made and no such contention was advanced by counsel for respondents. Therefore, SC need not dwell on that aspect. Appeals disposed of.

(D) Health & Drug - Administrative - Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 1995 - Retail price fixation - Formulation - Validity - Whether fixing the retail price of a formulation under para 8 of the 1995 Order without first fixing the sale price of a bulk drug under para 3 of the 1995 Order utilized in the manufacture of a formulation was valid in law.

Held, there was no obligation on UOI to fix the maximum sale price of every bulk drug, whether it was in the First Schedule to the 1995 Order or not. In fact, if a bulk drug was not in First Schedule to the 1995 Order UOI was not empowered to fix its maximum sale price. There could also be a situation where a formulation consists of two or more drugs, one of which was not a scheduled drug. In that event, if the contention of counsel for respondents was accepted then it would mean that retail price or ceiling price of that formulation cannot be fixed. That was surely not the intention of the 1995 Order nor was it a possible manner of reading the 1995 Order. If the 1995 Order were to be read in the suggested manner, then every drug would have to be included in the First Schedule to the 1995 Order as a pre-condition to fixing the retail price or ceiling price of a formulation which contains that drug. That would be doing utmost violence to the plain provisions of the 1995 Order and counsel for respondents has unfortunately overlooked that a formulation can contain one or more bulk drugs including a bulk drug not included in the First Schedule to the 1995 Order. The action of a repository of power was amenable to judicial review if it was contrary to or violates mandatory requirement of a subordinate legislation. Therefore, if UOI does not adhere to the formula given in para 7 of the 1995 Order and fixes the retail price or ceiling price of formulations without following the formula laid down, notification issued by UOI under para 8 or 9 of the 1995 Order was liable to quashed as being contrary to law. However, no instance was pointed out to SC compelling it to use power of judicial review and quash the notifications under consideration. Impugned judgments and orders of HC were set aside. Appeals disposed of.
