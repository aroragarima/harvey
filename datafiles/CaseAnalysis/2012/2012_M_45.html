Krishi Upaj Mandi Samiti, Narsinghpur v M/S. Shiv Shakti Khansari Udyog And Others

Supreme Court of India

30 August 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 454; (2012) 9 SCC 368; AIR 2012 SC 3511; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 416

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Practice & Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950, Madhya Pradesh Vanijyik Kar Adhiniyam, 1994, Madhya Pradesh Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Act, 1958, Punjab Village Common Lands (Regulation) Act, 1953, Karnataka Rice Procurement (Levy) Order, 1984, Sugarcane (Control) Order, Madhya Pradesh Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Rules, 1959

Summary: Administrative - Trade - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 254(2) - Madhya Pradesh Krishi Upaj Mandi Adhiniyam, 1972 - Madhya Pradesh Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Act, 1958, ss. 12, 15, 16, 19, 20, 21 and 22 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955, s. 3 - Market fee - Levied - Legality - Challenged - Respondents were operating sugar factories in different market areas of the State and have been purchasing sugarcane from Cane Growers and Cane Growers' Co-operative Societies - Thus, they were covered by the general sweep of the Market Act because sugarcane was a notified agricultural produce and by virtue of s. 19, the Market Committees were empowered to levy market fee on the transactions involving purchase of sugarcane - Respondents filed writ petitions for quashing the notices issued by the Market Committees requiring them to take licence under the Market Act and to pay market fee on the purchase of sugarcane, by asserting that the provisions of the Market Act were not applicable to the transactions which were exclusively governed by the 1958 Act (Sugarcane Act) and the Sugarcane (Control) Order (the Control Order) issued by the Central Govt. u/s. 3 of the 1955 Act - DB held that transactions involving the sale and purchase of sugarcane are governed by ss. 12, 15, 16, 19, 20, 21 and 22 of the Sugarcane Act and Cls. (3), (4), (5), (5A) and (6) of the Control Order, which were in the nature of special legislations vis-a-vis the Market Act and, as such, market fee cannot be levied by the Market Committees - Whether the provisions of the Market Act applicable to the transactions involving the purchase of sugarcane by the factories operating in the market areas of the State and whether market fee could be levied on such transactions - Held, analysis of the provisions of the Sugarcane Act and the Control Order along with the Market Act brings to fore the conflict between the three statutes insofar as they relate to the transactions involving sale of sugarcane by Cane Growers / Cane Growers' Co-operative Societies to the occupiers of factories - While the Sugarcane Act and the Rules framed thereunder constitute a complete code for regulating the supply of sugarcane by Cane Growers and Cane Growers' Co-operative Societies to the occupiers of the factories at the purchasing centres established and maintained by them and payment of price without delay, the Market Act regulates sale and purchase of notified agricultural produce in the market yards specified for the particular produce or at other places provided in the bye-laws and mandates that the price of the notified agricultural produce should be settled by tender bid or open auction system - Control Order not only lays down the mechanism for determination of the minimum price of sugarcane payable by the producers of sugar or their agents for the sugarcane purchased by them, but also prescribes the mode of payment of the price - Sugarcane Act and the Rules framed thereunder also prescribe the mode of payment of the price by the occupier of the factory - Likewise, the Market Act contained provisions for payment of the price of the notified agricultural produce brought into the market yard for sale - It was thus evident that so far as sugarcane was concerned, there was direct conflict between the provisions of the Sugarcane Act and the Market Act both, in matters relating to sale and purchase of sugarcane, and, payment of price - Likewise, there was conflict between the Control Order and the Market Act in the matter of determination of price of the sugarcane and mode of payment - Though, there was no significant difference in the Control Order and the Market Act insofar as the mode of payment of the price of sugarcane was concerned, but the mechanism enshrined in the two statutes for determination of price was vastly different - Control Order envisaged fixation of the minimum price of sugarcane by the Central Govt. after considering the factors enumerated in cl. 3 and consulting such authorities, bodies or associations as it may think fit and the producer of sugar was bound to pay at least that price to Cane Growers/Cane Growers Cooperative Societies - Market Act postulated determination of the price of the notified agricultural produce (sugarcane was only one of such produce) brought into the market yard for sale u/s. 36(3) by tender bid or open auction - In that exercise, the State Govt./the concerned Market Committee did not have any role to play - Such price could not be less than the support price declared by the State Govt. - Difference also indicated that the Control Order was a special legislation vis-a-vis the Market Act - It was clear that the State Govt. had not reserved the Market Act for Presidential assent on the ground of any repugnancy between the provisions of that Act and the Control Order - As a matter of fact, the State Govt. could not have even thought of any repugnancy between these statutes because at the relevant time, sugarcane was not treated as an agricultural produce and was not included in the Schedule appended to the Market Act - Nature and scope of Presidential assent u/art. 254(2) of Constitution was considered by the Constitution Bench in Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh 1985 Indlaw SC 403, Constitution Bench held that the assent of the President u/art. 254(2) of Constitution was not an empty formality and the President had to be apprised of the reason why his assent was being sought - Constitution Bench further held that if the assent was sought for a specific purpose, the efficacy of assent would be limited to that purpose and could not be extended beyond it, relied on - SC held that art. 254(2) of the Constitution was not available to appellants for seeking a declaration that the Market Act would prevail over the Control Order and that transactions involving the purchase of sugarcane by the factories operating in the market areas would be governed by the provisions contained in the Market Act - As a corollary, SC held that the HC did not commit any error by quashing the notices issued by appellant Market Committees to respondents requiring them to take licence under the Market Act and pay market fee on the purchase of sugarcane from Cane Growers/Cane Growers Cooperative Societies - Appeals dismissed.
