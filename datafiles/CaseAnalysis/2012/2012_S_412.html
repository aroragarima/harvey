Surinder Kumar v State Of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

21 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 616; (2012) 12 SCC 120; 2013 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 238; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 191; [2012] 9 S.C.R. 1019

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304-B, 498-A - Dying declaration - Reliability - Appellant was husband of deceased - Deceased was admitted to Hospital with burn injuries all over her body where she died - Appellant was charged for offences u/ss.304-B,498-A of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant for offences alleged against him - HC also affirmed conviction and sentence - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether dying declaration given by deceased to effect that appellant had driven her to commit suicide was voluntary and truthful - Held, there was no format prescribed for recording dying declaration - Therefore, it was not obligatory that dying declaration should be recorded in question-answer form - It was also not obligatory that either Executive Magistrate or Judicial Magistrate should be present for recording dying declaration - Dying declaration made by deceased was not under any pressure - On contrary, details given by deceased at time of her death were indicative of her consciousness and her fitness to make statement - Deceased did not introduce any exaggerations and narrated only basic and important facts, namely, about persistent demand for dowry by her husband - Further, deceased did not implicate anybody other than appellant and truthfully stated that since she was fed up with persistent demand of dowry made by him, she poured kerosene oil on herself and set herself on fire - Hence, dying declaration of deceased was voluntary and truthful - Appeal dismissed.
