Dropti Devi and another v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

02 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 204; (2012) 7 SCC 499; AIR 2012 SC 2550; 2012 (4) CTC 858; 2012 (3) RCR(Criminal) 687; 2012(6) SCALE 80; [2012] 6 S.C.R. 307

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Fema

Keywords: Specially Empowered, Constitutionality, Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Prevention of Blackmarketing and Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act, 1980, Prevention of Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Act, 1974, Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act, 1984, National Security (Amendment) Act, 1987, Bail Reform Act of 1984

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - FEMA - Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, s. 3(1) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19 and 22 - Constitutional validity of s. 3(1) - Preventive detention - Petitioners filed petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution challenging constitutional validity of s. 3(1) of Act to the extent it empowers the competent authority to make an order of detention against any person with a view to preventing him from acting in any manner prejudicial to the conservation or augmentation of foreign exchange - Petitioners contended that the change of legal regime by repeal of FERA and enactment of FEMA (the provisions contained in FEMA did not regard its violation a criminal offence) the intent and object behind the enactment of preventive detention in Act had ceased to exist and continuation of impugned provision in Act was violative of art. 21 r/w. arts 14 and 19 of the Constitution - Whether s. 3(1) of Act was constitutionally valid - Held, it was true that provisions of FERA and FEMA differ in some respects, particularly in respect of penalties - It was also true that FEMA does not have provision for prosecution and punishment like s. 56 of FERA and its enforcement for default was through civil imprisonment - However, insofar as conservation and/or augmentation of foreign exchange was concerned, the restrictions in FEMA continue to be as rigorous as they were in FERA - FEMA continues with the regime of rigorous control of foreign exchange and dealing in the foreign exchange was permitted only through authorised person - While its aim was to promote the orderly development and maintenance of foreign exchange markets in India - The conservation and augmentation of foreign exchange continues to be as important as it was under FERA - The whole intent and idea behind Act was to prevent violation of foreign exchange regulations or smuggling activities which have serious and deleterious effect on the national economy - In today's world the physical and geographical invasion may be difficult but it was easy to imperil the security of a State by disturbing its economy - The smugglers and foreign exchange manipulators by flouting the regulations and restrictions imposed by FEMA by their misdeeds and misdemeanours directly affect the national economy and thereby endanger the security of the country - In this situation, the distinction between acts where punishments were provided and the acts where arrest and prosecution were not contemplated pales into insignificance - Therefore, the relevance of provision for preventative detention of the anti-social elements indulging in smuggling and violation and manipulation of foreign exchange in Act continues even after repeal of FERA - On the touchstone of constitutional jurisprudence, as reflected by art. 22 r/w. arts 14, 19 and 21 of Constitution, SC do not think that the impugned provision was rendered unconstitutional - There was no constitutional mandate that preventive detention cannot exist for an act where such act was not a criminal offence and does not provide for punishment - An act may not be declared as an offence under law but still for such an act, which was an illegal activity, the law can provide for preventive detention if such act was prejudicial to the state security - After all, the essential concept of preventive detention was not to punish a person for what he has done but to prevent him from doing an illegal activity prejudicial to the security of the State - The law of preventative detention arms the State with precautionary action and must be seen as such - Of course, the safeguards that the Constitution and preventive detention laws provide must be strictly insisted upon whenever the Court was called upon to examine the legality and validity of an order of preventive detention - Hence, there was no merit in challenging to the constitutional validity of impugned part of s. 3(1) of Act - Petition dismissed.
