FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Mohammed Ajmal Mohammad Amir Kasab and others v State of Maharashtra and others

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Aftab Alam, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 258; (2012) 9 SCC 1; AIR 2012 SC 3565; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 3303; 2012 CRLJ 4770; 2012 (4) JCC 2929; JT 2012 (8) SC 4; 2012 (4) MLJ(Crl) 15; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 417; 2012(7) SCALE 553; [2012] 8 S.C.R. 295

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Criminal; Media & Entertainment; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Batteries, Electronic Media, Developments, Mahapalika, Right To Be Defended By a Legal Practitioner, Adaption of Laws Order, 1950

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B,121, 302 - Waging war against India - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant, a Pakistani national, along with other co-accused, allegedly waged war against Govt. of India and did terrorist acts including criminal conspiracy, murder, grievous hurt, explosion and thereby caused the death and grievous hurt of large number of people - Trial Court convicted appellant u/ss. 120B, 121 and 302 IPC and sentenced him to death, however, acquitted other two co-accused - HC, by impugned order, confirmed conviction and death sentence - Hence, instant appeal - Whether conviction of appellant u/s. 120B, 121 and 302 IPC was justifiable - Held, primary and first offence that appellant and his co-conspirators committed was the offence of waging war against Govt. of India - Attack was aimed at India and Indians by appellant foreign nationals - People were killed for no other reason than they were Indians - Conspiracy, in furtherance of which attack was made, was, inter alia, to hit at India; to hit at its financial centre; to try to give rise to communal tensions and create internal strife and insurgency; to demand that India should withdraw from Kashmir; and to dictate its relations with other countries - It was in furtherance of those objectives that attack was made, causing loss of a large number of people and injury to an even greater number of people - Hence, appellant was rightly held guilty of waging war against Govt. of India and other offences and rightly convicted u/ss. 120B, 121 and 302 IPC - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Miranda right - Applicability in India - Whether appellant was denied fair trial as against the law laid down by SC of US in Miranda v. Arizona, 384 US 436 (1966) - Held, right to silence and right to presence of an attorney granted by Miranda decision to an accused as a measure of protection against self-incrimination had no application under Indian system of law - Further, SC of US clearly acknowledged and pointed out that measures to protect accused against self-incrimination evolved by it under Miranda rules were already part of Indian statutory scheme - Provisions of CrPC and 1872 Act fully incorporate Constitutional guarantees, and that statutory framework for criminal process in India afforded fullest protection to personal liberty and dignity of an individual - Hence, no reason was found to draw any help from Miranda principles for providing protection against self-incrimination to accused - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 22(1) - Cognizable offence - Arrest - Right to access legal aid - Entitlement - Held, right to access to legal aid, to consult and to be defended by a legal practitioner, aroused when a person arrested in connection with a cognizable offence was first produced before a magistrate - It was the duty and obligation of magistrate before whom a person accused of committing a cognizable offence was first produced to make him fully aware that it was his right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner and, in case he had no means to engage a lawyer of his choice, that one would be provided to him from legal aid at the expense of State - Right flowed from arts. 21 and 22(1) of Constitution and ought to be strictly enforced - Hence, direction issued to all magistrates in the country to faithfully discharge said duty and obligation and failure to fully discharge the duty would amount to dereliction in duty and would make concerned magistrate liable to departmental proceedings - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Cognizable offence - Right of defence - Scope - Held, every accused unrepresented by a lawyer should be provided a lawyer at commencement of trial and failure to do so would vitiate trial and resultant conviction and sentence - However, failure to provide a lawyer to accused at pre-trial stage should not have the same consequence of vitiating trial - Appeal dismissed.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Waging war against India - Criminal trial - Defence by foreign lawyer - Admissibility - Whether appellant was entitled to engage a Pakistani lawyer for his defence - Held, appellant's refusal to accept services of an Indian lawyer and his demand for a lawyer from his country was his own independent decision - Demand for a Pakistani lawyer especially when Pakistan was denying that appellant was even a Pakistani citizen, might have been impractical, even foolish, but appellant certainly did not need any advice from an Indian court or authority as to his rights under Indian Constitution - Further, appellant was provided with a set of two lawyers for his choice - Hence, there was no question of any violation of any of rights of appellant under Indian Constitution - Appellant was offered services of a lawyer at time of his arrest and at all relevant stages in proceedings - Further, absence of a lawyer at pre-trial stage was not only as per wishes of appellant himself, but that absence also did not cause him any prejudice in trial - Appeal dismissed.

(F) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Advocates & Judges - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Waging war against India - Criminal trial - Insufficient time for defence preparation - Effect - Whether defence was not allowed sufficient time for preparation of the case and that denial would vitiated trial - Held, appellant's earlier counsel at Trial Court never complained about not being given sufficient time - Appellant's counsel did not appear to be a non-complaining type and raised all kinds of objections and left no opportunity to noisily protest against procedural decisions of Trial Court, yet he never complained that he was given insufficient time for preparation - Hence, it could not be said that defence was not allowed sufficient time for preparation of case and that denial vitiated trial - Appeal dismissed.

(G) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 164 - Waging war against India - Confession - Reliability - Whether confession made by appellant was liable to be eschewed from consideration due to being produced before magistrate after investigation - Held, appellant's confession was voluntary and without any influence or duress from any external agency - Further, production of accused before magistrate on a later date, even though he had expressed his willingness to make confessional statement in an earlier date, was equally legitimate and understandable - Once appellant was produced for recording of confession u/s. 164 of CrPC the law ordained magistrate to send him to judicial custody and not back on police remand - Police could not afford to lose custody of appellant at the stage of investigation - Thus, police was fully justified in producing appellant for confession only after completing its own investigation, when it no longer needed appellant in its custody - Further, appellant was not making a confessional statement from any position of weakness or resignation, or out of remorse - Appeal dismissed.

(H) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120B - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 10 - Criminal conspiracy - Proof of - Whether charge of conspiracy against appellant was fully established - Held, appellant's confession, recovery of material objects, different places of attack, bomb explosion in two locations and transcripts of phone conversations between terrorists and their collaborators and handlers from across border were sufficient to bring home charges relating to conspiracy against appellant - Appellant was actively involved in trying to fulfill objects of conspiracy - Further, transcription were fully covered by s. 10 of 1872 Act and there was no reason not to take them into consideration in support of charge of conspiracy against appellant - Appeal dismissed.

(I) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 121 - Waging war against India - 'Government of India' - Meaning - Held, expression 'Government of India' was used in s. 121 IPC to imply Indian State, juristic embodiment of sovereignty of country that derived its legitimacy from collective will and consent of its people - Thus, expression 'Government of India', as appearing in s. 121, should be held to mean the State or interchangeably the people of the country as repository of sovereignty of India which was manifested and expressed through elected Govt. - Appeal dismissed.

(J) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Waging war against India - Award of death sentence - Sustainability - Whether award of death sentence was warranted in instant case - Held, appellant told magistrate that he had absolutely no regret for whatever he had done and he wanted to make confession to set an example for others to become Fidayeen like him and follow him in his deeds - HC, too, had noticed that appellant never showed any remorse for large-scale murder committed by him - Possibility of any reform or rehabilitation of appellant was foreclosed - Instant case would not only satisfy all conditions laid down in Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab,1983 INDLAW SC 116, for imposition of death sentence but also present several other features - Case had element of conspiracy and presented element of previous planning and preparation - Case had element of waging war against Gov. of India and magnitude of war was of a degree as in no other case - Case had shocked collective conscience of Indian people as few other cases had - Number of persons killed and injured was not only staggeringly high but also as in no other or in extremely few cases - Only mitigating factor was appellant's young age, but that was completely offset by absence of any remorse on his part - Death sentence, therefore, was confirmed - Appeal dismissed.

(K) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.121 - Waging war against India - Acquittal - Sustainability - Appellants (two co-accused) allegedly waged war against Govt. of India and did terrorist acts including conspiracy, murder, grievous hurt - Trial Court acquitted appellant co-accused - HC confirmed acquittal - Hence, instant appeal by State - Held, Trial Court and HC considered evidences relating to two co-accused in far greater detail - Courts below had analyzed prosecution evidence and had given very good reasons to hold prosecution evidence unworthy of reliance to hold such grave charges against two accused - Reasons assigned by Trial Court and HC for acquitting two accused of all the charges was agreed upon - View taken by Trial Court and HC was not only correct but on the facts of case, that was only possible view - Appeal dismissed.

Obiter dictum

(L) Media & Entertainment - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 -Terrorist attack - Live coverage by TV channels - Propriety - (obiter) terrorists attacks at all places, in goriest details, were shown live on Indian TV from beginning to end almost non-stop - All channels were competing with each other in showing latest developments on a minute to minute basis, including positions and movements of security forces engaged in flushing out terrorists - Reckless coverage of terrorist attack by channels thus gave rise to a situation where on the one hand terrorists were completely hidden from security forces and they had no means to know their exact position or even the kind of firearms and explosives they possessed and on the other hand positions of security forces, their weapons and all their operational movements were being watched by collaborators across border on TV screens and being communicated to terrorists - Accused persons' operations were freely shown made task of security forces not only exceedingly difficult but also dangerous and risky - Any attempt to justify conduct of TV channels by citing right to freedom of speech and expression would be totally wrong and unacceptable in such a situation - An action tending to violate another person's right to life guaranteed u/art. 21 or putting national security in jeopardy should never be justified by taking plea of freedom of speech and expression - Thus, it was held that by covering live terrorists attack in the way it was done, Indian TV channels were not serving any national interest or social cause but acting in their own commercial interests putting national security in jeopardy - Appeal dismissed.
