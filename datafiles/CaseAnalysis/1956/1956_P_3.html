Â Pannalal Binjraj and Another v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 December 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 58; AIR 1957 SC 397; [1957] 31 ITR 565; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 233

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 5 (7A) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 19 (1) (g) - Transfer of cases - Validity of - Cases of petitioners was transferred to Income Tax Officer (ITO) by Central Board of Revenue and Commissioners of Income Tax u/s. 5(7A) of the Act whereby they instituted assessment proceedings against petitioners and passed assessment orders - Petitioners filed writ petitions challenging said transfer orders passed u/s. 5(7A) of the Act contending that s. 5(7A) of the Act was ultra vires the Constitution as s. 5(7A) of the Act invests the Commissioner of Income-tax and the Central Board of Revenue with arbitrary power to transfer any case from any one Income-tax Officer to another without any limitation in point of time, a power which was unguided and uncontrolled and was discriminatory in its nature and it was open to the Commissioner of Income-tax or the Central Board of Revenue to pick out the case of one assessee from those of others in a like situation and transfer the same from one State to another - Petitioners also contended that s. 5(7A) of the Act infringing the fundamental rights enshrined in arts. 14 and 19 (1) (g) of the Constitution - The Central Board of Revenue submitted that the s. 5(7A) of the Act was intended to minimize administrative inconvenience and there was no discrimination after transfer because the same relevant provisions of the Act as applied to other similarly situated were applied after the transfer - Whether s. 5 (7A) of the Act was ultra vires the Constitution as infringing the fundamental rights enshrined in arts. 14 and 19 (1)(g) of the Act? - Held, the power which was vested in the Commissioner of Income-tax or the Central Board of Revenue, u/s. 5 (7A) of the Act was not an arbitrary power unfettered, unguided or uncontrolled so as to enable the authority to pick and choose one assessee out to those similarly circumstanced thus subjecting him to discriminatory treatment as compared with others who fall within the same category - The power was guided and controlled by the purpose which has to be achieved by the Act itself, viz., the charge of income-tax, the assessment and collection thereof, and was to be exercised for the more convenient and efficient collection of the tax - A wide discrimination was given to the authorities concerned, for the achievement of that purpose, in the matter of the transfer of the cases of the assessees from one Income-tax Officer to another and it cannot be urged that such power which was vested in the authorities was discriminatory in its nature - Moreover, Commissioners of Income-tax and Central Board of Revenue were mainly administrative authorities over ITOs and could distribute and control work to be done by authorities - Thus, transfer of Petitioner's cases was administrative machinery which was set up for assessing incomes of assessees chargeable to income-tax and transfer of cases of petitioners from one ITO was not discriminatory in its nature - Section 5 (7A) of the Act was not violative of art. 14 of the Constitution and also does not impose any unreasonable restriction of the fundamental right to carry on trade or business enshrined in art. 19 (1) (g) of the Constitution - If there was any abuse of power it can be remedied by appropriate action either u/arts. 226 or 32 of the Constitution and what can be struck down was not the provision contained in s. 5 (7A) of the Act but the order passed thereunder which may be mala fide or violative of these fundamental rights - Writ petitions dismissed.
