Aveek Sarkar and another v State of West Bengal and others

Supreme Court of India

03 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Radhakrishnan, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 71; (2014) 4 SCC 257; AIR 2014 SC 1495; 2014 CRLJ 1560; 2014 (2) CTC 188; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 585; 2014 (1) OLR 833; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 919; 2014 (3) RLW 2449; 2014(2) SCALE 16; [2014] 2 S.C.R. 263

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Obscene, Indecent Representation of Women (Prohibition) Act, 1986, Vulgar, Canadian Criminal Code, Changing notion of obscenity, Contemporary community standards, Obscene photographs, Publication

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 292 - Indecent Representation of Women (Prohibition) Act, 1986, s. 4 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 482 - Changing notion of obscenity - Contemporary community standards - Obscene photographs - Publication - Criminal proceedings - Quashing of - A complaint u/s. 292 of IPC was filed against appellants, who were the Editors and the Publisher and Printer of a Newspaper and Magazine in question - Complaint alleged that appellants intentionally and deliberately published, printed, publicly exhibited and circulated a photograph in an inter-twined manner wherein a Tennis player placed the hand upon the breast of an actress, which was absolutely obscene and lascivious in nature and which was a criminal offence - Complainant sought prosecution of appellants for offence u/s. 292 of IPC and u/s. 4 of the Act - Magistrate issued summoned to appellants and ordered that they would be put to face the trial for offence u/s. 292 of IPC and u/s. 4 of the Act - Aggrieved appellants filed a revision petition before HC u/s. 482 for quashing of criminal proceedings, which was dismissed by impugned judgment - Aggrieved appellants filed instant appeal - Appellants contended that publication in question and photograph taken, as a whole and in the background of facts and circumstances, could not be said to be per se 'obscene' within the meaning of s. 291(1) of IPC so as to remand a trial of appellants in respect of alleged offence u/s. 292(1) of IPC and that obscenity has to be judged in the context of contemporary social mores, current socio-moral attitude of the community and the prevalent norms of acceptability/susceptibility of the community, in relation to matters in issue - Whether photograph of Tennis player with his fiancÃ©e, standing close to each other bare bodied but covering the breast of his fiancÃ©e with his hands could be stated to be objectionable in the sense it violated s. 292 of IPC -

Held, to apply 'community standard test' rather than 'Hicklin test' to determine what was 'obscenity' - A bare reading of s. 292(1) of IPC, make clear that a picture or article should be deemed to be obscene (i) if it was lascivious; (ii) it appeals to the prurient interest, and (iii) it tends to deprave and corrupt persons who were likely to read, see or hear the matter, alleged to be obscene - Once the matter was found to be obscene, question might arise as to whether impugned matter fall within any of the exceptions contained in s. 292 of IPC - A picture of a nude/semi-nude woman, as such, could not per se be called obscene unless it had the tendency to arouse feeling or revealing an overt sexual desire - Picture should be suggestive of deprave mind and designed to excite sexual passion in persons who were likely to see it, which would depend on the particular posture and the background in which the nude/semi-nude woman was depicted - Only those sex-related materials which have a tendency of 'exciting lustful thoughts' could be held to be obscene, but the obscenity has to be judged from the point of view of an average person, by applying contemporary community standards - Applying the community tolerance test, SC was not prepared to say such a photograph was suggestive of deprave minds and designed to excite sexual passion in persons who are likely to look at them and see them, which would depend upon the particular posture and background in which the woman was depicted or shown - Breast of lady was fully covered with the arm of Tennis player, a photograph, of course, semi-nude, but taken by none other than the father of said lady - Further, the photograph, had no tendency to deprave or corrupt the minds of people in whose hands the magazine or Newspaper would fall - Photograph wants to convey was that the colour of skin matters little and love champions over colour - Picture promoted love affair, leading to a marriage, between a white-skinned man and a black skinned woman - Picture or the article which was reproduced by magazine and Newspaper was said to be objectionable so as to initiate proceedings u/s. 292 of IPC and u/s. 4 of the Act - No offence was committed u/s 292 of IPC and question whether it falls in first part of s. 79 of IPC had become academic - Impugned criminal proceedings initiated against appellants were quashed - Appeal allowed.
