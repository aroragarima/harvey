Â A. K. Gopalan v Government of India

Supreme Court of India

27 October 1965

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Mohammad Hidayatullah, R.S. Bachawat, Vaidynathier Ramaswami

Where Reported

1965 Indlaw SC 232; AIR 1966 SC 816; 1966 CRLJ 602; 1965 KLT 1229; [1966] 2 S.C.R. 427

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 226 and 356 - Defence of India Rules, 1962, r. 30(1)(b) - Detention of petitioners u/r. 30 (1)(b) of Defence of India Rules (1962) by orders of Governor of Kerala passed on 29-12-1964 - President's rule in Kerala under his proclamation of 10-9-1964 - Cancellation of detention orders by President on 04-03-1965 - Fresh detention orders by Central Govt. on same date - Legality of these orders - General election in Kerala was to be held in beginning of March 1965 - (A) Whether detention under fresh order passed on 04-03-1965 was legal when petition for habeas corpus was made? - Order of Governor D/- 29-12-1964 was in circumstances order of President acting through agency of Governor of Kerala in respect of governance of State and it was open to President to cancel order passed by his agent and that is what he did on 04-03-1965 - Held, In circumstances order of cancellation cannot be assailed as illegal - (B) Whether orders are mala fide in as much as they were passed to circumvent possibility of petitioners being released? - Further held, orders passed on 04-03-1965 cannot be said to be mala fide if Central Govt. was satisfied that with a view to preventing petitioners from acting in a manner prejudicial to defence of India, civil defence, public safety and public order it was necessary to detain them - (C) Whether there was no application of mind by Govt. of India before orders in question were passed? - Further held, It is unacceptable that only because as many as 140 orders were passed on same day there was no satisfaction of Govt. of India with respect to each individual case - It was after Govt. had satisfied itself from those papers as to likely prejudicial activities of petitioners that it passed orders in question - (D) What are requisites for testing legality of a detention? - Further held, It is well settled that in dealing with a petition for habeas corpus Court has to see whether detention on date on which application is made to Court is legal, if nothing more has intervened between date of application and date of hearing - Petition dismissed.
