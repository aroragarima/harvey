Supreme Court of India

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranganath Misra, A.M. Ahmadi, M.N. Venkatachaliah, K.N. Singh, N.D. Ojha

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 912; (1991) 4 SCC 584; 1992 (1) ACC 332; AIR 1992 SC 248; 1991 (3) CCC 393; 1991 (3) CompLJ 213; JT 1991 (6) SC 8; 1991(2) SCALE 675; [1991] Supp1 S.C.R. 251; 1992 (1) UJ 360

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Human Rights; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Fairness, Code of Civil Procedure 1908, Torts, Constitutionality, Quashing Of Criminal Proceedings, Bhopal Gas Leak Disaster (Processing Of Claims) Act, 1985

Summary: (A) Constitution - Human Rights - Practice & Practice - Bhopal gas case - Claim for compensation - Enhancement - Legality - In early hours of 3-12-1984, there was a massive escape of lethal gas from MIC Storage Tank of plant into atmosphere which led to calamity - Respondent UOI under the Act filed suit for compensation on behalf of effected parties and filed suit for realization of compensation against appellant - District Court ordered compensation of Rs. 350 crores - Appellants filed writ petition before HC, which was reduced the quantum of interim compensation from 350 crores to sum of Rs 250 crores - Respondents challenged the HC order before SC, which was increased the compensation - Aggrieved, appellant filed instant revision - Whether the quantum of compensation settled was grossly low - Held, Whether jurisdiction of Court to withdraw or transfer a suit or proceedings to itself was exhausted by art. 139-A of Constitution and such transfer implicit in final disposal of suits had impermissible suits were not before Court so as to be amenable to final disposal by recording a settlement - Held, it was disputed that art. 139-A Constitution in terms did not apply in facts of case - Appeals were by special leave u/art. 136 of Constitution against an interlocutory order - If art. 139-A of Constitution exhausts power of transfer or withdrawal of proceedings, then contention had substance - Court had occasion to point out that art. 136 of Constitution was worded in widest terms possible - It vests in SC a plenary jurisdiction in matter of entertaining and hearing of appeals by granting special leave against any kind of judgment or order made by a Court or Tribunal in any cause or matter and powers could be exercised in spite of limitations under specific provisions for appeal contained in Constitution or other laws - However, powers given by art. 136 were in nature of special or residuary powers which were exercisable outside purview of ordinary laws in cases where needs of justice demand interference by SC - Power u/art. 139-A of Constitution should be held not to exhaust power of withdrawal and transfer - Order accordingly.

(B) Constitution - Human Rights - Practice & Practice - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 23 r. 3-B - Bhopal gas case - Whether original suit was really and in substance a representative suit for purposes and within meaning of O. 23 r. 3-B CPC inasmuch as any order made therein would affect persons not nominee parties to suit - Held, it was held that principles of natural justice underlying said provisions were not excluded - It was implicit in that reasoning that O. 23 r. 3-B CPC in terms did not apply - Discussion of effect of failure of compliance would indicate that Court declined to recognise any such fatal consequences - Though entering into a settlement without required notice was wrong - It was directed that notice should be given, would not result in doing justice in situation - In premises, no further consequential order was necessary by Court - If principle of natural justice underlying O. 23 r. 3-B CPC was held to apply, consequences of non-compliance should not be different from consequences of breach of rules of natural justice - Even assuming that right of affected persons of being heard was also available at a stage where a settlement was placed before Court for its acceptance, such a right was not referable to, and did not stem from, r. 3-B of O. 23 CPC - Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Human Rights - Practice & Practice - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 320, 321 and 482 - Bhopal gas case - Pendency of criminal proceedings - Legality - Whether termination of pending criminal proceedings brought about by orders dt.14-2-1989 and dt.15-2-1989 was bad in law and would require to be reviewed - Held, order terminating pending criminal proceedings was not supportable on strict terms of s. 320, 321 and 482 CrPC - Offences relate to and arose out of a terrible and ghastly tragedy - Nearly 4000 lives were lost and tens of thousands of citizens had suffered injuries in various degrees of severity - Indeed at one point of time appellant itself recognised possibility of accident had been result of acts of sabotage - It was matter of importance that offences alleged in context of a disaster of such gravity and magnitude should not remain uninvestigated - However, stand of respondent on point should not by itself lead to any miscarriage of justice - There was no specific ground or grounds for withdrawal of prosecutions had been set out at that stage quashing of prosecutions requires to be set aside - Order accordingly.

(D) Constitution - Human Rights - Practice & Practice - Bhopal gas case - Criminal proceedings - Constitutionality - Whether effect of orders under review interdicting and prohibiting future criminal proceedings against any person or persons whatsoever in relation to or arising to of Bhopal Gas Leak Disaster, in effect and substance, amounts to conferment of immunity from criminal proceedings - Held, it was compelled to give a negative answer to that inquiry - Indeed, sound reason and logic, as well as great weight of authority, to be found both in test books and in decided cases, affirm that no such power exists in district attorney and district court, either or both, except as placed there by constitutional or statutory language - However, grant of an immunity is in very truth assumption of a legislative power, and that was why legislature, acting alone, was incompetent to declare - It was assumption of a power to annual as to individuals or classes statutory law of crimes, to stem course of justice, to absolve grand jurors of country from performance of their duties, and prosecuting officer from his - All those changes might be wrought through enactment of a statute - Order accordingly.
