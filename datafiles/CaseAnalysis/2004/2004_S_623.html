Hydru v State of Kerala

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Agrawal, A.K. Mathur

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 1937; (2004) 13 SCC 374

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.376 - Rape - Conviction - Procedural Irregularity - Appellant/accused committed rape on victim and was convicted by Trial Court u/s.376 of IPC - First Appellate Court, on appeal passed order of acquittal - HC invoked revisional powers as suo motu and issued notice to appellant to show cause for setting aside order of acquittal - After considering evidence HC concluded that First Appellate Court was not justified in recording acquittal and affirmed order of Trial Court - Hence instant appeal.

Held, from a bare perusal of order, it would appear that HC upon reappraisal came to a conclusion different from one recorded by Appellate Court. It is well settled that in revision against acquittal by a private party, powers of Revisional Court are very limited. If upon reappraisal of evidence, two views are possible, it is not permissible even for Appellate Court in appeal against acquittal to interfere with same, much less in revision where powers are much narrower. No procedural irregularity has been found by HC in order of Appellate Court whereby appellant was acquitted. Therefore, Court view that HC was not justified in interfering with order of acquittal in exercise of its revisional powers, as such same is liable to be interfered with by Court. Order rendered by HC is set aside and order of acquittal passed by Appellate Court is restored. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - If there is any procedural irregularity or material evidence has been overlooked or misread by Lower Court then Revisional Court can interfere in order of Lower Court.
