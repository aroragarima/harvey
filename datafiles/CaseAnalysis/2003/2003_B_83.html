Bentool Steel Products Private Limited v O. M. A. Mohammed Omar and another

Supreme Court of India

27 February 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

R.C. Lahoti, Brijesh Kumar

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 1537; (2008) 17 SCC 679

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Summary: Rent Control - Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1960, s. 10(3)(c) - Eviction - Relevant considerations - Ground of comparative hardship - Revision jurisdiction - Landlord and tenant, both were carrying on their respective business activities in premises adjoining each other - Proceedings for eviction were initiated on certain ground, which was decided against landlord - As against in appeal Appellate Authority held that only on ground available u/s.10(3)(c) of the Act, tenant was entitled to decree of eviction - Revision petition preferred by tenant was allowed by HC - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether HC in exercise of its revisional jurisdiction u/s. 25 of the Act could have interfered therewith and, therefore, decision of HC was liable to be set aside on ground of comparative hardship decision of HC was liable to be interfered with - Held, on question of comparative hardship, HC had not interfered with any of findings arrived at by Appellate Authority - However, conclusion of Appellate Authority that tenant would suffer comparatively more hardship was based on two findings of fact, one, that business of tenant was more flourishing than that of landlord and two, that administrative office of tenant was situated on upper floor of same tenancy premises and in event of tenant being called upon to shift his business to another premises, he would have to move at distance from administrative office, which would cause serious inconvenience to him - However, Appellate Authority took irrelevant factors into consideration and ignored relevant factors from consideration while arriving at conclusion regarding comparative hardship caused to tenant - Further, simply because tenant had flourishing business, same could not be set up as defence, in name of comparative hardship, against bona fide requirement of landlord whose business had already outgrown and whose necessity was imminent - Thus, little inconvenience to tenant could not be called hardship - Hence, it could be inferred that HC had not committed any error much less jurisdictional error in recording finding on question of comparative hardship, in departure from one arrived at by Appellate Authority - Appeal dismissed.
