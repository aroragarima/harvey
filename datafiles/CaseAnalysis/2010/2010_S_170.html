Â Amarinder Singh v Special Committee, Punjab Vidhan Sabha and others

Supreme Court of India

26 April 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

K.G. Balakrishnan, R.V. Raveendran, P. Sathasivam, J.M. Panchal, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 672; (2010) 6 SCC 113; 2011 (1) AWC 449; JT 2010 (4) SC 350; 2010 (4) MLJ(SC) 929; 2010(4) SCALE 354; [2010] 4 S.C.R. 1105

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Parliament & Legislature; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Punitive Action, Parliamentary Privilege, Privileges Of Parliament, Punjab Land Improvement Act, 1922

Summary: (A) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Punjab Land Improvement Act, 1922, ss. 36, 56 - Punjab Apartment and Property Regulation Act, 1995, s. 5 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 194(3) - Scope of art. 194(3) - A private developer applied for license u/s. 5 of the 1995 Regulation to develop disputed land - Subsequently, Amritsar Land Improvement Trust (ALIT)/statutory body notified the plan for acquisition of the same tract of land u/s. 36 of the 1922 Act - Private developer approached the authorities seeking exemption from proposed acquisition by ALIT - ALIT granted No-objection certificate - State Government notified the exemption u/s. 56 of the 1922 Act - Election held and State Legislature Assembly reconstituted wherein appellant/erstwhile Chief Minister, was elected as a member - Members of Legislative Assembly moved a privilege motion alleging that the appellant unduly favoured the private developer to get the notified land exempted from acquisition during his tenure as Chief Minister of the State - Speaker of the House referred the notice of motion to Privileges Committee and subsequently, Privileges Committee tabled the report before the House - Based on the report of Privileges Committee and a motion of incumbent Chief Minister of State, Speaker constituted a Special Committee to inquire into the allegation of misconduct against appellant - Special Committee found that appellant was involved in corruption, conspiracy to cause wrongful loss and abuse of public office in relation to the exemption of notified land - Based on the report of Special Committee, Secretariat of Legislative Assembly issued a notification expelling the appellant from Legislative Assembly - In the meantime, appellant's assembly constituency seat was declared vacant and set aside his election through the impugned resolution - Appellant challenged the Special Committee's report before the HC and sought interim injunction against the order of his expulsion from Legislative Assembly - HC did not grant the stay, however, relief was granted to the extent that even though the appellant could not participate in the legislative proceedings, his seat would not fall vacant until the adjudication of present case - Since the HC found that the subject matter touched on substantial questions of law requiring the interpretation of art. 194(3) of the Constitution, matter referred to the Constitution Bench of SC - Whether the alleged misconduct on part of the appellant warranted the exercise of legislative privileges u/art. 194(3) of the Constitution - Held, the matter of improper exemption of a particular plot of land from an acquisition scheme cannot be an obstruction to the conduct of legislative business - If it is indeed felt that the allegations of misconduct on part of the former Chief Minister had brought disrepute to the entire House, then the proper course is to pursue criminal investigation and prosecution before the appropriate judicial forum - Alleged misconduct on part of the appellant had no effect of obstructing the ordinary legislative functions of the Legislative Assembly - Act of recommending the appellant's expulsion through the impugned resolution cannot be justified as a proper exercise of 'powers, privileges and immunities' conferred by art.194(3) of the Constitution - Expulsion of members should only be sustained if their actions have caused obstructions to legislative functions or are likely to cause the same - Power of a legislative chamber to punish for its own contempt should broadly coincide with the legislature's interest in protecting the integrity of its functions - In the present case the respondents have failed to demonstrate how the alleged misconduct on part of the appellant could have a effect on the business of legislative assembly - Therefore, alleged improper exemption of land was an executive act attributable to the appellant and it did not distort, obstruct or threaten the integrity of legislative proceedings in any manner, hence, the exercise of legislative privileges u/art. 194(3) of the Constitution was not proper in the present case - Appeals disposed off accordingly.

(B) Parliament & Legislature - Breach of privilege - Matter relating to previous term of Legislature Assembly - Enquiry by present Legislature Assembly - Justifiability - Whether it was proper for the present Legislature Assembly to take up, as a matter of breach of privilege, an incident that occurred during its previous term - Held, at the time of the reconstitution of the present Legislature Assembly following the State elections, there was no pending motion, report or any other order of business which had a connection with the allegedly improper exemption of land - Special Committee was constituted to inquire into the allegations in the present case at a later date, therefore, Special Committee proceeded to enquire into the executive acts of the appellants which had taken place during the previous term of the Legislature Assembly - Allowing the present Legislature Assembly to exercise legislative privileges to punish past executive acts especially when there was no pending motion, report or any other order of business that was relatable to the said executive acts at the time of the re-constitution of the House, is not tenable - In view of the facts of the present case, it was improper for the present Legislature Assembly to claim a breach of privileges on account of the alleged misconduct which actually took place during the previous term of the Legislative Assembly - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Advocates & Judges - Practice & Procedure - Parliament & Legislature - Rules of Business and Conduct of the Punjab Vidhan Sabha, rr. 39(10), 50, 93(2)(iv) and 150(d) - Sub judice matter - Privilege motion before Legislative Assembly - Legality - Whether the impugned acts of the Legislative Assembly in question violated the norms that should be respected in relation to sub judice matters - Held, rr. 39(10), 50, 93(2)(iv) and 150(d) of the Rules which govern the business and conduct of the Legislative Assembly in question are quite categorical in laying down a prohibition on the taking up of any matter which is pending adjudication before a court of law - Legislative Assembly in question proceeded to inquire into the allegedly improper exemption of land from the Scheme of ALIT, even though the same was questioned before the HC - Therefore, Legislative Assembly in question ought not to have constituted a committee to inquire into the allegations against appellant, hence, resolution passed by the Legislative Assembly, directing the expulsion of the appellant for the remainder of the term of the legislature is constitutionally invalid - Direction issued to the Secretariat of Legislative Assembly to restore the appellant's membership in the legislature - Appeal disposed of.
