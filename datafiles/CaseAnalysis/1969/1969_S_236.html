Abdul Karim and Others v State of West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

31 January 1969

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Ramaswami, A.N. Grover, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1969 Indlaw SC 262; (1969) 1 SCC 433; AIR 1969 SC 1028; 1969 CRLJ 1446; [1969] 3 S.C.R. 479; 1969 UJ 106

Case Digest

Subject: Defence & Security Forces; Human Rights

Keywords: Preventive Detention Act, 1950

Summary: Constitution - Foreigners Act, 1946 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Preventive Detention Act, 1950 - Release from detention - Denied - Legality - Trial Court passed detention order against petitioner No. 2 and other petitioners - Petitioner No. 2 made representation, to State Govt. against detention order - Advisory Board made a report stating that there was sufficient cause for detention and state/respondent confirmed detention order - Hence instant appeal - Petitioner contended that representations of petitioners were not considered by respondent and detention of petitioners was bad in law, because there, was failure on part of Govt. to consider representations submitted by petitioners before forwarding them to Advisory Board - Whether detention order against petitioners were just and proper - Held, obligation of detaining authority to consider representation was different from obligation of Advisory Board to consider representation later on at time of hearing reference - Therefore, even if reference was to be made to Advisory Board u/s. 9 of the Act, appropriate Govt. was under legal obligation to consider representation of detenu before such reference was made - A predominant position and role was given in our Constitution to human personality and human freedom as ultimate source of all moral and spiritual values - Preventive detention was serious invasion of personal liberty, therefore Constitution had provided procedural safeguards against improper exercise of power of preventive detention - All procedural requirements of art. 22 were mandatory in character and even if one of procedural requirement was not complied with, order of detention would be rendered illegal - Accordingly, order of detention against petitioner No. 2 and subsequent order of respondent confirming order of detention must be held to be illegal and ultra vires and petitioner No. 2 was entitled to be released - In case of petitioners No. 5, 6, 8 order of detention suffers' from same legal defect as order of detention in case of petitioner No. 2 - For reasons already stated, the order of detention and order of confirmation u/s. 11 of the Act in case of petitioners No. 5, 6 and 8 were also illegal and ultra vires and petitioners were consequently entitled to be released - Appeal Allowed.
