Polestar Electronic(P) Ltd. v Addl. Commissioner, Sales Tax Delhi

Supreme Court of India

20 February 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, M. Hameedullah Beg

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 138; (1978) SCC (Tax) 68; (1978) 1 SCC 636; AIR 1978 SC 897; 1978 (2) SCJ 601; [1978] 3 S.C.R. 98; 1978 (41) STC 409; 1978 TAXLR 1907

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Liability To Tax, Resale, Finance Act, 1972, Statement Of Objects And Reasons, Electricity Act, 1910, Bengal Fair (Sales Tax) Act 1941

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Bengal Finance (Sales Tax) Act, 1941, s. 5(2) (a) (ii) - A/Y 1971-72 and 1972-73 - Certified sale transaction outside Delhi - Taxability - Appellants/assessees filed appeals on question of interpretation of s. 5(2) (a) (ii) of the Act as applied to Union Territory of Delhi - Act was extended to Delhi subject to certain modifications by a Notification dt.28-4-1951 issued by Central Govt. in exercise of powers conferred by s. 2 of the Part C States (Laws) Act, 1950 and it came into force in Delhi on 28-5-1951 by virtue of Notification issued u/s. 1 (3) of the Act - There were several amendments made in the Act from time to time - Assessees were registered dealers and during relevant assessment periods they held certificates of registration specifying class or classes of goods intended for resale by them or for use by them as raw-materials in manufacture of goods for sale - Certificates of registration were in the form as it stood prior to its amendment on 29-3-1973 and they did not specify that resale of goods purchased or their use as raw-materials in manufacture of goods, or sale of manufactured goods should be inside Delhi - Whether 'resale' in s. 5 (2) (a) (ii) and Second Proviso meant resale anywhere without any geographical limitation or it was confined only to resale inside Delhi - Held, the word used in s. 5 (2) (a) (ii) and Second Proviso was 'resale' simpliciter without any geographical limitation and according to its plain natural meaning it would mean resale anywhere and not necessarily inside Delhi - Even where purchasing dealer resells goods outside Delhi, he would satisfy requirement of statutory provision according to its plain grammatical meaning - There were no words such as 'inside Union Territory of Delhi' qualifying 'resale' so as to limit it to resale within territory of Delhi - If legislative intent was that 'resale' should be within territory of Delhi and not outside, Legislature would have said so in plain unambiguous language which no laymen could possibly misunderstand - Further, resale was subsequent sale after first and it should, therefore, had same meaning as ' sale' defined in s. 2(g) - Thus there was no tax sought to be imposed on resale u/s. 5 (2) (a) (ii) or Second Proviso, but resale was made condition of granting deduction in respect of first sale - Further, it was clear from the scheme of the Act that dealer who carried on business of selling goods in territory of Delhi and who was liable to pay tax u/s. 4 of the Act was required to be registered and he should possess registration certificate - If such dealer purchased goods of class or classes specified in his certificate of registration on furnishing declaration that same were intended for resale by him, sale to him would be exempt from tax and hence he would not have to pay any amount by way of tax to selling dealer - There was no requirement of law that registered dealer should effect sales only in Delhi and not outside - There was nothing in the Act which prohibits registered dealer from selling goods outside Delhi - Even where assessees used goods purchased as raw-materials in manufacture of goods outside Delhi or having manufactured goods, sold them outside Delhi, there was no breach of intention expressed by them in declarations given to selling dealers and they could not be said to have utilised goods for any purpose other than that for which they were purchased so as to attract applicability of Second Proviso. - There was no evidence at all before assessing authority and order imposing penalty was, therefore, plainly unjustified - It was based on misconstruction of s. 5 (2) (a) (ii) and Second Proviso and it therefore quashed and set aside - Appeals allowed.
