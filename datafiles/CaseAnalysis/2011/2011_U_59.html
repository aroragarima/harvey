Amar Singh v Union Of India And Others

Supreme Court of India

11 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 333; (2011) 7 SCC 69; 2011 (4) AWC 3726; 2011 (2) CLT(SC) 304; JT 2011 (4) SC 593; 2011 (5) RCR(Civil) 386; 2011(5) SCALE 606; [2011] 6 S.C.R. 403

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Telecom

Summary: Constitution - Telecom - Media & Entertainment - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(a), 21 and 32 - Indian Telegraph Act, 1885, s. 5 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, o. 19 r. 3 - Supreme Court Rules, 1966, O. 11 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 419, 420 468, 471 and 120B - Fundamental right to privacy - Tapping of phone - Legality - Petitioner filed writ petition alleging that the Government of India and the Government of Delhi on pressure by respondent no. 7 (Ruling Party), had intercepted the petitioner's conversation on phone, monitoring them and recording them - Petitioner further alleged that such interception of conversation amounts to intrusion on the privacy of the affected people and was motivated by political ill will - Petitioner also lodged FIR u/ss. 419, 420 468, 471 and 120B of IPC, r/w. ss. 20, 21 and 26 of the 1885 Act - Subsequently, petitioner filed an interlocutory application submitting that the electronic and the print media be directed not to publish any part of recording of said conversation - SC vide order dt. 27.02.2006 directed the electronic and the print media not to publish any part of the said conversations - Respondents denied said allegations contending that the orders purporting to be issued by the Joint Commissioner of Police and Principal Secretary (Home) regarding tapping of petitioner's phone, were fabricated with forged signature and they were not genuine - Respondents also contended that affidavits filed by the petitioner was perfunctory, defective and not in accordance with the mandate of law - (A) Whether writ petition filed by the petitioner could be allowed? - Held, in 2006, the gravamen of the petitioner's grievances was against the respondent no. 7, and the basis of his petition was the information that he derived from the 'X' - On the basis of such a petition, he invoked the jurisdiction of SC and an interim order was issued in his favour - However, when the matter had come up for contested hearing, he suddenly withdraws his allegations against the respondent no. 7 and feels satisfied with the investigation of the Police in connection with the aforesaid case of forgery and also states that the 'X' 'edited and tampered certain conversations of the petitioner' - An action at law was not a game of chess - A litigant who comes to Court and invokes its writ jurisdiction must come with clean hands - He cannot prevaricate and take inconsistent positions - Hence, writ petition was frivolous and was speculative in character - No case of tapping of telephone had been made out against the statutory authorities - (B) Whether affidavit filed by the petitioner was not in accordance with the mandate of law? - Held, the affidavit filed by the petitioner in support of his petition, was not at all modelled either on O. 19 r. 3 of the CPC or O. 11 of the 1966 Rules - Petitioner invoked the extraordinary writ jurisdiction u/art. 32 of Constitution, without filing a proper affidavit as required in terms of O. 19 r. 3 of the CPC - The nature of the challenge in petitioner's petition was very serious in the sense that he was alleging an attempt by the government of intercepting his phone and he was further alleging that in making this attempt the government was acting on extraneous considerations, and was virtually acting in furtherance of the design of the ruling party - It was, therefore, imperative that before making such an allegation the petitioner should be careful, circumspect and file a proper affidavit in support of his averment in the petition - Interim order passed by the SC vide order dt. 27.02.2006 vacates and was not called upon to decide the merits, if any, of the petitioner's case - Writ petition dismissed.
