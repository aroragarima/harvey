Kalindi and Others v Tata Locomotive and Engineering Company, Limited

Supreme Court of India

25 March 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 157; AIR 1960 SC 914; 1960 (1) FLR 23; 1960 (2) LLJ 228; 1960 (1) SCJ 47; [1960] 3 S.C.R. 407

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate; Labour & Industrial Law; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Practice & Procedure - Industrial Disputes Act,1947, s. 33 A - (A) Whether in case of enquiries into a charge-sheet of misconduct against a workman, natural justice demands that he should be represented by a member of his Union? - Held, no right to be represented - Practice adopted by domestic tribunals is that person accused conducts his own case, it will be wrong to accept an argument that natural justice will be violated in that case - It is necessary to remember that if any enquiry is not otherwise fair, workman concerned can challenge its validity

in an industrial dispute - Workman against whom an enquiry is being conducted by management has therefore no right to be represented at such enquiry by a representative of his Union - An employer in his discretion can and may allow his employee to avail himself of such assistance - (B)Whether decision will be valid where charge sheet against a workman mentions certain items of misconduct and final order of dismissal includes an additional item not mentioned in charge sheet but order of dismissal was based on initial charges? - Further held, fact that this act of misconduct not mentioned in charge-sheet was also one of the items on which order of dismissal was based and does not affect validity of order - Where charge sheet mentions four items of misconduct against a workman but one of them is not proved and yet, through a clerical error, it is mentioned along with other three items in formal order of dismissal, such a clerical error does not affect validity of order of dismissal - Appeal dismissed.
