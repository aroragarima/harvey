Â Raja Kulkarni and Others v State of Bombay

Supreme Court of India

24 November 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Ghulam Hasan, M. Patanjali Sastri, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Vivian Bose, S.K. Das

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 45; (1981) SCC (Tax) 293; AIR 1954 SC 73; 1954 (56) BomLR 459; 1954 CRLJ 351; 1954 (1) LLJ 1; 1954 (1) MLJ(SC) 83; 1954 SCJ 50; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 384

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Industrial Disputes (Appellate Tribunal) Act, 1950, Trade Union Act, 1926, Trade Unions Act, Bombay Industrial Relation Act, 1946

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Bombay Industrial Relation Act, 1946, ss. 23, 442 - Industrial Disputes (Appellate Tribunal) Act, 1950, ss. 24(b), 12(33), 13, 15, 24, 25, 27, 30 - Trade Union Act, 1926 - Membership - Exhorting - Sustainability - Appellants were President and Secretaries of union of textile workers registered under the 1926 Act - It appeared that there were about 2,10,000 textile workers working and about 35 per cent of them belonged to three different labour unions - Representative, union gave notice of change u/s. 442 Of the 1946 Act to Mill Owners Association claiming bonus for that year - Dispute was referred by Sate Govt. to Industrial Court u/s. 23 of the 1946 Act - Industrial Court made award and same was published - Mill Owners Association filed appeal before Tribunal and ad interim order was passed - Appellants made speeches exhorting workers to go on strike - Labour Commissioner filed complaints before Presidency Magistrate charging appellants with offence u/s. 27 of the 1950 Act - Appellants were convicted by Presidency Magistrate, but their sentences were reduced on appeal by HC - Hence, instant appeals - Whether appellants were liable to prosecution u/s. 27 of the 1950 Act, because they instigated strike while appeal was pending before Tribunal - Held, s. 24 of the 1950 Act on plain and natural construction required for its application that appeal should be pending and there was nothing in language to justify introduction of qualification that it should be valid or competent - The 1946 Act made no discrimination between textile workers as class but laid down reasonable' classification to effect that certain percentage of membership possessed by union would be allowed to represent workers as class to exclusion of others, but there was nothing to prevent other unions or other workers from forming fresh union and enrolling higher percent-age so as to acquire sole right of representation - Appeal dismissed.
