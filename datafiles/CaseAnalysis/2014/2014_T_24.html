State of Rajasthan v Thakur Singh

Supreme Court of India

30 June 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Sharad Arvind Bobde

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 413; (2014) 12 SCC 211; 2014 CRLJ 4047; 2014 (3) JCC 2271; JT 2014 (8) SC 50; 2014 (4) RCR(Criminal) 612; 2014(8) SCALE 82; [2014] 8 S.C.R. 18

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302, 324 and 326 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Acquittal - Challenged - Charge-sheet was filed against accused alleging the commission of offences punishable u/ss. 302, 326 and 324 of the IPC - Trial Court found him guilty of offence punishable u/s. 302 of the IPC - Trial Court held, on the basis of the evidence on record, that no one except accused could have caused the death of deceased - Accused had confined her and their daughter inside a room and although no one saw him killing his wife, since the room was bolted from inside, he had not opened it for the whole day and the door had to be forced open, no one else could have caused her death - On appeal, HC found no evidence to link accused with the death of deceased and therefore appeal was allowed and he was acquitted of the charge of an offence punishable u/s. 302 of the IPC - Hence, instant appeal - Whether decision of the HC acquitting accused was correct -

Held, in the instant case, since deceased died an unnatural death in the room occupied by her and accused, the cause of the unnatural death was known to accused - There was no evidence that anybody else had entered their room or could have entered their room - Accused did not set up any case that he was not in their room or not in the vicinity of their room while the incident occurred nor did he set up any case that some other person entered the room and caused the unnatural death of his wife - Facts relevant to the cause of death being known only to accused, yet he chose not to disclose them or to explain them - Hence, principle laid down in s. 106 of the Act was applicable to the facts of the case and there was, therefore, a very strong presumption that deceased was murdered by accused- HC had not at all considered the provisions of s. 106 of the Act - Approach arrived at by the Trial Court was the correct approach under the law and the HC was completely in error in relying primarily on the fact that since most of the material prosecution witnesses (all of whom were relatives of accused) had turned hostile, the prosecution was unable to prove its case - Judgment and order passed by the HC was set aside and that of the Trial Judge restored - Appeal allowed.
