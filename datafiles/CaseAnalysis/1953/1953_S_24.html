Â Supreme Court Reports K.C. Gajapati Narayan Deo And Other v State Of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

29 May 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, M. Patanjali Sastri, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 20; AIR 1953 SC 375; 1953 SCJ 592; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Madras Estates Land Act, 1908, Bihar Land Reforms Act, Orissa Tenants Protection Act, 1948, Orissa Agricultural Income-tax (Amendment) Act, 1950, Orissa Agricultural Income-tax (Second (Amendment) Act, 1950, British North America Act (ENG), British Columbian Coal Mines Regulation Act, 1890 (ENG), Canadian Insurance Act (ENG), Wheat Industry Assistance Act, Commonwealth Australian Constitution Act, Madras Estates Land (Orissa Amendment) Act, 1947

Summary: Land & Property - Agriculture and Agri. Products - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 110 - Income Tax (Amendment) Act, 1950 - Orissa Estates Abolition Act, 1952 - Tax (Second Amendment) Act, 1950, s. 37 - Bihar Land Reforms Act - Insurance Act, 1938 - Orissa Tenants Protection Act, 1948 - Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 31 (4) - British North America Act (ENG) - British Columbian Coal Mines Regulation Act, 1890 (ENG) - Canadian Insurance Act (ENG) - Wheat Industry Assistance Act - Commonwealth Australian Constitution Act - Madras Estates Land (Oriss Amendment) Act, 1947 - Madras Estates Land Act, 1908 - (A) Whether Income-tax (Amendment) Act, 1950, is a colorable piece of legislation, in so far as it affects calculation of net income of an estate for purpose of determining compensation payable under Act? - Held, if a legislature is competent to do a thing directly, then mere fact that it attempted to do it in an indirect or disguised manner, cannot make Act invalid - Under entry 42 of List III which is a mere head of legislative power legislature can adopt any principle of compensation in respect to properties compulsorily acquired - Fact that deductions are unjust, exorbitant or improper does not make legislation invalid, unless it is shown to be based on something which is unrelated to facts - (B) Whether provisions of Act are unconstitutional to extent that they are applicable to private lands and buildings of proprietors, both of which vest as parts of estate, u/s 5 of Act? - Further held, there is nothing in law which prevents state legislature from providing as a part of estates abolition scheme that buildings, lying within ambit of an estate and used primarily for management or administration of estate would vest in Govt. as appurtenances to estate itself - This is merely ancillary to acquisition of an estate and forms an integral part of abolition scheme - (C) Whether manner of payment of compensation money, as laid down in s.37 of Act, render Act invalid and unconstitutional? - Further held, Orissa Act, like similar Acts passed by legislatures of other states, provides for payment of compensation on basis of net income of whole estate - There is no doubt that Act does not give anything like a fair or market price of properties acquired and appellants may be right in their contention that compensation allowed is inadequate and improper but that does not affect constitutionality of provisions - No question of inadequacy of compensation can be raised in view of provision of art. 31 (4) of Constitution and it cannot also be suggested that rule for payment of compensation on rental basis is outside ambit of Entry 42 of List III - Appeals dismissed.
