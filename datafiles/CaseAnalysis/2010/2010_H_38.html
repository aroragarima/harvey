Rajeev Kumar and Another v Hemraj Singh Chauhan and Others

Supreme Court of India

23 March 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, R.V. Raveendran

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 200; (2010) 4 SCC 554; AIR 2010 SC 1679; 2010 (168) DLT 158; 2010 (2) ESC 233; 2010 (125) FLR 403; JT 2010 (3) SC 623; 2010 (4) MLJ(SC) 380; 2010(3) SCALE 282; [2010] 3 S.C.R. 572; 2010 (4) SLR 506

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Service

Keywords: CAT (Procedure) Rules, 1987

Summary: Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.141, 226 and 227 - CAT (Procedure) Rules, 1987 - Jurisdiction - Appellants, Non-State Civil Service Officers filed an impleadment application in HC for being impleaded as respondents in writ petition filed by 'X' as their interests have been affected by judgment of CAT even though they were not parties to the proceedings in which the said judgment was rendered - HC allowed them to intervene and further allowed them to make submissions at the time of hearing of the writ petition - On appeal, SC asked appellants to satisfy the court about their locus to participate in the controversy at the stage when the matter was before the HC - Appellants submitted that as per SC decision in L. Chandra Kumar vs. Union of India and Others 1997 INDLAW SC 2816, they can come before the HC and raise their grievances against the judgment of CAT - Whether appellants have locus to participate in the controversy at the stage when the matter was before the HC? - Held, Constitution Bench in Chandra Kumar case held that the power of the HC u/arts. 226 and 227 of the Constitution is a part of the basic structure of our Constitution - Constitution Bench also held that various Tribunals created u/arts. 323-A and 323-B of the Constitution, will function as Court of first instance and are subject to the power of judicial review of the HC u/arts. 226 and 227 of the Constitution - Constitution Bench also held that these Tribunals are also empowered to deal with constitutional questions and can also examine the vires of statutory legislation, except the vires of the legislation which creates the particular Tribunal - Constitution Bench also held that even where any challenge is made to the vires of legislation, excepting the legislation under which Tribunal has been set up, in such cases also, litigants will not be able to directly approach the High Court 'overlooking the jurisdiction of the Tribunal - In view of such repeated and authoritative pronouncement by the Constitution Bench, approach made to the HC for the first time by appellants in respect of their service disputes over which CAT has jurisdiction, is not legally sustainable - DB fell into an error by allowing the appellants to treat the HC as a Court of first instance in respect of their service disputes, for adjudication of which CAT has been constituted - Moreover, grievances of the appellants are that they were not made parties in proceedings before the Tribunal - But in the impleadment application filed before the HC it was not averred by them that they were not aware of the pendency of the proceeding before the Tribunal - Rather from the averments made in the impleadment petition it appears that they were aware of the pendency of the proceedings before the Tribunal - It was therefore, open for them to approach the Tribunal with their grievances - Special Leave Petition filed by appellants not maintainable - Appeals dismissed.
