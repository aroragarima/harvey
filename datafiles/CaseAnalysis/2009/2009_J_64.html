Post Graduate Institute of Medical Education and Research, Chandigarh v Jaspal Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

29 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 690; (2009) 7 SCC 330; (2009) 3 SCC (Cr) 399; 2009 (76) ALR 159; 2009 (4) AWC 3376; 2009 (6) Bom.C.R. 846; 2009 (3) CompLJ 369; 2009 (2) CPJ(Sc) 92; 2009 (3) ESC 474; JT 2009 (7) SC 527; 2009 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1798; 2010 (1) MLJ(Crl) 834; 2009 (4) MLJ(SC) 1253; 2010 (1) MPLJ 12; 2010 (158) PLR 679; 2009 (3) RCR(Civil) 873; 2009(8) SCALE 601; [2009] 9 S.C.R. 889; 2010 (1) UC 317

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection

Keywords: Professional Negligence, Negligence In Diagnosis Or Treatment

Summary: Consumer Protection - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Medical/professional negligence - Deficiency in service - Mismatched blood transfusion - Negligence found by State Commission on part of PGI, Chandigarh and its attending doctor(s)/staff in transfusing wrong blood group to the patient which resulted in death of the patient, and damage was awarded thereupon - Finding of SC was confirmed by National Commission - Whether findings justified? - Held, Yes - Patient suffered burn injuries to the extent of 50%; initially treated at another hospital for about 20 days and therein her condition was satisfactorily improved; thereafter she was admitted to PGI; as per material on record with SC, at the time of her admission, patient was taking medicine orally and passing urine; 75% of eschar was removed, her condition had substantially improved at PGI and she had no signs of septicemia; it was only after mismatched blood transfusion B+ on two consecutive days, that she became anemic and her kidney and liver were deranged; Court observed that it is true that her hemoglobin was brought up in few days but her condition otherwise got deteriorated - Death occurred after 40 days of mismatched blood transfusion but Court observed that there was causal link between mismatched blood transfusion and the death; and it was an error which no hospital/doctor exercising ordinary care would have made; such an error is not an error of professional judgement but in the very nature of things a sure instance of medical negligence; breach of duty on part of hospital in such mismatch contributed to patient's death, if not wholly, but surely materially; Court further observed that such mismatched blood transfusion to a patient having sustained 50% burns by itself speaks of negligence - Impugned judgment upheld - Appeal dismissed.
