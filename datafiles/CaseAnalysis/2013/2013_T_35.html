Sanjay Dutt (A-117) and others v The State Of Maharashtra, Through Cbi (Stf), Bombay

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 165; AIR 2013 SC 2687; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 1498; 2013 CRLJ 3538; JT 2013 (5) SC 1; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 747; 2013(4) SCALE 462

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Acquaintance, Arms Rules, 1962, Explosives Rules, 1983

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 15 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 3 and 7 r/w ss. 25(1-A), (1-B)(a) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 201 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Destruction of evidence - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants accused (A-117, A-118, A-124) allegedly acquired and possessed arms and ammunition illegally in notified area as a part of criminal conspiracy with the intent to aid terrorists for carrying out Bombay serial blast - Appellants allegedly caused destruction of such arms in order to screen them and co-conspirators from legal punishment - Designated Trial Court convicted and sentenced appellants u/ss. 3 and 7 r/w ss. 25(1-A), (1-B)(a) of the 1959 Act and u/s. 201 IPC - Whether conviction recorded by Trial Court was sustainable - Held, appellantsâ confessions were recorded by PW193 (DCP), who had proved that confessions were recorded after following requirements of s.15 of TADA - PW193âs evidence withstood firmly without being shaken even after vigorous cross-examination - Voluntariness of confession on various occasions were considered in detail by Trial Court and being fully agreed with - Further, entire sequence of events were proved by evidence of PW680 Investigating Officer and PW475 Assistant Inspector of Police - Incident was also witnessed and proved by an independent witness PW211, whose credibility was not shaken despite vigorous cross examination - Material objects were recovered from the possession of A-124 were sent to FSL for examination - Requisition and report showed that seal on the packet containing object was perfect and was not tempered with - Prosecution had also established through one independent witness PW265 that A-118 and A-124 further made statement to police and pursuant whereof a gas cylinder used in destroying arms was recovered at the instance of A-124 and some of the ammunition were recovered at the instance of A-118 - In the instant case, at the time of taking cognizance by Trial Court, there were sufficient evidence against appellants to proceed against them in joint trial - In the case of A-117, Trial Court took a view on the basis of his own confession that weapons were not acquired for any terrorist activity but they were acquired for self-defence, therefore, acquittal was recorded in respect of charge u/s. 5 of TADA, which did not call for interference - However, conclusion arrived at by Trial Court with respect to all other offences by A-117, A118 and A-124 was upheld - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Retracted confession - Validity - Whether retracted confession of appellant (A-117) could be the basis for his conviction - Held, appellant confessed to PW193 (DCP), who had proved compliance with the provisions of law while recording confession - Further, confession established unauthorized possession of weapons in the notified area - Appellantâs confession was substantiated and corroborated with the confession of other co-accused - Since appellant retracted his confession after a considerable period of time, subsequent retraction was only as a result of afterthought - Further, appellantâs confession was corroborated by confessions of co-accused, as well as in oral testimony of witnesses, including eye witnesses to the incident who had identified appellant and other accused persons - Sequence of events after arrest of appellant till recovery of pistol from co-accused formed part of an unbroken chain inseparably connected to each other - Hence, confession of appellant could be a basis for his conviction - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Probation of Offenders Act, 1958, s. 4 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Conviction - Quantum of sentence - Whether appellant (A-117) was entitled to seek benefit of s. 4 of the Act - Held, while extending benefit of provisions of Act, Court should exercise its discretion having regard to the circumstances in which crime was committed, viz., the age, character and antecedents of offender - Further, such exercise of discretion needed a sense of responsibility - In the instant case, circumstances and nature of offence were so serious and hence they did not warrant A-117 the benefit of the provisions of the Act, however, taking note of various aspects, sentence was reduced to minimum period, viz., 6 years to 5 years - Appeals disposed of.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Destruction of evidence - Retracted confession - Validity - Whether confession of appellant (A-118) was valid for recording his confession - Held, appellantâs confession was recorded by PW193 (DCP) - Confession revealed that appellant, upon instructions, caused destruction of evidence related to unauthorized possession of automatic firearms/weapons in a notified area, attracting provisions of the 1959 Act - Confession also implicated another co-accused (A-124) that corroborated with the confession of A-117 as well as A-124 - Hence, appellantâs confession was valid for recording conviction - Impugned conviction and sentence awarded to appellant was upheld - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25(1-A), 25(1- B)(a) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 201 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Destruction of evidence - Confession - Validity - Whether confession of appellant (A-124) could be the basis for recording his conviction - Held, appellantâs confession was recorded by PW193 (DCP) - Confession revealed that appellant knowingly destroyed evidence related to the offence - Appellant was in unauthorized possession of fire-arm, which corroborated in material particulars and confession of A-118 - However, there was no substantive evidence for convicting appellant u/s. 25(1-A) of the 1959 Act, though Trial Court had referred to the same while awarding sentence to him - Further, considering his age, i.e. 82 years as on date and taking note of the fact that minimum sentence for the offence u/s. 25(1-B) (a) being one year, while confirming his conviction, sentence awarded to appellant u/s. 25(1- B)(a) as well as u/s. 201 IPC was reduced to 1 year - Appeals disposed of.
