Sikandar Singh and others v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

09 July 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 484; (2010) 7 SCC 477; AIR 2010 SC 3580; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 266; 2010 CRLJ 3854; 2010 (3) JCC 2265; JT 2010 (7) SC 637; 2011 (1) MLJ(Crl) 59; 2010 (3) RCR(Criminal) 638; 2010(6) SCALE 632; [2010] 8 S.C.R. 373; 2010 (2) UC 1125

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 302 and 307 - Unlawful assembly - Common object - Meaning of - Held, a 'common object' does not require a prior concert and a common meeting of minds before attack - It is enough if each member of unlawful assembly has the same object in view and their number is five or more and that they act as an assembly to achieve that object - For determination of common object of unlawful assembly, conduct of each of members of unlawful assembly, before and at time of attack and thereafter, motive for crime, are some of relevant considerations - Further, what common object of unlawful assembly is at a particular stage of incident is essentially a question of fact to be determined, keeping in view nature of assembly, arms carried by members, and behaviour of members at or near scene of incident - It is not necessary under law that in all cases of unlawful assembly, with an unlawful common object, same must be translated into action or be successful - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 96 - Plea of self defence - Exercise of right of private defence - Held, right of private defence is a defensive right - There is no right of private defence where there is no apprehension of danger - Right of private defence is available only to one who is suddenly confronted with necessity of averting an impending danger which is not self created - Further, burden of establishing plea of self defence is on accused but it is not as onerous as one that lies on prosecution - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 96, 147, 148, 149, 302 and 307 - Arms Act,1959, s. 27 - Murder - Unlawful assembly - Common object - Conviction - Challenged - In all, eight persons were put on trial for having committed murder - Additional Sessions Judge convicted appellants u/s. 302 r/w s. 149 and u/s. 307 r/w s. 149 of IPC - Appellant 'S' was further convicted u/s. 27 of Arms Act - Appellant 'B' was convicted u/s. 148 of IPC - Appeals filed against conviction was dismissed - Present appeal - (A) Whether all appellants can be convicted u/s. 302 with aid of s. 149 of IPC? - Held, provision has essentially two ingredients viz. (i) commission of an offence by any member of an unlawful assembly and (ii) such offence must be committed in prosecution of common object of assembly or must be such as members of that assembly knew to be likely to be committed in prosecution of common object - In present case, from conduct of appellants it can safely be held that murder of deceased and injuries to PW-4 were immediately connected with their common object and, therefore, their case falls within ambit of s. 149 of IPC - All of them had knowledge of common object of assembly - (B) Plea of self defence - Held, in order to find out whether right of private defence was available or not, occasion for and injuries received by an accused, imminence of threat to his safety, injuries caused by accused and circumstances whether accused had time to have recourse to public authorities are relevant factors, yet number of injuries is not always considered to be a safe criterion for determining who the aggressor was - Right to defend does not include a right to launch an offensive or aggression - In present case, appellants were in fact, aggressors and being members of aggressors' party none of the appellants can claim right of self defence - Appellants failed to establish that they were exercising right of private defence - (C) Effect of non-explanation of injuries on person of appellant 'S' - Held, having regard to nature of injuries allegedly suffered by said appellant, case of prosecution cannot be overthrown because of non-explanation of said injuries - In view of fact that evidence against appellants for having committed offences were found to be cogent and creditworthy, it outweighs effect of omission on part of prosecution to explain injuries - Appeal dismissed.
