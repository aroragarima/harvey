Chief Commissioner of Income Tax, Cochin v Messrs Kesaria Tea Company Limited

Supreme Court of India

19 March 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Venkatarama Reddi, S. Rajendra Babu, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 181; (2002) 3 SCC 684; AIR 2002 SC 1473; 2002 (173) CTR 394; [2002] 254 ITR 434; JT 2002 (3) SC 181; 2002(3) SCALE 48; 2002 (2) SCJ 514; [2002] 2 S.C.R. 571; 2002 (2) SLT 602; 2002 (2) Supreme 502; [2002] 122 TAXMAN 91

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income-tax Act, 1961, ss. 256(1), 41(1) - Addition of Purchase tax - Liability - Assessee reversed provision made earlier towards purchase tax and therefore made entries in books of account during A/y 1985-86 - AO added sum of Rs. 14, 65, 997/- which represented provision made towards purchase tax during earlier A/y treating same as income of previous year - CIT (Appeals) held that there was no justification to include sums which were already included in course of reassessments made for years earlier years, however, upheld addition of Rs. 3, 02, 758 pertaining to A/y 1978-79 - On appeal by assessee, Tribunal set aside addition of Rs. 3, 02, 758/- which was upheld by Appellate Commissioner - Hence, instant appeal - Whether Tribunal is right in deleting addition of Rs. 3, 02, 758/- sustained by CIT (Appeals).

Held, HC, agreeing with the Tribunal, rightly held that the resort to s. 41(1) of the Act could arise only if the liability of the assessee can be said to have ceased finally without the possibility of reviving it. There is no material on record to rebut these factual observations made by the Tribunal. Income Tax Department has contended that the assessee itself took steps to write-off the liability on account of purchase tax by making necessary adjustments in the books, which itself is indicative of the fact that the liability ceased for all practical purposes. But, what the assessee has done is not conclusive. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Unilateral action on part of assessee by way of writing-off liability in its accounts does not necessarily mean that liability ceased in eye of law.
