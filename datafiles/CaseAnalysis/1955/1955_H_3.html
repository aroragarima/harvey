Delhi Cloth and General Mills Company, Limited v Harnam Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 April 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

Vivian Bose, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 86; AIR 1955 SC 590; 1955 (2) MLJ(SC) 141; 1955 (57) PLR 313; 1955 SCJ 645; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 402

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Prohibition Act

Summary: Civil Procedure - Recovery of money - Plaintiffs before the partition of India carried on the business of cotton cloth dealers at Lyallpur which now in Pakistan - Defendant was carrying on business at Delhi and other places and one of the places at which it carried on business before the partition was Lyallpur - Plaintiffs' carried on business with the defendant company for three or four years before 1947 and purchased cloth from the company from time to time - In the course of their business they used to make lump sum payments to the defendant against their purchases - On 28-7-1947 the account stood in the plaintiffs' favour - There was a balance of Rs. 79-6-6 lying to their credit plus a deposit of Rs. 1,000 as security - Plaintiff deposited a further Rs. 55,000 - Defendant company delivered cloth worth Rs. 43,583-0-0 to the plaintiffs against this amount at or about that time - That left a balance of Rs. 11, 496-6-6 - Suit was filed to recover this balance plus interest - Claim was decreed for Rs. 12,496-6-6 and this was upheld on appeal to the HC - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, property aspect is relevant for purposes of assignment, administration, taxation and the like; the contractual aspect for performance. In the instant case, Pakistan Ordinance regards debts as property and vests all evacuee property in the Custodian and requires every person holding such property to surrender it to the Custodian on pain of penalties prescribed by the Ordinance. The payment was made and that, exonerated the defendant from further liability. Such payment would operate as a good discharge even under the English rules. Further, whether the proper law of the contract applies or the English law of situs in a case of this kind, the defendant is exonerated because, the debt being 'property', the Ordinance divested the plaintiffs of ownership in it and vested the debt in the Custodian and at the same time interfered with the obligation for performance by providing that payment to the Custodian shall operate as a discharge of the obligation. Further, Pakistan Ordinance is not confiscatory. The decrees of the lower Courts are set aside. A decree will now be passed dismissing the plaintiffs' claim. Appeal allowed.
