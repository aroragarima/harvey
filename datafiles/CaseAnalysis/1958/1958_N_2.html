Â Nagendra Nath Bora and Another v The Commissioner of Hills Divisionand Appeals,Assam and Other Appeals

Supreme Court of India

07 February 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Syed Jaffer Imam, J.L. Kapur, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 62; AIR 1958 SC 398; 1958 SCJ 798; [1958] 1 S.C.R. 1240

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax

Keywords: Eastern Bengal And Assam Excise Act, 1910, Assam Revenue Tribunal (Transfer Of Powers) Act, 1948, Assam Excise Rules

Summary: Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226 and 227 - Eastern Bengal and Assam Excise Act, 1910, s. 9 - Assam Excise Rules, 1945, r. 232 - Assam Revenue Tribunal (Transfer of Powers) Act, 1948 - Jurisdiction - Establishment of liquor shop - Tenders were invited by the Deputy Commissioner, for the settlement of spirit shop for the financial year 1957-58, in December, 1956 - Appellants as members of the partnership submitted a tender for the same - Commissioner of Hills Division and Appeals, Assam, (First respondent) and the Commissioner of Excise, Assam (Second respondent) constituted under Assam Revenue Tribunal (Transfer of Powers) Act, 1948, settled the order with the appellants - The other competitors for the settlement of the said shop being dissatisfied with the orders of the respondents, moved the HC and challenged the validity of the settlement made in the appellant's favour - HC quashed the orders passed by the respondents on the ground that the Excise Appellate Authority had acted in excess of its jurisdiction, and that its order was vitiated by errors apparent on the face of the record - Appellants filed appeal against said order contending that the HC had misdirected itself in holding that the Appellate Authority had exceeded its jurisdiction in passing the order it did - Appellants further contended that there was no doubt that if the Appellate Authority whose duty was to determine questions affecting the right to settlement of a liquor shop, in a judicial or quasi-judicial manner, acts in excess of its authority vested by law, that is to say, the Act and the rules there under, its order it subject to the controlling authority of the HC - Whether the HC was right in holding that the Appellate Authority had exceeded its legal power?- Held, HC had no power u/art. 226 of the Constitution to issue a writ of certiorari in order to quash an error of fact, even though it may be apparent on the face of the record - It can do so only where the error was one of law and that was apparent on the face of the record - Any error of law or fact which it can correct as a court of appeal or revision cannot be a ground for the exercise of its power under that Article - The jurisdiction u/art. 226 of the Constitution was limited to seeing that the judicial or quasi-judicial tribunals or administrative bodies exercising quasi-judicial powers, do not exercise their powers in excess of their statutory jurisdiction, but correctly administer the law within the ambit of the statute creating them or entrusting those functions to them - The Act has created its own hierarchy of officers and Appellate authorities, to administer the law - So long as those Authorities function within the letter and spirit of the law, the HC has no concern with the manner in which those powers have been exercised - In the instant cases, the HC appears to have gone beyond the limits of its powers u/arts. 226 and 227 of the Constitution and set aside the order of the Appellate Authority - The powers of judicial interference u/art. 227 of the Constitution with orders of judicial or quasi-judicial nature, were not greater than the powers u/art. 226 of the Constitution - Under art. 226, the power of interference may extend to quashing an impugned order on the ground of a mistake apparent on the face of the record - But u/art. 227 of the Constitution, the power of interference was limited to seeing that the tribunal functions within the limits of its authority - Hence, interference by the HC either u/art. 226 or 227 of the Constitution, was not justified - Appeal allowed.
