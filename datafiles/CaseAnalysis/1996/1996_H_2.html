Messrs Krishna Motor Service By Its Partners v H. B. Vittala Kamath

Supreme Court of India

19 April 1996

Case Analysis

Bench

G.B. Pattanaik, K. Ramaswamy

Where Reported

1996 Indlaw SC 8; (1996) 10 SCC 88; 1996 (5) AD(SC) 127; AIR 1996 SC 2209; 1996 (2) ARBLR 1; 1996 (3) AWC 1366; 1996 (2) CCC 418; 1996 (3) CLT 46; JT 1996 (5) SC 162; 1996(4) SCALE 412; [1996] Supp1 S.C.R. 594; 1996 (4) Supreme 663

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Arbitration Act

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Contract & Commercial - Arbitration Act, 1940, s. 20 - Indian Partnership Act, 1932, s. 69(1),(3) - Instant appeals arose from the order of DB - Respondent filed an application u/s. 20 of the Act, for reference to the arbitrator in terms of the agreement - Trial Court rejected three out of 4 claims made by him and referred claim No.1 to the arbitration - HC on further consideration, in appeal, added two more items to the reference - Hence instant appeals - Whether respondent is entitled to a reference u/s. 20 of the Act -

Held, admittedly, the partnership firm was not registered as required u/s. 69(1) of the Act. The partnership deed does contain a clause for reference to arbitrate the disputes that would arise under the contract. If the right to dissolve the firm itself is in dispute and is subject matter of the suit for dissolution of the partnership firm, if a party to the contract of partnership seeks a reference for arbitration to resolve that dispute, it would be a right from a contract arisen in the proceedings for enforcement of the right to dissolve the firm. In that event, necessarily, the main part of s. 69(3) of the 1932 Act stands attracted and no such reference is valid in law. But in a case where the parties have already agreed for dissolution of the partnership by mutual consent, the partnership stood dissolved. There is no dispute as regards the right arising from the contract of a firm. The dispute is only with regard to working out the rights flown from dissolution for settlement of accounts of the dissolved firm or any right or power to realise the property of the dissolved firm etc. That right would form part of the exception engrafted in s. 69(3) of the 1932 Act. The object intended by the legislature appears to be that in spite of the defect of non-registration and the prohibition created in the main part of non-enforceability of the right arising from a contract, the parties having worked under that contract, to the limited extent of the enforcement of a right to realise the assets, settlement of the accounts of the dissolved firm or any right or power to realise the property of the dissolved firm are exceptions engrafted therein and gives right to the parties to enforce the same, independent of the right arising from the contract. Therefore, the parties are relieved from the prohibition created by operation of s. 69 of the Act. It would be seen that item (1) clearly falls within the exception provided in s. 69(3) of the 1932 Act. In respect of items (2), though it is widely worded, the respondent would be entitled to the question of entitlement towards the goodwill only upto the date of dissolution of the firm but not thereafter. With regard to items (3) and (4), they arise from the contract and these items would not come under any exceptions engrafted u/s. 69(3) of the 1932 Act. Under these circumstances, the HC was not right in making the reference in item No.(4). Appeals Allowed.
