Rishiroop Polymers Private Limited v Designated Authority and Additional Secretary

Supreme Court of India

23 March 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

Ashok Bhan, G.P. Mathur

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 180; (2006) 4 SCC 303; 2006 (196) E.L.T. 385; JT 2006 (4) SC 78; 2006(3) SCALE 477; 2006 (4) SCJ 152; 2006 (4) SCJ 311; [2006] 3 S.C.R. 252

Case Digest

Subject: Anti-Dumping; Customs

Summary: Customs - Anti-Dumping - Central Excise Act, 1944, s. 3(1) - Customs Act, 1962, s. 9 - Customs Tariff Act, 1975, ss. 9(A), 9B - Customs Tariff (Identification, Assessment and Collection of Anti-Dumping Duty on Dumped Articles and For Determination of Injury) Rules, 1995 - Importer - Review - Anti-dumping duty - Liberalization - Govt. of India adopted the path of liberalization in its fiscal policies - This shift invited foreign capital, goods, products etc., in open Indian market - It resulted in stiff competition for the domestic industry which had to now compete with the foreign products both in terms of price as well as its quality - Although the process of liberalization had its positive side by making available foreign products to domestic users, but it was also having a negative impact which was affecting the domestic industry, thereby sometimes leading to closure of the same and/or retarding its growth leading to an economic crisis - Govt. of India took enough speedy measures to ensure level field playing for domestic and foreign producers - Concern of the Govt. in this regard was translated into various amendments which were made in the 1975 Act - Later amendments were made and it became permissible for the Central Govt. to impose Anti-Dumping duty on importation of foreign articles which were found to be dumped in India at a price which was lower than the normal price of such imported goods in their country of manufacture - It defined the margin of profit, normal value and export price - It also provided for duration of levy of anti-dumping duty, its review from time to time as well as its continuance for a further period of five years, if the cessation of duty was likely to lead to continuance or recurrence of dumping and injury - Duty was over and above any other duty in force - Whether there was a need for imposition of anti-dumping duty and whether in the absence of such continuance, dumping would increase and the domestic industry would suffer - Held, it was of vital importance to note that in the initial imposition of duty, the appellant had accepted the position that determination of injury by the Designated Authority was proper and in conformity with the requirements of Annexure-II of the 1995 Rules - U/s. 9A(1) of the 1975 Act, the said initial imposition of anti-dumping duty was ordinarily contemplated to be continued and remain in effect for a full period of five years, at the end of which it would be subject to sunset review, the possible consequence of which would be the extension of the operation of the period of anti-dumping duty for another period of five years - This was subject to the provisions of sub-r. (1) of r. 23 of the 1995 Rules, under which the Designated Authority was empowered to review the anti-dumping duty imposed from time to time - Once anti-dumping duty had been initially imposed, it would be continued for five years unless on a review it was found - Neither the Designated Authority nor the appellant had placed any material on record which could possibly displace the findings given by the Designated Authority at the stage of initial anti-dumping duty - Therefore, no merit or substance in the appellant's contention regarding non-compliance with Annexure-II of the 195 Rules - Appeals dismissed.
