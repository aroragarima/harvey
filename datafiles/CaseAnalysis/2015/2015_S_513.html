Sujitendra Nath Singh Roy v State of West Bengal and others

Supreme Court of India

13 March 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Shiva Kirti Singh, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 186; AIR 2015 SC 1831; 2015 (110) ALR 532; 2015 (2) CalLT(SC) 14; 2015 (2) CalLT(SC) 58; JT 2015 (3) SC 114; 2015 (2) KLT 425; 2015 (2) RCR(Civil) 406; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 259B; 2015 (128) RD 420; 2015(3) SCALE 502; [2015] 3 S.C.R. 535

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: West Bengal Land Reforms & Tenancy Tribunal Act, 1997

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts, 136, 226, 227 - West Bengal Land Reforms & Tenancy Tribunal Act, 1997, s. 15 - Writ application - Maintainability - Appellant filed instant appeal against the order of HC, where HC placed reliance upon DB judgment of that very Court in the case of Manju Banerjee v. Debabrata Pal and held, writ petition filed by appellant was not maintainable - Whether writ application is maintainable against order of Tribunal refusing to initiate contempt proceedings against an authority arrayed as respondent no.5 before Tribunal.

Held, on careful consideration of judgment of DB the case of Manju Banerjee which has been followed in impugned order, SC is unable to agree with the view that writ petition u/arts. 226, 227 of the Constitution is not maintainable when Tribunal refuses to initiate a contempt proceeding. Such inference has been drawn by DB on the basis of some judgments of SC, as in the case of D.N.Taneja v Bhajan Lal, 1988 Indlaw SC 431. In those cases the order refusing to initiate proceeding had been passed by HC and not by Tribunal and, therefore, HC observed that in fit and proper case the aggrieved person who informed the court of the alleged act of contempt can approach the SC u/art. 136 of the Constitution. In those cases there could be no occasion to observe that aggrieved person can also approach HC u/art. 226, 227 of the Constitution. The submission, that because of similar powers of contempt vested in the Tribunal u/s. 15 of the Act of 1997, Tribunal ceases to be inferior to the HC for exercise of writ jurisdiction is devoid of any substance because it ignores that HCs have Constitutional status and are vested with extraordinary writ jurisdiction whereas the Tribunal is only a creature of statute. Hence, in the case of Manju Banjerjee , DB does not lay down the law correctly that when Tribunal refuses to initiate contempt proceeding, aggrieved person has remedy only u/art. 136 and not u/arts. 226, 227 of the Constitution. As held by Constitution Bench in the case of L. Chandra Kumar v Union of India and Others, 1997 Indlaw SC 2816, the power of judicial review of HC u/arts. 226, 227 of the Constitution cannot be taken away by a law or even by a Constitutional amendment. Hence, it will be indeed a rare case where HC can hold that writ petition against any order of inferior court or Tribunal is not maintainable. However, it is always open for HC in appropriate cases, to hold that a writ petition is not entertainable on account of propriety, Constitutional scheme, some settled rules of self-restraint or its peculiar facts. Hence, impugned order is set aside and matter is remitted back to HC for considering the writ petition of the appellant afresh on its own merits and as per law. Appeal allowed.
