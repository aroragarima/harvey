Dilawar Singh and another v State of Haryana and others

Supreme Court of India

16 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R. Banumathi, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 618; (2015) 1 SCC 737; 2014 CRLJ 4844; 2014 (4) JCC 2899; JT 2014 (10) SC 477; 2014 (4) RCR(Criminal) 351; 2014(10) SCALE 548; [2014] 7 S.C.R. 844

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss.302 and 149 - Murder - Acquittal - Sustainability - Appellants allegedly committed offence of murder and unlawful assembly - However, Trial Court and HC acquitted A-4, A-6 and A- 8 of the charges framed u/ss.302 and 149 IPC - Being aggrieved by the acquittal of A-4, A-6 and A-8, State and PW6 had preferred criminal appeal -

Held, A-6, has put forth defence plea of alibi. A-6, was a practising lawyer at Kurukshetra and he was working as a junior advocate in the office of Senior Advocate. DW6, advocate was examined who was also practising with Senior advocate. DW6 stated that on the date of occurrence A-6 was in the office of Senior Advocate and not at the place of occurrence. Trial Court has not accepted the plea of alibi raised by him only on the ground that the Senior Advocate with whom A-6 was practicing was not examined. HC held that evidence of DW-6 cannot be doubted as there was no reason to disbelieve him and plea of alibi taken by A-6 cannot be rejected on the ground that his Senior Advocate was not examined and on those findings as recorded, HC acquitted AW6. No perversity in the appreciation of evidence by HC and no substantial ground to interfere with the acquittal of A-6. Insofar as A-4 and A-8 are concerned, the case of the prosecution is that A-4 gave gandasi blow to deceased on his left arm and A-8 gave a blow with his sword on the right leg of deceased. PW-10 stated that death of the deceased was caused by sharp edged weapon and could not have been caused by any blunt weapon. HC was of the view that the overt act of A-4 and A-8, do not find corroboration with medical evidence and on those findings the HC set aside the conviction of A-4 and A-8, and acquitted them. The court of appeal would not ordinarily interfere with the order of acquittal unless the approach is vitiated by manifest illegality. In an appeal against acquittal, SC will not interfere with an order of acquittal merely because on the evaluation of the evidence, a different plausible view may arise and views taken by the courts below is not correct. Unless there are substantial and compelling reasons, the order of acquittal is not required to be reversed in appeal. State of Rajasthan vs. Shera Ram 2011 Indlaw SC 716, relied on. On evaluation of the evidence found by the HC while recording an order of acquittal does not suffer from any infirmity or illegality or manifest error. No reason to interfere with the order of acquittal of A-4, A-6 and A-8. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio: Court of appeal would not ordinarily interfere with the order of acquittal unless the approach is vitiated by manifest illegality.
