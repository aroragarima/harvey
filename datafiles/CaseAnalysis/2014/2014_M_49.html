FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Pawan Kumar Ralli v Maninder Singh Narula

Supreme Court of India

11 August 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 515; AIR 2014 SC 3512; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 3745; 2014 (5) ALT(SC) 1; 2014 (4) BC 1; 2014 (6) Bom.C.R. 8; 2014 (4) CompLJ 193; 2014 CRLJ 4197; 2014 (4) JCC (NI) 210; JT 2014 (9) SC 124; 2014 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3463; 2014 (2) OLR 654; 2014 (176) PLR 129; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 1; 2014 (4) RCR(Criminal) 29; 2014(9) SCALE 327; [2014] 7 S.C.R. 1047; 2015 (1) WLN(SC) 31

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s.138 - Cheque bounce - Sustainability - Appeal arose out of the judgment and order dt. 15-1-2013 of the HC passed in Criminal Miscellaneous Case filed by the respondent u/s. 482 of CrPC - By the said judgment, the High Court quashed the criminal proceedings initiated by the appellant u/s. 138 of the 1881 Act - Whether quashing of criminal proceedings by HC was sustainable

Held, in the case on hand, the Cheques in question were issued by the respondent and the same were dishonoured by the Bank on his instructions of 'stop payment' - Two communications, one a handwritten note dt. 27-4-2012 by the appellant himself and the other a formal legal notice dt. 24-5-2012 issued by the advocate, were served on the respondent calling upon him to pay the Cheque amounts - Respondent did not respond to the handwritten communication, but has replied to the legal notice issued through advocate on 24-5-2012 denying the allegation - Upon failure of the respondent to obey the handwritten communication as well as the legal notice, the appellant initiated criminal proceedings by filing Complaint Case on 5-7-2012 - It appeared that the respondent contested the matter before the Trial Court and also filed an application u/s.91 CrPC warranting the appellant to produce various documents. He has also moved an application u/s.410 CrPC seeking transfer of the Complaint to a different Court - It was noteworthy that all through out the pendency of proceedings before the Trial Court, the respondent did not raise the issue of 'limitation' - The issue was raised for the first time before the High Court in s.482 CrPC proceedings - HC, considering the handwritten note sent by the appellant on 27-4-2012 as 'notice' u/s. 138 of 1881 Act, came to the conclusion that the complaint was barred by limitation - HC had already clarified in Central Bank of India & Anr. 1999 Indlaw SC 571 (supra) that s. 138 of 1881 Act did not prescribe any specific form of notice, but mandates that it should be issued in writing within thirty days (w.e.f. 6-2-2003) of receipt of information from the banker about the dishonour of Cheque, with a demand to the drawer for making payment of the said amount - SC had perused the handwritten note dt. 27-4-2012 and found that it was issued within the mandatory period of thirty days of dishonour of cheques and contained (a) the subject amount of Rs.60,00,000/- given by the appellant as loan to the respondent under promissory notes; (b) the details of Cheque numbers and dates of issue with amounts and particulars of Bank; (c) Returning of Cheques by the banker dishonouring them on the ground of 'Stop Payment' by the respondent; (d) a demand for immediate repayment of the amount; and (d) a caution to the respondent that in case of failure on the part of respondent, the appellant would initiate legal proceedings - Thus, in SC's opinion, the handwritten note dt. 27-4-2012 fulfilled the mandatory requirements u/s.138(b) of Act and could be said to be a valid 'notice' in the light of SC's Judgment - Central Bank of India Another v Saxons Farms and Others 1999 Indlaw SC 571, relied on - Moreover, the document (Annexure P4) stood admitted by the appellant in his cross examination also - Therefore, in SC's opinion, HC had committed no error in considering the handwritten note dt. 27-4-2012 as 'notice' u/s. 138 of 1881 Act - However, when the issue of limitation came up for the first time before the HC, it ought to have dealt with the same on merits as per proviso to s. 142(b) of the Act - Said proviso appended to s.142(b) of 1881 Act was inserted by the 2002 Act and the legislative intent was, no doubt, in order to overcome the technicality of limitation period - Statement of Objects and Reasons appended to the Amendment Bill, 2002 suggested that the introduction of this proviso was to provide discretion to the Court to take cognizance of offence even after expiry of the period of limitation - MSR Leathers Vs. S. Palaniappan 2012 Indlaw SC 624, relied on - Only with a view to obviate the difficulties on the part of the Complainant, Parliament inserted the proviso to s.142(b) of 1881 Act in the year 2002 - It conferred a jurisdiction upon the Court to condone the delay - Subodh S. Salaskar Vs. Jayprakash M. Shah 2008 Indlaw SC 1422, relied on - It was no doubt true that at the time of filing the complaint, the Magistrate had to take cognizance of the complaint when it was within limitation and in case of delay in filing the complaint, the complaint had to come up with the application seeking condonation of delay - But, the peculiar fact of the instant case was that in the complaint, the complainant had only averred that he has sent the legal notice dt. 24-5-2012 but not mentioned about the handwritten note dt. 27-4-2012 - Basing on the said averment, the Trial Court was satisfied that the complaint is within the prescribed period of limitation - Hence, in instant case, raising the plea of limitation and Court exercising the discretion to condone the delay did not arise at all - In the peculiar facts and circumstances of the case, while keeping in mind the legislative intent and the specific plea of the appellant raised in the grounds for the Special Leave Petition that he should have been allowed to move an application for condonation of delay before the Trial Court as the respondent has not suffered any prejudice by reason of 25 days delay, SC strongly felt that the appellant should not have been deprived of the remedy provided by the Legislature - In fact, the remedy so provided was to enable a genuine litigant to pursue his case against a defaulter by overcoming the technical difficulty of limitation - Hence, HC had committed an error by not considering the issue of limitation on merits - In view of the settled principles of law in one case and in the peculiar facts and circumstances of the case, SC was of the considered opinion that the HC was not right in quashing the complaint merely on the ground that complaint was barred by limitation, that too a plea which was taken for the first time before the HC - On the other hand, the HC ought to have remanded the matter to the Trial Court for deciding the issue of limitation - Rakesh Kumar Jain v State Through Cbi, New Delhi 2000 Indlaw SC 407, relied on - At the same time, SC wanted to make it very clear that by this observation SC was not laying down a legal proposition that without even filing an application seeking condonation of delay at an initial stage, complainant could be given opportunity at any stage of the proceeding - SC came to the conclusion, to afford an opportunity for the complainant to move an application seeking condonation of delay, under the peculiar facts and circumstances of the case - Thus, in order to meet the ends of justice, we exercise our discretion u/art. 142 of the Constitution and set aside the impugned judgment of the HC quashing the criminal proceedings and restore the criminal proceedings before the Trial Court - Appeal allowed.
