Nilesh Dinkar Paradkar v State Of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

09 March 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. Sudarshan Reddy

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 188; (2011) 4 SCC 143; 2011 (3) JCC 1972; JT 2011 (3) SC 429; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 533; 2011(3) SCALE 473; [2011] 3 S.C.R. 792

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Maharashtra Control of Organized Crime Act, 1999, s. 3 - Indian Arms Act, 1959, ss. 3 and 25 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 115, 120-B and 302 - Voice identification evidence - It was alleged that accused Nos. 1, 2, 3 and 5 were active members of the organized crime syndicate - Accused No. 4 aided, abetted and conspired with accused Nos. 1, 2, 3 and 5 in commission of various offences punishable under the MCOC Act and conspired to murder a prominent businessman - FIR was registered against them - Trial Court convicted all the five accused for the offence punishable under provisions of IPC, Arms Act and MCOC Act - On appeal, HC allowed the appeals of accused Nos. 1, 2, 3 and 4 and they were acquitted of all the charges leveled against them - HC disbelieved the prosecution version in so far as the accused Nos. 1 to 4 were concerned - However, the appeal filed by the appellant (accused No. 5) was dismissed - HC made a distinction in the case of appellant only on the basis of the voice identification evidence, that the appellant's voice was identified by the officer who had taken him in custody - Hence, instant appeal - Whether having disbelieved the prosecution version against accused Nos. 1 to 4, the HC erred in upholding the conviction of the appellant? - Held, evidence of voice identification was prone to such extensive and sophisticated tampering, doctoring and editing that the reality could be completely replaced by fiction - Therefore, the Courts have to be extremely cautious in basing a conviction purely on the evidence of voice identification - In the instant case, approach adopted by the HC was erroneous - Having disbelieved the voice identification in the case of accused Nos. 1 and 2, there was no reason to adopt a different yardstick in the case of the appellant - HC erroneously overlooked the infirmities in the evidence with regard to the authenticity of the tape recording produced in Court - Further, veracity of the voice identification would not improve merely because a recording has been made after receiving official approval - The crucial identification was of the voice of the person talking on the tape - HC committed a grave error in confirming the conviction of the appellant as recorded by the Trial Court only on the evidence of voice identification - Appellant was entitled to the benefit of doubt as the prosecution failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt - Appellant acquitted of all the charges levelled against him - Appeal allowed.
