(1) Tata Power Company Limited; (2) Municipal v (1) Reliance Energy Limited and Others; (2)

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 877; (2009) 16 SCC 659; 2009(7) SCALE 513; [2009] 9 S.C.R. 625

Case Digest

Subject: Electricity & Energy

Keywords: Default, Supply Of Energy, Overhead Lines, Sub-Station, Transmission Of Energy, Main Transmission Lines, Tie-Line, Grid Tariff, Bulk Licensee, Competent Government, Indian Electricity (Amendment) Act, 1959, MERC (Terms and Conditions of Tariff) Regulations, 2005

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Electricity Act, 2003, ss. 2(70), 11, 23, 60, 86, 125 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910 - Indian Electricity (Amendment) Act, 1959 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Regulatory Commissions Act, 1998 - MERC (Terms and Conditions of Tariff) Regulations, 2005, reg. 24 - Maharashtra Electricity Regulatory Commission - Power and jurisdiction thereof, esp. over electricity generating companies - Functions of State Commissions u/s. 86 of 2003 Act - Interpretation to be given to the provisions of 2003 Act - Power Purchase Agreement/Arrangement(PPA), obtaining approval therefor u/reg. 24 of Regulations, 2005 - Private generating companies vis-a-vis Distribution licensees, and transmission licensees - Core question involved in the case, whether despite the Parliamentary intent of giving a go-bye to its licensing policy to generating companies, whether through imposing stringent regulatory measures the same purpose should be allowed to be achieved? - Held, activities of a generating company are beyond the purview of the licensing provisions - Legislative history since 1910 Act noted and examined - Private generating companies were subjected to an extensive control by the States; at that time Boards were in monopoly; full fledged licensing system was in vogue - Afterwards liberalized policies were adopted by the C. Govt. and State Govts. for certain purposes, thus vide 1998 Act, system of approval was introduced - Ultimately 2003 Act was passed acknowledging the necessity of providing a greater room for generation of electrical energy so as to enable the country to meet its requirements; now liberalization policy of the State provided for de-licensing of the generating companies - Activities of the erstwhile licensees of power generation on the one hand and those of transmission and distribution in electricity on the other were separated - Primary object of 2003 Act was to free the generating companies from the shackles of licensing regime; 2003 Act encourages free generation and more and more competition amongst the generating companies and the other licensees so as to achieve customer satisfaction and equitable distribution of electricity - Court stressed that 'if de-licensing of the generation is the prime object of the Act, the Courts while interpreting the provisions of the stature must guard itself from doing so in such manner which would defeat the purpose thereof; it must bear in mind that licensing provisions are not brought back through the side door of Regulations' - P /&gt;(A) Whether recourse to s. 23 of the 2003 Act can be taken for issuance of any direction to the generating company? - Held, No - 'Supply' - Whether chapter headings and marginal notes should be taken into consideration for the purpose of interpretation of the main provision? - Yes, in a case where interpretation of section vis-a-vis the scheme of the Act, the purport and object of the legislation, particularly having regard to the mischief it seeks to remedy; the chapter heading as also marginal note, are relevant - 'If by reason of a provision of a statute the generating companies are excluded from the licensing provisions, one of the principal tool of interpretation is that the mischief which was sought to be remedied may not be brought back by a side door' - Power u/s. 23 has to be construed harmoniously with other powers -Conclusions drawn by the Court - Inter alia - Activities of a generating company are beyond the purview of the licensing provisions - S. 21 occurs in the chapter of 'licensing' under which the generating companies would not be governed - If regulatory clause is sought to be applied in relation to allocation of power, the same would defeat the de-licensing provisions; Generating companies have the freedom to enter into contract and in particular long term contracts with a distribution company subject to the regulatory provisions contained in the 2003 Act - PPA for a long term is essential for increasing and decreasing the capacity of generation of electricity by the generating company, which purpose by the 2003 Act must be allowed to be achieved - S. 86(1)(b) of the 2003 Act clearly shows that the generating company indirectly comes within the purview of regulatory jurisdiction as and when directions are issued to the distributing companies by the appropriate Commission but the same would not mean that while exercising the said jurisdiction, the Commission will bring within its umbrage the generating company also for the purpose of issuance separate direction - Impugned judgment of Tribunal being unsustainable, set aside - Appeals allowed.
