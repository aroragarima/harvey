Mithilesh Kumari and Another v Prem Behari Khare

Supreme Court of India

14 February 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

K.N. Saikia, G.L. Oza

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 736; (1989) 2 SCC 95; 1997 ACJ 981; AIR 1989 SC 1247; 1989 (2) CCC 33; 1989 (76) CTR 27; 1989 (40) E.L.T. 257; [1989] 177 ITR 97; JT 1989 (1) SC 275; 1989 (1) KarLJ 424; 1989 MahLJ 210; 1989 (2) MLJ(SC) 1; 1989(1) SCALE 358; [1989] 1 S.C.R. 621; [1989] 44 TAXMAN 45; 1989 (1) UJ 530

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Benami Property

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Act, 1988 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Plaintiff-respondent filed a suit for a declaration that he be declared to be the sole and real owner of the suit house, and that the defendant-appellant be permanently restrained from transferring the suit house - Trial Court decreed the suit in favour of plaintiff-respondent - It was held that plaintiff-respondent paid the consideration and purchased the suit house benami in the name of the defendant-appellant who therefore, had no right to create any equitable mortgage or to transfer the suit house - First appeal and second appeal filed thereagainst were dismissed - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether concurrent findings of fact of the courts below required to be interfered with? - Held, concurrent findings are based on reasonable appreciation of evidence on record and, therefore, can in no way be said to be perverse or unreasonable - HC has analysed the facts and correctly applied the tests to determine whether the transaction was benami or not discussing the case law - Hence, no justification in the appeal u/art. 136 of the Constitution to interfere with the concurrent findings of fact - (B) Whether despite the decree in favour of the plaintiff-respondent his suit or action will be affected by the subsequent legislation, namely, the Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Act, 1988 and its predecessor Ordinance? - Held, in case of a qualifying or disqualifying statute it may be necessarily retroactive - When an Act is declaratory in nature the presumption against retrospectivity is not applicable - In the present case, Act is both a penal and a disqualifying statute - Acts of this kind only declare - Further, when the law nullifies the defences available to the real owner in recovering the benami property from the benamidar the law must apply irrespective of the time of the benami transactions - Furthermore, on the date of the s. 4 of the Act coming into force, present appeal was pending - Consequently, the appellate court is competent to take into account legislative changes since the decision under appeal was given and its powers are not confined only to see whether the lower court's decision was correct according to the law as it stood at the time when its decision was given - Once the decree of the HC appealed against, the matter became sub judice again and thereafter SC had seisin of the whole case, though for certain purposes - Hence, plaintiff-respondent's suit cannot be decreed under the law; and hence the decree passed by the lower courts annihilated and the suit dismissed - Appeal allowed.
