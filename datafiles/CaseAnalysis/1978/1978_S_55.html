Vyankates Dhonddeo Deshpande v Sou. Kusum Dattatraya Kulkarni and Others

Supreme Court of India

27 September 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, V.R. Krishna Iyer

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 91; (1979) 1 SCC 98; AIR 1978 SC 1791; 1979 HLR 247; [1979] 1 S.C.R. 955

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Revenue Sale, Land Improvement Loans Act, 1883

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Civil Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Joint family property - Discharge of debt - Pious obligation - Validity - Respondents/plaintiffs filed suit for recovering possession of land from appellant/defendant - During pendency of suit, a portion of land in dispute was acquired under the Act - Both parties claimed compensation - A reference u/s. 30 of the Act was made for determining eligibility for amount of compensation which was answered in favour of plaintiffs - Trial Court decreed suit in favour of plaintiff - On appeal, HC dismissed appeal - Hence, instant appeals - Whether there was any perversity or illegality in impugned judgment and decree of Courts below - Held, HC was in error in holding that Dharmashastras of Hindus never contemplated improvement loans being given by Govts. of day which were usually monarchies - Therefore, a debt of such kind which was contemplated under the Act could never been under contemplation of writers in whose texts pious liability was imposed on sons and others - Thus, property which was subject-matter of dispute was joint family property - Debt for which property was sold was not shown to be tainted with illegality or immorality - Therefore, suit property was liable to be sold at Court auction for two reasons, one that debt was joint family debt for benefit of joint family estate and all segments of joint family property were liable for discharge of debt - Secondly, under doctrine of pious obligation of sons to pay father's debt - Hence, in instant case no attempt was made to establish that debt was tainted with illegality or immorality - Therefore, sale was valid and purchaser acquired a full and complete title to property - Thus, sale was not void - Part of property was acquired and compensation was taken by plaintiffs subject to orders of Court - Appeals allowed.
