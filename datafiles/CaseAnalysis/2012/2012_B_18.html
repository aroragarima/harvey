Smt. Badami (Deceased) By Her L.R. v Bhali

Supreme Court of India

22 May 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 142; (2012) 11 SCC 574; AIR 2012 SC 2858; 2012 (5) ALD(SC) 99; 2012 (93) ALR 245; 2012 (4) AWC 4058; 2012 (4) CLT(SC) 422; 2012 (3) CTC 850; JT 2012 (5) SC 607; 2012 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3464; 2012 (6) MLJ(SC) 745; 2012 (2) OLR 399; 2013 (1) RCR(Civil) 821; 2012 (116) RD 821; 2012(5) SCALE 715; [2012] 6 S.C.R. 75

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Decree - Legality - Plaintiff filed suit for declaration and for permanent injunction alleging that defendant was the owner of the ancestral land and had received it at a prior arrangement and when she was in possession, there was a family settlement on 1-6-1972 and in that family settlement the defendant gave her whole share to the plaintiff and the possession of the same was also handed over in pursuance of that settlement but the name of the defendant continued as owner in the revenue records and despite the request of the plaintiff not to interfere with the possession there was interference - Defendant admitted the assertions in the plaint to be correct and prayed for decree of the suit - Suit was decreed - However, record of entries stood in the name of defendant and she remained in possession and enjoyed the same - Plaintiff thereafter, filed suit seeking permanent injunction against her restraining from alienating the land in any manner - Trial Judge relying on the earlier judgment and decree did not accept the stand put forth by the defendant that the said decree was obtained by fraud and passed a decree for permanent injunction restraining the defendant from alienating the suit land to anyone in any manner - On appeals, Additional District Judge affirmed the said findings - Appellate court gave credence to the family settlement and held that the parties were related and hence, there was no reason to discard the family settlement; and that it was a common phenomenon that a member of a family was given property out of love and affection - Being aggrieved, defendant, filed second appeals - Single Judge held that defendant had failed to discharge the onus that the initial decree was obtained by fraud and that the conclusion drawn by the courts below were justified - Hence, instant appeal - Whether family arrangement was bona fide - Held, by giving the entire property and putting plaintiff in possession, defendant would have been absolutely landless and would have been in penury - No iota of evidence was brought on record that plaintiff had given anything to defendant in the arrangement - Family arrangement did not remotely appear to be a bona fide as it was unimaginable that a person would divest herself of one's own property in entirety in lieu of nothing - Hence, all facets of fraud get attracted to the instant case - Judgment and decree of the HC and of the courts below were set aside - Appeal allowed.
