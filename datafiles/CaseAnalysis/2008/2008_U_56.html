Rajiv Arora v Union Of India & Ors

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Cyriac Joseph

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2676; (2008) 15 SCC 306; AIR 2009 SC 1100; JT 2008 (9) SC 499; 2008(12) SCALE 90; [2008] 12 S.C.R. 1026; 2009 (1) SLJ 98; 2009 (1) SLR 427

Case Digest

Subject: Defence & Security Forces; Service

Keywords: Air Force Rules, 1969, Court-Martial, Air Force Act, 1950

Summary: Defence & Security Forces - Service - Air Force Rules, 1969, r. 43 - General Court Martial - Non-examination of charge-witnesses - Legality - Appellant/Commissioned Officer in Indian Air Force (AIF) was charge-sheeted for improper conduct - General Court Martial was convened wherein appellant filed an application for substitution of the Judge Advocate - Respondent/authority disallowed appellant's application - Appellant challenged the Court Martial proceedings before the HC - Appellant contended that witnesses named in the charge-sheet were not examined, hence it was a violation of r. 43 of the Rules - HC dismissed appellant's writ petition - Hence the present appeal by appellant/delinquent - Whether non-examination of witnesses mentioned in the charge-sheet would vitiate the Court Martial proceedings - Held, respondents never denied or disputed appellant's contention that the charge-witnesses were not examined - Respondents, however, urged that no prejudice had been caused to the appellant due to non-examination of the said witnesses during the summary of evidence - Charges framed as against the appellant were specific and misconducts alleged in the charge-sheet were in relation to the persons named therein - No reason has been assigned as to why the named witnesses who only could prove the change had not been examined - HC in its impugned judgment proceeded to consider the issue on a technical plea, namely, no prejudice has been caused to the appellant by such non-examination - If the basic principles of law have not been complied with or there has been a gross violation of the principles of natural justice, the HC should have exercised its jurisdiction of judicial review - Before a Court Martial proceeding is convened, legal requirements therefore must be satisfied - Satisfaction of the officer concerned must be premised on a finding that evidence justified a trial on those charges and such a satisfaction cannot be arrived at without any evidence - If an order is passed without any evidence, the same must be held to be perverse - Further, HC was also not correct in opining that the appellant did not raise any objection with regard to the non-examination of witnesses in the impugned proceedings - Impugned HC judgement, therefore, is set aside - Appeal allowed.
