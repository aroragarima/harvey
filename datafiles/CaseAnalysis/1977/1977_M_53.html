Â Mohinder Singh Gill & Anr v The Chiief Election Commissioner, New Delhi & Ors

Supreme Court of India

02 December 1977

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Bhagwati, P.K. Goswami, P.N. Shingal

Where Reported

1977 Indlaw SC 53; (1978) 1 SCC 405; AIR 1978 SC 851; [1978] 2 S.C.R. 272

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Krishna Iyer, J.

Election - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 324, 329 - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - By a notification made u/s. 14 of the Act, the President called upon the Parliamentary Constituencies to elect members to the House of the People - Simultaneously, a notification was issued by the Chief Election Commissioner with a calendar of dates for different Parliamentary Constituencies in the country - Poll in the entire Parliamentary Constituency was peacefully over- Assistant Returning Officers announced the number of votes received by each candidate in the Assembly segments - According to the result sheets the appellant secured 1,96,016 votes, excluding postal ballots, as against his nearest rival candidate respondent No. 3 who secured 1,94,095 votes, excluding postal ballots - Margin of votes between the appellant and respondent No. 3 at that stage was 1921 votes - Subsequently, in view of the violent situation created in the office of the Returning Officer, he was prevented from ascertaining the result of the postal ballot papers and declaring the result of the election - Chief Election Commissioner (respondent No. 1) cancelled of the whole poll and also issued a direction to hold a fresh poll for the whole constituency - Writ application was filed - Appellant contended that the Election Commission had no jurisdiction to order re-poll of the entire Parliamentary Constituency; Secondly, the impugned order was violative of the principles of natural justice as no opportunity of a hearing was afforded to the appellant before passing the order; Thirdly, that the HC u/art. 226 of the Constitution was competing to go into the matter notwithstanding the provisions of art. 329(b) of the Constitution - HC dismissed the writ application - HC held that Art. 324 confers 'plenary executive powers' on the Election Commission and there were no limitations on the functions contemplated in art. 324 - HC further held that the principles of natural justice were not specifically provided for in art. 324 but were 'totally excluded while passing the impugned order' - Instant appeal - (A) Whether art. 329(b) a blanket ban on all manner of questions which may have impact on the ultimate result of the election, arising between two temporal termini viz., the notification by the President calling for the election and the declaration of the result by the returning officer? - Held, art. 329(b) was a blanket ban on litigative challenges to electoral steps taken by the Election Commission and its officers for carrying forward the process of election to its culmination in the formal declaration of the result - (B) Whether art. 226 also covered by this embargo and, if so, was s. 100 broad enough to accommodate every kind of objection, constitutional, legal or factual, which may have the result of invalidation of an election and the declaration of the petitioner as the returned candidate and direct the organisation of any steps necessary to give full relief? - Held, election, in this context, had a very wide connotation commencing from the Presidential notification calling upon the electorate to elect and culminating in the final declaration of the returned candidate - (C) Can the Election Commission, clothed with the comprehensive functions u/art. 324 of the Constitution, cancel the whole poll of a constituency after it had been held, but before the formal declaration of the result had been made, and direct a fresh poll without reference to the guidelines u/ss. 58 and 64(a) of the Act, or other legal prescription or legislative backing? - Held, Constitution contemplates a free and fair election and vests comprehensive responsibilities of superintendence, direction and control of the conduct of elections in the Election Commission - This, responsibility may cover powers, duties and functions of many sorts, administrative or other, depending on the circumstances - (D) If such plenary power exists, was it exercisable on the basis of his inscrutable 'subjective satisfaction'? - Held, two limitations were laid on its plenary character in the exercise thereof - Firstly, when Parliament or any State Legislature had made valid law relating to or in connection with elections, the Commission shall act in conformity with, not in violation of such provisions but where such law was silent art. 324 was a reservoir of power to act for the avowed purpose of, not divorced from pushing forward a free and fair election with expedition - Secondly, the Commission shall be responsible to the rule of law, act bona fide and be amenable to the norms of natural justice in so far as conformance to such canons could reasonably and realistically be required of it as fairplay-in-action in a most important area of the constitutional order, viz., elections - (E) Doctrine of natural justice - Held, conspectus of provisions bearing on the subject of elections clearly expresses the rule that there was a remedy for every wrong done during the election in progress although it was postponed to the post election stage and procedure as predicated in Art. 329(b) and the 1951 Act - Election Tribunal has, under the various provisions of the Act, large enough powers to give relief to an injured candidates if he makes out a case and such amplitude of power extends to direct ions to the Election Commission or other appropriate agency to hold a poll, to bring up the ballots or do other thing necessary for fulfilment of the jurisdiction to undo illegality and injustice and do complete justice within the parameters set by the existing law - Appeal dismissed.

P. K. Goswami, J.

Election - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - (A) Scope and ambit of Art. 324 of the Constitution - Held, art. 324(1) vests in the Election Commission the superintendence, direction and control of the preparation of the electoral rolls for, and the conduct of, all elections to Parliament and to the Legislature of every State and of elections to the offices of the President and Vice-President held under the Constitution - Commission was, therefore, entitled to exercise certain powers u/art. 324 itself, on its own right, in an area not covered by the Acts and the rules - (B) Whether Chief Election Commissioner had power to cancel the poll in the entire constituency and to order re-poll of the entire Parliamentary Constituency? - Held, it was clear from s. 58 and 64A that the legislature envisaged the necessity for the cancellation of poll and ordering of repel in particular polling stations where situation may warrant such a course - Both u/s. 58 and u/s. 64A the poll that was taken at a particular polling station could be voided and fresh poll could be ordered by the Commission - Provisions in ss. 58 and 64A could not therefore be said to rule out the making of an order to deal with a similar situation arising in several polling stations or even sometimes as a general feature in a substantially large area - Election Commission was competent in an appropriate case to order repoll of an entire constituency where necessary - (C) Whether HC u/art. 226 of the Constitution was competent to go into the matter notwithstanding the provisions of art. 329(b) of the Constitution? - Held, art. 329(b) rules out the maintainability of the writ application - All the substantial reliefs' which 'the appellants seek ' in the writ application including the declaration' of 'the election to 'be void and the declaration of appellant No. 1 to be duly elected, can be claimed in the election petition - 'It would be within the power of the HC, as the election court, to give all appropriate reliefs to do complete justice between the parties - In doing so it would be open to the HC to pass any ancillary or consequential order to enable it to 'grant the necessary relief provided under the Act - Writ application was' therefore barred u/art. 329(b) of the Constitution and the HC rightly dismissed it on that ground - Appeal dismissed.
