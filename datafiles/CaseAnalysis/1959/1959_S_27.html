Balkrishna Savalram Pujari and Others v Shree Dnyaneshwar Maharajsansthan and Others

Supreme Court of India

26 March 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 62; AIR 1959 SC 798; 1959 (2) MLJ 87; 1960 (2) MLJ(SC) 87; 1960 SCJ 1203; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 476

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure; Tort

Keywords: Actionable Wrong

Summary: Family & Personal - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Indian Limitation Act, arts. 120 and 124 - Specific Relief Act, s. 23 - (A) Whether High Court was right in holding that art. 120 of limitation act is applicable and that cause of action accrued more than six years before institution of present suits? - (B) Whether HC was right in holding that s. 23 does not apply to suits? - Art. 124 governs suits for possession of an hereditary office - Period of limitation prescribed by article is twelve years and said period begins to run when defendant takes possession of office adversely to plaintiff - Held, for application of art. 124 it is essential that defendant to suit must be in adverse possession of hereditary office in question - Hence, art. 124 does not apply to suits filed by appellants and if, art. 124 does not apply, art. 120 does - U/art. 120, time begins to run against plaintiffs when right to sue accrued to them, and that naturally poses question as to when right to sue accrued to appellants - Cause of action to file present suits had accrued either when trustees filed their suit u/s 9 of Specific Relief Act or when said suit was decreed and Guravs were consequently dispossessed - Hence, present suits which have been filed long after expiration of six years are barred by time u/art. 120 - (B) Whether conduct of trustees amount to a continuing wrong u/s. 23? - S. 23 of Limitation Act refers not to a continuing right but to a continuing wrong - A continuing wrong is essentially one that creates a source of continuing injury as opposed to one that was complete and makes doer liable for such continuance - A completed injury would not be a continuing wrong even though it might give rise to continuing damage - Further held, thus considered it is difficult to hold that trustees act in denying altogether alleged rights of Guravs as hereditary, worshippers and in claiming and obtaining possession from them by their suit in 1922 was a continuing wrong - Appeal dismissed.
