Jose S/O Edassery Thomas v State Of Kerala

Supreme Court of India

22 May 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 351; (2013) 14 SCC 172; AIR 2013 SC 2284; 2013 CRLJ 3232; 2013 (3) KLT 106; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 288; 2013(7) SCALE 523; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 1154

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 307 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appeal depicted a gruesome and repulsive picture that painted the appellant justifiably as the cruel protagonist who, invaded by passion of an uncultivated mind, in satiated by sexual desire and a further sense of suspicion that leads one into the realm of the worst, committed an act of unthinkable depravity - Ghastly act was the murder of wife - In fact, appellant, as the prosecution story would reveal, was not only driven by the fierce frenzy of passion but also his rational thinking had been totally darkened - In the ultimate eventuate, consumed by the fire and ire of anger, he burnt his wife to death - Appellant might have thought that he would bring an end to the anarchy in his house but his uncontrolled act ushered in anarchy of the darkest hour in his own life - Result was the conviction u/ss. 302 and 307 of IPC and sentence for life and rigorous imprisonment for three years on both the counts by Trial Court which had received the stamp of approval by HC in respect of conviction u/s. 302 IPC - Hence instant appeal - Whether conviction of appellant was sustainable - Held, ample of evidence on record indicated that the deceased was conscious and hence, SC was inclined to accept the dying declaration which would reveal the cruel treatment meted out by the husband to the wife, the suspicion harboured by appellant and the threats given - True it was, wife of appellant had stated that she had suspected that her husband might have set her ablaze but to prove the said aspect, there were numerous circumstances which Trial Judge as well as the HC had taken into consideration - Circumstances which lead singularly to the guilt of appellant were that appellant was sleeping in the bed room on the eastern side of the room where she was sleeping and it was a small house; that the bed room was not having any shutters; that PW3 woke up on hearing the cries of the deceased; that the accused had purchased petrol from the petrol pump belonging to PW5 in a bottle; that Chemical Analysis Report, had clearly mentioned that kerosene was not detected in any of the material objects sent for chemical analysis; that appellant was seen running away from the house by PW3 and PW7; that it was clearly deposed by PW3, daughter, that the father used to demand that mother should sleep with him, but she could not oblige him; and that he had threatened to kill her - Elder daughter had deposed that the father was doubting the husband of PW3 to have illicit relationship with the mother - Deceased wife had also deposed that the mother was 52 years of age and was infirm and not in a position to cater to the desire of her husband - All these circumstances appreciated in the context of the dying declaration clearly established the involvement of appellant in causing burn injuries on the deceased - Appeal dismissed.
