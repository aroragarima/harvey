Central Bureau Of Investigation, Hyderabad v K. Narayana Rao

Supreme Court of India

21 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 313; (2012) 9 SCC 512; 2012 CRLJ 4610; 2012 (6) CTC 569; JT 2012 (9) SC 359; 2012 (4) KLT 92; 2013 (1) OLR 74; 2013 (170) PLR 160; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 601; 2012(9) SCALE 228; [2012] 9 S.C.R. 54

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 419, 420, 467, 468 471 r/w s. 109 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, s. 13(2) r/w s. 13(1)(d) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 482 - Complicity of offence - Quashing of criminal proceedings - Challenged - Respondent (accused) was legal practitioner and a panel advocate for 'V' Bank - Respondent and other accused officials of 'V' bank, abusing their official position as public servants, conspired with private individuals (other accused) for defrauding the said bank by sanctioning and disbursement of housing loans to borrowers in violation of bank's rules and caused wrongful loss to the bank and corresponded gain for themselves, thereby allegedly committed offences ss. 120-B, 419, 420, 467, 468 471 r/w s. 109 of IPC and s. 13(2) r/w s. 13(1)(d) of the Act - Appellant (CBI) filed charge sheet against accused persons and respondent was arrayed as an accused alleging that he gave false legal opinion in respect of 10 housing loans - Aggrieved respondent filed a petition u/s. 482 of CrPC before HC to quash the criminal proceedings against him, which was allowed - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant (CBI) contended that respondent (accused) did not verify the originals pertaining to housing loans before giving legal opinion - Whether impugned order of HC quashing criminal proceedings against respondent was justified - Held, it was an admitted case of prosecution that respondent's name was not mentioned in FIR - Only in charge sheet, respondent was shown as accused No. 6 stating that he submitted false legal opinion to Bank in respect of housing loans in the capacity of a panel advocate and did not point out actual ownership of properties in question - Though statements of several witnesses were enclosed along with charge-sheet, they speak volumes about others - However, there was no specific reference to the role of respondent along with the main conspirators - A professional might be held liable for negligence on one of the 2 findings, viz., either he was not possessed of the requisite skill which he professed to have possessed, or, he did not exercise, with reasonable competence in the instant case, skill which respondent did possess - Thus, liability against an opining advocate arises only when lawyer was an active participant in a plan to defraud the Bank - In instant case, there was no evidence to prove that respondent was abetting or aiding the original conspirators - Merely because respondent's opinion might not be acceptable, he could not be mulcted with criminal prosecution, particularly, in absence of tangible evidence that he associated with other conspirators - It was cleared that if there was a link or evidence to connect respondent with the other conspirators for causing loss to institution, undoubtedly, prosecuting authorities were entitled to proceed under criminal prosecution - Such tangible materials were lacking in instant case of respondent - There was no prima facie case for proceeding in respect of charges alleged against respondent - Impugned order of HC was justified - Appeal dismissed.
