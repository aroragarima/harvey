Â Sastri Yagnapurushadji and Others v Muldas Bhudardas Vaishya and Another

Supreme Court of India

14 January 1966

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Mohammad Hidayatullah, Vaidynathier Ramaswami, P. Satyanarayana Raju

Where Reported

1966 Indlaw SC 392; AIR 1966 SC 1119; 1967 MahLJ 289; 1967 MPLJ 289; [1966] 3 S.C.R. 242

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Family & Personal - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 41, r.1, O 3, r.4 - Constitution, 1950, art. 25(2)(b) - Bombay Hindu Places of Public Worship (Entry-Authorisation) Act, 1956, s. 3 - Swaminarayan sect - Rights of non-Satsangi Harijans to enter temples - (A) Whether Vakalatnama signed by in favour of Govt. Pleader when accepted by Assistant Govt. Pleader is valid and presentation of appeal is competent? - Held, O. 41, r. 1 of Civil Procedure requires, appeal signed by appellant or his Pleader - O. 3, r. 4 of Code relates to appointment of a Pleader - Vakalatnama was signed in favour of Govt. Pleader and Assistant Govt. Pleader could not have accepted it - Appeal was accepted by Office of Registrar of appellate side of High Court, appeal was admitted and it finally came up for hearing before HC - Failure of registry to invite attention of Assistant Govt. Pleader to irregularity committed in presentation of said appeal cannot be said to be irrelevant in dealing with validity of contention raised by appellants - If Registry had returned appeal as irregularly presented, irregularity could have been immediately corrected - One of rules framed by HC on its appellate side - R. 95 seems to authorise an Advocate practicing on appellate side of HC to appear even without initially filing a vakalatnama in that behalf - If an appeal is presented by an Advocate without a vakalatnama duly signed by appellant, he is required to produce vakalatnama authorising him to present appeal or to file a statement signed by himself that such vakalatnama has been duly signed by appellant in time - (B) Whether Swaminarayan Sampradaya (sect) is a distinct and separate religion and their temples do not come within ambit of provisions of Bombay Hindu Places of Public Worship (Entry-Authorisation) Act ? - Further held, s.3 of Act provides, inter alia, that every temple to which Act applied shall be open to Harijans for worship in same manner and to same extent as other Hindus in general - Thus it throws open Hindu temples to all classes and sections of Hindus and it puts an end to any effort to prevent or obstruct or discourage Harijans from entering a place of public worship or from worshipping or offering prayers threat, or performing any religious service therein, in like manner and to like extent as any other Hindu of whatsoever section or class may so enter, worship, pray or perform - In attempting to answer whether Swaminarayan sect is a religion distinct and separate from Hindu religion, and consequently, temples belonging to said sect do no fall within ambit of s.3 of Act one must inevitably enquire what are distinctive features of Hindu religion - In ascertaining broad and comprehensive aspect of Hindu religion, it may be permissible to enquire what, according to this religion, is ultimate goal of humanity - It is release and freedom from unceasing cycle of births and rebirths, Moksha or Nirvana, which is ultimate aim of Hindu religion and philosophy, represents state of absolute absorption and assimilation of individual soul with infinite - The, Hindu Marriage Act, 1952, Hindu Succession Act, 1956, Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956; and Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance extend application of these Acts to all persons who can be regarded as Hindus in this broad and comprehensive sense - Hence plea raised by appellants that Satsangis who follow Swaminarayan sect form a separate and distinct community different from Hindu community and their religion is a distinct and separate religion different from Hindu religion, is entirely misconceived - Philosophically, Swaminarayan is a follower of Ramanuja, and essence of his teachings is that every individual should follow main Vedic injuncitons of a goods, pious and religious life and should attempt to attain salvation by path of devotion to Lord Krishna - Right to enter temples which has been vouchsafed to Harijans by Act in substance symbolises right of Harijans to enjoy all social amenities and rights and social justice is main foundation of domestic way of life enshrined in provisions of Indian Constitution - Appeal dismissed.
