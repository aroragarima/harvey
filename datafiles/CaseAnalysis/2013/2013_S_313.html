Â Dharam Pal and others v State of Haryana and another

Supreme Court of India

18 July 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Vikramajit Sen, Ranjan Gogoi, Surinder Singh Nijjar, M.Y. Eqbal

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 455; (2014) 3 SCC 306; AIR 2013 SC 3018; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 3266; 2013 CRLJ 3900; JT 2013 (10) SC 572; 2013 (3) KLT 438; 2013 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2411; 2013 (3) MLJ(Crl) 444; 2014(4) M.P.H.T. 354; 2013 (2) OLR 532; 2013 (172) PLR 230; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 787; 2013 (3) RLW 2550; 2013(9) SCALE 207

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 173(3), 190, 200, 202, 482 - Application u/s. 482 - Quashing of - Maintainability - NS who was shown as an accused, appellants and others were included in column 2 of the police report, despite the fact that they too were named as accused in the FIR - After going through the police report, Trial Court, summoned appellant and 3 others, who were not included as accused in the charge-sheet for the purpose of facing trial along with NS - Order of the Trial Court was questioned by way of revision - Lower Appellate Court upheld the order of Trial Court and revision was dismissed - Aggrieved appellant filed application u/s. 482 CrPC, HC dismissed the application and affirmed the order of Trial Court - Hence instant appeals - Did the Committing Magistrate have any other role to play after committing the case to the Court of Session on finding from the police report that the case was triable by the Court of Session - Held, since a police report was submitted by the police, u/s. 173(3) CrPC sending up one accused for trial, while including the names of the other accused in column 2 of the report - On receiving such police report, the Magistrate did not straight away proceed to commit the case to the Court of Session but, on an objection taken on behalf of complainant, treated as a protest petition, issued summons to those accused who were named in column 2 of the charge sheet, without holding any further inquiry, as contemplated u/ss. 190, 200 or even 202 of CrPC, but proceeded to issue summons on the basis of the police report only - Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Disagreement with police report - Magistrate have jurisdiction to issue summons - Sustainability - If the Magistrate disagrees with the police report and was convinced that a case was made out for trial against the persons who were placed in column 2 of the report, does he have the jurisdiction to issue summons against them also in order to include their names, along with NS, to stand trial in connection with the case made out in the police report - Held, Magistrate did not accept the Final Report filed by IO against accused, whose names were included in column 2, as he was convinced that a prima facie case to go to trial was made out against them as well, and issued summons to them to stand trial with the other accused, NS - Order accordingly.

(C) Criminal - Issuance of summons - Procedure to be followed - Sustainability - Having decided to issue summons against appellants, was the Magistrate required to follow the procedure of a complaint case and to take evidence before committing them to the Court of Session to stand trial or whether he was justified in issuing summons against them without following such procedure - Held, Magistrate, if he was satisfied that a prima facie case was made out to go to trial despite the final report submitted by the police - In such an event, if the Magistrate decided to proceed against the persons accused, he would have to proceed on the basis of the police report itself and either inquire into the matter or commit it to the Court of Session if the same was found to be triable by the Session Court - Order accordingly.

(D) Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 319, 193, 209 - Case committed to sessions judge - Issuance of summons - Sustainability - Whether the Session Judge issue summons u/s. 193 CrPC as a Court of original jurisdiction and upon the case being committed to the Court of Session, could the Session Judge issue summons separately u/s. 193 of CrPC would he have to wait till the stage u/s. 319 of CrPC was reached in order to take recourse thereto and Was Ranjit Singh v. State of Punjab, 1998 Indlaw SC 1220, which set aside the decision in Kishun Singh v. State of Bihar, 1993 Indlaw SC 197, was rightly decided or not - Held, Session Judge was entitled to issue summons u/s. 193 CrPC upon the case being committed to him by Magistrate - S. 193 CrPC entail that a case should, first of all, be committed to the Court of Session by the Magistrate - Second condition was that only after the case was committed to it, could the Court of Session take cognizance of the offence exercising original jurisdiction - Decision in Kishun Singh's case 1993 Indlaw SC 197 was the correct decision and the Lower Appellate Court acting as a Court of original jurisdiction, could issue summons u/s. 193 CrPC on the basis of the records transmitted to him as a result of the committal order passed by the Trial Court - View taken by the referring Court was accepted and it was held that the decision in the case of Kishun Singh v. State of Bihar 1993 Indlaw SC 197 and not the decision in Ranjit Singh v. State of Punjab 1998 Indlaw SC 1220 lays down the law correctly in respect of the powers of the Session Court after committal of the case to it by the Trial Court u/s. 209 CrPC - Order accordingly.
