Â Union of India v V. Sriharan @ Murugan and others

Supreme Court of India

02 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla, H.L. Dattu, Pinaki Chandra Ghose, A.M. Sapre, Uday Umesh Lalit

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 879; (2016) 7 SCC 1; 2016 CRLJ 845; JT 2015 (11) SC 480; 2015 (4) MLJ(Crl) 645; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 234; 2015(13) SCALE 165; 2015(13) SCALE 312

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Sovereign, Fundamental Right, False Evidence, Life Imprisonment, Conspiracy, Pardon, Capital Punishment, Undertrial, Criminal Justice System, Wealth, Mercy Petition, CBI, Identical, Certified Copy, Territorial Jurisdiction, Forum, Assembly, Penal Provision, Transportation, Subordinate, Restriction, Criminal Court, Two Cases, Challenge, Secretary, Presiding Officer, Death Sentence, Rarest Of Rare, Prohibit, Public Order, Imprisonment For Life, Innocent, Criminal Justice, Proviso, Delhi Special Police Establishment, Recommendation, Background, Minimum Sentence, Justification, Dealing, Legislative Power, Mercy, Prisoner, Remission, Individual Case, Jail Manual, Government of India Act, 1935, Interfere, Explosive Substances Act, 1908, Commutation, Terrorist And Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, Caste, History, Alternative, Consultation, Advocate General, Foreigners Act, 1946, Character, Passports Act, 1967, Noting, Ministers, Judiciary, Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946, Legal Principles, Omitted, Judicial Power, Criminal Justice Act, 2003, Concurrent, Prevention Of Terrorism Act, 2002, Indian Wireless Telegraphy Act, 1933, Government of India Act, 1919, Maharashtra Control Of Organised Crime Act, 1999, Treaty, Karnataka Lokayukta Act, 1984, Prisons Act, 1894, Indian Penal Code (Amendment) Bill, Prison Rules, Association, Murder (Abolition of the Death Penalty) Act, 1965, Currency Notes Forgery Act, 1899

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 307, 326 - Indian Explosive Act, 1908, ss. 3, 5 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161 - Conviction for Murder - Remission Challenged - Respondent no.1 along with other 6 accused was charged for assassination of politician 'X' - Trial Court convicted three accused u/ss. 302, 120B, of IPC and awarded death sentence and remaining four accused were awarded imprisonment for life - Further, Appellate Court confirmed death sentence and review petitions filed by appellant/CBI against no offence u/s. 3(3) of TADA and other against confirmation of death sentence - HC allowed review petition of accused no.1 and sentenced was altered to imprisonment for life - Further, state govt. proposed to remit sentence of life imprisonment and to release respondent nos. 1-7 - Hence, instant writ petitions and reference to larger bench. (a) Whether imprisonment for life in terms of s. 53 r/w. s. 45 of IPC meant imprisonment for rest of life of prisoner or a convict undergoing life imprisonment has a right to claim remission.

Held, imprisonment for life in terms of s. 53 r/w s. 45 IPC, only means imprisonment for rest of the life of the prisoner subject, however, to the right to claim remission, etc. as provided u/arts. 72 and 161 of Constitution, to be exercisable by the President and the Governor of the State and also as provided u/s. 432 of CrPC Further, with regard to remission, the power derived from the Penal Code for any modified punishment within the punishment provided for in the Penal Code for such specified offences can only be exercised by the HC and in the event of further appeal only by the SC and not by any other Court in this country. To put it differently, the power to impose a modified punishment providing for any specific term of incarceration or till the end of the convict's life as an alternate to death penalty, can be exercised only by the HC and the SC and not by any other inferior Court. Hence, ratio laid in swamy sharddananda case, that special category of sentence, instead of death, for term of exceeding 14 years and put that category beyond application of remission is well found and answer said question in affirmative and opinion expressed in Sangeet case, that deprival of remission power of appropriate govt. by awarding sentence of 20-25 years without any remission is not in consonance with law and specifically overrule the same. Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 432, 433 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161, 32 - Whether appropriate govt. was permitted to grant remission u/s. 432/433 of CrPC, after pardon power was exercised u/art. 72 by President and u/art. 161 by Governor of State or by SC of its Constitutional Power u/art. 32 of Constitution.

Held, there is every scope and ambit for the Appropriate Government to consider and grant remission u/ss. 432 and 433 of CrPC even if such consideration was earlier made and exercised u/art.72 by the President and u/art. 161 of Constitution by the Governor. As far as the implication of art. 32 of Constitution, it is already held that the power u/ss. 432 and 433 of CrPC, is to be exercised by the Appropriate Government statutorily, it is not for this Court to exercise the said power and it is always left to be decided by the Appropriate Government, even if someone approaches this Court u/art. 32 of Constitution. Order accordingly.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 432(7) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 248, 249, 250, 251, 252, 73 - Power of Union and State - Distinction - Whether s. 432(7) of CrPC, clearly gives primacy to executive power of Union and excludes executive power of State where power of Union was co-extensive and whether there could be two Appropriate Governments u/s. 432(7) of CrPC and whether Union or State has primacy for exercise of power u/s. 432(7) of CrPC over subject matter enlisted in List III of Seventh Schedule for grant of remission as a co-extensive power.

Held, where the case falls under the first test, i.e., the offences fell under the provisions of the Penal Code, which law was covered by Entry 1 of List III of the Seventh Schedule, namely, the Concurrent List which enabled both the Centre as well as the State Government to pass any law, it will be governed by s. 432(7)(a) of the CrPC, in which event, the power will be exclusive to the Union. In cases which fall under the situation where the offence is dealt with by the prosecuting agency of the Central Government, by virtue of the proviso to art. 73 of Constitution, the Executive Power of the Central Government is saved and, therefore, in such cases, as was dealt with by this Court in G.V. Ramanaiah, there again the power would exclusively remain with the Centre. Cases falling under second situation like the one covered by arts. 248 to 252 of Constitution, wherein, the competence to legislate laws was with the State, and thereby if the Executive Power of the State will be available, having regard to the mandate of these Articles which empowers the Union also to make laws and thereby if the Executive Power of the Union also gets extended, though the power is co-extensive, it must be held that having regard to the special features set out in the Constitution, in these situations, the Union will get the primacy to the exclusion of the State. Order accordingly.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 432(1) - Power of remission - Suot moto power - Scope - Whether suo motu exercise of power of remission u/s. 432(1) of CrPC was permissible in the scheme of Section, if yes, whether procedure prescribed in sub- section (2) of the same section was mandatory or not.

Held, the exercise of power u/s. 432(1) of CrPC should always be based on an application of the person concerned as provided u/s. 432(2) and after duly following the procedure prescribed u/s. 432(2). Hence, therefore, fully approve the declaration of law made by this Court in Sangeet's case, that the power of Appropriate Government u/s. 432(1) of CrPC, cannot be suo motu for the simple reason that this Section is only an enabling provision. Further, such a procedure to be followed u/s. 432(2) of CrPC is mandatory. The manner in which the opinion is to be rendered by the Presiding Officer can always be regulated and settled by the concerned HC and the SC by stipulating the required procedure to be followed as and when any such application is forwarded by the Appropriate Government. Hence, answer the said question to the effect that the suo motu power of remission cannot be exercised u/s. 432(1) of CrPC that it can only be initiated based on an application of the persons convicted as provided u/s. 432(2) of CrPC and that ultimate order of suspension or remission should be guided by the opinion to be rendered by the Presiding Officer of the concerned Court. Order accordingly.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 432, 433, 435(1) - Whether term 'Consultation' stipulated in s. 435(1) of CrPC implies 'Concurrence'.

Held, s. 435(1) of CrPC, mandatorily requires the State Government, if it is the 'Appropriate Government' to consult the Central Government if the consideration of grant of remission or commutation u/ss. 432 or 433 in a case which falls within any of the three sub-clauses (a)(b)(c) of s. 435(1) of Cr.P.C. In other words, s. 435(1) of CrPC sets out three categories under clauses (a), (b) and (c) thereof and states inter alia that the powers conferred by ss. 432 and 433 of CrPC upon the State Government shall not be exercised except after consultation with the Central Government. Further, the meaning of expression 'Consultation' varies from case to case depending upon the fact situation and the context of the statute as well as the object it seeks to achieve. Hence, it is imperative that it is always safe and appropriate to hold that in those situations covered by sub-clauses (a) to (c) of s. 435(1) of CrPC falling within the jurisdiction of the Central Government it will assume primacy and consequently the process of 'Consultation' in reality be held as the requirement of 'Concurrence'. Order accordingly.
