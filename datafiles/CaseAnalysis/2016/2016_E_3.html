E. Subbulakshmi v Secretary to Government and others

Supreme Court of India

17 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Khanwilkar, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 837; (2017) 1 SCC 757; AIR 2016 SC 5410; 2017 CRLJ 1006; JT 2017 (2) SC 127; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 125; 2016(12) SCALE 225

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Maintenance, Signature, Confessional Statement, Commissioner Of Police, Detenu, Secretary, Reliance, Detaining Authority, Subjective Satisfaction, Tamil Nadu Prevention of Dangerous Activities of Bootleggers, Drug-offenders, Goondas, Immoral Traffic Offenders, Forest-offenders, [Sand-Offenders], Slum-grabbers and [Video Pirates] Act, 1982, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Criminal, Tamil Nadu Public Property (Prevention of Damage and Loss) Act

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 22(5) - Tamil Nadu Prevention of Dangerous Activities of Bootleggers, Drug-offenders, Goondas, Immoral Traffic Offenders, Forest-offenders, [Sand-Offenders], Slum-grabbers and [Video Pirates] Act, 1982, ss. 3(1), 3(2) - Illegal detention - Challenged - Detenu's mother filed a writ petition in HC challenging the Detention Order dt. 04-12-2015 contending that the typed set of booklet furnished along with impugned detention order to detenu was illegible, in particular copy of FIR in respect of crime - HC dismissed petition holding that detenu did not make any representation to Appropriate Authority nor brought the said fact to notice of concerned authority - Hence, instant special leave petition.

Held, language of impugned detention order it was seen that the stated G.O. dt. 18-10-2015 was an order issued by State Govt. authorizing or delegating power to Commissioner to issue order u/s. 3(2) of the Act. The order having been issued in exercise of powers conferred u/s. 3(1) of the Act and being a statutory order has nothing to do with the grounds of detention. What was imperative was to supply all the documents which were relied upon by Detaining Authority for forming subjective satisfaction for the purposes of art. 22(5) of Constitution. However, G.O. was regarding the delegation of power to Commissioner to issue detention order. The validity of that order was not challenged by petitioner. In other words, the power of Commissioner to pass a detention order was not put in issue. The said G.O. was not relevant for forming subjective satisfaction by Detaining Authority. The grounds of detention were about prejudicial activities in which detenu had indulged in the past or in view of Detaining Authority the detenu has the propensity to indulge in similar prejudicial activities even in future. That was the quintessence for exercising power to detain any person. Suffice it to observe that the subject document, not being a relied upon document on grounds of detention or for forming subjective satisfaction by Detaining Authority, failure to furnish copy thereof to detenu does not vitiate the action taken by Detaining Authority nor the continued detention of detenu. The ground of detention should be read as a whole, it principally relies on the ground case being crime also adverts to other serious offences registered against detenu and pending trial including conduct of detenu when he tried to escape from police custody. The totality of circumstances having been taken into account by Detaining Authority there was no reason to doubt the subjective satisfaction arrived at for issuance of impugned detention order, in the fact situation of instant case. Petition dismissed.
