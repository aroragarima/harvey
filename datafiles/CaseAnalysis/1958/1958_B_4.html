H. Venkatachala Iyengar v B. N. Thimmajamma and Others

Supreme Court of India

13 November 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, A.K. Sarkar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 127; AIR 1959 SC 443; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 426; [1959] Supp1 S.C.R. 426

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Evidence Act, Indian Succession Act, Survivorship, Succession Act 1925

Summary: Family & Personal - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Indian Succession Act, 1925, ss. 45, 47, 63 and 67 - Validity of Will - Held, u/s. 67, if a document was alleged to be signed by any person, the signature of the said person must be proved to be in his handwriting, and for proving such a handwriting u/ss. 45 and 47 of the Act the opinions of experts and of persons acquainted with the handwriting of the person concerned were made relevant - Question as to whether the will set up by the propounder was proved to be the last will of the testator had to be decided in the light of these provisions - Will had to be proved like any other document except as to the special requirements of attestation prescribed by s. 63 of the Indian Succession Act - In dealing with the proof of wills the court would start on the same enquiry as in the case of the proof of documents - Propounder would be called upon to show by satisfactory evidence that the will was signed by the testator, that the testator at the relevant time was in a sound and disposing state of mind, that he understood the nature and effect of the dispositions and put his signature to the document of his own free will - However, when the evidence adduced in support of the will was disinterested, satisfactory and sufficient to prove the sound and disposing state of the testator's mind and his signature as required by law, courts would be justified in making a finding in favour of the propounder - The onus on the propounder could be taken to be discharged on proof of the essential facts just indicated - Further, propounder of the will had to prove the due and valid execution of the will and that if there were any suspicious circumstances surrounding the execution of the will the propounder must remove the said suspicions from the mind of the court by cogent and satisfactory evidence - Appeal dismissed.
