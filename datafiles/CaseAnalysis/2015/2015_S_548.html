Summary: Majority Order (Justices J. S. Khehar, M. B. Lokur, Kurian Joseph and A. K. Goel)

Constitution - Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950 - Constitution (99th Amendment) Act, 2014 - National Judicial Appointment Commission Act, 2014 - Constitutional validity of NJAC Act and 2014 Act - Selection and appointment of Judges to the higher judiciary selection and appointment of Judges to the higher judiciary - Whether NJAC Act and 2014 Act are constitutionally valid.

Held, primacy of judiciary and limited role of the Executive in appointment of judges is part of the basic structure of the Constitution. The primacy of judiciary is in initiating a proposal and finalising the same. The Chief Justice of India (CJI) has the last word in the matter. The Executive is at liberty to give suggestions prior to initiation of proposal and to give feedback on character and antecedents of the candidates proposed and object to the appointment for disclosed reasons. The art. 124A of the Constitution as introduced in the Constitution by the 2014 Act impinges on the independence of the judiciary and in the matter of appointment of judges (which is a foundational and integral part of the independence of the judiciary) and alters the basic structure of the Constitution. It is accordingly declared unconstitutional. The other provisions of the 2014 Act cannot stand by themselves and are therefore also declared unconstitutional. Similarly, the NJAC Act confers arbitrary and unchartered powers on various authorities under the statute and it violates art. 14 of the Constitution and is declared unconstitutional. Even otherwise, the NJAC Act cannot stand alone in the absence of the 2014 Act. Moreover, the 2014 Act and the NJAC Act have completely taken away the discretion of the President to turn down a recommendation for the appointment of a judge, reducing the Constitutional significance of the President. It has completely changed this constitutional position and has changed the role of the President in the appointment process as also substantially modified the mandatory consultation with the CJI and substituted or replaced the entire process by a recommendation of the NJAC. The primacy of judiciary in appointment of judges cannot be compromised and on that ground not only s. 2 of the NJAC Act Amendment dispensing with the mandatory consultation with the judiciary as contemplated under the unamended provisions, s. 3 of the NJAC Act conferring power on the NJAC (under art. 124B of the Constitution) and providing for composition of the Commission under art. 124A of Constitution giving a role to the Law Minister and 2 eminent persons equal to the CJI in recommending appointments as CJI, Judges of SC, Chief Justices and other Judges of the HCs and recommending transfer of Chief Justices and Judges of the HCs are unconstitutional but also art. 124C of Constitution giving power to the Parliament to regulate the procedure and to lay down the manner of selection was also unconstitutional, the NJAC Act has to be struck down. It goes far beyond the procedural aspects. In s. 5 (2) of the NJAC Act 'suitability criteria' is left to be worked out by regulations. Second proviso to ss. 5 (2) and 6 (6) of the NJAC Act give veto to 2 members of the Commission which is not contemplated by the Amendment. Ss. 5 (3) and 6 (8) of the NJAC Act provide for conditions for selection to be laid down by regulations which are not mere procedural matters. S. 6 of the NJAC Act authorises the recommendations for appointment as judges of the HCs without the proposal being first initiated by the Chief Justice of the HC. S. 6 (1) of the NJAC Act provides for recommendation for appointment of Chief Justice of a HC on the basis of inter se seniority of HC Judges. This may affect giving representation to as many HCs as viable as, in inter se seniority, many judges of only 1 HC may be senior most. S. 6 (2) of the NJAC Act provides for seeking nomination from Chief Justices of HCs, but s. 6 (3) of the NJAC Act empowers the Commission itself to make recommendation for appointment as Judge of the HC and seek comments from Chief Justice after short listing the candidates by itself. S. 8 of the NJAC Act enables the Central Government to appoint officers and employees of the Commission and to lay down their conditions of service. The Secretary of the Government is the Convenor of the Commission. S. 13 of the NJAC Act requires all regulations to be approved by the Parliament. These provisions in the Act impinge upon the independence of judiciary. Even if the doctrine of basic structure is not applied in judging the validity of a parliamentary statute, independence of judiciary and rule of law are parts of arts. 14, 19 and 21 of the Constitution and absence of independence of judiciary affects the said Fundamental Rights. The NJAC Act is thus liable to be struck down. The contention that even if Amendment is held to be void, the pre-existing system cannot be restored has no logic. In exercise of power of judicial review, a provision can be declared void in which case the legal position as it stands without such void provision can be held to prevail. Power of SC to declare the effect of its order cannot be doubted nor the decisions relied upon by the respondents show otherwise. The impugned 2014 Amendment and the NJAC Act are struck down as unconstitutional. Pre-existing scheme of appointment of judges stands revived. The matter is listed for consideration of the surviving issue of grievances as to working of pre-existing system. Order accordingly.

Minority order (Justice J Chelameswar)

Constitution - Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950 - Constitution (99th Amendment) Act, 2014 - National Judicial Appointment Commission Act, 2014 - Constitutional validity of NJAC Act and Constitution (99th Amendment) - Selection and appointment of Judges to the higher judiciary selection and appointment of Judges to the higher judiciary - Whether NJAC Act and 2014 Act are constitutional valid.

Held, present Collegium system lacks transparency, accountability and objectivity. The trust deficit has affected the credibility of the Collegium system. Quite often, very serious allegations and many a time not unfounded too, have been raised that its approach has been highly subjective. Deserving persons have been ignored wholly for subjective reasons, social and other national realities were overlooked, certain appointments were purposely delayed so as either to benefit vested choices or to deny such benefits to the less patronised, selection of patronised or favoured persons were made in blatant violation of the guidelines resulting in unmerited, if not, bad appointments, the dictatorial attitude of the Collegium seriously affecting the self-respect and dignity, if not independence of Judges, the court, particularly the SC, often being styled as the Court of the Collegium, the looking forward syndrome affecting impartial assessment, etc., have been some of the other allegations in the air for quite some time. These allegations certainly call for a deep introspection as to whether the institutional trusteeship has kept up the expectations of the framers of the Constitution. It is not the trusteeship that failed, but the frailties of the trustees and the collaborators which failed the system. The Collegium system needs to be improved requiring a 'glasnost' and a 'perestroika', and hence the case needs to be heard further in this regard. Order accordingly.
