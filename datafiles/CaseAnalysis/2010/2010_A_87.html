Anokh Singh and another v Punjab State Election Commission and another

Supreme Court of India

29 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. Sudershan Reddy

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 946; (2011) 11 SCC 181; AIR 2011 SC 230; JT 2010 (12) SC 1; 2011 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 675; 2010(11) SCALE 531

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Office Of Profit Under, Punjab State Election Commission Act, 1994, Punjab Land Revenue Rules, Punjab State Legislature (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1952, State Election Commission Act, Panchayati Raj Act

Summary: Municipalities & Local Governments - Election - Punjab State Legislature (Prevention of disqualifications) Act, 1952 - Office of profit - Disqualification to contest election -Circular Memo issued conveyed that Lambardars and Anganwari workers were ineligible to contest elections as Member of Panchayat because they hold 'office of profit' - Writ petitions were filed for quashing the impugned memorandum - HC held that office of Lambardars is an 'office of profit' and, therefore, the Lambardar would be disqualified from contesting the election - With regard to the Anganwari workers, the HC held that the Anganwari workers did not hold any civil post under the Government and also held that the Anganwari workers do not hold an 'office of profit' under the State Government - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether office of a Lambardar would be an 'office of profit' so as to disqualify the incumbent of such an office to seek election as Panch of the Gram Panchayat? - Held, SC in the case of Shivamurthy Swami Inamdar v. Veerabhadrappa Veerappa, 1970 INDLAW SC 574 laid down tests that may be relevant to determine as to whether a particular office can be said to be an 'office of profit' - These tests are :(1) Whether the Government makes the appointment; (2) Whether the Government has the right to remove or dismiss the holder ; (3) Whether the Government pays the remuneration; (4) What are the functions of the holder? Does he perform them for the Government; and (5) Does the Government exercise any control over the performance of those functions" - In the present case, Lambardar was not holding any post under the Government, no salary was payable to him - He receives no emoluments, perquisites or facilities - Lambardar was entitled to receive an honorarium of Rs.900/- per month - This honorarium was merely compensatory to meet the out of pocket expenses, incurred in the performance of his duties - Hence, office of a Lambardar would not be an 'office of profit' - (B) Whether the State Election Commissioner was justified in issuing the Memorandum, Memo regarding contesting of election by Lambardars and Anganwari workers? - Held, perusal of the circular showed that State Election Commission failed to take note of the factual situation - Circular was based on a misinterpretation of the law laid down by SC in the cases of Shivamurthy and Ravanna Subanna - Impugned circular quashed and set aside - Appeal allowed.
