Chairman, Railway Board and Others v Chandrima Das (Mrs) and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 January 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Saghir Ahmad, R.P. Sethi

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 600; (2000) 2 SCC 465; AIR 2000 SC 988; AIR 2000 SCW 649; 2000 (3) CalLT(SC) 44; 2000 (1) CLT 292; 2000 CRLJ 1473; JT 2000 (1) SC 426; 2000 (1) KLT 655; 2000 (2) MLJ(SC) 26; 2000 (1) RCR(Criminal) 803; 2000(1) SCALE 279; 2000 (3) SCJ 1; [2000] 1 S.C.R. 480; 2000 (1) SLT 508; 2000 (1) Supreme 265

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Foreigners

Keywords: National, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949

Summary: Constitution - Foreigners - Compensation - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 14, 12, 32, 16, 19, 20 and 21 - Universal Declaration of Human Rights, 1948, arts. 3, 7 and 9 - Bangladeshi national was gang-raped by many including employees of Railways in a room at Yatri Niwas at Howrah Station - Respondent, a practising advocate of Calcutta High Court, filed a petition u/art. 226 of Constitution of India, 1950, against Chairman, Railway Board and others claiming compensation for victim - HC awarded compensation - (A) Whether victim should have approached civil court for damages and matter should not have been considered in a petition u/art. 226 of Constitution? - With expanding horizon of art. 14 r/w other Articles of Constitution, 1950, dealing with fundamental rights, every executive action of Govt. or other public bodies, including instrumentalities of Govt., or those which can be legally treated as 'authority' within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution, 1950 - Held, contention that victim should have approached civil court for damages and matter should not have been considered in a petition u/art. 226 cannot be accepted - It is not a mere matter of violation of an ordinary right of a person but violation of fundamental rights which is involved - (B) Whether respondent has locus standi to approach Court? - True nature of petition was that of a petition filed in public interest - Held, having regard to nature of petition filed by respondent and relief claimed therein, it cannot be doubted that this petition was filed in public interest which could legally be filed by respondent and argument that she could not file that petition as there was nothing personal to her involved in that petition rejected - (C) Whether, since victim was a foreign national, no relief under public law could be granted to her as there was no violation of fundamental rights available under Constitution, 1950? - Constitution, 1950, guarantees all basic and fundamental human rights set out in Universal Declaration of Human Rights, 1948, to its citizens and other persons - Fundamental rights are available to all 'citizens' of country but a few of them are also available to 'persons' - Since 'LIFE' is also recognised as a basic human right in Universal Declaration it has to have same meaning and interpretation as relating to art. 21 of Constitution, 1950 - Held, victim, who was not citizen of this country but came here as a citizen of Bangladesh was, nevertheless, entitled to all constitutional rights available to a citizen so far as 'right to life' was concerned - Right available to her u/art. 21 of Constitution, 1950, was thus violated - Consequently, State was under a constitutional liability to pay compensation to her - (D) Whether Central Govt. cannot be held vicariously liable for offence, of rape committed by employees of Railways? - Running of Railways is a commercial activity - Establishing Yatri Niwas at various railway stations to provide lodging and boarding facilities to passengers on payment of charges is a part of commercial activity of UOI and this activity cannot be equated with exercise of sovereign power - Held, if any employees commits an act of tort, Union Govt., of which they are employees, can, be held vicariously liable in damages to person wronged by those employees - Appeal dismissed.
