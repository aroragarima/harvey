Delhi Special Police Establishment, New Delhi v Lt. Col. S. K. Loraiya

Supreme Court of India

24 August 1972

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Dwivedi, D.G. Palekar, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1972 Indlaw SC 182; (1972) 2 SCC 692; (1973) SCC (Cr) 1101; AIR 1972 SC 2548; 1973 CRLJ 33; [1973] 1 S.C.R. 1010; 1973 UJ 350

Case Digest

Subject: Defence & Security Forces; Practice & Procedure; Service

Keywords: Court Martial

Summary: Defense & Security Forces - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Army Act, 1950, s. 122 (3) - Constitution of India, 1950 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 467, 471, 120B - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Illegal Gratification - Framing of charges - Quashing of - Respondent was in Army Service and was charged u/ss. 120B, 467, 471 of IPC r/w s. 5(1)(e)(d), 5(2) of 1988 Act - Trial Court framed charges against respondent - Aggrieved respondent filed revision against framing of charges before HC - HC allowed the revision and quashed charges - Hence instant appeal by appellant/Delhi Special Police Establishment - Whether HC was right in passing impugned order - Held, regard trial of offences committed by, army men, Army Act draws threefold scheme - Certain offences enumerated in Army Act were exclusively triable by Court- martial, certain other offences were exclusively triable by ordinary Trial Courts, and certain other offences were triable both by ordinary Trial Courts and court-martial - In respect of last category both courts have concurrent jurisdiction, s. 549(1) CrPC was designed to avoid conflict of jurisdiction in respect of last category of offences - Clause 'for which he was liable to be tried either by court to which this Code applied was qualified the preceding clause 'when any person was charged with an offence' in s. 549(1) - Accordingly phrase 'was liable to be tried either by court to which this Code applies or a court-martial' imports that offence for which accused was to be tried should be an offence of which cognizance could be taken by an ordinary Trial court as well as court-martial. In our opinion, the phrase is intended to refer to the initial jurisdiction of. the two courts to take cognizance of the case and not to their jurisdiction to decide it on merits. It is admitted that both the ordinary Trial Court and Court-martial have concurrent jurisdiction with respect to offences for which respondent had been charged - S. 549 of CrPC and rules made thereunder were attracted to case at hand - S. 122 (3) of 1950 Act provides that while computing period of three years specified in sub-section any time spent by accused as prisoner of war or in enemy territory, or in evading arrest after commission of offence shall be excluded - Word 'jurisdiction' in s. 125 really signifies initial jurisdiction to take cognizance of case - It referred to stage at which proceedings were instituted in court and not to jurisdiction of ordinary Trial Court and court-martial to decide case on merits - It appeared that s. 549(1) of CrPC should be construed in light of s. 126 of 1950 Act - HC had rightly held that as charges were framed without following procedure specified in rules framed u/s. 549 (1) CrPC they could not stand - Appeal dismissed.
