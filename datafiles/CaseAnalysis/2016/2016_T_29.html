Tilak Raj v State of Himachal Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

06 January 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 8; (2016) 4 SCC 140; AIR 2016 SC 406; 2016 CRLJ 1136; 2016 (1) JCC 518; JT 2016 (1) SC 141; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 802; 2016 (2) RLW 1589; 2016(1) SCALE 157

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Cheating, Dishonest, Reputation, Probation of Offenders Act, 1958

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 376, 415, 417, 503, 506 - Probation of Offenders Act, 1958, s. 4 - Alleged Rape - Conviction - Justification - Complaint was received from prosecutrix alleging that she was raped on pretext of marriage and physically assaulted by appellant - FIR was lodged against appellant u/ss. 376, 417, 506 of IPC - Trial court vide judgment acquitted appellant accused of all charges levelled against him by giving him benefit of doubt - HC upheld acquittal order passed by Trial court in favour of appellant for offence punishable u/s. 376 of IPC - However, HC convicted appellant for offences punishable u/ss. 417 and 506 part I of IPC but instead of imposing sentence on appellant for aforesaid offences, vide order dt. 17-3-2015, HC has released him u/s. 4 of Act on his entering into personal bond - Whether impugned order passed by HC against appellant accused is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, prosecutrix was adult and mature lady of around 40 years at time of incident. Further, it is admitted by prosecutrix in her testimony before Trial Court that she was in relationship with appellant for last two years prior to incident and appellant used to stay overnight at her residence. SC held that after perusal of copy of FIR and evidence on record case set up by prosecutrix seems to be highly unrealistic and unbelievable. Further, SC held that evidence as whole including FIR, testimony of prosecutrix and MLC report prepared by medical practitioner clearly indicate that story of prosecutrix regarding sexual intercourse on false pretext of marrying her is concocted and not believable. Further, SC held that evidence on record clearly shows that there is no evidence against appellant from which it can be conclusively inferred that there was any fraudulent or dishonest inducement of prosecutrix by appellant to constitute offence u/s. 415 of IPC. SC held that for conviction of appellant for cheating, it is important that all necessary ingredients constituting offence must be proved beyond reasonable doubt. Hence, appellant cannot be convicted for offence of cheating punishable u/s. 417 of IPC as prosecution has failed to prove all ingredients of said offence beyond reasonable doubt. Further, SC held that there is insufficiency of evidence to hold conviction of appellant for offence of criminal intimidation punishable u/s. 506 part I of IPC. SC held that impugned judgment and order passed by HC is not based on careful re-appraisal of evidence on record by HC and there is no material evidence on record to show that appellant is guilty of charged offences i.e., offence of cheating punishable u/s. 417 of IPC and offence of criminal intimidation punishable u/s. 506 part I of IPC. Impugned order passed by HC is set aside and appellant is acquitted of all charges levelled against him.

Appeal allowed.

Ratio - In order to convict accused for criminal offence, it is necessary that charges against appellant is proved beyond reasonable doubt.
