Bimal Kumar & Another v Shakuntala Debi & Others

Supreme Court of India

27 February 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 498; (2012) 3 SCC 548; AIR 2012 SC 1586; 2012 (91) ALR 888; 2012 (3) AWC 2661; 2012 (2) CTC 881; JT 2012 (3) SC 254; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 805; 2012 (2) RCR(Civil) 539; 2012 (116) RD 176; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 195

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Family & Personal; Land & Property - Partition of property - Compromise petition - Execution petition - Challenged - A filed partition suit against his father B and brother C - Suit was compromised leaving aside K, father of present appellants - Trial Court accepted the petition of compromise and passed compromise decree treating K as ex parte - Legal representative of K/present appellants initiated fresh petition on ground that earlier decree was obtained by fraud - In said suit, they claimed 1/11th share of property for themselves which was involved in earlier suit and said suit was dismissed - K preferred appeal which was dismissed for want of prosecution - Thereafter, respondents herein filed execution seeking execution of the decree - K was died and the execution was levied against legal heirs, appellants herein - Appellants raised objection that execution proceedings was barred by limitation - District Judge dismissed execution proceedings on ground that it was absolutely barred by limitation - Aggrieved, respondents preferred revision before Single Judge, which was allowed on ground that execution case preferred by respondents was not barred by limitation - Hence, instant appeal - Whether Single Judge was justified in allowing the revision preferred by respondents on ground that execution case was not barred by limitation - Held, it was clear that parties entered into compromise and clearly admitted that they were in separate and exclusive possession of properties and same had already been allotted to them - It was also admitted that they were in possession of their respective shares and, therefore, no final decree or execution was required to be filed - It was demonstrable that compromise application did not contain any clause regarding future course of action - Parties were absolutely conscious and rightly so, that their rights were fructified and their possession was exclusively determined - In instant case, compromise decree had the status of a final decree - However, latter suit filed by appellants was for partition and declaring ex parte compromise decree as null and void - There was no stay of earlier judgment or any proceedings emanating therefrom - In the absence of any interdiction from any Court, decree-holder was entitled to execute decree - Therefore, irresistible conclusion was that initiation of execution proceedings was indubitably barred by limitation - Thus analyzed, reasons ascribed by single Judge were absolutely unsustainable and order of Single Judge was set aside - Appeal allowed.
