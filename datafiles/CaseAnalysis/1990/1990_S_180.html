 Direct Recruit Class Ii Engineering Officers’Association And v State Of Maharashtra And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

02 May 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

L.M. Sharma, K. Ramaswamy, P.B. Sawant, S. Ratnavel Pandian, S. Mukharji

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 692; (1990) SCC (L&S) 339; (1990) 2 SCC 715; AIR 1990 SC 1607; 1990 (16) ALR 623; 1990 (13) ATC 348; 1990 (60) FLR 918; JT 1990 (2) SC 264; 1990 LabIC 1304; 1995 (3) LLJ 144; 1990(1) SCALE 839; [1990] 2 S.C.R. 900; 1990 (2) SLJ 40; 1990 (2) SLR 769; 1990 (2) UJ 314; 1990 (2) UPLBEC 833

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Bombay Reorganisation Act, 1960, Bombay Service of Engineers (Class I and Class H) Recruitment Rules 1960, Bombay Service of Engineers (Class I and Class II) Recruitment Rules 1970, Maharashtra Service of Engineers (Regulation of Seniority and Preparation and Revision of Seniority Lists for specified Period) Rules 1982, Maharashtra Service of Engineers Class I and the Ms of E Class II Regulation of seniority and Preparation and Revision of Seniority Lists Rules 1983, Reorganised Bombay State Assistant Engineers and Executive Engineers seniority Lists Rules 1981, Reorganised Bombay State Overseas and Deputy Engineers Seniority Lists rules 1978

Summary: (A) Service - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 16, 32, 136, 163, 226, 309 and 311 - Maharashtra Service of Engineers (Regulation of Seniority and Preparation and Revision of Seniority Lists for Specified Period) Rules, 1982, rr. 4 and 9 - Maharashtra Service of Engineers Class II (Regulation of Seniority and Preparation and Revision of Seniority Lists) Rules, 1983 - Reorganised Bombay State Assistant Engineers and Executive Engineers Senionity Lists Rules, 1981, r. 3(3) - (A) Seniority in service between direct recruits and promotes - Held, once an incumbent was appointed to a post according to rule, his seniority had to be counted from the date of his appointment and not according to the date of his confirmation - However, where the initial appointment was only ad hoc and not according to rules and made as a stop-gap arrangement, the officiation in such post could not be taken into account for considering the seniority - (B) Appointment not made by following procedure - Held, if the initial appointment was not made by following the procedure laid down by the rules but the appointee continues in the post uninterruptedly till the regularisation of his service in accordance with the rules, the period of officiating service would be counted - (C) Appointments made from more than one source - Held, when appointments were made from more than one source, it was permissible to fix the ratio for recruitment from the different sources, and if rules were framed in this regard they must be followed strictly - (D) Appointments made in violation of quota rule - Held, if it becomes impossible to adhere to the existing quota rule, it should be substituted by an appropriate rule to meet the needs of the situation - In case, however, the quota rule was not followed continuously for a number of years because it was impossible to do so the inference was irresistible that the quota rule had broken down - Further, where the quota rule had broken down and the appointments were made from one source in excess of the quota, but were made after following the procedure prescribed by the rules for the appointment, the appointees should not be pushed down below the appointees from the other source inducted in the service at a later date - (E) Relaxation from Rules - Held, where the rules permit the authorities to relax the provisions relating to the quota, a presumption should be raised that there was such relaxation when there was a deviation from the quota rule - (G) Whether quota for recruitment from the different sources may be prescribed? - Held, quota for recruitment from the different sources may be prescribed by executive instructions, if the rules were silent on the subject - However, if the quota rule was prescribed by an executive instruction, and was not followed continuously for a number of years, the inference was that the executive instruction had ceased to remain operative - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Civil Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 11 - Principles of res judicata - Held, a dispute raised by an application u/art. 32 of the Constitution must be held to be barred by principles of res judicata including the rule of constructive res judicata if the same had been earlier decided by a competent court by a judgment which became final - Appeals dismissed.
