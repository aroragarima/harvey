L.I.C. of India And Another v Ram Pal Singh Bisen

Supreme Court of India

16 March 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Verma, B. SUDARSHAN REDDY

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 181; (2010) 4 SCC 491; AIR 2010 SC (Supp) 753; 2010(2) ALL MR 970; 2010 (3) AWC 2287; 2010 (3) ESC 347; 2010 (125) FLR 325; 2010 (2) JhrCR(SC) 193; JT 2010 (3) SC 54; 2010 (3) LLJ 57; 2010 (3) MLJ(SC) 1370; 2010 (2) RCR(Civil) 459; 2010(3) SCALE 121; [2010] 3 S.C.R. 438; 2010 (2) SLR 792; 2010 (2) UPLBEC 886

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Service

Keywords: Notice Of Admission

Summary: Service - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 12 r. 2 - Life Insurance Corporation of India (Staff) Regulations, 1960 - Life Insurance Corporation Act, 1956 - Proof of documents - Respondent who worked with appellant was placed under suspension and charge sheet was served on him - Appellants by non-speaking order, dismissed respondent from service - Departmental appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - Respondent filed suit before Trial Court - However, appellants did not lead any oral evidence, yet some of the documents filed by appellants were exhibited, probably under misconception of law that they were not disputed in Court by respondent - Appellants did not serv any notice of admission or denial of documents on the respondent during trial as contemplated under O. 12 r. 2 of the CPC - Trial Court passed a decree in favour of the respondent - Appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - Hence, present appeal - In absence of any oral evidence having been tendered by the appellants, and especially in absence of putting their own defence to the respondent during his cross examination in the Court, what is the effect of documents filed by appellants and marked as Exhibits? - Held, mere admission of document in evidence does not amount to its proof - Under the Law of Evidence also, it is necessary that contents of documents are required to be proved either by primary or by secondary evidence - Documents having not been produced and marked as required under the Evidence Act cannot be relied upon by the Court - It was the duty of the appellants to have proved documents in accordance with law - That documentary evidence was also required to be proved by the appellants in accordance with the provisions of the Evidence Act, which they have failed to do - Thus, no advantage thereof could be accrued to the appellants, even if it is assumed that said documents have been admitted by respondent and were then exhibited and marked - Appellants failed to prove and establish their defence in the case - Courts committed no error in coming to the conclusion that respondent was denied opportunity of hearing, that being so, whole proceedings stand vitiated by non-adherence to the principles of natural justice - Appeal dismissed.
