Prabodh Verma and Others v State Of Uttar Pradesh And Others. Etc.

Supreme Court of India

27 July 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

D.P. Madon, V. Balakrishna Eradi, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 175; (1984) 4 SCC 251; (1984) SCC (L&S) 704; AIR 1985 SC 167; 1984 ALJ 931; 1985 LabIC 1196; 1985 (2) LLN 585; 1984(2) SCALE 87; [1985] 1 S.C.R. 216; 1985 (2) SLR 714

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Uttar Pradesh General Clauses Act, 1904, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Uttar Pradesh Schools and Intermediate College (Reserve Pool Teachers) (Second) Ordinance 1978 (22 of 1978)

Summary: Constitution - U.P. General Clauses Act, 1904, ss.6 and 30 - Uttar Pradesh High Schools and Intermediate Colleges (Reserve Pool Teachers) Ordinance, 1978 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 16 - Abolition of ordinance - Followed by termination of service of school appellantss - Question which arose for determination was with the Constitutional validity of two Uttar Pradesh Ordinances - (1) First Ordinance, 1978 - (2) Second Ordinace, 1978 which had been struck down by DB on the ground that its provisions were violative of Arts. 14 and 16(1) of the Constitution of India - Subsidiary questions being whether the termination of the services of appellants and petitioners as secondary school teachers and intermediate college lecturers was valid and if not, the reliefs to which they were entitled u/s. 30 Act would apply to Ordinances promulgated by respondent u/art. 213 of the Constitution - Held, s. 6 of Act dealt with the effect of repeal of an enactment and it provided that where any Act repealed any enactment, then, unless a different intention appeared, the repeal should not inter alia affect any right acquired under the enactment so repealed - On the basis of these sections it was submitted that the effect of the Governor withdrawing an Ordinance u/art. 213(2)(b) of the Constitution and the effect of Ordinance ceasing to operate u/art. 213(2)(a) of the Constitution were the same as the effect of the repeal of an Act and s. 6 of Act, therefore, applied in both these cases - Record was not clear whether Second Ordinance was in fact withdrawn by the Governor u/art. 213 (2) (b) of the Constitution nor had any notification to that effect been brought to our notice - Undoubtedly, appellants in the reserve pool had a right to be appointed to a substantive vacancy occurring in the post of a appellants in a recognized institution which was to be filled by direct recruitment - Explanation to s. 4 of both the Ordinances was not relevant for this purpose for all that was provided by it was that no appellants in the reserve pool was entitled to claim an appointment to a post which he had joined during the period of the strike or to any post carrying the same or a higher grade - What this Explanation meant was that no reserve pool appellants could claim that he should be appointed to the identical post which he had held during the period of the strike or to such post either in the same recognized institution or in any other recognized institution whether it carried the same grade or a higher grade - What was required to be noted was that the right which these appellantss had under first Ordinance, 1978 continued under Second Ordinance, 1978 because that Ordinance came into force with retrospective effect from 24-6-1978, that was, the date on which first Ordinance, 1978 was promulgated and by s. 8 of Second Ordinance, 1978 which repealed first Ordinance, 1978 it was expressly provided that anything done or any action taken under first ordinance, 1978 should be deemed to have been done or taken under Second Ordinance, 1978 as if Second Ordinance, 1978 were in force at all material times - Register of reserve pool appellants maintained under First Ordinance, 1978 should, therefore, be deemed to be a register of reserve pool appellants to be maintained under Second Ordinance, 1978 - These vacancies were required to be filled by direct recruitment - But for the orders of HC, all reserve pool appellants would therefore, have been appointed in accordance with the provisions of either first Ordinance, 1978 or Second Ordinance, 1978 - Where a court had passed an interim order which had resulted in an injustice, it was bound at the time of the passing of the final order, if it took a different view at that time, to undo that injustice as far as it lies within its power - Similarly, where an injustice was done by the final order of a court, the superior court, if it took a different view, should, as far as lied within its power, seek to undo that injustice - Great prejudice was suffered and injustice done to those reserve pool appellants who had not been appointed to substantive vacancies which had occurred in the posts requiring to be filled by direct recruitment - There were, however, certain difficulties in directing these appellants to be appointed from the dates on which they would have been respectively appointed but for the orders of the HC because those vacancies had been filled and in all likelihood those so appointed had been confirmed in their posts and ought not to be now thrown out therefrom for no fault of theirs - In view of the fact SC felt that it would be in consonance with justice and equity and fair to all parties concerned if the remaining appellants in the reserve pool are appointed in accordance with the provisions of Second Ordinance, 1978 to substantive vacancies in the posts of appellants in recognized institutions which were required to be filled by direct recruitment as and when each such vacancy occurred - It would apply equally to those reserve pool appellants whose services were terminated and who had not filed any writ petition or who had filed a writ petition but had not succeeded in obtaining a stay order as also to those reserve pool appellants who had hot been appointed in view of the interim orders passed by the HC - Appeals allowed.
