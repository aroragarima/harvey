Dhal Singh Dewangan v State of Chhattisgarh

Supreme Court of India

23 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Uday Umesh Lalit, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 811; AIR 2016 SC 4745; 2017 CRLJ 1143; 2016 (4) JCC 2732; JT 2016 (9) SC 336; 2016 (4) RCR(Criminal) 467; 2016(9) SCALE 142; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 36

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Circumstantial Evidence, Wrist, FIR, Marriage, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Appreciation, Version, Blood, Chain, Cause Of Death, Dead Body, Death Sentence, Diary, Innocence, Admissible, Knife, Clothes, False Plea, Distance, Skull, Villagers, General Diary, Hearsay, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Plea

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 313, 366 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 106, 32, 6 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant/accused allegedly attacked deceased (wife and 5 daughters) with iron knife causing their death - Charge-sheet was filed against appellant for offence punishable u/s. 302 IPC - Trial Court found accused guilty of offence punishable u/s. 302 IPC and sentence of death was awarded - On appeal, HC affirmed impugned order of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether statements of PWs 1, 2, 3 and 5 were admissible u/s. 6 of the Act and could be relied on.

Held, SC do not find the statements attributed to PW6 by PWs 3 and 5 to be satisfying the essential requirements. The house of appellant, according to the record, was at a distance of 100 yards from where those witnesses were stated to have found PW6 crying aloud. Both in terms of distance and time, the elements of spontaneity and continuity were lost. PW6 had disowned and denied having made such disclosure. However, even assuming that she did make such disclosure, the spontaneity and continuity was lost and the statements cannot be said to have been made so shortly after the incident as to form part of transaction. Therefore, SC reject the evidence sought to be placed on that behalf through PWs 3 and 5. Even if SC were to accept the version of PWs 1 and 2, the same would also suffer on that count and would have to be rejected. Prosecution should stand or fall on its own legs and it cannot derive any strength from the weakness of defence. In other words, before using the additional link it should be proved that all the links in the chain were complete and do not suffer from any infirmity. HC seems to have misconstrued that decision and used the so-called false defence put up by appellant as one of the additional circumstances connected with the chain. Prosecution was unable to prove any of the essential principles laid down in Hanumant v. State Of Madhya Pradesh, 1952 Indlaw SC 89, HC cannot supply the weakness or lacuna by taking aid of or recourse to a false defence or a false plea. Appeals allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 313, 366 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 106, 32, 6 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Circumstantial evidence - Acquittal - Validity - Whether circumstances on record satisfy the principles laid down by SC in its various judgments as regards appreciation of cases based on circumstantial evidence.

Held, appellant was lying unconscious at the scene of occurrence was accepted by all the prosecution witnesses including IO, who sent appellant to Primary Health Centre for medical attention. Since he was sent by IO himself, prosecution ought to have placed on record material indicating what made him unconscious, what was the probable period of such unconsciousness and whether appellant was falsely projecting it and nothing was placed on record. Neither any doctor who had examined him was called as witness, nor any case papers of such examination were made available. However, if appellant was found lying in the room in an unconscious state with five dead bodies around, possibility that his clothes had otherwise got stained with blood which was spotted everywhere including verandah cannot be ruled out. In SC view that circumstance was not conclusive in nature and tendency which could be considered against appellant. Prosecution did not gather finger prints either in the house or even on iron knife which was allegedly used for committing the offence in question. If finger prints on the knife were to be that of appellant alone, such factor could certainly have weighed against appellant. However, absence of such conclusive material coupled with other circumstances on record do suggest reasonable possibility of the hypothesis of innocence of accused. Thus, it do not form a complete chain of evidence as not to leave any reasonable ground for conclusion consistent with the innocence of appellant nor do the circumstances exclude every possible hypothesis except guilt of accused. Thus, prosecution on the basis of admissible evidence on record has not proved its case against appellant. Impugned judgments of Courts below were set aside. Appeals allowed.
