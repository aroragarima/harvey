Â Sasanka Sekhar Maity & Ors. Etc. v Union Of India & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Sen, Y.V. Chandrachud, P.N. Bhagwati, V.R. Krishna Iyer, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 187; (1980) 4 SCC 716; AIR 1981 SC 522; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1209

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Imposition of ceiling on agricultural holdings, Introduction of chap. IIB of the 1955 Act

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - West Bengal Land Reforms Act, 1955, Chap. IIB, ss. 14M(1), 14M(5) and 140(3) - West Bengal Land Reforms (Amendment) Act, 1971 - West Bengal Land Reforms (Amendment) Act, 1972 - Imposition of ceiling on agricultural holdings - Introduction of chap. IIB of the 1955 Act - Validity - Appellants filed instant writ petition challenging the provisions of chap. IIB of the 1955 Act inserted by the 1971 Act and replaced by the 1972 Act which provided for a fixation of ceiling on agricultural holdings - Whether the provisions of chap. IIB should be struck down on ground that it permits the taking away of homestead of a raiyat without payment of compensation - Held, respondent State had no intention to oust any raiyat from his homestead, or not to pay any compensation under existing provisions for any homestead which was vested in State under provisions of the 1955 Act - A raiyat was within his rights to retain land upto ceiling limit applicable to him in accordance with ss. 14M and 14T of the 1955 Act - Thus, a raiyat was at liberty to retain his homestead and not to allow it to be vested in or acquired by State under the 1955 Act - It was expected that normally raiyats should retain their homesteads and, question of ousting them from their homesteads did not arose at all - Power of eminent domain which was inherent in every sovereign State, should be capable of being exercised against every property held by any person in State - Being a fundamental attribute of sovereignty of State one could not imagine that framers of the Constitution intended to divest State of that attribute by implication in case of property owned by a private trust - Just as property of a private trust was held subject to a law imposing a tax upon it, so also was that property subject to eminent domain of State - However, s. 14M(5) of the 1955 Act provided that land owned by a trust or endowment other than of a public nature, should be deemed to be land owned by beneficiary of trust or endowment, and each such beneficiary should be deemed to be a raiyat under the 1955 Act to extent of share of his beneficial interest in said trust or endowment - If a raiyat was a beneficiary of a private trust, his beneficial interest consists in offerings or income - Provision in effect prescribed that land should be clubbed for computation of ceiling area u/s. 14M(1) of the Act - Imposition of such a ceiling should no doubt reduce holding of trust, but Govt. had power u/s. 140(3) of the 1955 Act to increase ceiling area in certain cases - Legislature had provided adequate safeguards u/s. 140(3) of the 1955 Act to soften rigour of the Act in relation to religious and charitable trusts - Petitioner failed in challenging the validity of chap. II B of the 1955 Act, introduced by the 1971 Act - Appeal dismissed.
