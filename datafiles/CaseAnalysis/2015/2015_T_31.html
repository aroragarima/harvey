N. M. Krishnakumari and others v Thalakkal Assiya and othes

Supreme Court of India

17 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, R. Banumathi

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 102; (2015) 8 SCC 257; 2015 (127) RD 434; 2015(2) SCALE 457

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Tenancy Rights, Kerala Land Reforms Act, 1963

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Kerala Land Reforms Act, 1963, ss. 72MM(1), 74, 75, 103 - Tenancy Rights - Legality - Predecessor of respondent (original applicant/PW1) along with another filed an application u/s. 72MM(1) of the Act before Land Tribunal, for purchase of Jenm rights in respect of land in dispute - Predecessor of appellant was impleaded as respondent no. 2 in proceedings before Tribunal and disputed tenancy of predecessor of respondent and that he has already obtained an order for the purchase of the Jenm right in respect of land in question as per order in S.M.P.no. 1474 of 1976 - Aggrieved predecessor of respondent filed application u/s. 72MM(7) of the Act, seeking to set aside the order passed in S.M.P.no. 1474 of 1976, by Tribunal, which was allowed holding that he was cultivating tenant of property - Aggrieved LRs appellants filed an appeal before Appellate Authority, which was allowed - Aggrieved respondents filed a revision petitions before HC, which was allowed holding that reasons stated by Appellate Authority in its judgment for reversing a well considered order passed by Tribunal was unsustainable in law and that transaction put forward by predecessor of appellants was hit by s. 74 of the Act - Hence, instant appeals - Whether divergent findings recorded by HC against appellants were legal and valid and whether HC has exceeded in its jurisdiction u/s. 103 of the Act.

Held, Tribunal has come to the right conclusion on the basis of the facts pleaded and the evidence adduced by both the parties and held that the respondent's predecessor, deceased PW1 is the cultivating tenant in respect of the land in question after proper appreciation of the evidence on hand and therefore, it has recorded that the finding on the order which was obtained by the predecessor of appellant in S.M.P. no. 1474 of 1976 is an act of fraud and foul play and thus, the Tribunal has rightly set aside the same, which finding has been concurred by the HC stating that the reversal of the finding of fact by the Appellate Authority are not only erroneous but also error in law. Appellants would be ineligible under the provisions of the Act to get the cultivating rights upon the land in question in view of the fact that their predecessor had allegedly got the possession of the schedule property only after 1-4-1964, after the Act came into force and thus, he could not have claimed the right of cultivating tenant as provided under the provisions of s. 74 of the Act. Thus, the contention of appellants that the property was leased out to the predecessors of the appellants as per the Marupat deed dt.30-10-1964 is not maintainable in law. Further, the predecessor of appellant was also ineligible for the purchase of Jenm right from the Tribunal under the provisions of the Act. Being a person who allegedly came in possession of the property subsequent to 1-4-1964, he cannot claim tenancy rights. Thus, the Tribunal as well as the HC have come to the right conclusion based on the fact and evidence on record in holding that the respondents have proved their tenancy right. The HC has rightly reversed the decision of the Appellate Authority after careful examination of the divergent findings of fact recorded by it as the same are contrary to both the documentary and oral evidence on record, particularly Ext.B1. Thus, in the light of all the material evidence on record and the statutory provisions u/ss. 74 and 75 of the Act, the relevant and glaring error on fact and in law committed by the Appellate Authority has been rightly interfered with by the HC, after it had satisfied itself that the divergent findings of the Appellate Authority are not only erroneous but also error in law and it has exercised its Revisional jurisdiction and set aside the divergent findings of the Appellate Authority. The power exercised by the HC u/s. 103 of the Act has been rightly exercised by it in setting aside the judgment and order of Appellate Authority, as the same is not only erroneous but also error in law. Appeals dismissed.
