Manohar Lal Chopra v Rai Bahadur Rao Raja Seth Hiralal

Supreme Court of India

16 November 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Raghubar Dayal, J.C. Shah, K.C. Das Gupta, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 266; AIR 1962 SC 527; 1963 ALJ 169; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 450; [1962] Supp1 S.C.R. 450

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Civil Procedure - Civil Procedure Code, 1908, ss. 94, 10, 151 and O. 39, r.1 - (A) Whether court could issue temporary injunction u/s 94 of CPC? - Held, provisions of Code are not exhaustive - As legislature is incapable of contemplating all possible circumstances which may arise in future litigation - So do not provide procedure for them - S. 94 prescribe circumstances in which temporary injunction can be issued - Court may use its inherent powers merely to see whether circumstances of case bring it within prescribed rule - Even in absence of s. 94 court can issue temporary injunctions, but it could do that in exercise of its inherent jurisdiction - (B) What is objective behind s. 151 of CPC, 1908? - Further held, inherent power of court cannot be limited by any provision of code - For ends of justice - So, not possible to hold that provisions of Code control inherent power by limiting it or otherwise affecting it - Inherent power has not been conferred upon court; it is a power inherent in court by virtue of its duty to do justice between parties before it - (C) Whether civil courts have inherent jurisdiction in cases not covered by rr. 1 and 2 of O. 39 of CPC, 1908 to issue temporary injunctions restraining parties to proceedings before them from doing certain acts? - Further held, it does not have - A temporary injunction may be issued only in those cases which come strictly within those rules, and normally civil courts have no power to issue injunctions by transgressing limits prescribed by rules - (D) Whether s. 10 of CPC, 1908 is applicable in cases where previously instituted suit is vexatious? - Further held, provisions u/s. 10 is clear, definite and mandatory - A court in which a subsequent suit has been filed is prohibited from proceeding with trial of that suit in certain specified circumstances - When there is a special provision in Code for dealing with contingencies of two such suits being instituted, recourse to inherent powers u/s 151 is not justified - Provisions of s. 10 do not become inapplicable on a court holding that previously instituted suit is a vexatious suit or has been instituted in violation of terms of contract - Mere fact that that Court is situate at a long distance from place of residence of defendant is not sufficient to establish that suit has been filed in that court in order to put defendant to trouble and harassment and to unnecessary expense - Appeal allowed.
