Tata Chemicals Limited v Commissioner of Customs (Preventive) Jamnagar

Supreme Court of India

14 May 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 366; 2016 (1) G.L.R. 547; JT 2015 (5) SC 554; 2015 (5) MLJ 378; 2015 (4) RCR(Civil) 88; 2015(6) SCALE 419; 2016(6) SCALE 419; [2015] 7 S.C.R. 132

Case Digest

Subject: Customs

Keywords: Give, Samples

Summary: Customs - Customs Act, 1962, s. 18(b) - Exemption of duty - Failure to grant benefit - Justification - Appellants were engaged in manufacture of soda ash and Coke - Notification was exempted coking coal having ash content below 12% from basic customs duty that was in excess of 5% - Further, in addition, other notifications exempted coking coal with ash content of less than 12% from whole of auxiliary duty and additional duty of customs - Appellant imported coking coal and inspection was conducted by independent agency for which report stated that ash content was within prescribed limit - However, sample was collected by department and it was held that ash content was not within prescribed limit, hence appellant was not entitled to exemption and vide order amount was demanded from appellant - However, appellant authority set aside order of lower authority - Further, vide impugned order, Tribunal vide impugned held allowed appeal of Revenue on ground that even though samples drawn by Inspector were contrary to law, yet since representative of appellants was present, appellants are estopped from turning around at later stage inasmuch as they did not immediately object to drawing of samples contrary to law - Whether appellant was entitled to exemption provided under notification.

Held, expression 'deems it necessary' means that proper officer must have good reason to subject imported goods to chemical or other tests. With respect to present case, SC held that it is clear that where importer has furnished all necessary documents to support fact that ash content in coking coal imported is less than 12%, proper officer must, when questioned, state that, at very least, documents produced do not inspire confidence for some good prima facie reason. Further, revenue has never stated that authority's certificate of quality ought to be rejected or is defective in any manner. Hence, it is clear that entire chemical analysis of imported goods done by Department was ultra vires s. 18(b) of Act. Further, samples drawn by Inspector have been drawn contrary to express provisions. SC held that reasoning given by Tribunal is perverse finding both on fact and law. Further, it is proved that no representative of appellant was present at time Customs Inspector took samples. Hence, order passed by Tribunal is set aside. Appeals allowed.

Ratio - Denial to grant exemption of duty is not justified when product in question confirms standards mentioned by authority.
