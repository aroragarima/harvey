Â S. R. Bommai and Others v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 March 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Ratnavel Pandian, A.M. Ahmadi, P.B. Sawant, K. Ramaswamy, S.C. Agrawal, Yogeshwar Dayal, B.P. Jeevan Reddy, Kuldip Singh

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 2206; (1994) 3 SCC 1; AIR 1994 SC 1918; AIR 1994 SCW 2946; JT 1994 (2) SC 214; [1994] 2 S.C.R. 644

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Fundamental Duties, Finance Commission, Constitutional Safeguards, Employment Under The State, Emergency Provisions

Summary: Ahmadi, Verma, Sawant, Kuldip Singh, Ramaswamy, JJ

(A) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356 - Power of the President to invoke emergency provision - Scope - Held, art. 356 of the Constitution confers a power upon the President to be exercised only where he is satisfied that a situation has arisen where the government of a State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution - Power, in fact, vested with the Union Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at its head and 'satisfaction of the President' contemplated by the art. 356 is subjective in nature - Power of the President u/art. 356 is not absolute but conditional - Existence of material for invoking art. 356 which may comprise of or include the report(s) of the Governor, is a pre-condition - Satisfaction must be formed on relevant material and serious consideration at the hands of all concerned is required - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 74 - Invocation of emergency powers by the President - Advice of the Cabinet - Consideration of - Whether the provision of art. 74(2) of the Constitution permits withholding of the reasons and material forming the basis for the ministerial advice tendered to the President -Held, President should act in accordance with the advice tendered by the Council of Ministers - Proviso, however, entitles the President to require the Council of Ministers to reconsider its advice if he has any doubts or reservation but once the Council of Ministers has reconsidered the advice, he is obliged to act in accordance therewith - Art.74(1) re-enforces that on the advice tendered by the Council of Ministers to the President, the latter actively applies his mind and reaches the satisfaction that a situation has arisen in which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution - Art. 74(2) merely bars the court an enquiry into the question whether any, and if so, what advice was tendered by the ministers to the President , however, it does not bar the court from calling upon the Union Council of Ministers to disclose to the court the material upon which the President had formed the requisite satisfaction - Material on the basis of which advice was tendered does not become part of the advice and even if the material is looked into by or shown to the President, it does not partake the character of advice - Further, while defending the proclamation, the minister or the concerned official may claim the privilege u/s. 123 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 and if and when such privilege is claimed, it would be decided on its own merits in accordance with the provisions of s. 123 of the said Act - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 74(1), 356(1) - Proclamation of emergency by the President - Advice tendered by the Cabinet - Judicial review - Scope - Whether judicial review of proclamation u/art. 356(1) would be barred on the reason that the art. 74(2) barred the enquiry into the question whether any, and if so, what advice was tendered by Ministers to the President which led to the issuance of such Proclamation - Held, object of art. 74 (2) was not to exclude any material or documents from the scrutiny of the Courts but to provide that an order issued by or in the name of the President could not be questioned on the ground that it was either contrary to the advice tendered by the Ministers or was issued without obtaining any advice from the Ministers - Object of art. 74(2) was only to make the question whether the President had, followed the advice of the Ministers or acted contrary thereto and hence what advice, if any, was tendered by the Ministers to the President was thus to be beyond the scrutiny of the Court - When the Courts undertake an enquiry into the existence of material in the Cabinet advice, the prohibition contained in art. 74(2) does not negate their right to know about the factual existence of any such material -Court is concerned only with the validity of the order and legality of the proceedings or action taken by the President in exercise of his functions and not with what happened in the inner councils of the President and his Ministers - No one can challenge such decision or action on the ground that it is not in accordance with the advice tendered by the Ministers or that it is based on no advice - If, in a given case, the President acts without, or contrary to, the advice tendered to him, it may be a case warranting his impeachment, but so far as the Court is concerned, it is the act of the President - Appeals disposed of.

(D) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356(1) - Invocation of emergency powers by the President - Judicial review - Scope of - Held, power conferred by art. 356 is of an extraordinary nature to be exercised in grave emergencies and, therefore, the exercise of such power cannot be equated to the power exercised in administrative law field and cannot, therefore, be tested by the same yardstick - Opinion which the President would form on the basis of the Governor's report or otherwise would be based on his political judgement and it is difficult to evolve judicially manageable norms for scrutinising such political decisions, therefore, nature of the decision of the President is not justiciable - Court cannot interdict the use of the constitutional power conferred on the President u/art. 356 unless the same is shown to be mala fide - Before exercise of the Court's jurisdiction sufficient caution must be administered and unless a strong and cogent prima facie case is made out, the President i.e. the executive must not be called upon to answer the charge - Materials 'could consist of several imponderables including some matter which is not strictly legal evidence, the credibility and authenticity of which is incapable of being tested in law Courts - Ultimate opinion formed in such cases, would be mostly a subjective political judgments and there are no judicially manageable standards for scrutinising such materials and resolving such a controversy - Situations wherein the failure of constitutional machinery has to be inferred subjectively from a variety of facts and circumstances, including some imponderables and inferences leading to a subjective political decision, judicial scrutiny of the same is not permissible for want of judicially manageable standards - Political decisions call for judicial hands off envisaging correction only by a subsequent electoral verdict, unless corrected earlier in Parliament - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356 - Invocation of emergency power by President - Judicial review - Scope - Whether the validity of' the Proclamation issued u/art. 356(l) can be challenged even after it has been approved by both Houses of Parliament u/art. 356(3) - Held, no distinction between the Proclamation so approved and a legislation enacted by the Parliament - If the Proclamation is invalid, it does not stand validated merely because it is approved of by the Parliament - Grounds for challenging the validity of the Proclamation may be different from those challenging the validity of a legislation, however, that does not make any difference to the vulnerability of the Proclamation on the limited grounds available - Appeals disposed of.

Jeevan Reddy, SC Aggarwal, Ramaswamy, JJ

(F) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356 - Proclamation u/art. 356 - Judicial review - Scope - Held, Court is entitled to examine whether the condition laid down by the art. 356 has been satisfied or not - Proclamation u/art. 356(1) is not immune from judicial review, though the parameters thereof may vary from an ordinary case of subjective satisfaction - If a proclamation is found to be mala fide or is found to be based wholly on extraneous and/or irrelevant grounds, it is liable to be struck down - Truth or correctness of the material cannot be questioned by the court nor will it go into the adequacy of the material - Court cannot substitute its opinion for that of the President - Even if some of the material on which the action is taken is found to be irrelevant, the court would still not interfere so long as there is some relevant material sustaining the action - Ground of mala fide' lakes in inter alia situation where the proclamation is found to be a clear case of abuse of power, or what is sometimes called fraud on power-cases where this power is invoked for achieving oblique ends - Appeals disposed of.

(G) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356 - Dissolution of Legislative Assembly - Approval of Proclamation by the Parliament - Requirement of - Held, power of dissolving of the Legislative Assembly implicit in art. 356(1) - President shall exercise u/art. 356 only after the proclamation is approved by both Houses of Parliament u/art. 356(3) and not before - Until such approval, the President can only suspend the Legislative Assembly by suspending the provisions of Constitution relating to the Legislative Assembly u/art. 356(1)(c) - Dissolution of Legislative Assembly is not a matter of course and it should be resorted to only where it is found necessary for achieving the purposes of the proclamation - Proclamation for dissolution of the State Legislative Assembly u/art. 356 (1) can be issued only where the situation contemplated by the provision - Once the State machinery has been suspended, President cannot take over some of the functions and powers of the State government while keeping the State government in office - Art. 356(3) is conceived as a control on the power of the President and also as a safeguard against abuse - In case both Houses of Parliament disapprove or do not approve the proclamation, the proclamation lapses at the end of the two-months period and in such circumstance, the government which was dismissed revives - Since the proclamation lapses and is not retrospectively invalidated, the acts done, orders made and laws passed during the period of said two months do not become illegal or void subject to the review, repeal or modification by the government/ Legislative Assembly or other competent authority - However, if the proclamation is approved by both the Houses within two months, the government, which was dismissed, does not revive on the expiry of period of proclamation or on its revocation - Similarly, if the Legislative Assembly has been dissolved after the approval u/art. 356 (3), the Legislative Assembly does not revive on the expiry of the period of proclamation or on its revocation - Appeals dismissed.

(H) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356 - Dissolution of State Assembly - Power of the President - Scope - Held, art. 356(1) empower the President to dissolve the Legislative Assembly - Power to dissolve the Legislative Assembly is implicit in sub-cl.(a) of cl.(l) of art. 356 though there is no such thing as dissolution of the 'Legislature of the State' where it consists of two Houses - Existence of power does not mean that dissolution of Legislative Assembly should either be treated as obligatory or should invariably be ordered whenever a Government of the State is dismissed - President has taken into consideration all the relevant facts and circumstances, whether the Legislative Assembly should also be dissolved or not - If the President thinks that it should be dissolved, it would be appropriate, indeed highly desirabe, that he states the reasons for such extraordinary step in the order itself - Power of dissolution can be exercised by the President only after both Houses of Parliament approve the proclamation and not before such approval - Once the Parliament places its seal of approval on the proclamation, further steps as may be found necessary to achieve the purposes of the proclamation, i.e. dissolution of Legislative Assembly, can be ordered - Until the approval, the President can only keep the Assembly under suspended animation but shall not dissolve it - Appeals disposed of.

(I) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 356(1) - Dissolution of State Legislature - Judicial review - Scope -Held, no writ petition can be entertained by any court before the actual issuance of proclamation u/art. 356(1), however, HC or SC can entertain a writ petition questioning the proclamation if it is satisfied that the writ petition raises arguable questions with respect to the validity of the proclamation - Court would be entitled to entertain such a writ petition even before the approval of the proclamation by the Parliament and as also after such approval - In an appropriate case and if the situation demands, the HC/SC can also stay the dissolution of the Assembly but not in such a manner as to allow the Assembly to continue beyond its original term - Appeals disposed of.

(Obiter) Ramaswamy , J

(J) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Judicial review - Power of judicial review is a constituent power and cannot be abdicated by judicial process of interpretation - Justiciability is one of exercise of the power by the Court hedged by self-imposed judicial restraint - Justiciability is not a legal concept with a fixed content, nor is it susceptible of scientific verification and its use is the result of many pressures or variagated reasons - Justiciability may be looked at from the point of view of common sense limitation - Judicial review may be avoided on questions of purely political nature, though pure legal questions camouflaged by the political questions are always justiciable - Justiciability on a subjective satisfaction conferred in the widest terms to the political co-ordinate executive branch created by the constitutional scheme itself is one of the considerations to be kept in view in exercising judicial review - Appeals disposed of.
