Varala Bharath Kumar and another v State of Telangana and another

Supreme Court of India

05 September 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohan M. Shantanagoudar, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 676; (2017) 9 SCC 413; JT 2017 (8) SC 592

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Breach Of Trust, Criminal Breach Of Trust, Cruelty, Harassment, Marriage, Quash, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Complainant

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498A, 405, 406 - Cruelty - Quashing of Proceedings - Respondent no.2/wife/complainant was married to appellant/ husband, however, wife was subjected to cruelty - Appellant and his family members were charged for offences punishable u/ss. 498A and 406 of IPC - HC rejected prayer of husband to quash proceedings against them and directed appellant no.2 to file application u/s. 205 of CrPC seeking dispense with his presence before Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the extraordinary power u/art. 226 or inherent power u/s. 482 of CrPC can be exercised by the HC, either to prevent abuse of process of the court or otherwise to secure the ends of justice. However, while exercising power u/s.482 or u/art. 226 in such matters, the court does not function as a Court of Appeal or Revision. Inherent jurisdiction u/s. 482 of the Code though wide has to be exercised sparingly, carefully or with caution and only when such exercise is justified by the tests specifically laid down u/s. 482 itself. It is to be exercised ex debito justitiae to do real and substantial justice, for the administration of which alone courts exist. The court must be careful and see that its decision in exercise of its power is based on sound principles. The inherent powers should not be exercised to stifle a legitimate prosecution. No hard and fast rule can be laid down in regard to cases in which the HC will exercise its extra ordinary jurisdiction of quashing the proceedings at any stage. In instant case, the ingredients of cruelty and criminal breach of trust are not forthcoming from the records as against the appellants. In other words, there is a total absence of allegations for the offences punishable u/s. 498A and s. 406 of IPC. Hence, the impugned order of the HC is set aside and quash the proceedings initiated against both the appellants. Appeal allowed.
