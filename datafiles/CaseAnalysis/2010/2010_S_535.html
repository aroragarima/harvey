S. Arul Raja v State of Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

30 July 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 602; (2010) 8 SCC 233; 2011 (1) CGLJ 326; 2010 (4) JCC 2451; JT 2010 (8) SC 158; 2010 (4) MLJ(Crl) 67; 2010 (3) RCR(Criminal) 918; 2010(7) SCALE 545; [2010] 9 S.C.R. 356

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 302 and 307 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 164 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 10 and 32 - Murder - Attempted murder - Criminal conspiracy - Conviction - Challenged - Appellant was accused of hatching a conspiracy wherein he engaged accused No. 1 (A1), and deceased-accused 'B' to murder deceased - Executive Magistrate recorded dying declaration of A1 - In his statement made to Executive Magistrate, A1 implicated appellant in the crime, and declared that he was given a contract killing - Trial Court acquitted appellant - On appeal, HC reversed judgment of acquittal of appellant passed by Trial Court and convicted appellant u/ss. 302 and 307 r/w s. 120-B of IPC - HC convicted appellant primarily on basis of dying declaration of A1 that implicated appellant in a conspiracy to murder - Hence, present appeal - (A) Motive - It was contended by respondent that murder was motivated by animosity between deceased and appellant - Held, it may be true that appellant had grievances against deceased, however, chain of events that was said to have driven appellant to commit murder do not provide a clear motive to substantiate argument of respondent, or decision of HC - (B) Criminal conspiracy - Held, a meeting of minds to form a criminal conspiracy has to be proved by placing substantive evidence and respondent has not adduced any evidence which underlines same - Further, mere circumstantial evidence to prove involvement of appellant was not sufficient to meet requirements of criminal conspiracy u/s. 120-A of IPC - Prosecution failed to substantiate allegation of conspiracy against appellant - (C) Whether A1's statement could constitute valid dying declaration? - Held, for a statement to be admissible in evidence as a dying declaration, person making statement should no longer be alive - In the present case, alleged dying declaration cannot be said to be a dying declaration as A1 did not die after making alleged dying declaration and there was no reason forthcoming as to why A1 was brought to hospital - (D) Whether A1's statement could constitute a confession so as to be used to implicate appellant for murder? - Held, statement has neither been recorded by a Judicial Magistrate nor has it fulfilled procedural requirements, including that of a certificate to be appended by Magistrate - Hence, statement not admissible against appellant as a confession u/s. 164 of CrPC - (E) Whether statement made by A1 may be considered as an extra-judicial confession? - Held, evidentiary value of extra-judicial confession must be judged in facts and circumstances of each individual case - Extra-judicial confession, if voluntary, can be relied upon by court along with other evidence in convicting accused - In present case, A1 being co-accused, it is not proper to convict appellant solely on basis of confession of A1 - More so, when confession is not corroborated by any evidence - Furthermore, statement made by A1 is insufficient to implicate appellant in said conspiracy as same is hit by s. 10 of Evidence Act which refers to statement of a fellow conspirator that pertains to common intention behind act, and such a statement can be used against other conspirators - Furthermore, statement made by A1 in police custody cannot be used to implicate appellant in conspiracy to murder - Evidence adduced by prosecution against appellant not sufficient to justify his conviction - Decision of HC reversed and appellant acquitted of charges against - Appeals allowed.
