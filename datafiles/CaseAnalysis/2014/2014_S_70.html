Security Association of India and another v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

25 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 503; (2014) 12 SCC 65; AIR 2014 SC 3812; 2015 (1) CLT(SC) 192; JT 2014 (8) SC 461; 2014(8) SCALE 514; [2014] 9 S.C.R. 880

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution

Keywords: Maharashtra Private Security Guards (Regulation of Employment and Welfare) Act, 1981, Private Security Agencies (Regulation) Act, 2005, Maharashtra Private Security Guards (Regulation of Employment and Welfare) Scheme, 1981, Maharashtra Private Security (Regulation of Employment and Welfare) Amendment Act, 1996, Maharshtra Private Security (Regulation of Employment and Welfare) Scheme, 2002, Maharashtra Private Security Agencies (Regulation) Rules, 2007

Summary: Administrative - Constitution - Private Security Agencies (Regulation) Act, 2005 - Maharashtra Private Security Guards (Regulation of Employment & Welfare) Act, 1981 - Two Acts - Applicability - HC by means of the common impugned judgment disposed of the writ petitions filed by various security agencies which claimed that after enactment of 2005 Act/Central Act by the Parliament, the 1981/State Act was not applicable to the private security agencies and if the State Act remained in operation with respect to private security agencies then the State Govt. should be directed to expeditiously pass orders on the pending applications for exemption under the provisions of the State Act as allowed u/s. 23 of the State Act - HC found the State Act to be in consonance with the Central Act and directed the State Govt. to pass orders on the applications for exemption or applications for renewal of exemption filed by the security agencies - As the appeals challenge the validity of a State Act in light of a Central Act, the legislative history of the same has to be examined in the light of the current facts - State Act which came into force on 29-6-1981 received the Presidential assent envisaged u/art. 200 of the Constitution of India on 24-9-1981 - Whether the Two Acts would be applicable at the same time was the question raised in appeal -

Held, in the instant case, after perusing the two Acts (Central Act and State Act) in entirety, HC found that two statutes occupy different fields - Central Act aimed to regulate the business of private security agencies and s. 13(1)(j) of the Central Act which read as under, did not turn the Central Act into a labour welfare statute as the same was an incidental provision - Thus, HC accepted the arguments of appellant with regard to the same - State Act was in contrast to the Central Act as it contained express provisions pertaining to labour welfare and contains mechanism to ensure that the same were complied with - Furthermore, the State Act also imposed penal liability if the said provisions were not complied with - Therefore, SC was of the opinion that the two statutes occupy distinct fields - Appellants had also challenged the State Act to be violative of arts. 14 and 19 of the Constitution - However, SC found that the same did not hold good as the restrictions imposed by the State Act were reasonable restrictions envisioned by the Constitution and that they protect the rights and ensure the welfare of private security guards engaged by private security agencies by means of s. 23 and relevant provisions of the Scheme of 2002 - Furthermore, HC while answering the same question and considering the situation of the private security guards held the State Act was not to be in violation of arts. 14 and 19 or any other Fundamental Rights contained in the Constitution - Therefore, SC accepted the arguments put forth by appellant in that regard - Other impediment which tried to be pointed out by appellant in respect of s. 23 of the State Act that it compelled all the security guards employed by the agencies and deployed with various principal employers to seek exemption is totally misconceived because it would bring about total stoppage of the agency's business - After analyzing s. 23 of the State Act, it appeared to SC that exemption could be granted to a class or classes of Security Guards, employed by agencies and deployed with the principal employer and who were in the enjoyment of benefits which were on the whole not less favourable to such security Guards than the benefits provided for or under State Act or any Scheme made thereunder - SC had noticed that the HC had duly taken care of that and considered the said scheme and pointed out that to seek the benefit of exemption u/s.23 of 1981 Act, three conditions were necessary - Firstly, the class or classes of Security Guards should be employed by the agency or agent - Secondly, those Guards should be deployed by the concerned agency in a factory or establishment or in any class or classes of factories or establishments and Thirdly, in the opinion of the State Govt., all such Security Guards or such class or classes of Security Guards at the time of seeking exemption were in the enjoyment of benefits which were on the whole not less favourable to such Security Guards than the benefits provided by or under 1981 Act or any Scheme made thereunder - Since after the enactment of the State Act, the principal employer was prohibited from taking private Security Guards from Security Agencies, the exemption could be asked only in respect of private Security Guards who satisfied the aforementioned three conditions - Thus, it was only a one time exercise for seeking exemption for private Security Guards who were employed by the agency and deployed by that agency in factory or establishment - That exercise could be repeated as and when the provisions of the Security Guards Act were made applicable to different areas of the State on different dates as provided u/s.1(3) of 1981 Act - Discussion which SC had in the preceding paragraphs are reasons to come to the conclusion, and SC held that there was no repugnancy between the State Act and the Central Act in the given facts - SC had also found that the Central Act did not occupy the field of labour welfare and thereby there could not be any conflict between the State Act and the Central Act - Question of applicability of the Central Act and the State Act, applied concurrently and SC accepted the submission of respondent to that extent relying upon the decisions cited before SC - Vijay Kumar Sharma & Ors. vs. State of Karnataka & Ors. 1990 Indlaw SC 732, State of Uttar Pradesh & Anr. vs. Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd. & Anr., 1991 Indlaw SC 702 and State of Bihar & Ors. vs. Shree Baidyanath Ayurved Bhawan (P.) Ltd. & Ors 2005 Indlaw SC 19, relied on - Furthermore, SC had also noticed that the State Act duly received the assent of the President - SC had further noticed that Cl. 28(1) of the Scheme of 2002 provided that every registered principal employer might either engage a security guard registered with the Board or private employer agency or directly and the said clause was nothing but declaratory of the object of the Act and the Scheme - Therefore, it could not stand in the way of performing the business by the private security agencies - Appeals dismissed.
