Rattan Lal v Vardesh Chander and Others

Supreme Court of India

09 December 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, Y.V. Chandrachud, A.C. Gupta

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 241; (1976) 2 SCC 103; AIR 1976 SC 588; 1976 RCR(Rent) 355; [1976] 2 S.C.R. 906

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Rent Control; Tort

Summary: Rent Control - Delhi Rent Control Act, 1958 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 111(g) - Lease - Termination without written notice - Legality - Challenged - Appellant/tenant of a building inducted into possession by respondent/landlord under a letting - Respondent filed eviction petition on the ground of unauthorized sub-letting of a portion of the premises and possession, by tenant, of alternative accommodation - Rent Controller, ordered eviction and the Appellate Tribunal affirmed it, upholding the vice of sub-letting without consent of the landlord - Further on appeal Single Judge deserved consideration by a DB and referred the appeal for determination accordingly to a larger Bench - DB dismissed the appeal - Hence instant appeal - What was the essence of equity in the matter of determining a lease on the ground of forfeiture caused by the breach of a condition - Held, if Law and Justice-in the Indian context should speak to each other, statutory technicality such as notice in writing prescribed in s. 111(g) of 1882 Act could not be called a rule of equity - It was no more than a legal form binding on those transactions which were covered by the law by its own force - Substance of the matter-the justice of the situation was whether a condition in the lease was breached and whether lessor had, by some overt act, brought home to the lessee his election to eject on the strength of the said breach - Notice in writing formally determining the tenancy was not a rule of justice or canon of commonsense - Realism, married to equity, being the true test, SC persuaded that the pre-amending Act provision of s. 111 (g) of 1882 Act was in consonance with justice - If so, the mere institution of the legal proceeding for eviction fulfilled the requirement of law for determination of the lease - Conscience of the Court need nothing more and nothing else - Respondent's termination of the tenancy in instant case was good even without a written notice - Appeal dismissed.
