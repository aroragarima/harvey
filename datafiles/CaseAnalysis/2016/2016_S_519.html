Suresh Chand Gautam v State of Uttar Pradesh and others

Supreme Court of India

11 March 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 220; AIR 2016 SC 1321; 2016 (2) ILR(Cut) 1; JT 2016 (3) SC 540B; 2016(3) SCALE 246; [2016] 1 S.C.R. 727

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Mandamus, Affirmative Action, SERVICE, Review, Relief, Selection, Vacancies, Promotion, Representation, Seniority, Employment, Rule, Date, Judicial Review, Posts, Validity Of, Human Rights, Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, Policy, Government Servants, Amendment, Scheme, Police, Protection Of Human Rights Act, 1993, Reservation, Uttar Pradesh Public Services (Reservation for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes and Other Backward Classes) Act, 1994, Gujarat Minor Mineral Rules, 1966, Uttar Pradesh Government Servants Seniority Rules, 1991, Uttar Pradesh Government Servants Seniority (Third Amendment) Rules, 2007, Uttar Pradesh Government Servants Seniority (Second Amendment) Rules, 2005, Uttar Pradesh Government Servants Seniority (First Amendment) Rules, 2002, Uttar Pradesh Sheera Niyantran Act, 1964, Constitution of India, 1950, Right to, Appointment, Agriculture Marketing Act, 1958, Uttar Pradesh Public Services (Reservation for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes and Other Backward Classes) (Amendment) Ordinance, 2012

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 16(4), 16(4A), 16(4B), 32, 335 - Pray to issue direction to legislators - Issuance of mandamus - Jurisdiction of Courts - Writ Petitions preferred u/s. 32 of Constitution praying to issue of direction in nature of mandamus commanding respondents to enforce appropriately Constitutional mandate as contained under provisions of arts. 16(4-A), 16(4-B) and 335 of Constitution or, in alternative, directing respondents to constitute Committee or appoint Commission chaired either by retired Judge of HC or SC in making survey and collecting necessary qualitative data of Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST) in services of State for granting reservation in promotion - Whether in context of arts. 16(4-A) and 16(4-B) of Constitution, writ or direction can be issued to State Govt. or its functionaries or instrumentalities of State to collect and gather necessary data for purpose of taking decision as regards promotion and consequential fixation of seniority.

Held, arts. 16, 16(4),16(4-B) of Constitution being enabling provisions, there is no power coupled with duty. SC held that existence of provision for reservation in matter of selection or promotion is sine qua non for seeking mandamus. Further, right accrues in favour of SC and ST candidates when there is provision. M. Nagaraj & Others v Union Of India & Others, 2006 Indlaw SC 722, relied on, where it was held that State concerned is required to show in each case existence of compelling reasons, namely, backwardness, inadequacy of representation and overall administrative efficiency before making provision for reservation. Further, State is not bound to make reservation for SCs/STs in matters of promotion. However, if State wishes to exercise discretion and make such provision, it has to collect quantifiable data showing backwardness of class and inadequacy of representation of that class in public employment in addition to compliance with art. 335 of Constitution. SC held that expression of opinion clearly demonstrates that regard being had to enabling provisions of arts. 16(4-A) and 16(4-B) of Constitution, State is not bound to make reservation. It has discretion to do so and State's discretion can only be exercised on certain conditions being satisfied. SC held that writ of mandamus is sought to collect material or data which is in realm of condition precedent for exercising discretion which flows from enabling Constitutional provision. SC held that direction of this nature would not come within principle of exercise of power coupled with duty. Further, SC held that State is not bound to make reservation in promotion, hence, there is no Constitutional obligation. SC held that Courts do not formulate any policy, remains away from making anything that would amount to legislation, rules and regulation or policy relating to reservation. Further, SC held that court cannot direct for making legislation or for that matter any kind of sub-ordinate legislation. SC held that to issue mandamus to collect data would tantamount to asking authorities whether there is ample data to frame rule or regulation would be entering into domain of legislation, for it is step towards commanding to frame legislation or delegated legislation for reservation. Hence, prayer of petitioner to issue mandamus to take step towards framing of rule or regulation for purpose of reservation for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in matter of promotions is not justified. Hence, writ of mandamus of such nature cannot be issued. Petitions dismissed.

Ratio - Courts are not to indulge into policy-making by adding something to policy by ways of issuing writ of mandamus.
