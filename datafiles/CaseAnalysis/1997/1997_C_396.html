B.O.I. Finance Limited v Custodian and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 March 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

S.P. Bharucha, B.N. Kirpal

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 1086; (1997) 10 SCC 488; AIR 1997 SC 1952; AIR 1997 SCW 1722; 1997 (3) Bom.C.R. 655; 1997 (3) CLT 81; [1997] 89 Comp Cas 74; 1997 (2) CompLJ 129; JT 1997 (4) SC 15; 1997(3) SCALE 126; [1997] 3 S.C.R. 51; 1997 (5) Supreme 334

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance

Summary: Banking & Finance - Securities Exchange Board of India - Banking Regulation Act, 1949 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Securities, Contract, (Regulation) Act, 1956, s. 16 - Appellant banks had prior to 06-06-1992, entered into contracts with for purchase and sale of certain securities which were not listed on any stock exchange - These contracts have been regarded buy-back transactions - Nature of such a transaction is that it consists of two inter-connected legs, namely, first or ready leg, consisting of purchase or sale of certain securities at a specified price, and second or forward leg, consisting of sale or purchase of same of similar securities at a latter date at a price determined on first date - Validity of the contract in question before the Special Court - Special Court held that circulars issued under Banking Act were binding and, since transactions were contrary thereto, same were illegal and void in respect of third parties - It rejected contention that contract was severable and that first leg was not hit by illegality - It further came to conclusion that contracts were also illegal under provisions of Securities Regulation Act, 1956 and notification issued u/s. 16 thereof - It accordingly directed banks to return securities to be custodian - While giving this direction it further observed that if these securities had been transferred by banks to third parties then no right could be created in their favour as banks had no right to transfer them and, therefore, banks should purchase securities of same value from market and deliver same to custodian - Whether aforesaid decision of Special Court is correct in law? - Held, there can be little doubt that appellants, when they paid market price and took delivery of securities, had become owners of same - According to s. 5 of Transfer of Property Act, 1882 'transfer of property' inter alia means an Act by which a person conveys property to another person - S. 6 of this Act deals with what property may be transferred - What is relevant in s. 6(h) according to which no transfer can be made, (1) insofar as it is opposed to nature of interest affected thereby, or (2) for an unlawful object, or consideration within meaning of s. 23 of Indian Contract Act, or (3) to a person legally disqualified to be transferee - According to s. 23 of Contract Act consideration or object of an agreement will be unlawful if it is forbidden by law, or is of such a nature that, if permitted, it would defeat provisions of any law, or is fraudulent, or involves or implies injury to person or property of another, or court regards it as immoral or opposed to public policy - In instant case object of contracts entered into between banks and notified parties was for transfer and, subsequently, retransfer of securities on payment of market price as consideration - Consideration for transfer of securities, in ready leg, was payment of market price - The validity of transfer of securities has to depend on provision of Transfer of Property Act and Sale of Goods Act relating to transfer and not to validity of agreement preceding transfer - Like any other movable goods securities could validly be purchased on delivery against payment of price as per ss. 4, 19 and 20 of Sale of Goods Act - Price paid, while taking delivery, was consideration for transfer of securities - When transfer of title has taken place agreement between parties preceding this cannot invalidate transfer - Infringements of instructions issued by Reserve Bank of India under Banking Regulations Act prohibiting banks from entering into buy-back arrangements do not invalidate such contracts entered into between banks and its customers' - Ready forward contract is severable into two parts, namely, ready leg and forward leg ready leg of transaction having been completed, forward leg, which alone is illegal, has to be ignored - With ready leg having been performed illegality of forward leg contained in agreement cannot affect transfers which had already taken place - Appeal allowed.
