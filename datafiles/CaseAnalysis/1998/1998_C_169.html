Operative Land Development Bank Limited v U. P. State Co-Chandra Bhan Dubey and Others

Supreme Court of India

18 December 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

D.P. Wadhwa

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 193; 1999 (1) LLJ 63

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Service

Summary: Service - Constitution - Uttar Pradesh Co-operative Societies Act, 1965 s. 122 - Constitution of India,1950 arts. 12, 226 - Dismissal orders - Writ direction - Maintainability - Respondent nos. 1 to 3/employees were working in Bank of appellant/society - It was alleged that respondents committed various irregularities and charge-sheet was served upon them containing various charges - Inquiry Officer sent report holding charges 2 to 5 proved against them, show cause notice was issued to respondents as to why they be not dismissed from service of Bank - Disciplinary authority held charges were established against respondents and it dismissed them from Bank's service - Against dismissal orders 3 writ petitions were filed in HC, which were allowed - HC negatived plea of appellant that it was not amenable to writ jurisdiction being not an 'authority' or 'State' within meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - Further, HC concluded that dismissal orders were vitiated by non-compliance with rules of natural justice and also in violation of statutory rules as applicable to employees of appellant - Hence instant appeals - Whether impugned judgment of HC was justified - Held, it would be seen that all requirements for initiation and conclusion of disciplinary proceedings were followed in instant case and rules of natural justice observed - Proceedings against respondents were initiated on reports of officers under whom they were working and these reports formed part of evidence in proceedings, inquiry proceedings was not held as if it was trial in criminal case or as if it was civil suit - Rules of natural justice require that party against whom allegation was being inquired into should be given hearing and not condemned unheard - As to what were rules of natural justice to be followed in particular case would depend upon circumstances in each case and most also depend on provisions of law under which charges were being inquired intoto disciplinary proceedings - Further, all these provisions unmistakably show that affairs of appellant were controlled by State Govt. though it functions as co-operative society and it was certainly extended arm of State and thus instrumentally of State or authority as mentioned under art. 12 of Constitution - SC, therefore, hold that appellant was authority controlled by State Govt. and service conditions of employees of appellant particularly with regard to disciplinary proceedings against them were statutory in nature and thus writ petition was maintainable against appellant, to this extent, SC agreed with HC - However, disciplinary proceedings were held against respondents in accordance with law with due observance of the rules of natural justice, judgment of HC was, therefore, not correct to that extent - Thus, impugned judgment of HC holding that dismissal of respondents was not legal was set aside and writ petitions filed by respondents were dismissed - Appeals allowed.
