J. Jayalalitha and Others v Union of India and Another

Supreme Court of India

14 May 1999

Case Analysis

Bench

G.T. Nanavati, S.P. Kurdukar

Where Reported

1999 Indlaw SC 1705; (1999) 5 SCC 138; (1999) SCC (Cr) 670; AIR 1999 SC 1912; AIR 1999 SCW 1579; 1999 (39) AllCriC 217; 1999 CRLJ 2859; 2000 (3) G.L.R. 2437; JT 1999 (3) SC 573; [1999] 3 S.C.R. 653; 1999 (5) Supreme 463

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Administrative - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 arts. 14, 235 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 ss. 3, 4 - Trial of cases - Central Govt. notification - Validity - Govt. of Tamil Nadu for expedient disposal of prevention of corruption cases obtained concurrence of HC and by notification dt. 30-4-1997 Special Judges were appointed - Writ petitions were filed in HC by which s. 3 of the Act was challenged, HC dismissed writ petitions holding that it was constitutionally valid and not violative of the Constitution - Subsequent to filing of SLPs, Central Govt. issued notification on 5-2-1999 appointing Special Judges for trial of offences specified in s. 3(1) of the Act - By another notification of same date issued in exercise of powers conferred by s. 4(2) of the Act, Central Govt. specified some Special Judges in city of Chennai to be Judges who should try offences specified in s. 3(1) of the Act - Hence, instant petitions - It was contended by Attorney General that, s. 4(2) of the Act did not refer to 'group of cases' and that indicates that there was no exclusivity of jurisdiction of such special Judge and consequently power conferred on Central Govt. to allot cases u/s. 4(2) would apply to cases tried by such special Judges - Whether impugned notification dt. 5-2-1999 was valid - Held, on applying well known Principle of interpretation of Statute that singular includes plural unless context requires otherwise, it might be held that word 'case' in 2nd part of 4(2) of the Act includes 'cases' and therefore, when special judge or judges was/were appointed for case or group of cases then only that special judge or those special judges could hear those cases, otherwise very purpose of making such appointment or appointments would be frustrated - Power conferred upon Central Govt. u/s. 4(2) of the Act is also to be exercised if that becomes 'necessary' and same guideline contained in s. 3(1) of the Act should apply while exercising power u/s. 4(2) of the Act - Trial of cases specified in impugned State notification was going on since May, 1977 and no necessity had arisen till February, 1999 to exercise power of allocation in respect of those cases - Further, Central Govt. had not placed any material before SC to show why it became necessary for it after such long time, to make reallocation of cases to be tried by special judges in city of Chennai and it had pleaded only its power to do so and allocation was made in consultation with HC - Really, allocation amongst 3 special judges could be said to have been done by HC though formal notification in that behalf was issued by State Govt. - Central Govt. issued impugned notification, while SLPs challenging judgment of HC was pending in SC and Central Govt. had failed to establish necessity of issuing impugned notification dt. 5-2-1999, same was held to be not in accordance with s. 4(2) of the Act - Therefore, it was regarded as bad and impugned notification No. 37I/69/98-A.V. III(II), dt. 5-2-1999 issued by Central Govt. was quashed and set aside - Petitions disposed of.
