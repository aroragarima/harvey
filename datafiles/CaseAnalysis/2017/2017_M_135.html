Om Prakash and another v Mishri Lal (Dead) represented by his Lr. Savitri Devi and another

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 197; (2017) 5 SCC 451; AIR 2017 SC 1597; 2017 (122) ALR 123; 2017 (2) AWC 2024; 2017 (2) CLT(SC) 155; JT 2017 (3) SC 301; 2017 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1318; 2017 (1) RCR(Rent) 409; 2017(3) SCALE 634

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: Compromise Decree, Default In Payment Of Rent, Transfer Of Property Act, 1882, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Indian Succession Act, 1925, Suit Premises, Revisional, Suit For Eviction, Prescribed Authority, Uttar Pradesh Urban Buildings (Regulation of Letting, Rent and Eviction) Act, 1972, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Rent Control - Uttar Pradesh Urban Buildings (Regulation of Letting, Rent and Eviction) Act, 1972, ss. 20, 20(4), 21, 3(j), 30 - Suit for eviction - Bonafide requirement - Dismissal - Appellants/landlords filed suit before Trial Court against respondents seeking his eviction from suit premises on ground of default in payment of rent and sub-letting of suit premises without the knowledge and approval of landlords - Trial Court decreed suit both on ground of default in payment of rent and sub-letting of suit premises - On appeal, HC dismissed suit and application under the Act for eviction of respondents from suit premises on ground of default and bona fide requirement - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, fact that no evidence was forthcoming to attest that requirements of s. 20(4) of the Act had been fully complied with, construction of his own house elsewhere did dis-entitle original defendant and respondents to avail the benefit of such protection as contemplated by the Act. Judicially propounded proposition was that when property forming the subject matter of eviction proceedings was owned by several co-owners, every co-owner owns every part and every bit of joint property along with others and it cannot be said that he was only a part owner or a fractional owner of property and that he could alone maintain a suit for eviction of tenant without joining other co-owners if such other co-owners do not object. Not only the compromise decree declared appellants to be joint owners of suit premises, their status as such was not been questioned at any stage by anyone interested in the title thereto. Further, appellants, being son of landlord who at all relevant time was landlord vis-a-vis original defendant and respondents in terms of s. 3(j) of the Act, their status as landlords for the purpose of eviction under the Act, could not have been questioned so as to non-suit them for want of locus. HC dismissed suit and application for release of suit premises u/s. 21 of the Act, principally on the ground of want of standing of appellants. Impugned judgments and orders of HC were set aside. Respondents were directed to vacate suit premises within 3 months. Appeals allowed.
