Nand Kishore Prasad v State of Bihar and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 April 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, P.S. Kailasam

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 172; (1978) 3 SCC 366; (1978) SCC (L&S) 458; AIR 1978 SC 1277; 1978 LabIC 1106; 1978 (2) LLJ 84; 1978 (2) LLN 278; [1978] 3 S.C.R. 708; 1978 SLJ 591; 1978 (2) SLR 46; 1978 UJ 395

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Labour & Industrial Law; Land & Property

Summary: Service - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 409, 466, 468, 474 and 477 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.133 and 226 - Appellant was appointed as a ministerial servant by District Magistrate - Sub-Divisional Office summoned appellant and asked him to explain outstanding fine of case decided by Magistrate - Suspended from service - FIR for prosecution in respect of offences u/ss. 120-B, 409, 466, 468, 474 and 477(A) IPC - Trial Magistrate discharged appellant - Suspension continued - Departmental inquiry - District Magistrate dropped proceeding against him - Commr. after inquiry directed removal of appellant from service - Whether impugned orders do not rest on any evidence whatever, but merely on suspicions, conjectures and surmises? - First of principles is that disciplinary proceedings before a domestic tribunal are of a quasi-judicial character; therefore minimum requirement of rules of natural justice is that tribunal should arrive at its conclusion on basis of some evidence - Second principle, which is a corollary from first, is, that if disciplinary inquiry has been conducted fairly without bias or predilection, in accordance with relevant disciplinary rules and Constitutional provisions, order passed by such authority cannot be interfered with in proceedings u/art. 226 of Constitution, merely on ground that it was based on evidence which would be insufficient for conviction of delinquent on same charge at a criminal trial - Desirability of writing a self-contained speaking order in disciplinary proceeding culminating in an order of removal of delinquent from service, cannot be over-emphasized - Held, it is clear from evidence that this was not a case of no evidence, but of evidence which was not adequate enough to carry conviction at a criminal trial - Impugned orders did not suffer from any error of law which may warrant an interference in proceedings u/art. 226 - Appeal dismissed.
