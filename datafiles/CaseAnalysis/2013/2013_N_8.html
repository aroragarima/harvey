Suresh Kumar Koushal and another v NAZ Foundation and others

Supreme Court of India

11 December 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 816; (2014) 1 SCC 1; AIR 2014 SC 563; 2013 (4) CLT(SC) 225; 2014 CRLJ 784; 2013 (204) DLT 533; JT 2014 (1) SC 27; 2014 (3) KarLJ 10; 2014 (1) MLJ 68; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 286; 2013(15) SCALE 55

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Human Rights

Keywords: child rights, Public Health, Morality, Law In Force, Existing Law, Reasonableness, Constitutionality, Severability, Notions, Tamil Nadu Tax on Consumption or Sale of Electricity Act, 2003, Offences against the Person Act, 1861, Homosexuality, Buggery Act, 1533, Commissions for Protection of Child Rights Act, 2005, Criminal Law (India) Act 1828, Indian Penal Code, 1848, French Penal Code, Draft Penal Code, 1837, Offences against the Person Act, 1828

Summary: (A) Criminal - Constitution - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 377 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 15, 21 - Validity of pre-Constitutional law - Power of superior courts - Respondent No. 1 NGO filed PIL challenging the constitutional validity of s. 377 IPC on the ground that it criminalizes consensual sexual acts of adults including sexual minorities in private, was violative of arts. 21, 14 and 15 of Constitution - HC allowed writ petition - Hence, instant appeal by various NGOs and individuals - Whether superior courts were empowered to declare pre-Constitutional law as void - Held, HC and SC were empowered to declare as void any law including pre-Constitution law - Such power could be exercised to the extent of inconsistency with Constitution/contravention of Part III - There was a presumption of constitutionality in favour of all laws, including pre-Constitutional laws as Parliament, in its capacity as the representative of the people, was deemed to act for the benefit of people in light of their needs and constraints of Constitution - Further, doctrine of severability ensured that only that portion of law which was unconstitutional was so declared and the remainder was saved - This doctrine should be applied keeping in mind the scheme and purpose of law and intention of Legislature and should be avoided where the two portions were inextricably mixed with one another - Court should resort to reading down a law in order to save it from being rendered unconstitutional - However, while doing so, it should not change the essence of law and create a new law which in its opinion was more desirable - Appeals allowed.

(B) Criminal - Constitution - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 377 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Constitutionality of s. 377 IPC - Whether s. 377 IPC was Constitutionally valid - Held, while HC and SC are empowered to review Constitutionality of s. 377 IPC and strike it down to the extent of its inconsistency with the Constitution, self restraint should be exercised and analysis should be guided by presumption of Constitutionality - Unless a clear constitutional violation was proved, SC was not empowered to strike down a law merely by virtue of its falling into disuse or perception of society having changed as regards legitimacy of its purpose and its need - Further, acts which fell within the ambit of s. 377 could only be determined with reference to the act itself and the circumstances in which it was executed - However, it was difficult to prepare a list of acts which would be covered by s. 377 - S. 377 IPC would apply irrespective of age and consent - S. 377 IPC did not criminalize a particular people or identity or orientation - It merely identified certain acts which if committed would constitute an offence - Such a prohibition regulated sexual conduct regardless of gender identity and orientation - Those who indulge in carnal intercourse in ordinary course and those who indulge in carnal intercourse against order of nature constitute different classes - People falling in the later category could not claim that s. 377 suffered from the vice of arbitrariness and irrational classification - Thus, s. 377 IPC did not suffer from the vice of unconstitutionality - Appeals allowed

(C) Criminal - Constitution - Human Rights - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 377 - Constitution of India, 1950 , arts, 14, 15, 21 - Challenge to vires of s. 377 IPC - Failure to provide data - Whether HC was justified in entertaining challenge to s. 377 IPC despite the fact that respondent No.1 had not laid factual foundation to support its challenge - Held, writ petition filed by respondent No.1 was singularly laconic in as much as except giving brief detail of work being done by it for HIV prevention targeting MSM community - It miserably failed to furnish particulars of incidents of discriminatory attitude exhibited by State agencies towards sexual minorities and consequential denial of basic human rights to them - Respondent No.1 also not furnished particulars of cases involving harassment and assault from public and public authorities to sexual minorities - Details provided in the affidavit were wholly insufficient for recording a finding that homosexuals, gays, etc., were being subjected to discriminatory treatment either by State or its agencies or the society - S. 377 was merely defined particular offence and prescribed punishment for the same which could be awarded if in the trial conducted in accordance with the provisions of the CrPC and other statutes of the same family the person was found guilty - Hence, HC was not right in declaring s. 377 IPC ultra vires arts.14 and 15 of Constitution - HC overlooked that a miniscule fraction of country's population constitute lesbians, gays, bisexuals or transgender and in last more than 150 years less than 200 persons were prosecuted (as per the reported orders) for committing offence u/s. 377 IPC - This figures should not be made sound basis for declaring that s. 377 ultra vires the provisions of arts. 14,15 and 21 of the Constitution - Appeals allowed

(D) Criminal - Constitution - Human Rights - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 377 - Constitution of India, 1950 , art. 21 - Whether there was any violation of art. 21 of the Constitution - Held, a test which was required to be satisfied while judging Constitutionality of a provision which purported to restrict or limit the right to life and liberty, including rights of privacy, dignity and autonomy, as envisaged u/art. 21 - In order to fulfill this test, law should not only be competently legislated but it should also be just, fair and reasonable - Respondent No.1 attacked s. 377 IPC on the ground that the same was used to perpetrate harassment, blackmail and torture on certain persons, especially those belonging to LGBT community - This treatment was neither mandated by section nor condoned by it and the mere fact that the section was misused by police authorities and others was not a reflection of vires of the section - It might be a relevant factor for Legislature to consider while judging the desirability of amending s. 377 IPC - In its anxiety to protect rights of LGBT persons and to declare that s. 377 IPC violated the right to privacy, autonomy and dignity, HC extensively relied upon judgments of other jurisdictions - Though those judgments shed considerable light on various aspects of that right and were informative in relation to the plight of sexual minorities, they should not be applied blindfolded for deciding Constitutionality of law enacted by Indian legislature - Hence, s. 377 IPC did not suffer from the vice of unconstitutionality and declaration made by HC was legally unsustainable - Impugned order was set aside - However, competent legislature would be free to consider the desirability and propriety of deleting s. 377 IPC from the statute book or amend the same as per the suggestion made by Attorney General - Appeals allowed.
