Raj Kumar Singh @ Raju @ Batya v State Of Rajasthan

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 295; (2013) 5 SCC 722; AIR 2013 SC 3150; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 2240; 2013 CRLJ 3276; JT 2013 (8) SC 462; 2013 (2) RLW 1798; 2013(6) SCALE 635; [2013] 8 S.C.R. 599

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Conjectures

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 201, 302, 376 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 313, 315 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 3 - Murder - Benefit of doubt - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) allegedly committed murder of deceased, who was minor girl and thereby committed offences u/ss. 302, 376 and 201 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellants and sentenced them to undergo RI for 10 years - Aggrieved appellants filed appeals before HC - DB by its impugned judgment dismissed appeals - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contended that circumstances said to have established against appellant did not provide a complete chain that was required to prove the guilt of appellant and that there were material contradictions in depositions of PW1 to 4, who were father, mother, grandmother and grandfather of deceased - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC affirming conviction of appellants was justified - Held, there was no ocular version of incident and entire case of prosecution was based on circumstantial evidence - PW1 to 4 witnesses (family members of deceased) were examined and all 4 witnesses deposed that appellant had approached them and had asked them for payment of a sum of Rs.2,000/-, to bring deceased home, and that circumstance has thus been held to have been proved against him - Statement u/s. 313 of CrPC was recorded to meet requirement of principles of natural justice as it required that an accused might be given an opportunity to furnish explanation of incriminating material which had come against him in trial - However, his statement could not be made a basis for his conviction - His answers to questions put to him u/s. 313 CrPC could not be used to fill up the gaps left by prosecution witnesses in their depositions - In case prosecution's evidence was not found sufficient to sustain conviction of accused, inculpatory part of his statement could not be made the sole basis of his conviction - Statement u/s. 313 of CrPC was not recorded after administering oath to accused -Thus, it could not be treated as an evidence within meaning of s. 3 of 1872 Act, though accused has a right if he chooses to be a witness, and once he makes that option, he could be administered oath and examined as a witness in defence as required u/s. 315 of CrPC - An adverse inference could be taken against accused only and only if incriminating material stood fully established and accused was not able to furnish any explanation for the same - However, accused had a right to remain silent as he could not be forced to become witness against himself - In instant case, there were major contradictions/ improvements/embellishments in deposition of witnesses which could not be ignored when they were examined in correct perspective - Chain of links connecting the appellant with crime appeared inconclusive - Evidence produced by prosecution was very shaky - Circumstantial evidence was completely wanting in that respect - To accept description of evidence collected as flimsy, or no evidence would be too short for convicting appellant for the offence, as many issues/circumstances virtually remained unexplained - Prosecution failed to prove case against appellant beyond reasonable doubt and thus, appellants becomes entitled for benefit of doubt - Impugned conviction and sentences were set aside - Appeals allowed.
