Assistant General Manager, State Bank of India and another v Radhey Shyam Pandey and others

Supreme Court of India

26 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 156; 2015(3) SCALE 39

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Leave Encashment, Option, Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1970, Banking Companies (Acquisition And Transfer Of Undertakings) Act, 1980, State Bank of Patiala (Employees) Pension Regulations, 1995, Entitled to, Bank of Baroda Employees Voluntary Retirement Scheme 2001, State Bank of India Employees Pension and Provident Fund Rules, Bank of Baroda (Employees) Pension Regulations 1995, S.B.I. Voluntary Retirement Scheme, Applicability of general pension rules, Extent of

Summary: (A) Service - State Bank of India Employee's Pension Fund Rules , 1955, r. 22(1)(c ) - State Bank of Patiala (Employees) Pension Regulations, 1995, regn. 28 - Voluntarily Retirement - Pension - Entitled to - Respondent no.1 ex-service man joined as clerk with appellant-Bank - The Indian Banks Association (I.B.A.) after obtaining approval from Govt. of India evolved a Voluntary Retirement Scheme (VRS) and appellant adopted the Scheme with certain modifications, despite it having its own VRS in the existing service conditions meant for its employees to seek voluntary retirement/premature retirement/resignation - Respondent submitted his application seeking voluntary retirement and it was accepted - Further, respondent No. 1 submitted application to withdraw his request for voluntary retirement which was declined by appellant and stated that the date for withdrawal of application had already expired on 15-2-2001- Respondent no.1 claimed for withdrawal of his application for voluntary retirement and grant of pension and leave encashment, which was refused by appellant on 4-7-2001 - Respondent no.1 filed petition before HC , which was allowed - Hence instant appeals - Whether respondents are to be considered for pension benefits under the provisions of r. 22(i)(c) of the alone, as claimed by the appellant Bank.

Held, in cl. (b) of the definition of 'retirement' will also be read in the light of the amended regn. 28 which was intended to provide relief to the employees seeking voluntary retirement under the VRS 2000, after providing 15 years of pensionable service. Thus, employees who availed VRS 2000 have 'retired' from the Bank as per the definitions. From the memorandum of the VRS presented by appellant itself, it is clear that SBI-VRS scheme was introduced for the purpose of business enhancement and profitability of the Bank itself and not for the benefits of the employees per se. Thus, in ordinary situation, an employee who retires either on reaching the age of superannuation, or by request in writing after completing the prescribed number of years, become eligible to pension under the Rules. In absence of SBI-VRS, respondents had the option of seeking voluntary retirement u/r. 22(i)(c) of the Rules which in fact, the respondents did not avail. Instead they availed the SBI-VRS. It is therefore pertinent to see how the SBI-VRS was functioning and what the respondents seeking SBI-VRS might have reasonably foreseen while availing the scheme. If the respondent had chosen to retire by superannuation after attaining 60 years of age which was the normal age of retirement, he would have put in a little more than 20 years of pensionable service. He consequently, would have become eligible to pension. However, when he retired on 31-3-2001, he still had 2 and months short to complete 20 years of service. It is pertinent to understand what prompted him to opt for the SBI-VRS at this stage. Pension on the other hand, is calculated as half month's salary per month. Therefore, by utilizing the SBI-VRS, although the respondent had given up 9 months service still left, he would have gained 40 months incentive. Respondent becomes eligible for pension, then he in addition to ex-gratia, will get half month's salary as his pension from the time he retires. On reasonable presumption, it can be ascertained that it is this benefit provided by the SBI-VRS through ex-gratia payment along with pension which prompted the employees in availing the benefits of the scheme rather than retiring on superannuation under the rules. On the other hand, if he is not entitled to pension, then availing SBI- VRS is unwise since the respondent has given up his half month's salary worth pension for his working period in return of 40 months' salary.

Considering that the incentives of SBI-VRS are distinct from the benefits provided u/r. 22(i)(c) of the Rules cl. 6(c) of SBI-VRS does not specifically state that the pension benefits are to be provided u/r. 22(i)(c) of the Rules, claim for pension by the respondents cannot be decided solely on the basis of the provision of r. 22(1)(c) of the Rules. Appeals disposed of

(B) Service - Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1970 - Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1980 - Employee's Pension Regulations, 1995 -State Bank of India Act, 1955, s. 50 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Applicability of general pension rules - Extent of - Whether SBI is entitled to retain its own employment Rules which is not in consonance with the subsequent amendments made in the Regulations in all the public sector undertaking Banks in the light of the correspondence between Finance Ministry and I.B.A.

Held, it has been claimed by appellant Bank that SBI has its own Pension Rules that are different from the Employees' Pension Regulations 1995 which operate in the other Public Sector Banks. The claim made by the appellant that it is not bound by the Regns is premised on the assumption that the employees of the State Bank of India form a distinct class of employment from the employees of the other Public Sector Banks on the ground of reasonable and intelligible differentia. This conclusion by appellant is not warranted since all the employees of Public Sector Bank forms one homogenous class since all the fourteen Public Sector Banks which were formed under the 1970 Act and the six banks under the 1980 Act are subject to the control of the Central Government. It is pertinent to note that s. 19 of both the 1970 and 1980 Act and s. 50 of the 1955 Act vest the power on Central Government to make consistent rules for all the Public Sector Banks. In the process of implementation of the VRS on its own terms, appellant being an associate Bank of the Indian Banks Organization, it cannot set rules and procedures which deviates from the standard and safeguards set by the Central Government in consensus with the Indian Banks Association. Therefore, depriving the respondents seeking SBI-VRS of their right to pension solely on the ground that they have availed voluntary retirement under a scheme while providing less than 20 years of service and also on ground that they have been provided with handsome ex-gratia amount on their retirement, is arbitrary and attracts the wrath of art. 14 of the Constitution because SBI-VRS was introduced for the benefit of the Public Sector Banks which included the appellant Bank. Therefore, the appellant Bank at this stage, cannot absolve itself from the responsibility of granting the respondents what is due to them by virtue of providing pensionable services, on the pretext of having provided ex-gratia amount. Thus, classification between employees who have voluntarily retired under SBI-VRS and those who have retired under the same scheme introduced by other Public Sector Banks, is not rational since they constitute the employees of the appellant Bank into a distinct class on the basis of the VRS 2000 scheme introduced by the appellant Bank and the same scheme introduced by other Public Sector Banks, with no intelligible differentia. The payment of ex-gratia cannot be held against the employees since it cannot be expected of a person to give up his service before superannuation without reasonable incentives. What the appellant Bank intends to show as the benefit of the employees seeking VRS under the scheme, is actually meant for the benefit of the appellant Bank itself. Appeals disposed of.
