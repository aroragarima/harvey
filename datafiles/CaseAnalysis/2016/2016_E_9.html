Eitzen Bulk A/s and another v Ashapura Minechem Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

13 May 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Sharad Arvind Bobde, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 374; AIR 2016 SC 2438; 2016 (3) AWC 3154; 2016 (2) CLT(SC) 284; 2016 (3) CompLJ 397; JT 2016 (5) SC 186; 2016 (4) MLJ 546; 2016 (3) RCR(Civil) 448; 2016(5) SCALE 514; [2016] 2 S.C.R. 634

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitration Agreement, Jurisdiction, Contract, CONTRACT & COMMERCIAL, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Arbitration, Arbitration Clause, Conciliation, Arbitration Proceedings, Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, English Arbitration Act, 1996, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 34 - Jurisdiction over Foreign Award - Parties entered into Contract of affreightment for shipment of bauxite from India to China and as disputes arose between them, matter was referred to Arbitration - Arbitration was held in London according to English Law and Sole Arbitrator passed award holding Ashapura as liable - Ashapura filed petition u/s.34 of the Act in India, before District Judge, for setting aside of Foreign Award made in London and Eitzen also filed petition for execution of Foreign Award before Bombay HC - Later, in proceedings emanating from such petitions reaching HC, DB of Gujarat held that Ashapura is entitled to challenge Foreign Award u/s. 34 of Part I of the Act and Single Judge of Bombay HC allowed arbitration petition filed by Eitzen for enforcing Foreign Award - Hence, instant appeals challenging orders of HC of Gujarat by Eitzen and Bombay HC by Ashapura - Whether Part I of the Act is excluded from its operation in case of a Foreign Award where the Arbitration is not held in India and is governed by foreign law.

Held, by cl.28, parties chose to exclude application of Part I to Arbitration proceedings between them by choosing London as venue for Arbitration and by making English law applicable to Arbitration. Hence, court in India could not have jurisdiction to entertain objections u/s. 34 in such a case. Further, mere choosing of the juridical seat of Arbitration attracts the law applicable to such location and it would not be necessary to specify which law would apply to Arbitration proceedings, since the law of the particular country would apply ipso jure. Hence, judgment of HC of Gujarat holding that Ashapura's objections u/s. 34 of the Act are tenable before Court in India is contrary to law and the same are dismissed as untenable. Further, appeals of Eitzen are allowed and judgment of Bombay HC dt. 3-12-2015 enforcing Foreign Award under Part II of the Act is upheld. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - Where the parties choose a juridical seat of Arbitration outside India and provide that the law which governs Arbitration will be a law other than Indian law, then part I of the Act would not have any application and the award debtor would not be entitled to challenge the award by raising objections u/s. 34 before a Court in India.
