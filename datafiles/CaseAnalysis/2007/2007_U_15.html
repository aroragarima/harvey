Udai Singh Dagar and Others v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 May 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1212; (2007) 10 SCC 306; AIR 2007 SC 2599; JT 2007 (7) SC 127; 2007(7) SCALE 278; 2007 (5) SCJ 66; [2007] 6 S.C.R. 707

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Maharashtra Veterinary Practitioners Act, 1971, Bombay Veterinary Practitioners Act, 1953, Indian Army Act, 1911, Maharashtra Zilla Parishads and Panchayat Samities Act, 1961, East Punjab Ayurvedic and Unani Practitioners Act, 1949, Punjab Ayurvedic and Unani Practitioners Act, 1963, English Interpretation Act, 1978, Indian Veterinary Practitioners Regulation, 1992, Maharashtra Veterinary Practitioners (Conditions for registration of persons actually conducting veterinary practice) Regulations 1981, Drugs[and Cosmetics] Act, 1940, Pharmacy Act,1948

Summary: Constitution - Freedom to practice profession, carry on occupation, trade or business - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(g) - Indian Veterinary Council Act, 1984, s. 30 - Respondent issued notification specifying minor veterinary services to be rendered by Certificate or Diploma holders in Govt. services or semi Govt. organization - Notification had effect of curtailing right of petitioners to practice - Petitioners filed a petition before High Court on grounds that restriction was violative of art. 19(1)(g) - Petition dismissed by HC - Whether respondent can pass a legislation limiting right to practice of petitioner? - Held, Yes - In matter of laying down of qualification by a statute, restriction imposed as envisaged under cl. (6) of art. 19 of Constitution must be construed in interest of general public - Notification issued is in consonance with mandate laid down in art. 19(6) - Further, provisions contained in s. 30 of Indian Veterinary Council Act constitute a reasonable restriction for art. 19(1)(g) thereof - Laying down essential qualification does not violate fundamental right to practice profession, carry on occupation, trade or business - If a restriction to be imposed by a statute is a reasonable one and is in public interest then same is constitutionally valid - Test of reasonableness wherever prescribed, should be applied to each individual statute impugned, and no abstract standard or general pattern - Impugned notification upheld - Appeal dismissed.
