Centre For Pil & Anr. … v Union Of India & Anr

Supreme Court of India

03 March 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 141; (2011) 4 SCC 1; 2011 (2) ACR 1435; AIR 2011 SC 1267; 2011 (1) AllCriC 133; 2011 (3) AWC 2177; 2011 (266) E.L.T. 6; 2011 (1) JIC 438; JT 2011 (2) SC 613; 2011 (1) KLT 973; 2011 (3) MLJ(SC) 355; 2011 (2) RCR(Criminal) 491; 2011(3) SCALE 148; [2011] 4 S.C.R. 445; 2011 (3) SLR 390

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Practice & Procedure; Service

Keywords: Maharshi Dayanand University Act, 1975, Jammu and Kashmir Shri Mata Vaishno Devi Shrine Act, 1988, Endowments Act, 1966, Prevention of Food Adulteration Rules, 1956

Summary: (A) Service - Constitution - Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, s. 4 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Central Vigilance Commissioner (CVC) - Appointment - Validity - High Powered Committee (HPC), duly constituted under proviso to s. 4(1) of 2003 Act, had recommended name of 2nd respondent for appointment to post of CVC - Petitioner NGO challenged validity of HPC's recommendation in instant writ petition on ground that 2nd respondent was accused in a corruption case - Respondents contended that once Central Vigilance Commission (Commission) clearance had been granted and once candidate stood empanelled for appointment at Centre and in fact stood appointed as Secretary, Parliamentary Affairs and, thereafter, Secretary Telecom, it was legitimate for HPC to proceed on basis that there was no impediment in way of appointment of 2nd respondent on basis of pending case which had been found to be without any substance - Whether relevant material and vital aspects having nexus to object of 2003 Act were taken into account by HPC when decision to recommend 2nd respondent as CVC - Held, appointment to post of CVC should satisfy not only eligibility criteria of candidate but also decision making process of recommendation by HPC as per SC decision in N. Kannadasan v. Ajoy Khose and Others, 2009 Indlaw SC 876 - Decision to recommend had got to be an informed decision keeping in mind fact that Commission as an institution had to perform an important function of vigilance administration - If a statutory body like HPC failed to look into relevant material having nexus to object and purpose of 2003 Act or took into account irrelevant circumstances then its decision would stand vitiated on ground of official arbitrariness - Commission, Department of Personnel and Training (DoPT) and HPC had placed emphasis only on bio-data of empanelled candidates and they had not looked at matter from larger perspective of institutional integrity including institutional competence and functioning of Commission - DoPT, on various dates, noted that penalty proceedings should be initiated against 2nd respondent on number of occasions, however, such notings were not considered in juxtaposition with Commission's clearance for 2nd respondent's appointment - Further, even in bio-data of 2nd respondent, there was no reference to earlier notings of DoPT - HPC's recommendation was entirely premised on blanket clearance given by Commission and on fact of earlier appointments of 2nd respondent as Chief Secretary of a State - Further, in process, HPC had failed to take into consideration pendency of a corruption case against 2nd respondent - HPC, therefore, had failed to consider relevant material keeping in mind purpose and policy of 2003 Act and hence its recommendation was non est in law - Impugned appointment of 2nd respondent as CVC was quashed - Petitions allowed.

(B) Service - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Writ of quo warranto - Issuance of - 2nd respondent contended that instant case was neither a case of infringement of statutory provisions of 2003 Act nor of appointment being contrary to any procedure or rules, therefore, writ of quo warranto could not be issued - Whether writ of quo warranto was issuable in instant case - Held, procedure of quo warranto conferred jurisdiction and authority on judiciary to control executive action in matter of making appointments to public offices against relevant statutory provisions - A writ of quo warranto was issued to prevent a continued exercise of unlawful authority, however, in instant case, a declaratory relief was also sought beside issuance of a writ of quo warranto - Instant writ petition, therefore, was maintainable and writ of quo warranto was issuable - Petitions allowed.

(C) Service - Constitution - Administrative - Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, s. 4 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 74, 77 - Appointment of CVC - Role of President of India - Determination - Whether appointment of 2nd respondent by President of India should be questionable in a Public Interest Litigation since President was not acted on advice of Council of Ministers u/art. 74 of Constitution - Held, CVC was appointed u/s. 4(1) of 2003 Act by President by warrant under her hand and seal after obtaining recommendation of a Committee consisting of Prime Minister as Chairperson and two other Members - Although under 2003 Act CVC was appointed after obtaining recommendation of HPC, such recommendation had got to be accepted by Prime Minister, who was concerned authority u/art. 77(3), and if such recommendation was forwarded to President u/art. 74, then President was bound to act in accordance with advice tendered - Advice tendered to President by Prime Minister regarding appointment of CVC, therefore, would be binding on President - Hence, there was no merit in contention advanced on behalf of 2nd respondent that in matter of appointment of CVC u/s. 4(1) of 2003 Act President was not to act on advice of Council of Ministers as was provided in art. 74 of Constitution - Petitions allowed.

(D) Service - Constitution - Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, s. 4 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 74, 77 - Appointment of CVC - Recommendation of CVC - Nature of - Whether recommendation of HPC under proviso to s. 4(1) was required to be unanimous - Held, 2003 Act was enacted with intention that such HPC would act in a bipartisan manner and should perform its statutory duties keeping in view larger national interest - Each of Members was presumed by legislature to act in public interest and if veto power was given to one of three Members, working of Act would become unworkable - U/s. 4(2) of 2003 Act it had been stipulated that vacancy in Committee should not invalidate appointment, therefore, recommendation u/s. 4 was not required to be unanimous - Petitions allowed.
