Â Harikisan v The State Of Maharasthtra & Others

Supreme Court of India

31 January 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, K. Subba Rao, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 217; AIR 1962 SC 911; 1962 (64) BomLR 522; 1962 CRLJ 797; 1963 (2) MLJ(SC) 82; [1962] Supp2 S.C.R. 918

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 22 - Bombay Condition of Detention Order, 1951 - Detention - Legality - Appellant was detained under the provisions of 1951 Order - Appellant wrote a letter to the District Magistrate that he had been served with an Order of Detention, and that the Order and the grounds of detention being in English, he was unable to understand them, and therefore, asked for a Hindi version of the same so that he may be able to follow and understand the charges levelled against him and take necessary steps for his release from jail - However, District Magistrate did not give the Hindi translation of Detention Order - Appellant challenged the detention before the HC - HC dismissed appellant's writ petition holding that under the Constitution English still continued to be the official language of the respondent/State, and that service of the Order in English upon the detenue was sufficient compliance with the requirements of art. 22 (5) of the Constitution - Hence the present appeal - Respondent/State contended that they had orally explained the ground of detention to the appellant - Whether appellant's detention was vitiated due to non-supply of translated version of Detention Order - Held, HC did not find that the appellant knew enough English to understand the grounds of his detention - Appellant, in fact, did not know enough English to understand the grounds in order to be able effectively to make his representation against the Order of Detention - In order that the detenue should be in a position effectively to make his representation against the Order, he should have knowledge of the grounds of detention, which are in the nature of the charge against him setting out the kinds of prejudicial acts which the authorities attribute to him - Communication, in the context of detention, must, therefore, mean imparting to the detenue sufficient knowledge of all the grounds on which the Order of Detention is based - Any oral translation or explanation given by the police officer serving grounds of detention on the detenue would not amount to communication - A detenue who is not so conversant with the English language, in order to satisfy the requirements of the Constitution, the detenue must be given the grounds in a language which he can understand and in a script which he can read, if he is a literate person - Art. 22 (4) (5) provides that the detained person has the right to be communicated the grounds on which the order of detention has been made against him, in order that he may be able to make his representation against the order of detention - Therefore, appellant, in the present case, did not have the opportunity, which the law contemplates in his favour, making an effective representation against his detention, hence the detention is illegal, and set aside the HC order - Appeal allowed.
