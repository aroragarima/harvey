Mohan Baitha and Others v State of Bihar and Another

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

G.B. Pattanaik, B.N. Agrawal

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 19891; (2001) 4 SCC 350; (2001) SCC (Cr) 710; AIR 2001 SC 1490; 2001 (2) Crimes 83; 2002 (1) CRJ 757; 2001 CRLJ 1738; 2001 (1) JCC 265; JT 2001 (4) SC 27; 2001 (2) RCR(Criminal) 381; 2001(2) SCALE 623; 2001 (3) SLT 112; 2001 (2) Supreme 542; 2001 (2) UC 132; 2001 (1) UC 535

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (as amended by Act No.25 of 2005 & Act No.2 of 2006)

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 177 and 220 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304-B and 34/406 - Territorial jurisdiction -Whether Magistrate in a state will have jurisdiction to try offence occurred in another state? - Held, scope of word 'ordinarily' in s. 177 - Which indicates, provision is a general one and must be read subject to special provisions contained in CrPC, 1973 - This Court has taken view that exceptions implied by word 'ordinarily' need not be limited to those specially provided for by law and exceptions may be provided by law on considerations of convenience or may be implied from other provisions of law permitting joint trial of offences by same court - U/s. 220 of CrPC, 1973, offences more than one committed by same persons could be tried at one trial, if they can be held to be in one series of acts, so as to form same transaction - Expression 'same transaction' from its very nature is incapable of an exact definition - It is not intended to be interpreted in any artificial or technical sense - Common sense and ordinary use of language must decide whether on facts of a particular case, it can be held to be in one transaction - It is not possible to enunciate any comprehensive formula of universal application for purpose of determining whether two or more acts constitute same transaction - But circumstances of a given case indicating proximity of time, unity or proximity of place, continuity of action and community of purpose or design are factors for deciding whether certain acts form parts of same transaction or not - HC was correct in holding that s. 220 was applicable in present case - Appeal dismissed.
