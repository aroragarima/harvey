Ramesh Vithal Patil v State of Karnataka and others

Supreme Court of India

31 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 209; (2014) 11 SCC 516; 2014 (2) JCC 1257; JT 2014 (8) SC 219; 2014 (4) KarLJ 353; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2335; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 498; 2014(4) SCALE 438

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498A, 304B, 306, 34 - Cruelty - Dowry death - Abetment to suicide - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant was married to deceased - Deceased was subjected to cruelty by appellant and other accused - Charge sheet against appellant was filed u/ss. 498A, 304B r/w s. 34 IPC - Trial Court held that prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt and acquitted appellant and other accused persons - On appeal filed by State/respondent, acquittal of appellant for the offence u/s. 304-B IPC was set aside and instead he was convicted for offence punishable u/s. 306 IPC, acquittal of the other accused was confirmed and HC partly allowed the appeal - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in convicting appellant -

Held, prosecution led evidence to establish cruelty or harassment caused to the deceased, which was rightly taken into account by HC - Thus, the foundation for the presumption existed - Appellant, however, has led no evidence to rebut the presumption - Therefore, it could be safely concluded that appellant abetted the suicide of deceased - Trial Court erred in giving undue importance to trivial matters - Trial Court missed the core of the prosecution case which was established by straightforward and honest evidence of the brothers of deceased - Trial Court should have seen that when a woman was harassed and ill-treated in her matrimonial house, it was not possible to get independent witnesses to depose about the harassment - No doubt, the brothers of deceased were interested witnesses - It was, therefore, necessary to scrutinize their evidence carefully - Trial Court should have taken note of the callous and indifferent attitude of appellant - It was not the case of the defence that the death was accidental - When a married woman jumps in a river along with her small child that too within seven years of marriage and when the prosecution leads reliable evidence to establish harassment caused to her in her matrimonial house in connection with demand of money for her husband's business and the accused-husband leads no evidence to prove to the contrary the logical and legal conclusion that must follow was that she committed suicide and her suicide was abetted by her husband - Trial Court's order acquitting appellant was replete with gross errors of facts resulting in miscarriage of justice - HC had rightly held that the other members of appellant's family could be given benefit of doubt, but appellant could not escape the liability - Impugned order of HC was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
