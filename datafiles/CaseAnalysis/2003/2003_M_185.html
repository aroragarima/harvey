Secretary, Ministry of Chemicals & Fertilizers v Messrs Cipla Limited and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 August 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Venkatarama Reddi, S. Rajendra Babu, Arun Kumar

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 570; (2003) 7 SCC 1; AIR 2003 SC 3078; JT 2003 (6) SC 414; 2003(5) SCALE 654; 2003 (7) SLT 470; [2003] Supp2 S.C.R. 177; 2003 (5) Supreme 348

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Socio-Economic

Keywords: Drugs (Price Control) Order, 1995

Summary: [A.] Administrative - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 14, 226 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955, s. 3 - Drugs And Cosmetics Act, 1940 - Drugs (Price Control) Order, 1995, s. 2(a) - Inclusion of certain bulk drugs in First Schedule of Order - Held, where Central Govt. as delegate of legislative power announces a rational policy in keeping with purposes of enabling legislation and even lays down specific criteria to promote policy, criteria so evolved become guide-posts of its legislative action - While classifying drugs for purpose of price control, it is not open to Govt. to flout or debilitate set norms, which it professed to follow in interest of transparency and objectivity - Otherwise, there will be an element of arbitrariness and delegated legislation will not withstand test of art 14 - Expression 'turnover' in Drug Policy, 1994 represents sale value of bulk drug sold as such or in form of formulations - Export sales should not be taken into account while computing turnover - Sum total of production and imports of bulk drug cannot be equated to turnover, though they are not altogether irrelevant in calculating turnover - ORG data does not give exhaustive account of turn over of bulk drug It may furnish basis for estimating turnover, but is not sole guide - Burden lies on those who challenge legislation on ground of violation of art 14 to make out their case by furnishing all relevant material which is within their reach and knowledge - There should be frank disclosure of material facts, more so, when plea is founded on certain factual aspects mere vagueness or lack of clarity in stand taken by Union of India does not by itself advance case of petitioners.
