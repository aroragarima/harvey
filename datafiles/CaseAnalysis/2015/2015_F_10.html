Poona Employees Union v Force Motors Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

01 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 808; (2016) 2 SCC 531; 2016(1) ALL MR 978; 2016 (1) AWC 388; 2016 (1) Bom.C.R. 1; 2016 (148) FLR 217; JT 2015 (11) SC 402; 2016 (1) LLJ 1; [2015] 11 S.C.R. 935; 2016 (1) SLR 751

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Verification, Deduction, Recognition, Trade Unions Act, 1926, Membership, Bombay Trade Unions Regulations, 1927, Bombay Trade Unions Regulation, 1947

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Unions and Prevention of Unfair Labour Practices Act, 1971 - Recognition of Trade Union - Determination of majority members - Respondent no.2-union (BKS) was recognized union of company and while so, appellant union filed application before Industrial Court for adjudging as recognized union in place of BKS - However, company and BKS resisted said application and Industrial Court in course of adjudication entrusted IO to submit report after verifying membership of both unions - Later, Industrial Court returned finding that appellant union was entitled to be adjudged eligible to be conferred the status of recognized union and issued direction to that effect - Aggrieved, company and respondent no. 2 filed writ petitions separately before HC and HC held that decision of Industrial Court was flawed and untenable and reversed findings of Industrial Court - Hence, instant appeals by appellant union - Whether decision of HC is sustainable.

Held, though it had been undertaken by appellant union that if permitted to file its affidavits, the same would not be utilized to decide the issues of membership and was endorsed as well by Industrial Court, yet, decision of Industrial Court clearly reveals that the contents of the affidavits not only had been taken note of by it but also relied upon along with other materials on record, to eventually hold that the appellant union held in its ranks, the majority membership of the employees of the undertaking. To such extent, approach of Industrial Court in deciding issue of membership is not sustainable being in derogation of the letter, spirit and objectives of procedure prescribed by the Act to determine the issue of majority of membership for the purpose of identifying the recognized union of an industrial establishment. Further, dehors the affidavits, the evidence or the materials laid by the appellant union are not overwhelmingly determinative of its claim of majority membership as required u/ss.11, 12 and 14 of the Act. Further, view taken by HC is plausible and rational being based on a logical analysis of the materials on record and the law applicable does not merit any interference. Appeals dismissed.
