D. Cawasji and Company v State of Mysore and Others

Supreme Court of India

26 September 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

Amarendra Nath Sen, P.N. Bhagwati, Ranganath Misra

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 294; (1984) Supp SCC 490; AIR 1984 SC 1780; 1985 (19) E.L.T. 5; [1984] 150 ITR 648; 1984(2) SCALE 545; [1985] 1 S.C.R. 825; 1985 (58) STC 1; 1985 UJ 167

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Sale Price, Rate Of Tax, Rate, Collected

Summary: Sales Tax & Indirect Tax - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, s. 19 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 133 (1) - Whether Mysore Sales Tax (Amendment) Act, 1969 (Mysore Act of 1969), was constitutional? - Held, the State instead of remedying the defect or removing the lacuna has by the impugned amendment sought to raise the rate of tax from 61/2% to 45% with retrospective effect from the 1-04-1966 to avoid the liability of refunding the excess amount collected and has further purported to nullify the judgment and order passed by the High Court directing the refund of the excess amount illegally collected by providing that the levy at the higher rate of 45% will have retrospective effect from 1-04-1966 - The judgment of the HC declaring the levy of sales tax on Excise Duty, education cess and health cess to be bad become conclusive and is binding on the parties - It may or may not have been competent for the State Legislature to validly remove the lacuna and remedy the defect in the earlier levy by seeking to impose sales tax through any amendment on excise duty, education cess and health cess, but, in any event, the State Govt. has not purported to do so through the Amending Act - Court declare s. 2 of amendment Act to extent that it imposes higher levy of 45% with retrospective effect from 1-04-1966 and s. 3 of impugned Act seeking to nullify judgment and order of HC are invalid and unconstitutional - Set aside the judgment and order of the HC to the extent it upholds the validity of the impugned amendment with retrospective effect from 1-04-1966 - Appeals allowed.
