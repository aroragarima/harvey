M/S. Som Datt Builders Ltd. v Union Of India And Ors

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1517; (2010) 1 SCC 311; 2010 (1) ADJ 316; 2010 (1) AWC 1039; JT 2009 (14) SC 344; 2009 (4) KLT 638; 2010 (1) MLJ(SC) 107; 2009 (108) RD 728; 2009(13) SCALE 642; [2009] 15 S.C.R. 772; 2010 (1) WLN(SC) 9

Case Digest

Subject: Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Uttar Pradesh Minor Minerals (Concession) Rules, 1963, Waterworks Clauses Act, 1847, Railways Clauses Consolidation (Scotland) Act, 1845

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, s. 3(e) - Ordinary earth - Nature of - Whether 'ordinary earth' used for filling or levelling purposes in the construction of embankments, roads, railways, buildings has validly been declared to be a 'minor mineral' by the Central Government vide notification dated February 3, 2000 issued u/s. 3(e) of Act - Held, 'ordinary earth' is to be comprehended as per the meaning of the word 'any other mineral' - If the expression 'minor mineral' as defined in s. 3(e) of the Act includes 'ordinary clay' and 'ordinary sand', there is no reason why 'ordinary earth' should not fall within the meaning of the word 'any other mineral' - Once the 'ordinary earth' is found to be contained within the meaning of the word 'any other mineral' for the purposes of s. 3(e) of the Act, there is no impediment for the Central Government to include or exclude the same based on a particular use or purpose - Nature of use can be a valid reason for exclusion as well as inclusion in declaring mineral, 'minor mineral' in exercise of the powers conferred upon the Central Government u/s. 3(e) of the Act and exercise of any such power cannot be said to be arbitrary - Therefore, declaration of the 'ordinary earth' for the uses and purposes mentioned in the notification dated February 3, 2000 is not ultra vires the power conferred upon the Central Government - Appeal dismissed.
