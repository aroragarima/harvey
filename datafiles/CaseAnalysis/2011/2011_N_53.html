Noorul Huda Maqbool Ahmed v Ram Deo Tyagi and others

Supreme Court of India

04 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 819; 2012 (2) MLJ(Crl) 268; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 550

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Civil Service Rules

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302,307, r/w s. 34 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s.227 - Common object - Few shots were fired from building of bakery - Joint Commissioner of police respondent no.1 came to spot along with team called Special Operations Squads (SOS) - Persons in bakery were not deterred by presence of joint Commissioner or members of SOS and continued to pelt bottles, acid bulbs and stones towards police - Therefore, Joint Commissioner ordered squad to enter bakery - Door of bakery was bolted therefore respondent no.1, directed police force to break open door of bakery and to arrest miscreants - Door was broken and members of SOS team entered in bakery but in process they had to resort to firing due to which 12 persons inside got injured and 8 persons succumbed to death - State lodged complaint against 18 police personnel for offences punishable u/ss. 302 and 307 r/w S. 34 of IPC - Sessions Case was lodged in which 18 accused persons moved application for discharged u/s. 227 of CrPC - Sessions Judge discharged accused persons named and dismissed application of rest of accused persons - Revision filed by accused - HC dismissed revision - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether members of SOS team and respondent no.1 had common object - Held, there was no question of there being unlawful assembly and any act having been committed by respondent in pursuance of that common object - However, respondents were concerned not firing single bullet would certainly take them out of prosecution area that they could not be discharged - However, missiles were being thrown at police and API was injured and there was material to support that in that situation when after breaking of doors police men entered and yet some of policemen did not fire bullets, they could not be clothed with common intention - Thus, trial Court as well as revisional Court had taken view that there could be no common intention shared on part who did not even fire single bullet was right - Thus, there was no merit in appeal - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Unlawful assembly - Whether members of SOS team could be called unlawful assembly - Held, there could be no dispute that they were all members of SOS and had duty to quell riots -They were not doing anything illegal in coming out and trying to control riots - There was also no dispute that riots were undoubtedly going on - There was no reason for Trial Court and revisional court and to believe that SOS squad came on its own without there being any apprehension of further troubles - However, those apprehensions were apparent enough in wireless message on which Trial Court rightly relied on - Therefore, there was no point in holding that SOS itself was an unlawful assembly - Appeal dismissed.
