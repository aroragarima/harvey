Charles Sobraj v Supdt. Central Jail, Tihar, New Delhi

Supreme Court of India

31 August 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, D.A. Desai, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 213; (1978) 4 SCC 104; (1978) SCC (Cr) 542; AIR 1978 SC 1514; 1978 CRLJ 1534; 1978 CrLR(SC) 497; [1979] 1 S.C.R. 512

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure; Tort

Summary: Constitution - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 32, 21, 14 and 19 - Violation of fundamental rights in prison - Petitioner foreigner - (A) Whether court has jurisdiction to decide prisoners' charges of violation of rights? - Held, imprisonment does not spell farewell to fundamental rights although, by a realistic re-appraisal, courts will refuse to recognise full panoply of Part III enjoyed by a free citizen - Art. 21, r/w art. 19(1)(d) and (5) of Constitution is capable of wider application than imperial mischief which gave it birth and must draw its meaning from evolving standards of decency and dignity that mark progress of a mature society - Next axiom of prison justice is court's continuing duty and authority to ensure that judicial warrant which deprives a person of his life or liberty is not exceeded, subverted or stultified -(B) Whether courts can interfere with prison administration and its problems of security and discipline from an 'innocent' distance? - Held, where a prison practice or internal instruction places harsh restrictions on jail life, breaching guaranteed rights, court directly comes in - Judicial policing of prison practices is implied in sentencing power - (C) Whether there has been any legal injury to sobraj? - Courts cannot be critical of Administration if it makes a classification between dangerous prisoners and ordinary prisoners - In present case, Superintendent swears, and it is undisputed, that petitioner is not under solitary confinement - We further aver that a distinction between under-trials and convicts is reasonable and petitioner is now a convict - Held, Sobraj has done litigative service for prison reform but he has signally failed to substantiate any legal injury -(D) Whether petitioner is entitled to rights u/art. 19 of constitution? - Further held, strictly speaking petitioner being a foreigner cannot claim rights u/art. 19 of constitution - Petition dismissed.
