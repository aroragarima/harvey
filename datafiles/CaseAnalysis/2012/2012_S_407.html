Surajit Sarkar v State Of West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

04 December 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 621; (2013) 2 SCC 146; 2013 CRLJ 1137; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 409; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 634

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s.154 - Conviction for murder - Telephonic conversation - Treatment as FIR - Deceased was murdered and daughter of deceased wrote in complaint - Trial Judge held that there was sufficient evidence that accused had assaulted deceased with iron rod and had caused severe injuries on his head and prosecution had successfully proved beyond all reasonable doubt that accused had murdered and was held punishable for offence of murder - HC upheld conviction of accused - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether cryptic telephonic intimation given to police could be described as First Information Report for purposes of s. 154 of CrPC - Held, bare reading of s. 154 made it clear that even though oral information given by officer in charge of police station could be treated as FIR, yet some procedural formalities were required to be completed and they included reducing information in writing and reading it over to informant and obtaining his or her signature on transcribed information - However, in case of telephonic conversation received from unknown person, question of reading over that information to anonymous informant did not arise nor did appending of signature to information, as recorded, arise - Thus, cryptic telephonic information could not be treated as FIR - Appeal disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Conviction for murder - Testimony of witnesses - Whether testimony of PW7 and PW8 could be accepted for upholding conviction of accused/ appellant - Held, conduct of PW7 was quite unnatural and little odd and ought to be looked into by police - PW7 was neighbour of victim and it appeared from his testimony that after he witnessed attack on deceased, he did not bother to inform victim's family, or anybody else and simply went home and deposed that he came to know of death of deceased only on next morning - PW7 was examined by investigating officer more than month and half after occurrence; that presence of witness was not mentioned by PW8 who was also eyewitness to incident - Thus there was doubt about presence of PW7 at place of occurrence - Further, merely because PW8 and his family acted little strangely would not necessarily lead to conclusion that this witness should not be believed - There was nothing on record to suggest that he was not at place of occurrence when his father was attacked - There was also nothing on record which could lead to any inference or conclusion that PW8 made up story about attack on his father by accused - There was some discrepancy or some gap in time of attack and his returning home but that by itself was not enough to discredit witness, more so when he was not asked any question on his whereabouts - Thus, PW8 had successfully withstood his cross-examination and Trial Court and HC ought to have believed that he was credible witness - Appeal disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Conviction for murder - Acquittal of co-accused - Benefit of doubt - Whether appellant/accused could be convicted of murder even though his co-accused was acquitted by giving benefit of doubt - Held, appellant could not be absolved of his involvement in death of deceased merely because other accused persons were either not identified by eyewitnesses or had no role to play in attack - There was cogent and reliable evidence of PW8 to hold that appellant attacked deceased which ultimately resulted in his death - However, appellant submitted that there was delay in forwarding FIR to concerned Magistrate - Since no foundation was laid for that contention nor was contention urged either before trial Court or before HC - Thus, there was no reason to entertain that point - Appeal disposed of.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.304 - Conviction for murder - Offence of culpable homicide - Whether appellants intended to murder deceased or was it case of culpable homicide not amounting to murder - Held, given nature of injuries, it was difficult to accept view that appellant intended to cause death of deceased or that injuries were so imminently dangerous that they would, in all probability, cause death - Thus, murder of deceased would be ruled out - Nevertheless, injuries were quite serious and inflicted by appellant on deceased's head with iron rod, as stated by PW8 - Hence, appellant was held guilty of offence of culpable homicide not amounting to murder and punished under second part of s. 304 of IPC - Appeal disposed of.
