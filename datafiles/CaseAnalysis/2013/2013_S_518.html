Guiram Mondal v State of West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

26 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 267; (2013) 15 SCC 284; 2013 (3) CGLJ 88; 2013 (3) JCC 1792; JT 2013 (8) SC 510; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 69; 2013(6) SCALE 388; [2013] 2 S.C.R. 1107

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 323, 302 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25, 27 - Unlawful assembly - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused no. 10) allegedly committed offences u/ss. 147, 148, 149, 323, 302 of IPC and u/ss. 25/27 of the Act along with other co-accused - Trial by its judgment acquitted appellants and other accused persons except accused no. 3 - Aggrieved respondent (State) filed an appeal before HC - HC by impugned judgment reversed the judgment of Trial Court after finding appellant guilty u/s. 302 r/w 148 of IPC and sentenced of him imprisonment for life - Whether HC was justified in reversing the judgment passed by Trial Court and convicting appellant - Held, evidence of eye-witnesses (PW2, 3, 4, 8 and 11) and their versions corroborate fully version of PW1 (first informant and eye-witness), related to incident of assault and murder of both deceased persons - Specific part played by accused persons, including appellant, was narrated by eye-witnesses - PW2 had deposed that on the date of incident he was in cow-shed and as soon as he heard a hue and cry, he came out and found that some persons, including appellant, forcibly taking away one of the deceased from his house, who took shelter in the house of PW2 - HC after examining oral and documentary evidence came to conclusion that Trial Court was completely in error by over-looking some crucial and important evidence and placed much reliance on non-mention of name of accused persons in inquest report - HC correctly applied the legal principle that non-mention of name of few accused persons in inquest report was of no consequence - There was nothing to show that FIR was anti dated, anti-timed or fabricated - Merely because FIR was placed before Magistrate, three days after registration of FIR, it could not be said that FIR was anti-timed, anti-dated and fabricated - In fact, no question was put to IO as to the cause of delay in sending FIR to Magistrate - Considering totality of evidence and circumstances of case, HC rightly reversed judgment of Trial Court - Appeal dismissed.
