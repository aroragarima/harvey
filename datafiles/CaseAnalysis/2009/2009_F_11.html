Fomento Resorts And Hotels Ltd. And Another v Minguel Martins And Others

Supreme Court of India

20 January 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, B.N. Agrawal

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 2181; 2009 (4) Bom.C.R. 348; 2010 (1) CLT(SC) 36; JT 2009 (1) SC 470; 2009(1) SCALE 758; [2009] 3 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: River, Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974, H.P. Village Common Lands Vesting and Utilization Act, 1973, Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1961

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 5A, 6 - Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974, ss. 44, 49 - Land acquisition - General purpose - Original title holder sold a tract of land to 2nd appellant who in turn leased the same to 1st appellant/Company - 1st appellant submitted a plan to the concerned Panchayat for constructing a hotel in the land - Chief Town Planner approved the plan with a condition that right of public to access the nearby beach must be maintained by providing necessary footpath - 1st appellant sought to shift the location of the footpath and parking area - Panchayat sanctioned the change/shift without getting any confirmation from Chief Town Planner - 1st appellant, subsequently, sought to construct another hotel complex in the suit property by highlighting its benefit to the State - State Government issued notification u/s. 4(1) of 1894 Act for acquisition of suit property for 1st appellant's hotel complex - State Government, consequently, conducted enquiry u/s. 5A and issued declaration u/s. 6 of the Act - Land owners challenged the acquisition before the HC - HC allowed landowners writ petitions and quashed the notification u/s. 4(1) of the Act - 1st appellant filed an appeal before the SC - SC remanded the matter back to HC for fresh consideration, however, in the meantime, parties entered into a compromise and consequently, landowners withdrew their writ petitions - 1st appellant entered into an agreement with the State Government - 1st appellant sought extension of its existing hotel structure to the suit property (acquired land), which was expressly against the provisions contained in cl. 4(viii) of the agreement - Development Authority granted permission for such extension u/s. 44(3)(c) r/w s. 49(2) of 1974 Act - 1st appellant, subsequently, made another application u/s. 46 r/w s. 44 of the 1974 Act for renewal of the permission granted for extension with a deviation from the permission already granted - Town and Country Planning Board sanctioned the deviation and forwarded the decision to State Government for further approval - However, without even waiting for consideration by the competent body, 1st appellant started construction by deviating from the approved plan - Respondents filed a writ petitions against the illegal construction - HC allowed the writ petitions and ordered the demolition of constructions made by the appellants - Hence the instant appeal - Whether the land in question was acquired u/s. 40(1)(aa) or it was an acquisition u/s. 40(1)(b) of the 1894 Act - Held, a particular acquisition can be treated to have been made u/s. 40(1)(aa) if it is for construction of some building or work for a company which is engaged or is likely to engage itself in any industry or work which may not necessarily be useful to the public in general, however, usefulness of the construction of some work to the general public is sine qua non for acquisition u/s. 40(1)(b) - Notification issued by the State Government u/s. 4(1) showed that the land was needed for a public purpose, namely, the tourism development project, i.e., construction of hotel at suit property - 1st appellant was engaged in executing a project of tourism development, i.e., construction of hotel along with amenities like Yoga Centre, health club and water sports facilities, therefore, acquisition of land was clearly relatable to its project - Further, language of the agreement entered into between appellants and the State Government revealed that the land was needed for the purpose of executing tourism development project of 1st appellant - Cl. 4(ii) of the agreement showed that 1st appellant was required to undertake the work of creation of sports and recreational facilities/ amenities within one year of getting possession and complete the same within three years and which was certainly ancillary to the tourism development project being executed by 1st appellant - Therefore, there is no escape from the conclusion that the acquisition was u/s. 40(1)(aa) of the 1894 Act and the contrary finding recorded by the HC is legally unsustainable - Hence, acquisition of land for tourism development project is certainly for a public purpose - Appeals dismissed.(B) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 5A, 6 - Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974 - Public access - Acquisition - Whether any public access was available to the beach through acquired land before its acquisition by the State Government and whether in terms of cl. 4(ix) of the agreement, 1st appellant is required to maintain the said access/road to the beach, without any obstruction - Held, appellants did not plead before the HC nor did argue on their behalf that the admissions contained in the written statement filed in an earlier suit about existence of access to the beach through acquired land were made under a bona fide mistake - Therefore, HC cannot be said to have erred in relying upon the admissions made in the written statement of appellant in the earlier suit that there existed access to the beach through acquired land before its acquisition by the State Government - Therefore, the alternative road, parking and public footpath provided by 1st appellant in lieu of the access available through acquired land cannot be made basis for depriving members of the public of their age old right to go to the beach through acquired land - State cannot transfer public trust properties to a private party, if such a transfer interferes with the right of the public and the Court can invoke the public trust doctrine and take affirmative action for protecting the right of people to have access to light, air and water and also for protecting rivers, sea, tanks, trees, forests and associated natural eco-systems - Therefore, cl. 4(ix) of the agreement is binding on the appellants and 1st appellant is under a statutory obligation to maintain access/road to the beach through acquired land without any obstruction of any kind and the HC did not commit any error by issuing a mandamus in that regard - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 5A, 6 - Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974 - Public access - Acquisition - Effect - Whether public access to the beach through acquired lands stood extinguished with the vesting of land in the State Government u/s. 16 of the 1894 Act - Held, despite hand over the possession of land u/s. 16 of the 1894 Act, the parties had consciously decided to protect the traditional right of the members of public to go to the beach by using the existing pathway through the acquired land - Both, the appellants and State functionaries knew that there exist public access to the beach through acquired land, that members of public were using the same since time immemorial and that it was necessary to protect that right - Therefore, it is not possible to find any fault with the view taken by the HC that access to the beach is not an encumbrance and in any case, the traditional pathway available to the public for going to the beach through acquired land cannot be treated as having been extinguished in the face of specific provision contained in the agreement which is statutory in character - When the State volunteered to take possession of the land subject to the right of the members of public to access the beach through the acquired land and a specific provision to that effect was incorporated in the agreement executed u/s. 41 (5), s. 16 of the 1894 Act cannot be invoked for nullifying the right of the public to access the beach through acquired land -Appeals dismissed.

(D) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 5A, 6, 42 - Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974, ss. 44, 49 - Land acquisition - Purpose - Whether construction of hotel building on a portion of acquired land is contrary to the purpose of acquisition and is violative of the prohibition contained in cl. 4(viii) of agreement and the HC rightly directed demolition thereof in accordance with cl. 6 of the agreement - Held, a conjoint reading of various clauses in agreement unmistakably showed that 1st appellant was to use the acquired land only in furtherance of and for the purpose for which it was acquired, namely, creation of sports and other recreational facilities/amenities and to maintain the same in good order and condition and was not to use the land for any other purpose - Management of 1st appellant was very much aware of the embargo contained in first part of cl. 4(viii) against construction of any building or structure on the acquired land and that was the reason why in the application made by 1st appellant to the Development Authority u/s. 44(1) r/w s. 49 of the 1974 Act for grant of permission for extension of the existing hotel building, survey number of acquired land was not mentioned - Town and Country Planning Board considered and approved extension/deviation albeit in violation of the negative covenant contained in first part of cl. 4(viii) of the statutory agreement - While doing that, the Town and Country Planning Board was fully cognizant of the fact that in view of cl. 4(viii), 1st appellant cannot use the land for constructing any structure - Neither the State Government nor the Town and Country Planning Board could allow extension of the hotel building on the acquired land in violation of first part of cl. 4(viii) of agreement which has the force of law by virtue of s. 42 of the 1894 Act - Further, no provisions of the 1974 Act empowers the Town and Country Planning Board and/or the Development Authority to modify, amend, alter or change an agreement entered into as per the requirement of s. 41 of the 1894 Act or allow violation thereof by the 1st appellant - Therefore, the decision taken by the Town and Country Planning Board and order issued by the Development Authority granting permission for extension/deviation were non est and the HC rightly did not give any credence to those decisions while adjudicating the issue relating to legality of construction made on acquired lands - Therefore, HC did not commit any error by declaring that extension of the hotel building on acquired land is illegal and directed its demolition after following the procedure prescribed u/cl. 6 of the agreement - Appeals dismissed.

(E) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 5A, 6 - Goa, Daman & Diu Town and Country Planning Act, 1974 - Whether denial of the facilities and amenities created by 1st appellant in acquired land to the members of public is contrary to the purpose of acquisition and is also violative of the agreement and this could be made a ground for resumption of the acquisition of land - Held, a reading of application made by 1st appellant made it clear that it had no intention of making available the facilities of yoga centre, health club and amenities like water sports to the general public - Rather in paragraph 6 of its application, 1st appellant made it clear that the facilities provided by the hotel would be open for use by non-residents also on membership basis - Agreement is totally silent on the issue of making the facilities created by the appellants open for public use without permission and payment of fees - Therefore, the facilities and amenities created by the appellant cannot be made available to the general public free of costs nor the denial of the facilities and amenities created by 1st appellant in acquired land to the members of public is contrary to the purpose of acquisition - Appeals dismissed.
