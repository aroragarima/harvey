M.S. Narayana Menon @ Mani v State Of Kerala & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

04 July 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1308; (2006) 6 SCC 39; AIR 2006 SC 3366; 2007 (1) ALT 103; 2006 (3) BC 433; 2006 CRLJ 4607; 2006 (2) JCC (NI) 198; JT 2006 (6) SC 72; 2006 (3) RCR(Criminal) 504; 2006(6) SCALE 393; [2006] Supp3 S.C.R. 124; 2006 (2) UC 1289

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Punjab Land Revenue Act

Summary: Banking & Finance - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instrument Act, 1881, ss. 138, 139 & 118(a) - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 3 & 4 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 499 - Dishonour of Cheque - Conviction - Sustainability - Respondent filed complaint against appellant for dishonor of cheque u/s. 138 of the 1881, Act, towards amount due to respondent as commission - Trial Court convicted accused/appellant and sentenced to 1 year RI - Appellant filed appeal and Appellate Court set aside judgment of Trial Court - Respondent/complainant filed appeal before HC, which was allowed - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that Trial Court and HC misconstrued and misinterpreted s. 139 of the 1881 Act - Furthermore failed to take into consideration principle of law that once accused discharges initial burden placed on him, burden of proof would revert back to prosecution - Whether order of Trial Court and HC was sustainable - Held, standard of proof evidently was pre-ponderance of probabilities - Inference of pre-ponderance of probabilities could be drawn not only from materials on records but also by reference to circumstance upon which he relies - Presumption drawn under statue had only evidentiary value - Presumption drawn in one respect of fact might be evidence even for purpose of drawing presumption under another - In instant case, appellant had issue blank cheque, as in business practice some security was always asked for in stock exchange transactions - Courts of law might also take judicial note of practice prevailing in such business , which Appellate Court had rightly did so - Further, onus on accused was not as heavy as that of prosecution - It might be compared with a defendant in a civil proceeding - While exercising appellate power against a judgment of acquittal, HC should had borne in mind well-settled principles of law that where two views were possible, Appellate court should not interfere with finding of acquittal recorded by court below - Hence, impugned judgment of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
