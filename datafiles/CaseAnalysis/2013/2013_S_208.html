State Of Maharashtra v Fazal Rehman Abdul

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 166; JT 2013 (5) SC 479; 2013(4) SCALE 401

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Visa, Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, 1987, Quran, Job

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 3(3), 3(2)(i)(ii), 3(3), (4), 5 and 6 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 3 and 7 r/w ss. 25(1-A), (1-B)(a) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 307, 326, 324, 427, 435, 436, 201 and 212 - Explosives Act, 1884, s. 9B(1)(a)(b)(c) - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms - Acquittal - Sustainability - Respondent/accused(A-76) was charged u/s. 3(3) of TADA and ss. 120-B IPC r/w ss. 3(2)(i)(ii), 3(3), (4), 5 and 6 of TADA r/w ss. 302, 307, 326, 324, 427, 435, 436, 201 and 212 IPC and offences u/ss. 3 and 7 r/w ss. 25 (I-A), (I-B)(a) of the 1959 Act, ss. 9-B (1)(a)(b)(c) of the 1884 Act, ss. 3, 4(a)(b), 5 and 6 of the 1908 Act, and s. 4 of the 1984 Act, for persuading A-39 in weapons' training in Pakistan and keeping in his possession arms and ammunition brought to him by A-39 and for handing over same to A-93-dead, showing that same had been smuggled into India for committing terrorist activities - Designated Trial Court acquitted respondent - Hence, instant appeal by State - Whether conviction recorded by Trial Court was sustainable - Held, Designated Court after appreciating entire evidence came to conclusion that there was nothing on record to show that respondent though facilitated sending A-39 to Dubai, had any knowledge of purpose of going to Dubai or Pakistan for simple reason that A-39 himself disclosed that he was told in Dubai that he would go to Pakistan and purpose for going there would be explained to him later on - However, confessional statement of A-39 did not reveal involvement of respondent in persuading A-39 to undergo weapons' training in Pakistan - Appellate Court should bear in mind presumption of innocence of accused and further that Trial Court's acquittal bolsters presumption of his innocence - Interference in a routine manner where other view was possible should be avoided, unless there were good reasons for interference - Thus, finding might be said to be perverse if it was 'against the weight of evidence', or if finding so outrageously defies logic as to suffer from vice of irrationality - Hence, no reason to interfere with cogent reasons given by Special Judge - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Criminal conspiracy - Intercepted contraband - Bribe - Acquittal - Validity - Whether acquittal of respondents/constables against alleged charges was justifiable - Held, none of witnesses had named either of respondents - However, respondents were acquitted on ground that in absence of Test Identification Parade or their identification by any of witnesses/accused in Court, it was not safe to make a guess work that they or either of them could also be member(s) of said police team which intercepted contraband - Statements made by Sarpanchas that none of respondents had visited their village on patrolling could not be a proof that respondents were members of team, which intercepted said trucks - Hence, no reason to interfere with cogent reasons given by Designated Court - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms and explosives - Acquittal - Sustainability - Whether acquittal of respondent (A-12) against alleged charges was justifiable - Held, only allegation against respondent was that she had accompanied her husband (A-71) while he carried arms, ammunition and explosives - Further, there was nothing on record to show that A-71 had any knowledge of such arms, and purpose for which same had been brought - However, sister-in-law of respondent was neither made accused nor a witness and her husband was still absconding - Thus, findings recorded by Designated Court did not warrant any interference - Appeal disposed of.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms and explosives - Acquittal - Sustainability - Whether acquittal of respondent (A-24) against alleged charges was justifiable - Held, A-24 was aware of arms and ammunition being landed - Further, contraband used in Bombay Blast, was same as had been landed by him and other co-accused at (A-15, A-50, A- 64, A-62, A-91, A-29 and A-58) had corroborated knowledge of respondent and fact that he was present when landing was taking place - Hence, involvement and participation of A-24 was throughout main conspiracy - Therefore, conspiracy stood proved - Thus, order of Designated Court acquitting him on charge of larger conspiracy was perverse, in view of evidence on record and was set aside and sentence was enhanced to life imprisonment - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Terrorist activities - Fictitious passport - Conspiratorial meeting - Acquittal - Sustainability - Whether acquittal of respondent (A-75) against alleged charges was justifiable - Held, Special Judge recorded finding that respondent did not do anything to further object of conspiracy - However, respondent received co-accused at airport and attended 7 conspiratorial meetings held in Dubai and had a fictitious passport for a particular purpose - However, Designated Court did not convict him on charge of conspiracy and finding being perverse, was set aside - Respondent was convicted and awarded life imprisonment - Appeals disposed of.

(F) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 - Arms Act, 1959 - Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Terrorist activities - Possession of illegal arms and explosives - Acquittal - Sustainability - Whether acquittal of respondent(A-94) against alleged charges was justifiable- Held, in view of fact that respondent (A-94) had gone to Pakistan and took training in handling arms, ammunition and explosives and also attended conspiratorial meeting at Dubai and took oath in name of Quran not to divulge any information regarding conspiracy, it was abundantly clear that respondent was aware of purpose of training in Pakistan and he undertook training there without any protest - Special Judge committed an error in not convicting respondent for larger conspiracy - Therefore, respondent was directed to surrender before Designated Court within a period of 4 weeks to serve out remaining sentence - Appeals disposed of.
