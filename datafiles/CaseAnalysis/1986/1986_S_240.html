A.K. Roy and Another v State of Punjab and Others

Supreme Court of India

29 September 1986

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Sen, B.C. Ray

Where Reported

1986 Indlaw SC 202; (1986) 4 SCC 326; (1986) SCC (Cr) 443; AIR 1986 SC 2160; 1986 CRLJ 2037; 1986 CrLR(SC) 456; 1986 (3) FAC 66; JT 1986 SC 566; 1986(2) SCALE 566; [1986] 3 S.C.R. 961

Case Digest

Subject: Socio-Economic

Keywords: Prevention of Food Adulteration (Punjab) Rules 1958

Summary: Health & Drug - Prevention of Food Adulteration (Punjab) Rules, 1958, r. 3 - Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954, ss. 16(1)(a) (ii), 20(1) and 24 - Whether on a true construction of Act, it is intended that a power conferred upon A may be exercised on A's authority by B? - Held, use of words 'in this behalf' in s. 20(1) of Act shows that delegation of such power by Central Govt. or State Govt. by general or special order must be for a specific purpose, to authorize a designated person to institute such prosecutions on their behalf terms of s. 20(1) of Act do not postulate further delegation by person so authorized - Further delegator can only give his consent in writing when he is satisfied that a prima facie case exists in facts of a particular case and records his reasons for launching of such prosecution in public interest - In case of statutory powers important question is whether on a true construction of Act, it is intended that a power conferred upon A may be exercised on A's authority by B - Maxim delegatus non potest delegare merely indicates that this is not normally allowable but legislature can always provide for sub-delegation of powers - Provision contained in s. 24(2)(e) enables State Govt. to frame a rule for delegation of powers and functions under Act - But it clearly does not envisage any sub-delegation - That apart, a rule framed u/s. 24(2)(e) can only provide for delegation of minor administrative functions - In case of important executive functions like one contained in s. 20(1) of Act to authorize launching of prosecutions for an offence under Act which is in nature of a safeguard, courts may be disposed to construe general powers of delegation restrictively - Keeping in view language of ss. 20(1) and 24(2)(e) of Act, r. 3 of Punjab Rules can be treated to be a general order issued by State Govt. to authorize Food (Health) Authority i.e. Director of Health Services to institute prosecutions for an offence under Act - Unfortunately, draftsmen of r. 3 more or less employed language of s. 20(1) of Act - If r. 3 were to be literally interpreted, words 'to authorize launching of prosecutions' may lead to consequence that Food (Health) Authority who had been delegated power of State Govt. u/s. 20(1) of Act could, in his turn, sub-delegate his powers to Food Inspector - Such a consequence is not envisaged by s. 20(1) of Act - It is well-settled that rules framed pursuant to a power conferred by a statute cannot proceed or go against specific provisions of statute - It must therefore follow as a logical consequence that r. 3 of Prevention of Food Adulteration (Punjab) Rules, 1958 must be read subject to provisions contained in s. 20(1) of Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954 - Cannot be construed to authorize sub-delegation of powers by Food (Health) Authority, Punjab to Food Inspector, Faridkot - It was also permissible for Food (Health) Authority being person authorized u/s. 20(1) of the Act to give his written consent for institution of such prosecutions by Food Inspector, Faridkot - In premises, impugned notification D/- 7-9-1972 issued by Food (Health) Authority must be declared as ultra vires - Food (Health) Authority insofar as purported to delegate his powers to institute prosecutions for an offence under Act u/s. 20(1) to Food Inspector, Faridkot - It must accordingly follow that Food Inspector, Faridkot was not competent to lodge complaint against appellants for having committed an offence punishable u/s. 16(1)(a)(ii) r/w s. 9 of Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954 - Appeal allowed.
