Rangachari v Bharat Sanchar Nigam Limited

Supreme Court of India

19 April 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

P.K. Balasubramanyan, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1076; (2007) 5 SCC 108; (2007) 2 SCC (Cr) 460; AIR 2007 SC 1682; 2007 (3) ALT 32; [2007] 137 Comp Cas 198; 2008 (1) CompLJ 124; 2007 CRLJ 2448; 2007 (3) CTC 495; 2007 (2) JCC (NI) 205; JT 2007 (6) SC 292; 2007 (2) KLT 1030; 2007 (5) MahLJ 375; 2007 (4) MPLJ 375; 2007 (2) RCR(Criminal) 875; 2007(5) SCALE 821; 2007 (6) SCJ 756; [2007] 5 S.C.R. 329

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Corporate; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Bouncing Of Cheques, Commercial Transaction, Public Financial Institutions, Dishonoured Cheque, Cheques, Offences By Companies, Law Merchant, Notice Of Dishonour, Banking, Public Financial Institutions and Negotiable Instruments Laws (amendment) Act, 1988

Summary: Banking & Finance - Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, s. 482 - Negotiable Instruments Act 1881, ss. 138, 141 and 141(2) - Criminal liability of directors and officers of company in case of dishonour of cheques for insufficiency of funds - Complaint filed u/s. 138 of Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 - Appellants were directors of company and they were in charge of and responsible for conduct of business of company - Appellant's petition u/s. 482 of CrPC, 1973 seeking quashing of complaint insofar as it related to him was dismissed - Challenged - Prosecution could be launched not only against company on behalf of which cheque issued has been dishonoured, but it could also be initiated against every person who at time offence was committed, was in charge of and was responsible for conduct of business of company - Sub-s.(2) of s. 141 also roped in directors, managers, secretaries or other officers of company, if it was proved that offence was committed with their consent or connivance - Held, in present case, reading complaint as a whole, it is clear that allegations in complaint are that at time at which two dishonoured cheques were issued by company, appellant and another were directors of company and were in charge of affairs of company - High Court rightly came to conclusion that it is not a fit case for exercise of jurisdiction u/s. 482 for quashing complaint - An advertence to ss. 138 and 141 of Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 shows that on other elements of an offence u/s. 138 being satisfied, burden is on Board of Directors or Officers in charge of affairs of company to show that they are not liable to be convicted - Any restriction on their power or existence of any special circumstance that makes them not liable is something that is peculiarly within their knowledge and it is for them to establish at trial such a restriction or to show that at relevant time they were not in charge of affairs of company - Reading complaint as a whole, it is found that it is a case where contentions sought to be raised by appellant can only be dealt with after conclusion of trial - Appeal dismissed.
