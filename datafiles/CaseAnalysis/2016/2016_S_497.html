Delhi Development Authority v Sukhbir Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

09 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 650; AIR 2016 SC 4275; 2016 (118) ALR 883; 2017 (1) ALT(SC) 1; 2016 (5) AWC 4932; 2016 (4) CLT(SC) 31; 2016 (6) CTC 624; JT 2016 (9) SC 65; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 407; 2016 (133) RD 588; 2016(8) SCALE 655; [2016] 5 S.C.R. 227; 2016 (4) UPLBEC 2946

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Protest, Municipal Corporation, Apportionment, Land Acquisition Officer, Tender, Delhi Lands (Restrictions on Transfer) Act, 1972, Attendance, Beneficiaries, Alienate, Competent To Alienate, Constitution of India, 1950, Land Acquisition Act, 1894, Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Delhi Lands (Restrictions on Transfer) Act, 1972, s. 4 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11A, 31, 31(2), 4 - Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013, s. 24(2) - Acquisition proceedings - Compensation - Lapse - Appellants issued notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act was issued on 24-10-1961 stating that acquisition was public purpose for planned development - Land Acquisition Officer (LAO) passed an award and possession of land was taken by a Panchnama dt. 27-1-2000 - Appellants filed an affidavit before SC pursuant to an order of SC dt. 17-4-2015 stating that since land had been sold to respondent nos. 3 to 5 there was a dispute as to who would receive compensation and compensation could neither be paid nor tendered - LAO filed a writ petition before HC seeking HC to accept cheques towards compensation amounts under the award - HC disposed of petition holding that cheques tendered in each petition would be treated as a tender to District Judge - Respondents filed writ petition before HC seeking that as possession had not been taken and compensation had neither been tendered nor paid to respondents acquisition proceedings had lapsed u/s. 24(2) of the 2013 Act - HC allowed petition based on judgment in Pune Municipal Corporation and another v. Harakchand Misirimal Solanki and others, 2014 Indlaw SC 45 - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that it is governed by a Standing Order of 26-6-1909 as amended up to date, in which one method of making payment u/s. 31 of the 1894 Act is by deposit in the treasury and that Pune Municipal Corporation, 2014 Indlaw SC 45, would not apply to the facts of instant case.

Held, Standing Order fleshes out s. 31 of the 1894 Act by insisting that compensation must be paid as soon as the award is announced Sufficient notice must be given to enable all payees to assemble at a place where they will receive their dues immediately. It is emphasized that much trouble will be avoided if the principle that payment of compensation should be made at the time of award is strictly observed. Also, it is important that the authorities draw in advance a sum sufficient to cover the probable amount of the award and to make payments. It is clear that neither tender, that is offer to original claimant nor payment has been made in the manner provided by s. 31 of the 1894 Act r/w Standing Order no. 28 of 1909. In instant case as has been admitted in the affidavit filed in SC, appellants were not ready with compensation payable on the day award was pronounced, but only handed over such compensation to LAO 5 years after the award was pronounced in 2002. LAO in its turn did nothing whatsoever to offer the said sum or pay it to original owners. On contrary, by moving an application on the eve of coming into force of the 2013 Act, and by depositing the said amount of compensation paid to it in the year 2002 only on 30-12-2013, it is clear that aforesaid mandatory provision and procedure were not followed by the authorities. The original land owners' lands were notified for acquisition on 24-10-1961, of which possession was taken four decades later, in 2000 after which land owners have yet to see colour of paltry amount of compensation offered which has neither been tendered nor paid to them. Therefore, there could not be stronger facts to hold such acquisition non est in accordance with the object sought to be achieved by s. 24(2) of the 2013 Act. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Delhi Lands (Restrictions on Transfer) Act, 1972, s. 4 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11A, 31, 31(2), 4, 6 - Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013, s. 24(2) - Land acquisition - Physical possession - Entitled for - Appellants contended that to the effect that Pune Municipal Corporation, 2014 Indlaw SC 45, did not notice that since possession had been taken in instant case in the year 2000, vesting of land in State had already taken place and the original owner had been divested of his title.

Held, SC in Meera Sahni v. Lt. Governor of Delhi and Others, 2008 Indlaw SC 1166, that the moment s. 4 of the 1972 Act applies, a sale subsequent to s. 6 of the 1894 Act notification becomes illegal, being hit by s. 4 of the 1972 Act. Therefore, it is of no avail to State, to submit that there is a dispute between original owner and subsequent purchaser, as a result of which compensation could neither be tendered nor paid. Further, it is also of no avail to either appellants or LAO who in any case were not in any dilemma as to who should be paid. It is clear that the deposit made in Court pursuant to the order of HC was only been made in order to pay the original owner of land. Accordingly, this plea has also to be turned down. Thus, instant case will fall within s. 24(2) of the 2013 Act, physical possession of the land can be said to have been taken on the facts of instant case. Impugned judgment of HC calls for no interference. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Once the award has been made and compensation has been deposited LAO is entitled to take possession and possession stands vested in State free from all encumbrances.
