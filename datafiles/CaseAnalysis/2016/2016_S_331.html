Deepak Surana and others v State of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

08 February 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Uday Umesh Lalit, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 129; AIR 2016 SC 1008; 2016 (4) CalLT 69; 2016 CRLJ 1602; 2016 (2) JabLJ 242; JT 2016 (2) SC 304; 2016 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1572; 2016 (2) RCR(Civil) 171; 2016(2) SCALE 438

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: FIR, Indian Penal Code, 1860

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120B - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 13(1)(d), 13(2) - Alleged Conspiracy - Conviction - Justification - Land in question was acquired by State Development Authority - Further, due to proposal initiated by State authority, part of land belonging to land owners was given at no profit no loss - Further, land released was sold vide sale agreement, however, intending purchasers in said four agreements to be appellants were not signed by appellant - Land Owners filed petition before HC submitting inter-alia that Development Authority was avoiding implementation of direction issued by State Govt. - FIR u/ss. 13(1(d), 13(2) of Act and 120B of IPC was filed alleging that conspiracy was hatched between certain public servants including then Ministers, Additional Secretary and owners of land - Further, appellant were also charge-sheeted - Trial court held that there was no material to proceed against appellants, hence, discharged appellants of charges leveled against them - However, HC vide impugned order reversed order passed by Trial Court on ground that merely because agreement of sale did not bear signatures of appellants it would not mean that agreements could not be relied upon - Hence, present appeal.

Held, agreements relied upon by prosecution do not bear signatures of appellants. Further, agreements in question were not recovered from custody of appellants and were recovered from vendors themselves. SC held that agreements being unilateral and not bearing signatures of appellants, mere execution of such agreements cannot be considered as relevant circumstance against appellants. There is nothing on record to indicate that consideration mentioned in agreement could be traced to appellants, nor is there any statement by any of witnesses suggesting even proximity or meeting of minds between appellants and any of other accused. Hence, impugned order passed by HC is set aside and order passed by Trial Court is restored. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - When material on record completely falls short of and cannot justify framing of charges against accused, criminal proceedings against accused is liable to be quashed.
