Prahlad Singh Bhati v Nct, Delhi and Another

Supreme Court of India

23 March 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 19889; (2001) 4 SCC 280; (2001) SCC (Cr) 674; AIR 2001 SC 1444; 2001 (2) CCR 69; 2001 (2) Crimes 87; 2001 CRLJ 1730; 2001 (1) JCC 229; JT 2001 (4) SC 116; 2001 (2) RCR(Criminal) 377; 2001(2) SCALE 572; 2001 (2) SCJ 588; [2001] 2 S.C.R. 684; 2001 (3) SLT 105; 2001 (2) Supreme 550; 2001 (1) UC 627

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (as amended by Act No.25 of 2005 & Act No.2 of 2006)

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 161, 437 and 438 - Murder - Anticipatory bail - Held, s. 436 provides that when any person accused of a bailable offence is arrested or detained without warrant by an officer in charge of police station, or appears or is brought before a court and is prepared at any time while in custody of such officer or at any stage of proceedings before such court to give bail, such person shall be released on bail u/s. 437 of Code when a person accused of, or suspected of commission of any non-bailable offence is arrested or detained without warrant by an officer in charge of a police station or appears or is brought before a court, he may be released on bail by a court other than HC and Sessions subject to condition that he does not reasonably appear to have been guilty of an offence punishable with death or imprisonment for life - Powers of Magistrate, while dealing with applications for grant of bail, are regulated by punishment prescribed for offence in which bail is sought - Assumption of jurisdiction to entertain application is distinguishable from exercise of jurisdiction jurisdiction to grant bail has to be exercised on basis of well-settled principles having regard to circumstances of each case and not in an arbitrary manner - While granting bail, court has to keep in mind nature of accusations, nature of evidence in support thereof, severity of punishment which conviction will entail, character, behavior, means and standing of accused, circumstances which are peculiar to accused, reasonable possibility of securing presence of accused at trial, reasonable apprehension of witnesses being tampered with, larger interests of public or State and similar other considerations - It has also to be kept in mind that for purposes of granting bail legislature has used words 'reasonable grounds for believing' instead of 'the evidence' which means court dealing with grant of bail can only satisfy it (itself) as to whether there is a genuine case against accused and that prosecution will be able to produce prima facie evidence in support of charge - It is not expected, at this stage, to have evidence establishing guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt - Further held, in instant case while exercising jurisdiction, apparently u/s. 437 of Code, Metropolitan Magistrate appears to have completely ignored basic principles governing grant of bail - Magistrate referred to certain facts and provisions of law which were not, in any way, relevant for purposes of deciding application for bail in a case where accused was charged with an offence punishable with death or imprisonment for life - Mere initial grant of anticipatory bail for lesser offence, did not entitle respondent to insist for regular bail even if he was subsequently found to be involved in case of murder - Neither s. 437(5) nor s. 439(1) of Code was attracted - Was no question of cancellation of bail earlier granted to accused for an offence punishable u/ss. 498-A, 306 and 406, IPC, 1860 - With change of nature of offence, accused becomes disentitled to liberty granted to him in relation to a minor offence, if offence is altered for an aggravated crime - Instead of referring to grounds which entitled respondent-accused grant of bail, Magistrate adopted a wrong approach to confer on him benefit of liberty on allegedly finding that no grounds were made out for cancellation of bail -Despite involvement of important questions of law, HC failed in its obligation to adjudicate pleas of law raised before it and dismissed petition of appellant by a one-sentence order - Orders of Magistrate as also of HC being contrary to law are liable to be set aside - Appeal allowed.
