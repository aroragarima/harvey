State of J & K v Wasim Ahmed Malik @ Hamid and another

Supreme Court of India

01 July 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Uday Umesh Lalit, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 466; (2015) 8 SCC 465; 2015 (1) JCC 2248; JT 2015 (6) SC 85; 2015 (3) MLJ(Crl) 607; 2015(7) SCALE 347; [2015] 9 S.C.R. 1084

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Explosive Substances Act, 1908, Bombs, TADA Rules

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 4, 5 - Jammu and Kashmir State Ranbir Penal Code, 1989 (1932 A.D), ss. 120B, 302, 307, 34 - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 3,4, 15(1) - Bomb Explosions at public place - Confession Inadmissible - Acquittal - On occasion of celebration of Republic Day on 26-1-1995, huge gathering was addressed by State officials when three powerful bomb explosions took place at site of public address system resulting in killing of eight persons, and in causing grievous injuries to eighteen persons and disruption of celebrations - FIR was lodged and accused person was arrested which led to certain recoveries - Accused was convicted of offences u/ss. 3 and 4 of 1987 Act, section 120-B read with sections 302, 307 and 34 of Code and ss. 4 and 5 of the 1908 Act - Further, Trial Court acquitted all respondents and rejected evidence regarding confessional statement of accused on ground that confessional statement was recorded in Hindi i.e. not in language of accused. Further, other accused had not given any confessional statement and case against him completely depended upon confessional statement of co-accused, hence, other accused was also acquitted - Whether acquittal order passed by Trial Court rejecting confessional statement is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, confessing accused was produced before PW1 and was given statutory warning and time to reflect. Everything was explained to him and only thereafter his thumb impression was taken. On next occasion when confessing accused was again produced before witness, soon after recording of confession it was again explained to him, read over and only thereafter thumb impression was taken. At no stage during recording on these two occasions, nor at stage when witness was in box, there is anything on record, or even suggestion that confessing accused did not understand or was not made to understand contents of confession. Further, contents of confession also disclose that many of assertions are personal to confessing accused which could only be gathered after due conversation with Recording Officer. Further, language used as means of communication between confessing accused and recording officer being Hindi or Hindustani, such recording of confession in Hindi language is completely in conformity with requirement of Rule. SC held that conclusion drawn by Trial court that accused being Pakistani national his language must be Urdu, hence, recording of confession in language other than Urdu, must be held to be not in conformity, is wrong. Nothing has been placed on record that confessing accused did not understand line of questioning or that he was not made to understand contents of confession after recording was complete. Further, contents of confession showed that accused admitted his guilt and his involvement right from hatching of conspiracy to execution thereof. Hence, confessing accused is guilty of offences with which he was charged. Further, with respect to other accused, SC held that apart from confession of confessing accused, nothing has been placed on record which could lend corroboration as regards his role in conspiracy and execution thereof. Principles of State vs. Nalini, 1999 Indlaw SC 810, relied on , SC found no justification to reverse finding of acquittal as recorded in respect of said accused no.2, hence, acquittal order passed against accused no.2 is affirmed. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio - Confession recorded u/s. 15(1) of Act in accordance with statutory requirements and in keeping with guidelines is admissible against maker, his co-accused, abettor or conspirator in trial for offence under Act, subject to condition stipulated in proviso to s. 15(1) of Act.
