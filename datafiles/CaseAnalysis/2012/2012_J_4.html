Medical Council of India v JSS Medical College and another

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 590; (2012) 5 SCC 628; 2012 (2) AWC 1850; JT 2012 (1) SC 127; 2013 (2) KarLJ 275; 2012 (2) MLJ(SC) 570; [2012] 1 S.C.R. 136; 2012 (2) SLR 153

Case Digest

Subject: Education; Health & Drug

Summary: Education - Administrative - Increase in seats - Challenged - Medical Council of India, aggrieved by the interim order passed by DB of HC whereby it had permitted Medical College/respondent No. 1 to increase the seats for MBBS Course from 150 to 200 for the academic year 2011-2012, had preferred the petition - Whether HC rightly permitted increase in the number of seats in Medical colleges - Held, in normal circumstances HC should not issue interim order granting permission for increase of the seats - HC ought to realize that granting such permission by interim order had cascading effect - By virtue of such order students were admitted as in instant case and though many of them had taken the risk knowingly but few might be ignorant - In most of such cases when finally the issue was decided against the College the welfare and plight of the students were ultimately projected to arouse sympathy of the Court - It results in very awkward and difficult situation - If on ultimate analysis it was found that the College's claim for increase of seats was untenable, in such event the admission of students with reference to the increased seats should be illegal - SC could not imagine anything more destructive of the rule of law than direction by the court to allow continuance of such students, whose admissions was found illegal in the ultimate analysis - SC was entrusted with the task to administer law and uphold its majesty - Courts could not by its fiat increase the seats, task entrusted to the Board of Governors and that too by interim order - In matter like instant one, decisions on issues had to be addressed at the interlocutory stage and they could not be deferred or dictated later when serious complications might ensue from the interim order itself - There were large number of authorities which took that view and instead of burdening that judgment with all those authorities it would be sufficient to refer to three Judge Bench decision of SC - Medical Council of India v. Rajiv Gandhi University of Health Sciences, 2004 Indlaw SC 406, relied on - For all these reasons SC was of the opinion that the interim order passed by HC was unsustainable - Appeal allowed.
