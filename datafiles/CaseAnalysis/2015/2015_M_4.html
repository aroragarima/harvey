State of Kerala and others v M. K. Jose

Supreme Court of India

14 August 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 553; (2015) 9 SCC 433; 2015 (112) ALR 631; 2015 (5) AWC 4551; 2015 (3) CLT(SC) 371; JT 2015 (7) SC 286; 2016 (3) MahLJ 5; 2015 (6) MLJ 503; 2015 (4) RCR(Civil) 1; 2015(8) SCALE 737; [2015] 9 S.C.R. 17

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contractual dispute - Writ Jurisdiction - Determination - Respondent was awarded work by appellant to be completed within stipulated time for which agreement was executed - However, respondent failed to complete work with stipulated time and extended time - Hence, appellant authority terminated contract awarded to respondent and forfeitingsecurity deposit placed by contractor for work to state and further stating that work had been put end to at cost and risk of contractor - Lower Court failed to interfere with order passed by authority - However, DB reversed order by lower Court and held that order of termination was founded on erroneous facts inasmuch as competent authority had opined that more than 50% of work remained to be done - DB opining that as there was factual defect, which was evident from commission's report, order of termination of contract was set aside - Further, HC directed matter to be taken into consideration afresh - Whether Appellate Bench in intra-court appeal arising from petition u/art. 226 of Constitution, should have quashed order terminating contract by competent authority on ground that it was passed on erroneous facts.

Held, respondent has been invoking jurisdiction of HC u/art. 226 of Constitution on various occasions challenging every action which pertain to extension of time, denial of revised estimate by State Govt. and many other facets of that nature and HC is passing orders for consideration by appropriate authority, for grant of opportunity of being heard to contractor and to consider his representation in accordance with law. SC held that this kind of orders in contractual matter is ill-conceived. SC held that writ court should ordinarily not entertain writ petition, if there is breach of contract involving disputed questions of fact and present case clearly indicates that factual disputes are involved. Further, SC held that HC has appointed Commission to collect evidence, accepted same without calling for objections from respondent and quashed order of termination of contract which is not permitted under powers granted u/art. 226 of Constitution. Hence, impugned order by DB is set aside. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - Order passed by HC in writ petition outside its jurisdiction u/art. 226 i.e. writ jurisdiction is not justified and is liable to be set aside.
