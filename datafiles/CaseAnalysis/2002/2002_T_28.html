Madhukar D. Shende v Tarabai Aba Shedage

Supreme Court of India

09 January 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

R.C. Lahoti, Brijesh Kumar

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 12; (2002) 2 SCC 85; AIR 2002 SC 637; 2002 (6) Bom.C.R. 522; 2002 (1) CalLT(SC) 44; 2002 (1) JhrCR(SC) 448; JT 2002 (1) SC 74; 2002 (1) MLJ(SC) 181; 2002 (1) RCR(Civil) 724; 2002 (93) RD 98; 2002(1) SCALE 103; 2002 (1) SCJ 231; [2002] 1 S.C.R. 132; 2002 (1) SLT 137; 2002 (1) Supreme 83; 2002 (1) UC 323

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Family & Personal

Summary: Civil Procedure - Family & Personal - Will - Validity of - - Suit property was initially owned by 'X', who executed a registered deed of will in favour of 'Y', who was her own sister's daughter - Having inherited the property under the will of 'X', 'Y' transferred her title and possession to the plaintiff under a registered deed of sale - Suit for declaration of title and issuance of preventive injunction, in the alternative for recovery of possession was filed alleging that defendant was threatening to dispossess the plaintiff and was claiming title in herself - Trial Court found the will not proved, and so the plaintiff having not acquired any title under the sale deed executed by 'Y' in his favour, and therefore, directed the suit to be dismissed - Finding was affirmed by appellate court and HC - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the conscience of the court has to be satisfied by the profounder of will adducing evidence so as to dispel any suspicions or unnatural circumstances attaching to a will provided that there is something unnatural or suspicious about the will. The law of evidence does not permit conjecture or suspicion having the place of legal proof nor permit them to demolish a fact otherwise proved by legal and convincing evidence. The onus is discharged by the profounder adducing prima facie evidence proving the competence of the testator and execution of the will in the manner contemplated by law. In the instant case, there is nothing to doubt the mental and physical capacity of 'X' but the same has been suspected because of 'complete absence of any medical evidence, of a doctor which would show that the testator was in a sound and disposing state of mind'. There is no rule of law or of evidence which requires a doctor to be kept present when a will is executed. The courts below have allowed their findings to be influenced by such suspicion and conjecture as have no foundation in the evidence and have no relevance in the facts and circumstances of the case and unwittingly allowed their process of judicial thinking to be vitiated by irrelevant reasoning's and considerations. The findings of the Trial Court and the first appellate court as also of the HC are vitiated for adopting an approach not permitted by law and because of overlooking the material and relevant legal considerations. The judgment and decree of the Trial Court as upheld by the first Appellate Court and the HC are set aside. Instead the suit filed by the plaintiff is directed to be decreed. The plaintiff is declared to be the owner of the suit property and entitled to recovery of possession from the defendant. Appeal allowed.
