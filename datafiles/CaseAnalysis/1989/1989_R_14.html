Â Raipur Development Authority Etc. Etc. v Chokhamal Contractors Etc. Etc.

Supreme Court of India

04 May 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

E.S. Venkataramiah, M.N. Venkatachaliah, R.S. Pathak, Ranganath Misra, N.D. Ojha

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 567; (1989) 2 SCC 721; AIR 1990 SC 1426; 1989 (1) ARBLR 430; JT 1989 (2) SC 285; 1989(1) SCALE 1279; [1989] 3 S.C.R. 144

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Challenged

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940 - Arbitral award - Challenged - Appellants filed instant appeals against the arbitral award - Whether award passed under the provisions of the Act is liable either to be remitted u/s. 16(1)(c) of the Act or liable to be set aside u/s. 30(c) of the Act thereof merely on ground that no reasons have been given by the arbitrator or umpire, as the case may be in support of the award.

Held, if the parties to in the dispute feel that reasons should be given by arbitrators for the awards it is within their power to insist upon such reasons being given at the time when they enter into arbitration agreement or sign the deed of submission. It is significant that although nearly decade ago Indian Law Commission submitted its report on the law of arbitration specifically mentioning therein that there was no necessity to amend law of arbitration requiring arbitrators to give reasons, Parliament has not chosen to take any step in the direction of amendment of law of arbitration. Even after passing of the English Arbitration Act, 1979 unless court requires the arbitrators to give reasons for award an award is not liable to be set aside merely on the ground that no reasons have been given in support of it. SC and all HC have taken view that merely because reasons are not given award is not liable to be remitted or set aside except where the arbitration agreement or the deed of submission, or an order made by the court such as the one u/s. 20 or s. 21 or s. 34 of the Act or the statute governing the arbitration requires that arbitrator or umpire should give reasons for the award. The arbitrators or umpire have passed the awards which are involved in cases relying on the law declared by SC that the awards could not be questioned merely on the ground that they have not given reasons. At the same time it cannot also be said that all awards are contrary to law and justice. It would be wholly unjust to pass order either remitting or setting aside the awards, merely on the ground that no reasons are given in them, except where the arbitration agreement or the deed of submission or an order made by the court such as the one u/s. 20 or s. 21 or s. 34 of the Act or the statute governing the arbitration required that the arbitrator or the umpire should give reasons for the award. There is, however, one aspect of non-speaking awards in non-statutory arbitrations to which Government and Governmental authorities are parties that compel attention. The trappings of a body which discharges judicial functions and required to act in accordance with law with their concomitant obligations for reasoned decisions, are not attracted to private adjudication of the nature of arbitration as the latter, It is not supposed to exert the State's sovereign judicial power. However, arbitral awards in disputes to which the State and its instrumentalities are parties affect public interest and the matter of the manner in which Government and its instrumentalities allow their interest to be affected by such arbitral adjudications involve larger questions of policy and public interest. Government and its instrumentalities cannot simply allow large financial interests of the State to be prejudicially affected by non-reviewable except in the limited way allowed by the Statute, non-speaking arbitral awards. Indeed, this branch of the system of dispute-resolution has, of late, acquired a certain degree of notoriety by the manner in which in many cases the financial interests of Government have come to suffer by awards which have raised eye-brows by doubts as to their rectitude and propriety. It will not be justifiable for Governments or their instrumentalities to enter into Arbitration agreements which do not expressly stipulate the rendering of reasoned and speaking awards. Governments and their instrumentalities should, as a matter of policy and public interest, if not as compulsion of law ensure that wherever they enter into agreements for resolution of disputes by resort to private arbitrations, requirement of speaking awards is expressly stipulated and ensured. Any lapse in that behalf might lend itself to and perhaps justify, the legitimate criticism that Government failed to provide against possible prejudice to public-interest. Thus, law should be allowed to remain as it is until the competent legislature amends the law. In the result SC held that award passed under the Act is not liable to be remitted or set aside merely on the ground that no reasons have been given in its support except where the arbitration agreement or the deed of submission or an order made by the Court such as the one u/s. 20 or s. 21 or s. 34 of the Act or the statute governing the arbitration requires that the arbitration or the umpire should give reasons for the award. These cases will now go back to the DB for disposal in accordance with law and the view expressed by us in this decision. Order accordingly.
