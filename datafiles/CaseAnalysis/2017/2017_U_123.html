Kalpana Mehta and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

05 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Rohinton Fali Nariman

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 379; (2017) 7 SCC 295; 2017 (4) ALT(SC) 21; JT 2017 (5) SC 379; 2017(4) SCALE 541

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Health & Drug

Keywords: Mandamus, Speaker, Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business in Lok Sabha, Special Court Trial of Offences Relating to Transactions in Securities Act, 1992, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Constitution - Health & Drug - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 105, 121, 122, 136, 145(3), 32 - Approval of vaccine - Reports of committee - Review sought - Petitioners filed instant writ petitions relating to action taken by Drugs Controller General and Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) pertaining to approval of a vaccine, namely, Human Papilloma Virus (HPV) manufactured by respondent nos. 7 and 8 for preventing cervical cancer in women and experimentation of vaccine was done as an immunization by Govt. with charity provided by respondent no. 6 and issue with regard to death of certain persons and grant of compensation.

Held, reports of Committees were for the assistance of Parliament. The procedure for drawing up such reports, was entirely a matter for Committee and it has authority to receive evidence from witnesses but it was for their own assistance. No person has a right to be heard by Committee even if Committee was examining a matter which might result in an adverse comment on the conduct of such person. The principles of natural justice were not applicable. Controversy has to be seen from the perspective of judicial review. The basic principle of judicial review was to ascertain the propriety of decision making process on the parameters of reasonableness and propriety of executive decisions. When a writ of mandamus was sought on the foundation of a factual score, Court was required to address the facts asserted and averments made and what was stated in impugnation. Once Court was asked to look at the report, the same could be challenged by the other side, for it cannot be accepted without affording an opportunity of being heard to respondents. The invitation to contest a Parliamentary Standing Committee report was likely to disturb the delicate balance that Constitution provides between constitutional institutions. If Court allows contest and adjudicates on the report, it might run counter to the spirit of privilege of Parliament which Constitution protects. SC was of view that Parliamentary Standing Committee report might not be tendered as a document to augment the stance on the factual score that a particular activity was unacceptable or erroneous. However, regard being had to the substantial question of law relating to interpretation of Constitution involved, it appropriate that issue be referred to Constitution Bench u/art. 145(3) of Constitution. The papers should be placed before Chief Justice for constitution of appropriate Bench. Order accordingly.
