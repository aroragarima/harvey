State Through C.B.I v Mahender Singh Dahiya …

Supreme Court of India

28 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. SUDARSHAN REDDY

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 67; (2011) 3 SCC 109; AIR 2011 SC 1017; 2011 CRLJ 2177; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 69; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 706; 2011(2) SCALE 7; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 1104

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Horn Lane Act

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 201 and 302 - Acquittal - Challenged - Respondent, Orthopedic Surgeon, allegedly murder his wife, a British national of Indian origin - It was further alleged that after committing the murder, the respondent had dismembered and extensively mutilated the body of the victim and subsequently disposed of the body parts at different places in the city of Brussels with the intention of destroying the evidence of the murder - Trial Court convicted respondent u/ss. 302 and 201 of IPC - On appeal, HC upon re-appraisal of the entire evidence accepted the appeal and acquitted the respondent of both the charges - Hence, present appeal - (A) Motive - It was contended that the prosecution failed to establish any motive for the alleged crime - Held, merely because the respondent objected to the behaviour of deceased towards her male friends would not be sufficient to hold that the respondent had the necessary motive to kill her - Further, it was inconceivable that the respondent would have married deceased only for the purpose of committing her murder - (B) Last seen circumstance - Relevancy - Held, last seen evidence would not necessarily mean that the respondent had killed his wife - Also, given the previous attitude of deceased, it was quite possible that she had walked out on her husband - (C) Recovery of body parts from the rubbish container and the identification thereof - Held, HC reached the appropriate conclusion that the possibility of the clothing and articles having been planted by the police by obtaining the same from the house of deceased with the object of fixing the identity of the body parts belonging to deceased by means of the clothes cannot be ruled out - Further, there was omission of the details of the allegedly recovered clothes in the statement of the witnesses - HC correctly concluded that on consideration of the relevant evidence of the witnesses and various documents on record, the prosecution had failed to establish the recovery of clothes or shoes by means of any cogent and reliable evidence - (D) Identity of the body parts - Held, HC upon considering the entire evidence relating to this issue concluded that no reliance could be placed on the reports presented by the prosecution for the purpose of establishing the identity of the body parts as that of deceased - (E) Recovery of the blood from the bathroom - Held, there was no reliable evidence to indicate that the blood that was recovered from the bathroom definitely belonged to deceased - HC was justified in rejecting the evidence with regard to the recovery of blood - (F) Acquittal - Held, prosecution failed to establish that the respondent had any intention of committing the murder of his wife at the commencement of the honeymoon trip - Hence, prosecution failed to connect the respondent with the alleged murder of his wife - Conclusions recorded by the HC fully justified by the evidence on record - Appeal dismissed.
