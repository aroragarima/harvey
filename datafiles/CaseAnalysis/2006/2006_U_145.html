Ashoka Smokeless Coal Ind. Private Limited and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

01 December 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1470; (2007) 2 SCC 640; 2007 (5) AWC 5156; JT 2007 (1) SC 125; 2006(13) SCALE 102; [2006] Supp9 S.C.R. 954

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; IT & E-Commerce; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Coking Coal, U.P. Sales Tax Act, Colliery, Coking Coal Mines, Colliery Control, Colliery Control Order, 1945, Coal Mines (Nationalisation) Amendment Act, 1976, Colliery Control Order, 2000, Kerala Industrial Establishments (National and Festival Holidays) (Amendment) Act, 1990, Coal Mines (Conservation and Development) Act, 1974

Summary: (A) Mines & Minerals - IT & E-Commerce - Constitution - Trade - Mines and Minerals (Regulation and Development) Act, 1957, s. 9(3) - Essential Commodities Act, 1955, s. 3(1) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14 - Supply of coal - Introduction of E-Auction - Constitutionality - 1st respondent/Union of India introduced E-Auction Scheme as a part of its liberalization policy - Appellant Small Scale Industries, which were linked industries in non-core sectors, challenged the E-Auction Scheme due to varied price fixation by respondent coal companies (suppliers) - HC dismissed appellants writ petition holding that dual pricing policy adopted could not be found fault with and variation of price due to E-Auction being a normal facet in commercial transaction - Hence the instant appeals - Whether a State within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution of India can be permitted to take recourse to E-Auction, which was not commensurate with the constitutional scheme of the country - Held, respondent coal companies were 'State' within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - Coal was an essential commodity in terms of s. 3(1) of the Essential Commodities Act - Colliery Control Order was made, inter alia, for securing equitable distribution and availability of higher price of essential commodity - Coal companies as also the Central Government, therefore, had a constitutional and statutory obligation to fulfill the equitable distribution - Coal companies exercising monopolistic power, thus, were required to distribute coal equitably and at a fair price - In E-Auction, the quantity and quality of coal depending upon its grade, size, colliery from which the same had been extracted, were specified - In such a situation invariably the price for same quality of coal would greatly vary as the bidders would bid having regard to their own requirement - By allowing repeated bids, a person who may be requiring the essential commodity would not be able to prove his requirement and its non-availability may result in stoppage of production which would lead to various complications - E-Auction had effect both on price of coal as also the availability thereof to the non-core sector consumers, however, core sectors were also taking part in E-Auction - Price range of all the subsidiaries had been taken cumulatively instead of taking subsidiary-wise figures - Consumers belonging to core sectors, such as big companies, who were taking part in the E-Auction as a result whereof the price of coal had shot up at the cost of appellant Small Scale units - Even the linked consumers of core and non-core sectors had been participating in E-Auction, participation of core sector in E-Auction was destructive of its own policy of 1st respondent - Further, auction price being online, no other bidder was aware of the contents of the bid submitted by the bidder - No bidder should have access to the records pertaining to E-Auction so as to ascertain who was the highest bidder or what was the highest bid price; or no bidder would have knowledge or access to the various bids submitted by the bidders against the particular grade of coal so as to arrive at an average E-Auction price of particular grade of coal - Only the companies who were conducting the E-Auction, would have access to the details of the bids submitted by the bidders - No eligibility criteria having been fixed, any person including traders could participate and bid in the E- Auction - Consequently, the manufacturers of hard coke and smokeless coal as also other small units had to buy coal at prohibitive rates from traders - Methodology for allocation of coal to a bidder of E-Auction was, thus, inequitable, irrational and fortuitous - Since a particular grade was allocated/sold at different prices to different bidders, E-Auction ultimately led to sale of a particular grade of coal at variable prices in the market - In spite of Government of India's office memorandum promising sale of coal to the linked consumers at average E-Auction rate, sale to linked consumers was being made at the highest bid price and not at the average bid price - Necessity of having a fixed price of coal was supported by s. 9(3) of the 1957 Act, therefore, a constantly variable price per se unreasonable and unfair being opposed to the professed policies under the 1957 Act - Effect of the E-Auction was that core sector (big companies) and non-core non-linked SSI/tiny units were being supplied coal at a fixed price without any rational classification by supplying the coal to the latter on the price to be ascertained by the trader-controlled process of E-Auction and thereby putting the appellant units at par with the big Companies in core sector - Scheme of E-Auction was, therefore, ultra vires art. 14 of the Constitution of India - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Mines & Minerals - IT & E-Commerce - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 226, 77 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955 - Colliery Control Order, 2003 - Introduction of E-Auction - Interference by SC - Justifiability - Whether the policy decision of introduction of E-Auction could be the subject matter of judicial review - Held, E-Auction was not a policy decision of the Central Government - Policy decision on the part of the executive of the Central Government should be strictly construed in terms of art. 77 of the Constitution - State while exercising its power under the Essential Commodities Act, fixed the price keeping in mind several factors, in particular the larger interest of the people - Price fixation of an essential commodity, therefore, was determined on the touchstone of public interest - While doing so the State was expected to follow a rational and fair procedure and for the said purpose may collect data, obtain public opinion, and may appoint an expert committee - Recourse to E-Auction had been taken primarily by way of a profit motive - No public opinion was sought for and no expert committee was appointed - Statutory and constitutional duties had not been kept in view - Respondent coal companies were instrumentalities of the State and hence they were bound to adhere to the Directive Principles of the State and the prime object for which the Nationalization Acts were enacted - Even the coal companies in taking recourse to E-Auction did not give effect to the concept of corporate social responsibility - Respondent coal companies which were, therefore, public authorities when seeking to give effect to the constitutional scheme as contained in the preamble of the Nationalization Acts of 1972 and 1973 were acting at the behest of the Central Government and not entirely on their own - E-Auction was not related to policy for supply of coal but essentially the price thereof - Central Government in that view of the matter either directly or indirectly while purportedly exercising its power u/cl. 6 r/w cl. 9 of the Colliery Control Order could not have issued any direction in the garb of disposal of coal by way of E-Auction - Central Government itself said that it had allowed the coal companies to fix their own price; if that be so in terms of the statute it could not issue any direction which would have direct or indirect impact on price of coal - Further, no notification had been issued by the Central Government regulating supply of coal - In such circumstances, judicial review was justifiable in the instant case - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Mines & Minerals - IT & E-Commerce - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 226, 77 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955 - Introduction of E-Auction - Promissory estoppel - Applicability - Whether the doctrine of promissory estoppel had any application in the instant case - Held, appellant units had set up their units at the behest of the respondent coal companies in order to share the burden of coal companies to supply soft coke to the small consumers - Doctrine of promissory estoppel would, therefore, be applicable in the instant case - Appeals disposed of.
