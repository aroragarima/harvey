Inder Pal Yadav And Ors. Etc. v Union Of India And Ors Etc.

Supreme Court of India

18 April 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, Ranganath Misra

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 155; (1985) SCC (L&S) 526; (1985) 2 SCC 648; 1985 (51) FLR 138; 1985 (2) LLJ 406; 1986 (1) LLN 18; 1985(1) SCALE 703; [1985] 3 S.C.R. 837; 1985 (2) SLJ 58; 1985 (2) SLR 248; 1985 UJ 1040

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Uttar Pradesh Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Bombay Industrial Disputes Act, 1938 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Termination from service - Legality - Petitioners/workmen were styled as 'project casual labour' - Petitioners were put in continuous service from 1974 till 1983 - Respondents passed termination order as project on which petitioners were employed was completed without any chance of being re-employed - Some workmen rushed to HC and obtained interim relief and others were not so fortunate - These petitions were set down for final hearing and judgment was reserved - Respondent/Additional Solicitor General, requested Court not to render judgment because he would take up matter with Railway Ministry to find a just and humane solution affecting livelihood of these petitioners - Railway Ministry framed Scheme and circulated same amongst others to all General Managers of Indian Railways - Hence instant petitions for examining fairness and justness of Scheme - Whether Court would dispose of these petitions in view of Scheme - Held, scheme certainly was an improvement on situation though not wholly satisfactory - However, railway being biggest employer and regard to nature of its work, it would have to engage casual labour and therefore, as preliminary step towards realization of ideal enshrined in arts. 41 and 42, SC proposed to put their stamp of approval on scheme with one major variation which SC proceed to herein set out - Scheme envisages that it would be applicable to casual labour on projects who were in service as on 1-1-1984 - Choice of this date does not commend to us, for it was likely to introduce an invidious distinction between similarly situated persons and expose some workmen to arbitrary discrimination flowing from fortuitous court's order - Those in respect of whom Court granted interim relief be stay/suspension of order of retrenchment, they would be treated in service on 1-1-1984 while others who fail to obtain interim relief though similarly situated would be pushed down in implementation of Scheme - There was another area where discrimination was likely to rear its ugly head - These workmen come from lowest grade of railway service - They could ill afford to rush to court - Their Federations have hardly been of any assistance - Those who could not come to SC need not be at comparative disadvantage to those who rushed in here - If they were otherwise similarly situated, they were entitled to similar treatment if not by anyone else at hands of SC - In view of all aspects of matter, SC would modify part 5.1 (a)(i) by modifying date from 1-1-1984 to 1-1-1981 - With this modification and consequent rescheduling in absorption from that date onward, Scheme framed by Railway Ministry was accepted and direction was given that it must be implemented by re-casting stages consistent with change in date as herein directed - To avoid violation of art. 14 scientific and equitable way if implementing scheme was for Railway administration to prepare, list of project casual labour with reference to each division of each railway and then start absorbing those with longest service - If in process any adjustments were necessary, same must be done - SC was considerably influenced by statutory recognition of principle well known in industrial jurisprudence that men with longest service should have priority over those who had joined later on s. 25G of 1947 Act was accepted - Petitions disposed of.
