S. F. Engineer v Metal Box India Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

28 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 202; (2014) 6 SCC 780; AIR 2014 SC 2189; 2014(3) ALL MR 918; 2014 (104) ALR 473; 2014 (3) Bom.C.R. 822; JT 2014 (4) SC 284; 2014 (6) MahLJ 478; 2014 (1) RCR(Rent) 474; 2014(4) SCALE 260; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 1047

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: Bonafide requirement, Bombay Rent Act, 1947, Unlawful sub-letting, Principle of legitimate inference

Summary: Rent Control - Bombay Rent Act, 1947, s. 13(1)(e), 13(1)(k) - Companies Act, 1956- Suit for eviction - Unlawful sub-letting - Bonafide requirement - Principle of legitimate inference - Legality - Appellant (plaintiff/landlord) let out the premises in question to respondent (defendant no. 1) exclusively for the purpose of providing residential accommodation to its executive staff and not for any other purpose - Appellant filed a suit for eviction of respondent (defendant no. 1) and its former employee, the respondent no. 2 (defendant no. 2) - Trial Court held that plaintiff failed to prove that it required suit premises reasonably and bona fide for his use and occupation and also it was not proven that greater hardship would be caused to plaintiff and that that plea of non-user in respect of premises was not established and that no case of unlawful subletting was made out - However, appellant filed instant appeal against the judgment and order of HC passed in revision application, allowing respondent (tenant) appeal and in reversal of concurrent findings of Courts below that there was an unauthorized subletting, dismissing appellant's application u/s. 13(1)(e) of the Act for an order for grant of possession - Whether in obtaining factual matrix the principle of legitimate inference could have invoked to come to a conclusion that defendant no. 2 was inducted as a sub-tenant -

Held, it was settled in law that requisite conditions for establishing the factum of sub-letting are parting of legal possession, and availing of monetary consideration which could be in cash or kind and which fact might not be required to be directly proven by the landlord in all circumstances - As was perceptible, defendant no. 2 was given possession by the defendant no. 1 as an executive of the company - It was made available to him under the conditions of service and such provision was in consonance with the agreement entered into by landlord and tenant, i.e., plaintiff and defendant no. 1 - Perusal of assertions made by defendant no. 2 it was luminous that he was allowed to occupy the premises as an executive by company and thereafter as his dues could not be paid to him, he remained in occupation and also tried to become the owner of premises - True it was, the defendant no. 1 did not initiate action at an early stage but in 1993 when the Provident Fund Commissioner made a demand, it moved the writ court and ultimately the matter was settled before SC - Thus, handing over of possession of the premises to defendant no. 2 was in accord with the terms and conditions of agreement entered between landlord and tenant and, thus, entry of defendant no. 2 into the premises was legal - Trial Court and Appellate Court had drawn inference that after the defendant no. 2, employee, resigned from service and remained in occupation while he was not entitled to, the defendant no. 1 did not take any steps to get back the possession and proceedings initiated under 1956 Act were dismissed for non- prosecution and at a belated stage only a suit for recovery of occupational charges was instituted - It was a settlement between defendant 1 and 2, but it was a settlement between an employer and an erstwhile employee and, thus, landlord had no role - Court noted the settlement only to show that barring withholding of retiral dues the employer had not received any thing either in cash or in kind or otherwise from the defendant no. 2 and hence, under those circumstances, it was extremely difficult to hold that factum of sub-letting was established - When inferences drawn did not clearly flow from facts and were not legally legitimate, any conclusion arrived at on that basis becomes absolutely legally fallible - Thus, it could not be said that HC had erred in exercise of its revisional jurisdiction by substituting the finding of fact which was arrived at by Courts below - Thus, HC had not committed any illegality in its exercise of revisional jurisdiction under the obtaining facts and circumstances - Appeal dismissed.
