Rani Drig Raj Kuer v Raja Sri Amar Krishna Narain Singh

Supreme Court of India

14 December 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sarkar, S.K. Das, K. Subba Rao

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 123; AIR 1960 SC 444; 1960 SCJ 594; [1960] 2 S.C.R. 431

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Oudh Estates Act, Oudh Estates Act, 1869, Uttar Pradesh Court Of Wards Act, 1912

Summary: Land & Property - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Uttar Pradesh Court of Wards Act, 1912 - Oudh Estates Act, 1869 - Constitution Of India,1950 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Representation of People Act, 1951 - Position subsequently was that estates of both appellant, as a result of her mental illness and respondent, on his own application, came under superintendence of same court of wards and were placed in charge of same deputy Commr. in whose name each party sued and was sued in appeals - On 03-12-1951, Court of Wards passed a resolution settling appeals on certain terms as it thought that such settlement was in best interests of two contending wards - After appellant had regained her mental competence, she began to entertain a feeling that compromise in appeals had not done full justice to her - On 14-01-1957, appellant made two applications to High Court at Lucknow for an order that work of preparation of paperbooks of said appeals be resumed - (A) Whether compromise decrees were a nullity as terms of s. 56 of Act which are mandatory, had not been complied with? - Held, it is well-known that use of word 'shall' is not conclusive of question whether a provision is mandatory - Court of Wards could itself settle a litigation in which two of its wards were involved even where representatives had been appointed u/s. 56 - Appointment of representatives could not hence have been intended to be obligatory - Section is clearly directory - Failure to observe provisions of section did not render compromise decree in this case a nullity - (B) Whether compromise decree is not a contract as there was only one party, Deputy Commr. to compromise? - Further held, Court of Wards does not loose its powers when it has two wards and can therefore make a compromise between them - When it does so, it makes a contract between two of them - Therefore, to present compromise, there were two parties - U/s. 61(1) of Act, a contract executed by Court of Wards for a ward may be executed in its own name or on behalf of ward - Nothing in Act to indicate that such power does not exist for making a contract between two wards - (C) Whether compromise decrees were a nullity in view of principles embodied in O. XXXII of CPC? - Further held, under provisions of O. XXXII, r.4, Deputy Commr., Barabanki, was entitled to act as appellant's guardian for appeals and court had not made any order appointing another person to be her guardian - No fault can be found with court in having left interests of appellant in charge of Court of Wards though it was also in charge of interests of respondent - Failure of court to appoint another person as guardian of appellant for suits or appeals did not make compromise decrees a nullity - Appeals dismissed.
