Gangadhar Pillai v M/S. Siemens Ltd

Supreme Court of India

10 November 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1359; (2007) 1 SCC (L&S) 346; (2007) 1 SCC 533; 2007(2) ALL MR 449; JT 2006 (10) SC 265; 2006(11) SCALE 635; 2007 (1) SLR 302; [2006] Supp8 S.C.R. 900; 2007 (1) UPLBEC 516

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Industrial Disputes, Backwages, Reappointment

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 2(oo)(bb), 25-F, 25-G - Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Unions and Prevention of Unfair Labour Practices Act, 1971, ss. 26, 27, 32 - Minimum Wages Act, 1948 - Temporary personnel - Termination of services - Maintainability - Respondent/company used to engage temporary personnel in the category of skilled, semi-skilled and unskilled workers - Appellant was appointed by respondent on temporary basis for duration of project and on completion his services used to be terminated - Respondent terminated services of appellant on 10.5.2000 - Appellant filed complaint before Tribunal which was dismissed - Aggrieved by same appellant preferred petition before HC which was dismissed - Hence instant appeal - Whether an employee engaged as casual or temporary could be considered as perennial and whether there was any unfair labour practices by respondent was maintainable - Held, it was not law that on completion of 240 days of continuous service in a year, concerned employee becomes entitled to for regularization of his services and/ or permanent status - Further, concept of 240 days in a year was introduced in industrial law for a definite purpose under the 1947 Act, concept of 240 days was introduced so as to fasten a statutory liabilities upon employer to pay compensation to be computed in manner specified in s. 25-F of the 1947, Act before he was retrenched from services and not for any other purpose - Furthermore, in event a violation of said provision takes place, termination of services of employee may be found to be illegal, but only on that account, his services could not be directed to be regularized and direction to reinstate workman would mean that he gets back same status - Therefore, there was no provision for prevention of unfair labour practices under the 1947 Act and view of HC as upheld by SC merely negatived a contention that such appointment came within purview of s. 2(oo)(bb) of the 1947 Act - Thus, SC noticed various decisions rendered by it as regards payment of backwages and instead and place of reinstatement in service, compensation was directed to be paid - Appeal dismissed.
