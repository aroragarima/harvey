Â Additional District Magistrate, Jabalpur v Shivakant Shukla and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 April 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

A.N. Ray, M. Hameedullah Beg, Y.V. Chandrachud, Hans Raj Khanna, P.N. Bhagwati

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 655; (1976) 2 SCC 521; AIR 1976 SC 1207; [1976] Supp S.C.R. 172

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Wrongful Confinement, Foreign Power, Emergency Provisions, Power Of The Court, Past Conduct, Deem, Habeas Corpus Act, 1679, Defence of India Rules, 1962, Constitutionality, Defence of India Act, 1962, Constitution (Thirty-Ninth Amendment) Act, 1975, Civil Liberty, Notions, Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Emergency Powers (Defence) Act, 1939, Obedience, Fetters, Mysore (Personal and Miscellaneous) Inams Abolition Act, 1955, Defence of Realm Consolidation Act, 1914, Emergencies, Kerala Education Bill, 1957, Defence And Internal Security Of India Act, 1971, Constitution (Thirty-Eighth Amendment) Act, 1975, Defence of India Ordinance, 1962, Bombay Taluqdari Tenure Abolition Act, 1949, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1923, Andhra Pradesh Sugar Dealers Licensing Order, Bombay Conditions of Detention Order, 1951, British Constitution, Defence Act, 1842, Emergency Powers (No. 41F) Order, 1941, Emergency Powers (No. 41) Order, 1940, Emergency Powers Act, 1964, Emergency Powers Act, 1920, Espionage Act, Government of India Act, 1934, Habeas Corpus A Act, 1640, Habeas Corpus Act, 1816, Habeas Corpus Suspension Act, Habeas groups Act, Jammu & Kashmir Preventive Detention Act, Jammu and Kashmir Preventive Denotation (Amendment) Act, 1967, Madhya Pradesh Public Security Act, 1959, Maintenance of Internal Security (Amendment) Act, 1976, Presidential Order, 1962, Presidential Order, 1975, Prevention of Crime (Ireland) Act, 1882, Sedition Act, Sugar Control Order, United States Constitution

Summary: Majority ViewA. N. Ray, C.J.

(A) Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14, 16, 21, 226, 341, 352 and 359 - Maintenance of Internal Security Act, 1971, s. 16A(9) - Issuance of writ of habeas corpus - Power of HCs - Emergency period - Respondents filed applications in different HCs for the issue of writ of habeas corpus and challenged the legality and validity of the orders of their detention in all the cases - HCs held that notwithstanding the continuance of emergency and the Presidential Order suspending the enforcement of fundamental rights conferred by Arts. 14,21 and 22 the HCs can examine whether an order of detention is in accordance with the provisions of Act - HCs also held that in spite of suspension of enforcement of fundamental rights conferred by arts. 21 and 22 of the Constitution a person's right to freedom from arrest or detention except in accordance with law can be enforced only where such arrest and detention except in accordance with law can be enforced only where such arrest and detention are not in accordance with those provisions of the statue which form the conditions precedent to the exercise of power under the status as distinguished from merely procedural provisions or are malafide or are not based on relevant materials by which the detaining authority could have been satisfied that the order of detention was necessary - HCs held that the HCs could not go into the questions whether the Proclamation of Emergency was justified or whether the continuance thereof was malafide - Hence, present appeals - (A) Whether in view of the Presidential orders u/art. 359(1) of the Constitution any writ petition u/art. 226 before a HC for habeas corpus to enforce the right to personal liberty of a person detained under the Act on the ground that the order of detention or the continued detention is for any reason not under or in compliance with the Act is maintainable? - Held, in view of the Presidential order u/art. 359(1) of Constitution no person has locus standi to move any writ petition u/art. 226 before a HC for habeas corpus or any other writ or order or direction to enforce any right to personal liberty of a person detained under the Act on the grounds that the order of detention or the continued detention is for any reason not under or in compliance with the Act or is illegal or malafide - (B) Scope or extent of judicial scrutiny particularly in view of the Presidential order mentioning, inter alia, Art. 22 of the Constitution and also in view of sub-section (9) of s. 16A of the Act? - Held, s. 16A(9) of the Act is valid - It is a rule of evidence and it is not open either to the detenu or to the court to ask for grounds of detention - (C) Whether Art. 21 is the sole repository of the right to personal liberty? - Held, art. 21 is the sole repository of rights to life and personal liberty against the State - Any claim to a writ of habeas corpus is enforcement of Art. 21 and, is, therefore, barred by the Presidential order - Further, it is not competent for any court to go into questions of malafides of the order of detention or ultra vires character of the order of detention or that the order was not passed on the satisfaction of the detaining authority - Judgments of the HCs are set aside - Appeals accepted.

P. N. Bhagwati, J.

(B) Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14, 16, 21, 226, 341, 352 and 359 - Maintenance of Internal Security Act, 1971 - (A) Maintainability and hearing of Habeas Corpus petitions, u/art. 226 of Constitution - In view of the Presidential order u/art. 359(1) of the Constitution - Held, Presidential order bars maintainability of a petition for a writ of habeas corpus where an order of detention is challenged on the ground that it is vitiated by Mala fides, legal or factual, or is based on extraneous considerations or is not under the Act or is not in compliance with it - (B) Scope or extent of judicial scrutiny, particularly, in view of the Presidential order which covers, inter alia, clause (5) of Art. 22, and also in view of sub-section (9) of s. 16A of the Act -- Held, sub-section (9) of s. 16A enacts a genuine rule of evidence and it does not detract from or affect the jurisdiction of the HC u/art. 226 and hence it cannot be successfully assailed as invalid - Also, there is no warrant for reading down sub-section (9) of s. 16A so as to imply an exception in favour of disclosure to the Court, and, on the interpretation placed by me on that provision - It does not constitute an encroachment on the constitutional jurisdiction of the HC u/art. 226 and is accordingly not void - Judgments of the HCs impugned in the appeals set aside - Appeals allowed.

M. Hameedullah Beg, J.

(C) Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14, 16, 21, 226, 341, 352 and 359 - Maintenance of Internal Security Act, 1971 - Maintainability and hearing of Habeas Corpus petitions, u/art. 226 of Constitution - Emergency period - (A) Whether, in view of the Presidential order u/art. 359(1), any writ petition is maintainable u/art. 226, before a HC for Habeas Corpus to enforce the right to personal liberty of a person detained under the Act on the ground that the order of detention or the continued detention is, for any reason, not under or in compliance with Act? - Held, a prima facie valid detention order, that is to say, one duly authenticated and passed by an officer authorised to make it, recording purported satisfaction to detain the petitioner under the Act, which is operative either before or after its confirmation by the Government, is a complete answer to a petition for a writ of Habeas Corpus - Once such an order is shown to exist in response to a notice for a writ of Habeas Corpus, the HC cannot inquire into its validity or vires on the ground of either mala fides of any kind or of non-compliance with any provision of the Act in Habeas Corpus proceedings - Preliminary objection of the State must be accepted in such a case - (B) Objects of the Act and the amendments of it - Held, result of the amendments of the Act, together with the emergency provisions and the Presidential order is clearly that the jurisdiction of HCs is itself affected and they cannot go beyond looking at the prima facie validity of the return made - Production of a duly authenticated order, purporting to have been made by an officer competent to make it u/s. 3 of the Act, is an absolute bar to proceeding further with the hearing of a Habeas Corpus petition - (C) Effect of the Presidential orders on the rights of detenus - Held, Presidential order was a part of an unmistakably expressed intention to suspend the ordinary processes of law in those cases where persons complaint of infringement of their fundamental rights by the executive authorities of the State - Intention of the Parliament itself to bring about this result, so that the jurisdiction of Courts u/art. 226, in this particular type of cases is itself affected for the duration of the emergency, seems clear enough from the provisions of s. 16A(9) of the Act - Object of the Presidential order by suspending the enforcement of the specified rights, was to affect the powers of Courts to afford relief to those the enforcement of whose rights was suspended - Hence, the objection that the powers of the Court u/art. 226 may indirectly be affected is no answer to the direct suspension of rights which was bound to have its effect upon the manner in which jurisdiction is or could reasonably be exercised even if that jurisdiction cannot be itself suspended for all types of cases - It is enough if the ambit of the power to suspend u/art. 359(1) is such as to make exercise of the jurisdiction to protect guaranteed fundamental rights not reasonably possible - Judgment and order of the HC in each case set aside - Appeals allowed.

Y. V. Chandrachud, J.

(D) Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14, 16, 21, 226, 341, 352 and 359 - Maintenance of Internal Security Act, 1971 - Personal liberty - (A) Maintainability and hearing of Habeas Corpus petitions, u/art. 226 of Constitution - Emergency period - Ground that the order of detention or the continued detention is, for any reason, not under or in compliance with Act - Held, order issued by the President u/art. 359(1) of the Constitution does not suspend the fundamental principle that all executive action must have the authority of law to support it - Nor does the Presidential order give to the executive a clatter to disobey the laws made by the Parliament, which is the supreme law-making authority - Presidential order, however, deprives a person of his locus standi to move any court, be it the Supreme Court or the HC, for the enforcement of his fundamental rights which are mentioned in the order - Such deprivation or suspension ensures during the period that the Proclamation of Emergency is in force or for such shorter period as may be specified in the order - Dominant purpose of the petitions filed by the respondents in the HCs is to obtain an order of release from detention by enforcing the right to personal liberty - Former plea is barred by reason of the Presidential order - Latter plea also barred because regard must be had to the substance of the matter and not to the form in which the relief is asked for - (B) Scope or extent of judicial scrutiny, particularly, in view of the Presidential order which covers, inter alia, Clause (5) of Art. 22, and also in view of sub-section (9) of s. 16A of the Act - Held, Presidential order barring investigation or inquiry into the question whether the order of detention is vitiated by mala fides factual or legal, or whether it is based on extraneous considerations or whether the detaining authority had reached his subjective satisfaction validly on proper and relevant material - Presidential order takes away such legal capacity by including Art. 21 within it, the source of the right to personal liberty is immaterial because the words" "conferred by" which occur in Art. 359(1) and in the Presidential order are not words of limitation - Presidential order does not bring about any amendment of Art. 226 and is not open to challenge on that ground - (C) Validity of ss. 16A and s. 18 of the Act - Held, s. 16A(9) of the Act is not unconstitutional on the ground that it constitutes an encroachment on the writ jurisdiction of the HC u/art. 226 - There is no warrant for reading down that section so as to allow the court to inspect the relevant files to the exclusion of all other parties - S. 18 of the MISA does not suffer from the vice of excessive delegation and is a valid piece of legislation - Order accordingly.

Minority View

(E) Hans Raj Khanna, J.

Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14, 16, 21, 226, 341, 352 and 359 - Maintenance of Internal Security Act, 1971 - Issuance of writ of habeas corpus - Power of HCs - Emergency period - Enforcement of fundamental rights - (A) Whether in view of the Presidential order u/art. 359(1) of the Constitution, any petition u/art. 226 before a HC for writ of habeas corpus to enforce the right of personal liberty of a person detained under Act as amended was maintainable? - Held, a Presidential order u/art. 359(1) can suspend during the period of emergency only the right to move any court for enforcement of the fundamental rights mentioned in the order - Rights created by statutes being not fundamental rights can be enforced during the period of emergency despite the Presidential order - Obligations and liabilities flowing from statutory provisions likewise remain unaffected by the Presidential order - Any redress sought from a court of law on the score of breach of statutory provisions would be outside the purview of art. 359(1) and the Presidential order made thereunder - Further, art. 226 under which the HCs can issue writs of habeas corpus is an integral part of the Constitution - No power has been conferred upon any authority in the Constitution for suspending the power of the HC to issue writs in the nature of habeas corpus during the period of emergency - Such a result cannot be brought about by putting some particular construction on the presidential order in question - (B) Whether Art. 21 is the sole repository of the right to personal liberty? - Held, art. 21 cannot be considered to be the sole repository of the right to life and personal liberty - Even in the absence of art. 21 in the Constitution, the State has got no power to deprive a person of his life or personal liberty without the authority of law - That is the essential postulate and basic assumption of the rule of law in every civilised society - According to art. 21, no one can be deprived or his life or personal liberty except in accordance with procedure established by law - Procedure for the exercise of power of depriving a person of his life or personal liberty necessarily postulates the existence of the substantive power - Then art. 21 is in force, law relating to deprivation of life and personal liberty must provide both for the substantive power as when as the procedure for the exercise of such power - When right to move any Court for enforcement of right guaranteed by art. 21 is suspended, it would have the effect of dispensing with the necessity of prescribing procedure for the exercise of substantive power to deprive a person of his life or personal liberty, it cannot have the effect of permitting an authority to deprive a person of his life or personal liberty without the existence of such substantive power - Appeals disposed of.
