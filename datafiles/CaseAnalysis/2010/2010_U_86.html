Union of India and others v Alok Kumar

Supreme Court of India

16 April 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 693; (2010) 5 SCC 349; AIR 2010 SC 2735; 2010 (6) AWC 6257; 2010 (125) FLR 1015; 2010 (5) MLJ(SC) 316; 2010(4) SCALE 92; [2010] 5 S.C.R. 35; 2010 (4) SLR 569

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Service

Keywords: Railway Servants (Discipline & Appeal) Rules, 1968, Public Servants (Inquiries) Act, 1850, Andhra Pradesh Civil Services (Disciplinary Proceedings Tribunal) Rules, 1961, Railway Service Conduct Rules 1966

Summary: Service - Constitution - Central Administrative Tribunal Act, 1985, s. 19 - Railway Servants (Discipline & Appeal) Rules, 1968, r. 9(2) - Indian Railway Act, 1890, s. 3 - Appointment of retired employee as 'inquiry officer' - Irregularities in tender process - Inquiry initiated against the respondent an officer of the Indian Railway Services alleging that respondent had misused his official status by awarding the contract to the contractor of the Department at high rates - Retired officer of Northern Railways appointed as the Inquiry Officer - On completion of inquiry Railway Board imposed punishment on respondent - Appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed by competent authority - On appeal, Tribunal held that the appellants had no authority to appoint a former employee as 'Inquiry Officer' - Tribunal also held that the orders of punishment were vitiated for non-supply of copy of advice/notes to the delinquent - HC accepted the view of the Tribunal and dismissed the writ petitions - Hence, present petitions - (A) Whether under the relevant Rules and provisions of the Act, the Railway Authorities have the jurisdiction to appoint a retired employee of the Department as 'Inquiry Officer' within the ambit of r. 9(2) of the Railway Servants (Discipline & Appeal) Rules, 1968? - Held, r. 9(2) requires the authority to form an opinion, whether it should hold the inquiry itself or should it appoint some other authority to do the needful - Thus, there is an element of discretion vested in the competent authority to appoint 'other authority' for the purposes of conducting a departmental inquiry - Functions of the Inquiry Officer are that of a delegating nature and this delegation ex facie, is limited delegation - Further, r. 9(2) of Rules, 1968 has not specified any qualifications or pre-requisites which need to be satisfied before a person can be appointed as an inquiry officer - It has been left to the discretion of the disciplinary authority - Therefore, unless such exclusion of a former employee of the Government was spelt out specifically in the Rule, it will be difficult for the Court to introduce that element and the principle of implication simplicitor - Thus, of inquiry officers not vitiated within the meaning of r. 9 (2) of Rules, 1968 - (B) Ambit, scope and ramifications of the Rules in relation to the departmental inquiries - Held, Rules in question are a complete code in itself - Language of r. 9 of the Rules, clearly demonstrates that the Rules and the Act are neither interdependent nor convey a legislative intent that a departmental inquiry has to be held under both collectively or at the discretion of the disciplinary authority - (C) Whether expression 'person' used u/s. 3 of the Act and expression 'authority' used u/r. 9(2) contemplates the person to be in service and excludes appointment of a retired railway officer/official as the inquiry officer? - Held, r. 9(2) as well as s. 3 of the Act have used a very different expression i.e. 'other authority' and 'person/persons' - Absence of the word public servant of the Government is conspicuous by its very absence - Thus, both these expressions, should be interpreted as understood in the common parlance - Thus, a person who is engaged by a competent authority to work on a fee or a fixed remuneration can be a public servant - (D) Whether circulars, specifically contemplating preparation of a panel of former officers/employees of the railway department, who can be appointed as inquiry officers in contradiction to the language of r. 9(2)? - Held, when a circular is issued for the purposes of supplementing the removal of ambiguity in the Rule or to achieve the purpose of the Rule more effectively, it can hardly be said that there is a conflict between the two - In the present case, u/r. 9(2), the disciplinary authority has the discretion to appoint a 'Board of Inquiry' or 'other authority' to conduct inquiry against the delinquent officer/official - Circular only aids it further while stating that in the interest of the administration and in consonance with the Rules, former/retired officers of the railway department can be appointed as inquiry officer and submit their report to the disciplinary authority in accordance with law - Circular issued is only supplementing r. 9(2) and is in no way in conflict with the language or Spirit of r. 9(2) - (E) Whether expression "public servant" can be equated to the term "other authority"? - Held, both these expressions cannot be treated as inter-changeable or synonymous - "Public servant" is a term which is well defined and explained in the field of law, while "authority" is a generic term and is used in different places with different meanings and purposes - 'Authority' thus is an expression of wide magnitude and is frequently used not only in legal jurisprudence but also in administrative and executive field - (F) Non-furnishing of advise of Central Vigilance Commission and its consequences - Held, some element of prejudice is essential before an order of imposing penalty can be interfered with by the Court, particularly when the inquiry otherwise had been conducted in accordance with law and no grievance was raised by the respondent on that behalf except the points raised for consideration of the Tribunal - In the present case, documents were of a very general nature and in no way suggested that the concerned disciplinary authorities had taken into consideration any particular notes advising action against the said officer - Thus, no statutory rule or regulation was violated by the appellant nor any CVC notes were actually taken into consideration for imposing the punishment upon the respondent - Judgment of the Tribunal and the HC were contrary to the settled principles of law and thus cannot be sustained - Impugned orders of the Tribunal and HC set aside - Appeals allowed.
