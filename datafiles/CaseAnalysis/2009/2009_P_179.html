M/S Sardar Associates & Ors. v Punjab & Sind Bank & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

31 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Deepak Verma

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 934; (2009) 8 SCC 257; AIR 2010 SC 218; 2010 (1) AWC 158; 2009 (3) BC 705; 2009 (4) CompLJ 35; 2009 (2) DRTC 409; JT 2009 (10) SC 410; 2009 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2425; 2009 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 2929; 2009 (3) RCR(Civil) 927; 2009(10) SCALE 566; [2009] 11 S.C.R. 803

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; RBI

Keywords: Quarterly Rest, Public Sector Banks, Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934 (As Amended up to 2000), Banking Companies (Amendment) Act, 1959

Summary: RBI - Banking & Finance - Securitization and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act, 2002, s. 13(2) - Banking Regulation Act, 1949 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12 and 14 - Recovery of Debts Due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993, s. 21 - Guidelines - Issuance - Binding nature - Banking companies - Appellants defaulted in discharging liabilities - Assets of appellants declared as Non-Performing Assets as per guidelines issued by RBI (RBI) - Proceeding initiated by respondent-Bank for recovery of said amount - Offer made for one time settlement for a sum not accepted by respondent-Bank - Respondent-Bank issued a circular bearing No. 176 dated 18.10.2005 - Appellants approached Debt Recovery Appellate Tribunal (Appellate Tribunal) in terms of s. 21 of 1993 Act - Appellate Tribunal directed respondent-Bank to make one time settlement in terms of guidelines issued by RBI as was prevailing at relevant time - HC set aside said direction - P /&gt;(A) Whether guidelines issued by RBI is binding on respondent-bank? - Reserve Bank is empowered to control and supervise functioning of Scheduled Banks and is entitled to formulate policies which banking companies are bound to follow - Held, Respondent-Bank concededly is a public sector bank and therefore, bound by said guidelines - P /&gt;(B) RBI Guidelines - One time settlement scheme - Discriminatory application of such guidelines - Respondent-bank formulated its scheme and contended that only because the appellants had furnished security to the extent of Rs. 11 crores, the same by itself would entitle it to take recourse to a discriminatory treatment - Whether or not a circular issued by a statutory body for governance and regulation of certain agreements confers a legal right upon aggrieved party in case of non-compliance or complete and absolute deviation from said guidelines by body formulating such circulars? - Respondent-Bank is a 'State' within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution apart from fact that it is bound to follow guidelines issued by RBI - If, therefore, broad policy decisions contained in guidelines were required to be followed, power of Board of Directors to make deviation would only be in relation to some minor matters which does not touch broad aspects of policy decision and in particular one governing non-discriminatory treatment - Held, respondent-Bank is guilty of violation of equality clause contained in RBI guidelines as also art. 14 of Constitution - P /&gt;(C) Whether directions issued by Appellate Tribunal correct? - Central Bank of India v. Ravindra and Others 2001 INDLAW SC 19930 referred - Appellate Tribunal in passing its order followed dicta laid down in Constitution Bench judgment in Central Bank of India wherein it was held that RBI directive have not only statutory flavour, any contravention thereof or any default in compliance therewith is punishable under sub-section (4) of S. 46 of Act, 1949 - Distinction must be made between statutory and non-statutory guidelines - Distinction must also be made between circular which are relevant but not binding on third parties and which are imperative in character Held, judicial discipline mandates bench comprising of two Judges to follow judgments of Constitution Bench having regard to art. 141 of Constitution - Judgment of HC set aside - Appeals allowed.
