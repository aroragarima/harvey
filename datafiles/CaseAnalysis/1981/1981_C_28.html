Radhakanta Deb & Anr. v Commissioner Of Hindu Religious Endowments, Orissa

Supreme Court of India

13 February 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A. Varadarajan, Amarendra Nath Sen

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 207; (1981) 2 SCC 226; AIR 1981 SC 798; 1981 (51) CLT 495; 1981(1) SCALE 304; [1981] 2 S.C.R. 826; 1981 UJ 671

Case Digest

Subject: Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Orissa Religious Endowments Act 1939

Summary: Trusts & Association - Orissa Religious Endowments Act, 1939, s. 62 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Nature of temple - Determination - Respondent/Commissioner of Hindu Religious Endowments/defendant passed order by which temple of appellants, whose deity was appellant, was declared to be public temple and trust and endowment was held to be of public nature and, therefore, was to be governed by Act - Aggrieved appellants/plaintiffs filed suit before Trial Court u/s. 62(2) of the Act - Trial Court decreed suit in favor of appellant and held that deity installed in temple was family deity of 'P' family and endowment being of private nature, Act had no application and Order passed by respondent regarding management was set aside - Respondent appealed before HC - HC reversed the decision of Trial Court and held that temple and deity installed therein being a public endowment fell within four corners of Act and respondent was fully entitled to pass orders for its management - Hence instant appeal - Whether or not appellant-temple was a public endowment as alleged by respondent or a family deity as alleged by appellant - Held, even oral evidence to prove that endowment was of private nature was clear and had not been rebutted by defense - It was surprised to find how HC could hold that endowment was of public nature - HC seemed to have been carried away by factors or considerations which were of very minor nature and by themselves do not prove that endowment was of public nature - HC overlooked fact Shebait or Marfatdars were appointed by founders of endowment and entire management and control of temple was retained by family - SC was unable to agree as to how in these circumstances could it be said that endowment was of public nature - HC seemed to have relied on cl. 15 of Ext-A to come to its decision that endowment was of public nature - HC viewed that under this clause in certain contingencies any member of Vaishnav sect or Hindu resident of village was authorized to exercise powers and functions mentioned in cl. 7 of deed - However SC unable to agree with interpretation placed by HC on cl. 15 of Ext.A, cl.15 merely provides that if in future family becomes extinct and no fit person could be found then any of Baisnab Sampraday or any reputed Hindu of village could take action, namely, to perform the work of deity - This was contingent provision and here also founders did not confer duty of performing all work on members of public but they chose only particular person belonging to particular community which also shows that even if family was to become extinct, private nature of endowment was not to be changed - Indeed if intention was to install the idol in temple by way of public endowment, cl. 15 would have clearly provided that in cast family became extinct members of public or of brotherhood or Govt. could have taken over management - Interpretation of various clauses of documents clearly showed that sufficient care had been taken by Pani family to see that dedication to family deity was not changed even if family becomes extinct - SC was satisfied that conclusions arrived at HC were wrong and were based on misinterpretation of Ext.A and Ext. 1 and misreading of oral evidence led in case, which, runs counter to conclusions arrived at by HC - Order passed by HC was set aside - Decree passed by Trial Court was restored - Appeal allowed.
