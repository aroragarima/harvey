State of Andhra Pradesh v Thakkidiram Reddy and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 August 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M.K. Mukherjee, D.P. Wadhwa

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 1286; (1998) 6 SCC 554; (1998) SCC (Cr) 1488; AIR 1998 SC 2702; AIR 1998 SCW 2750; 1998 (3) CCR 179; 1998 (3) Crimes 167; 1998 CRLJ 4035; 1998 (2) JCC 55; JT 1998 (5) SC 398; 1998 (3) RCR(Criminal) 749; 1998(4) SCALE 416; [1998] 3 S.C.R. 1088; 1998 (6) SLT 586; 1998 (6) Supreme 374

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 148,149,302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 211, 464 - Unlawful assembly - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Trial Court convicted 11 persons u/ss. 148 and 302 r/w s. 149 IPC for murdering one and injuring few others after trespassing into house at night - Accused nos. 1 to 11 preferred appeal before HC which set aside convictions of accused nos. 2 to 11 u/s. 148 and 302 r/w 149 on ground that without strict proof of their specific overt acts they could not be convicted only on statements of five eye witnesses about their culpability and that their testimony was not supported by medical evidence - Hence instant appeals both by accused no. 1 and State Govt against acquittal of accused nos. 2 to 5 and 9 - Whether Order of HC could be sustained - Held, to ascertain whether particular person shared the common object of unlawful assembly, it was not essential to prove that he committed some illegal overt act or had been guilty of some illegal omission in pursuance of common object - Once it was demonstrated from all facts and circumstances of given case that he shared common object of unlawful assembly in furtherance of which some offence was committed or he knew was likely to be committed by any other person, he would be guilty of that offence - In instant case, the fact that at the dead of night mob of persons armed with various weapons forcibly trespassed into house of deceased after breaking open door, would clearly indicate that they had formed an unlawful assembly with common object to commit some offence and each of them would be liable for offence committed or knew likely to be committed by any of the members of the mob - Thus, six accused would be guilty for offence committed by any other member of that mob, in furtherance of common object, without proof of any overt act committed by them - When injuries were considered in the context of facts, that there was bitter enmity between the parties, only conclusion that should be drawn was that they formed unlawful assembly with common object of committing murder of deceased - Appeal of accused no. 1 was dismissed and appeal by State Govt was allowed - Masalti v. State of U.P, 1964 Indlaw SC 393, relied on - Appeals disposed of.
