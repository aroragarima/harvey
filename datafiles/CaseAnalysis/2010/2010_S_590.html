Competition Commission of India v Steel Authority of India Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

09 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, S.H. Kapadia, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 737; (2010) 10 SCC 744; 2010(5) ALL MR 934; 2010 (4) CompLJ 1; JT 2010 (10) SC 26; 2011 (2) MLJ(SC) 271; 2010(9) SCALE 291; [2010] 11 S.C.R. 112

Case Digest

Subject: Competition; Trade

Keywords: Enterprise Act, 2002, Clayton Act, 1914, U.P. Imposition of Ceiling of Land Holdings Act, 1960, Federal Trade Commission Act, 1914, Competition (Amendment) Act, 2007, Restrictive Practices Act, 1956, Competition Act, 1988, Robinson-Patman Act, 1936, Competition Act, 1998, Sherman Act, 1890, European Council Regulation

Summary: (A) Competition - Trade - Competition Act, 2002, ss. 26, 57 - Competition Commission of India (General) Regulations, 2009, regn. 35 - A private Company (informant) informed Competition Commission (Commission) alleging that 1st respondent/Company had entered into an exclusive supply agreement with Indian Railways for supply of rails - Informant contended that 1st respondent had abused its dominant position in the market and deprived others of fair competition and therefore, acted contrary to ss. 3(4), 4(1) of the Act - Commission referred the matter to Director General for investigation and submission of the report and passed an ad-interim report restraining the 1st respondent from proceeding further - 1st respondent challenged the direction of the Commission before the Appellate Tribunal - Commission filed an impleadment petition before the Appellate Tribunal and questioned the maintainability of appeal - Appellate Tribunal dismissed Commission's impleadment petition and held that present appeal was maintainable - Appellate Tribunal, further, held that Commission had failed to give reasons for the order and hence, there was a violation of principles of natural justice - Hence the present appeal by the Commission - Whether direction of the Commission is appealable - Held, the direction u/s. 26(1) after formation of a prima facie opinion is a direction simpliciter to cause an investigation into the matter - Issuance of such a direction is an administrative direction to one of its own wings departmentally and is without entering upon any adjudicatory process and it does not effectively determine any right or obligation of the parties to the lis - Mere direction for investigation to one of the wings of the Commission is akin to a departmental proceeding which does not entail civil consequences for any person, particularly, in light of the strict confidentiality that is expected to be maintained by the Commission in terms of s. 57 of the Act and regn. 35 of the Regulations - Wherever, in the course of the proceedings before the Commission, the Commission passes a direction or interim order which is at the preliminary stage and of preparatory nature without recording findings which will bind the parties and where such order will only pave the way for final decision, it would not make that direction as an order or decision which affects the rights of the parties and therefore, is not appealable - Such orders cannot be held to be appealable within the meaning and language of s. 53A of the Act and also on the principle that they are not orders which determine the rights of the parties - Therefore, Commission's order directing investigation is not made specifically appealable u/s. 53A(1)(a) of the Act and hence no appeal would lie against it - Appeal partly allowed.

(B) Competition - Trade - Competition Act, 2002, s. 26 - Direction by Competition Commission - Principles of natural justice - Requirement of - Whether the person against whom the direction issued by the Commission for investigation is entitled to be heard before passing such order - Held, jurisdiction of the Commission, to act u/s. 26(2), does not contemplate any adjudicatory function - Commission is not expected to give notice to the parties, i.e. the informant or the affected parties and hear them at length, before forming its opinion - Function of forming of opinion is of a very preliminary nature and it is a departmental function and at that stage, it does not condemn any person and therefore, application of audi alteram partem is not called for - Formation of a prima facie opinion departmentally (Director General, being appointed by the Central Government to assist the Commission, is one of the wings of the Commission itself) does not amount to an adjudicatory function but is merely of administrative nature - Commission can direct the investigation to be conducted and report to be submitted to the Commission itself or close the case in terms of s. 26(2) of the Act, which order itself is appealable before the Tribunal and only after this stage, there is a specific right of notice and hearing available to the aggrieved/affected party - In view of the nature of the functions required to be performed by the Commission in terms of s. 26(1), the right of notice of hearing is not contemplated under the provisions of s. 26(1) of the Act - Appeal partly allowed.

(C) Competition - Trade - Investigation order by Competition Commission - Reasonable order - Requirement of - Whether the direction for investigation issued by the Commission would be required to support with reasons - Held, at the stage of forming a prima facie view, as required u/s. 26(1) of the Act, the Commission may not really record detailed reasons, but must express its mind in no uncertain terms that it is of the view that prima facie case exists, requiring issuance of direction for investigation to the Director General - Such view should be recorded with reference to the information furnished to the Commission and such opinion should be formed on the basis of the records, including the information furnished and reference made to the Commission under the various provisions of the Act - Therefore, Commission is similarly expected to write appropriate reasons on every issue while passing an order u/ss. 26 to 28 of the Act - Appeal partly allowed.

(D) Competition - Trade - Order of Commission - Appeal proceedings before the Appellate Tribunal - Necessary parties - Whether the Commission would be a necessary party to the appeal proceedings before the Appellate Tribunal - Held, wide discretion is vested in the Court/appropriate forum in regard to impleadment of necessary and proper parties to the proceedings and such discretion has to be exercised in accordance with provisions of law and the principles enunciated by various judicial pronouncements - Consideration before the Court, while determining such a question, is whether the said party is a necessary or a proper party and its presence before the Court is essential for complete and effective adjudication of the subject matter, inter alia, it should also be kept in mind that multiplicity of litigation is to be avoided and that the necessary or proper party should not be left out from the proceedings, particularly, before the tribunal or the forum - In the proceedings, which are initiated by the Commission suo moto, it should be dominus litis of such proceedings while in other cases, the Commission being a regulatory body would be a proper party discharging inquisitorial, regulatory as well as adjudicatory functions and its presence before the Tribunal would be proper - Appeal partly allowed.

(E) Competition - Trade - Ad-interim restraint order - Legality - Whether the Commission can pass an ad-interim restraint order against 1st respondent - Held, Commission can pass ex-parte ad interim restraint orders in terms of s. 33, only after having applied its mind as to the existence of a prima facie case and issue direction to the Director General for conducting an investigation in terms of s. 26(1) of the Act - Commission has the power to pass ad interim ex-parte injunction orders, but only upon recording its due satisfaction as well as its view that the Commission deemed it necessary not to give a notice to the other side - In all cases where ad interim ex parte injunction is issued, the Commission must ensure that it makes the notice returnable within a very short duration so that there is no abuse of the process of law and the very purpose of the Act is not defeated - Therefore, restraint order passed by the Commission is valid - Appeal partly allowed.
