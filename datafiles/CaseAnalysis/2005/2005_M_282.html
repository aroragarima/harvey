Â State of Gujarat and Others v Mirzapur Moti Kureshi Kassab Jamat And Others.

Supreme Court of India

26 October 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

R.C. Lahoti, A.K. Mathur, B.N. Agrawal, Arun Kumar, G.P. Mathur, C.K. Thakker, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1247; (2005) 8 SCC 534; AIR 2006 SC 212; 2006 (1) G.L.R. 294; 2006 (2) JhrCR(SC) 272; JT 2005 (12) SC 580; 2005(8) SCALE 661; [2005] Supp4 S.C.R. 582

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Environment, Wildlife & Animal; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Fundamental Duties, Companies Act, 1862, The Directive Principles Of State Policy, Municipal Corporation Act, Constitutionality, Bombay Animal Preservation Act, 1954, Bombay Animal Preservation (Gujarat Amendment) Act, 1994, Uttar Pradesh Prevention of Cow Slaughter Act, 1955, Bihar Preservation And Improvement Of Animals Act, 1956, Kerala Education Bill, 1957, Bombay Animal Preservation (Gujarat Amendment) Act, 1979, Bombay Animal Preservation (Gujarat Amendment) Ordinance, 1993, Bombay Animal Preservation (Gujarat Extension and Amendment) Act, 1961, Bombay Animal Preservation Act, 1948, C.P. and Berar Animal Preservation Act, 1949, Fines and Recoveries Act, 1833, M.P. Agricultural Cattle Preservation Act, 1959, M.P. Krishik Pashu Parirakshan (Sanshodhan) Adhiniyam, 1991, Prescription Act, 1832, Saurashtra Animal Preservation Act, 1956

Summary: (A) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Constitution - Bombay Animal Preservation Act, 1954 - Bombay Animal Preservation (Gujarat Amendment) Act, 1994, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 39, 47, 48 - Total prohibition against slaughter of calves - S. 2 of the Amendment Act - Constitutionality of - State imposed total prohibition against slaughter of calves in order to give effect to the policy of the State towards securing the principles laid down in arts. 47, 48 and 39(b)(c) of the Constitution by way of amending the 1954 parent Act through s.2 of the 1994 Amending Act - Respondents challenged the vires of 1994 Act before HC - Having struck down the impugned legislation as ultra vires the Constitution, HC held that the Amendment Act imposed an unreasonable restriction on the fundamental rights and animals could be slaughtered consistently with the provisions of the parent Act as it stood prior to the amendment - Whether HC order is sustainable - Held, recognizing the fact that the cow and its calves have a significant role to play in the agricultural and rural economy of the country, the Government felt that it was necessary to formulate measures for their development in all possible ways - In view of the persistent demands for action to be taken to prevent cow slaughter, the Government also felt and expressed the need to review the relevant laws of the land relating to protection, preservation, development and well-being of cattle and to take measures to secure the cattle wealth of India - Therefore, ban on slaughter of calves as imposed by the impugned enactment is in the interests of the general public within the meaning of cl. (6) of art. 19 of the Constitution - No apparent inconsistency between the Directive Principles which persuaded the State to pass the law and the Fundamental Rights canvassed before the HC by the respondents and hence the constitutionality of Amendment Act is upheld - Appeals allowed.

(B) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 48 - Whether art. 48 of the Directive Principles of State Policy is subject to the Fundamental Right guaranteed u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Held, while interpreting the interplay of rights and restrictions, Part-III (Fundamental Rights) and Part-IV (Directive Principles) have to be read together - Restriction which can be placed on the rights listed in art. 19(1) are not subject only to arts. 19(2) to 19(6); the provisions contained in the chapter on Directive Principles of State Policy can also be pressed into service and relied on for the purpose of adjudging the reasonability of restrictions placed on the Fundamental Rights - Appeals allowed.

(C) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 48 - Expression 'milch or draught cattle' in art. 48 - Meaning of - Held, expression 'milch or draught cattle' as employed in art. 48 of the Constitution is a description of a classification or species of cattle as distinct from cattle which by their nature are not milch or draught and the said words do not include milch or draught cattle, which on account of age or disability, cease to be functional for those purposes either temporarily or permanently - Said words take colour from the preceding words 'cows or calves' - A specie of cattle which is milch or draught for a number of years during its span of life is to be included within the said expression - On ceasing to be milch or draught it cannot be pulled out from the category of 'other milch and draught cattle' - Appeals allowed.

(D) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Preamble and Statement of Objects - Whether Preamble and Statement of objects and Reason of the legislation need to be considered while judging the reasonableness of any restriction imposed on the Fundamental Rights of the individuals - Held, Preamble and the Statement of Objects and Reasons appended to any legislation are evidence of legislative judgment and intend and they indicate the thought process of the elected representatives of the people and their cognizance of the prevalent state of affairs, impelling them to enact the law - Therefore, Objects and Reasons constitute important factors which amongst others will be taken into consideration by the court in judging the reasonableness of any restriction imposed on the Fundamental Rights of the individuals - Court would begin with a presumption of reasonability of the restriction, more so when the facts stated in the Statement of Objects and Reasons and the Preamble are taken to be correct and they justify the enactment of law for the purpose sought to be achieved - Appeals allowed.

(E) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - Total prohibition - Scope - Whether regulation or restriction u/arts. 19(5) and 19(6) includes total prohibition - Held, ban is total with regard to the slaughter of one particular class of cattle - Ban is not on the total activity of butchers; they are left free to slaughter cattle other than those specified in the Act - It is not that the respondents survive only by slaughtering calves and they can slaughter animals other than calves and carry on their business activity - Though it is permissible to place a total ban amounting to prohibition on any profession, occupation, trade or business subject to satisfying the test of being reasonable in the interest of the general public, yet, in the present case banning slaughter of cow progeny is not a prohibition but only a restriction - Appeals allowed.
