State of West Bengal and others v Associated Contractors

Supreme Court of India

10 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, R.M. Lodha, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 605; (2015) 1 SCC 32; AIR 2015 SC 260; 2015 (1) ALD(SC) 181; 2015(1) ALL MR 458; 2014 (4) CalLT(SC) 22; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 173; 2014 (4) CompLJ 226; 2015 (1) CTC 775; JT 2014 (11) SC 260; 2014 (4) KLT 91; 2015 (3) MahLJ 9; 2015 (2) MPLJ 355; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 694; 2014(10) SCALE 394; [2014] 10 S.C.R. 426

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Summary: (A) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss.2(1)(e) and 31(4) - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss.9. 34 and 37 - Constitution of India, 1950, art.136 - Dispute - Arbitration - Sustainability - Whether the SC is a court within the meaning of s. 2(1)(e) of 1940 Act -

Held, s. 2(1)(e) of 1940 Act is materially different from its predecessor contained in S. 2(c) of the 1940 Act. There are a variety of reasons as to why the SC cannot possibly be considered to be 'court' within the meaning of s. 2(1)(e) of 1940 Act even if it retains seisin over the arbitral proceedings. Definition is exhaustive and recognizes only one of two possible courts that could be "court" for the purpose of s. 2(1)(e) of 1940 Act. Secondly, under the 1940 Act, the expression 'civil court' has been held to be wide enough to include an appellate court and, therefore would include the SC under the 1940 Act. Even though this proposition itself is open to doubt, as the SC exercising jurisdiction u/art. 136 of Constitution is not an ordinary Appellate Court, suffice it to say that even this reason does not obtain under the present definition, which speaks of either the Principal Civil Court or the HC exercising original jurisdiction. Thirdly, if an application would have to be preferred to the SC directly, the appeal that is available so far as applications u/ss. 9 and 34 of 1996 Act are concerned, provided for u/s. 37 of 1996 Act, would not be available. Any further appeal to the SC u/art. 136 of Constitution would also not be available. The only other argument that could possibly be made is that all definition sections are subject to context to the contrary. The context of s. 42 of 1940 Act does not in any manner lead to a conclusion that the word 'court' in s. 42 of 1940 Act should be construed otherwise than as defined. The context of s. 42 of 1940 Act is merely to see that one court alone shall have jurisdiction over all applications with respect to arbitration agreements which context does not in any manner enable the SC to become a 'court' within the meaning of s. 42 of 1940 Act. It has aptly been stated that the rule of forum conveniens is expressly excluded by s. 42 of 1940 Act. JSW Steel Ltd. vs. Jindal Praxair Oxygen Co.Ltd., 2006 Indlaw SC 451, relied on. S. 42 of 1940 Act is also markedly different from s. 31(4) of the 1940 Act in that the expression 'has been made in a court competent to entertain it' does not find place in s. 42 of 1940 Act. This is for the reason that, u/s. 2(1)(e)of 1940 Act, the competent Court is fixed as the Principal Civil Court exercising original jurisdiction or a HC exercising original civil jurisdiction, and no other court. Thus, SC cannot be 'court' for the purposes of s. 42 of 1940 Act. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss.2(1)(e) and 31(4) - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss.9. 34 and 37 - Constitution of India, 1950, art.136 - Dispute - Arbitration - Sustainability - Whether s. 42 of 1940 Act applies after the arbitral proceedings come to an end -

Held, expression 'with respect to an arbitration agreement' are words of wide import and would take in all applications made before during or after the arbitral proceedings are over. In an earlier judgment, Kumbha Mawji v. Dominion of India, 1953 Indlaw SC 12, the question which arose before the SC was whether the expression used in s. 31(4) of the 1940 Act 'in any reference' would include matters that are after the arbitral proceedings are over and have culminated in an award. It was held that the words 'in any reference' cannot be taken to mean 'in the course of a reference', but mean 'in the matter of a reference' and that such phrase is wide enough and comprehensive enough to cover an application made after the arbitration is completed and the final Award is made. Expression used in s. 42 of 1940 Act is wider being 'with respect to an arbitration agreement' and would certainly include such applications. Appeals dismissed.

(C) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss.2(1)(e) and 31(4) - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss.9. 34 and 37 - Constitution of India, 1950, art.136 - Dispute - Arbitration - Sustainability - Whether s. 42 of 1940 Act would apply in cases where an application made in a court was found to be without jurisdiction -

Held, u/s.31(4) of 1940 Act, it has been held in FCI represented by Managing Director & Anr. v. A.M. Ahmed & Co., through MD & Anr., 1998 Indlaw SC 2252 and Neycer India Ltd. v. GNB Ceramics Ltd., 2002 Indlaw SC 823 that s. 31(4) of the 1940 Act would not be applicable if it were found that an application was to be made before a court which had no jurisdiction. In Jatinder Nath v. Chopra Land Developers Pvt. Ltd., 2007 Indlaw SC 228 and Rajasthan State Electrical Board v. Universal Petrol Chemical Limited, 2009 Indlaw SC 9 and Swastik Gases (P) Ltd. v. Indian Oil Corporation, 2013 Indlaw SC 414, it was held that where the agreement between the parties restricted jurisdiction to only one particular court, that court alone would have jurisdiction as neither s. 31(4) of 1940 Act nor s. 42 of 1940 Act contains a non-obstante clause wiping out a contrary agreement between the parties. It has thus been held that applications preferred to courts outside the exclusive court agreed to by parties would also be without jurisdiction. Appeals dismissed.
