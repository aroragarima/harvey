Jumni and Others v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

12 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 163; (2014) 11 SCC 355; 2014 CRLJ 1936; JT 2014 (8) SC 235; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1460; 2014 (2) OLR 52; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 150; 2014(3) SCALE 588; [2014] 3 S.C.R. 673

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Severability of a dying declaration

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Severability of a dying declaration - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) allegedly poured kerosene on the deceased and set her on fire and thereby committed offence punishable u/s. 302 of IPC - Trial Court found appellants guilty u/s. 302 of IPC - Aggrieved appellants filed an appeal before HC, which was dismissed by impugned judgment - Hence, instant appeals - Whether deceased dying declaration could split up to segregate the case of alibi witnesses from the case of other accused persons -

Held, testimony of DW2 (tenant of alibi witness/accused) was not correctly appreciated because of a typing error in transcribing it from the original record - DW2 had seen alibi witnesses/accused 'P' and 'R' at 7.45 a.m on 5-4-1996 (and not at 5.45 a.m. as wrongly transcribed in impugned judgment) - Consequently, accused 'P' and 'R' could not have been in village at 7.30 a.m on 5-4-1996 - That evidence was gone unchallenged - Although HC has given due weightage to dying declaration of deceased but having accepted it, it has tried to pick holes in the defence evidence to justify the contents of the dying declaration -That was not the correct manner of approaching the evidence brought forth by accused 'P' and 'R' - Alibi witnesses have made out a strong case of demonstrating the improbability of 'P' and 'R' being involved in the incident of beating up deceased at about 12.00 noon on 4-4-1996, of stopping her at about 3.00 p.m. from going to the police to lodge a complaint and setting her on fire at about 7.30 a.m on 5-4-1996 - It was not possible to accept the involvement of 'P and 'R' in the events that took place on the 2 fateful days - Nevertheless, it was quite possible that the other 4 accused were involved in beating up deceased on 4-4-1996 and setting her on fire on 5-4-1996 - But, what was of equal importance was that neither the Trial Court nor HC adverted to the crucial evidence of DW8 who stated that he saw smoke coming out of DW2's tenement and children were making a noise - When he reached there, he saw flames and smoke coming out from the ventilator of DW2's tenement and along with 'G', he had to break down the door of the tenement which was locked from inside and they found deceased on fire - If that statement of neigbour of DW2 was correct, and there did not seem any reason to doubt it since nothing was put to him in that regard in cross examination, a case of suicide by deceased was a possibility - At that stage, it might be noted that IO (PW10) could not say if the bolt of the tenement was broken or not - From the dying declaration it was quite clear that deceased was very disturbed on the morning of 5-4-1996 and that was why she broke her bangles in the presence of mother in-law - That might be because of the events of the previous day or her being a victim of continuous harassment - That, coupled with a lack of response from mother in-law on the morning of 5-4-1996 might have completely frustrated deceased Devi leading her to commit suicide - Whatever be the cause of deceased being upset, evidence of DW8 was not been challenged and so it could not be glossed over - Testimony of the alibi witnesses of two of 4 appellants deserves acceptance and dying declaration so closely concerns all 4 appellants that it was not possible to sever the role of the sets of appellants, resulting in giving the benefit of doubt to the remaining two appellants - Charge of murder against appellant was not proved beyond reasonable doubt - Appeals allowed.
