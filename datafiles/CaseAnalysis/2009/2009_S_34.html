Dr. C. P. Sreekumar, M. S. (Ortho) v S. Ramanujam

Supreme Court of India

01 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

H.S. Bedi, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 568; (2009) 7 SCC 130; 2009 (5) ALD(SC) 93; 2009 (4) AWC 3381; 2009 (6) Bom.C.R. 832; 2009 (2) CPJ(Sc) 48; 2009 (3) G.L.R. 2244; 2009 (4) MPLJ 606; 2009(6) SCALE 613; [2009] 7 S.C.R. 272; 2010 (1) UC 303

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection

Keywords: Appeals Disposed Of, Medical Profession, Professional Negligence

Summary: Consumer Protection - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Medical Negligence - Leg injury - Simple hairline fracture Garden type I developed into type III - Decision by appellant/doctor to perform hemiarthroplasty instead of going in for the internal fixation procedure - (A) Whether appellant had the competence to perform a hemiarthroplasty and whether he had chosen this procedure as he was not qualified for the internal fixation procedure? - (B) Whether it was the negligence of the staff of hospital, of which appellant was the director, in handling the patient/respondent while taking him for X-ray which had aggravated Garden type I fracture to type III fracture? - (C) Assuming that some radical procedure was necessary, whether hemiarthroplasty was the appropriate one in the light of the fact that the respondent was at the relevant time 42 years of age? - Evaluation of case of medical negligence as criminal offence and tort laid down in 'Jacob Mathew vs. State of Punjab (2005)6 SCC 1', wherein test laid down in 'Bolam vs. Friern Hospital Management Committee' was adopted - Standard of care and the skill attained was that of the ordinary competent medical practitioner exercising an ordinary degree of professional skill - (A) Evidence of appellant on various qualifications, and expertise in the field perused - MBBS, Masters in General Surgical Science, Masters in Orthopaedic Sciences from London University, 15 years of experience in the field - No evidence from respondent - Held, contention as to expertise and qualification, rejected - (B) No evidence in support for staff's negligence; 'it is the obligation of the complainant to provide facta probanda as well as the facta probantia' - Appellant in his evidence contended that due to the condition of patient and the use of de-rotation boots, muscular spasm can be the possible consequence and thus the reason for displacement from type I to type III fracture - Held, contention as to staff negligence, rejected - (C) Various texts of schools of thought looked into - Held, no firm conclusion can be drawn as to adoption of two of the methods, though internal fixation is ordinarily the favoured one but not the only option as some of the texts also proceed on a school of thought which prescribe that in order to avoid long drawn out recovery and other complications, it is advisable to go in for a hemiarthroplasty notwithstanding the age factor - Thus it cannot be said with certainty that the appellant was grossly remiss in going in for hemiarthroplasty - Held finally, no professional negligence - Appellant/doctor's appeal allowed -Appeals disposed of.
