Dnyanoba Bhaurao Shemade v Maroti Bhaurao Marnor

Supreme Court of India

05 February 1999

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Majmudar, M. Jagannadha Rao

Where Reported

1999 Indlaw SC 1133; (1999) 2 SCC 471; AIR 1999 SC 864; 1999 (2) AWC 1110; 1999 (3) Bom.C.R. 85; 1999 (1) CLT 155; 1999 (1) CRC 439; JT 1999 (1) SC 265; 1999 RD 180; 1999(1) SCALE 262; 1999 (1) SLT 504; 1999 (1) Supreme 316

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 908, s. 100 - Second appeal - Intereference by HC - Appellant was in need of money and when the respondent-defendant advanced him Rs. 1,000, he got executed a sale deed for land in question from the appellant as a security - However, when appellant tries to return the amount, respondent demanded Rs. 500 more for re-executing the sale deed - As the respondent tried to interfere with the appellant possession, appellant filed suit for a declaration that the sale deed was null and void - Trial Court held that the appellant had proved that he executed the sale deed as a security for the amount of Rs. 500 and also proved that he was in possession of the suit land - Trial Court held that the document in question was liable to be declared as void and that the appellant was entitled to a permanent injunction against the respondent - On appeal, appellate court dismissed the appeal subject to the modification that the defendant shall re-convey the suit property to the plaintiff on receipt of the amount of Rs 500 - HC in second appeal reversed the decrees of both the Courts below and dismissed the appellant's suit - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, both the Courts below, held, on appreciation of evidence, that the theory of oral agreement to exchange was not believable as the version regarding the same was full of contradictions. Thus, respondent's version for supporting the theory of oral agreement to exchange of land was disbelieved by the Trial Court as well as the Appellate Court. The Single Judge of the HC, however, disagreed with the said finding reached by both the Courts below. The approach adopted by the Single Judge in the impugned judgment to the effect that as both the Courts below ignored the weight of prepondering circumstances and allowed their judgments to be influenced by inconsequential matters, the HC would be justified in re-appreciating the evidence and in coming to its own independent conclusions is, to say the least, patently erroneous in law and cannot be sustained. Whether a finding of fact reached by courts below is against the weight of evidence or not is a question which will remain in the realm of appreciation of evidence and does not project any question of law, much less, any substantial question of law which can enable the HC in second appeal to upset such a finding of fact. The impugned judgment of the HC is set aside and the decision of the learned trial Judge decreeing the appellant's suit and as confirmed with modification in appeal are restored. Appeal allowed.
