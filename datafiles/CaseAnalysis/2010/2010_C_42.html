Â Economic Transport Organization v Charan Spinning Mills (P) Limited and Another

Supreme Court of India

17 February 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, K.G. Balakrishnan, D.K. Jain, P. Sathasivam, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 122; 2010 ACJ 2288; 2010 (3) ALD(SC) 58; 2010 (3) AWC 2551; 2010 (4) Bom.C.R. 599; 2010 (1) CCC 433; 2010 (2) CTC 295; JT 2010 (2) SC 271; 2010 (3) MLJ(SC) 1347; 2010 (2) RCR(Civil) 232; 2010(2) SCALE 427; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 887; 2010 (2) UC 1046

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation; Consumer Protection; Insurance

Keywords: Carrier, Presumption Of Negligence, Subrogation, Right Of Subrogation, Doctrine of subrogation

Summary: (A) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Carriers & Transportation - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Carriers Act, 1865, ss. 8 and 9 - Matter referred to a larger bench on the ground that decision in Oberai Forwarding Agency v. New India Assurance Co. Ltd. 2002 (2) SCC 407, required reconsideration - SC in said case held that where there is a subrogation simpliciter in favour of the insurer, insurer could maintain an action in the Consumer Forum in the name of the assured, who as consignor was a consumer - However, when there is an assignment of the rights of the assured in favour of the insurer, the insurer as assignee cannot file a complaint under the Act, as it was not a 'consumer' under the Act - Even if the assured was a co-complainant, it would not enable the insurer to maintain a complaint under the Act, if it was an assignee of the claim - Further, that the document executed by the assured though titled as 'letter of subrogation' was, in fact, an assignment by the assured of its rights in favour of the insurer - Held, Oberai is not good law insofar as it construes a Letter of subrogation-cum-assignment, as a pure and simple assignment - But to the extent it holds that an insurer alone cannot file a complaint under the Act, the decision was correct - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Carriers & Transportation - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Carriers Act, 1865, ss. 8 and 9 - Assured entrusted the consignment for transportation to the carrier (appellant) - Consignment was insured by the assured with the insurer - Goods in the consignment were damaged in an accident - Insurer settled the claim of the assured - On receiving the payment, the consignor executed a Letter of Subrogation-cum-Special Power of Attorney in favour of the Insurer - Thereafter, consignor and insurer filed a complaint under the Consumer Protection Act, 1986 against the appellant claiming compensation, alleging negligence and deficiency in service - District Forum allowed the complaint and held that the failure to deliver the consignment in sound condition was a deficiency in service, in view of the unrebutted presumption of negligence arising u/ss. 8 and 9 of the Carriers Act, 1865 - Said order was affirmed by the State Forum and the National Forum - Whether relief could be granted in a complaint against the carrier/service provider, in the absence of any proof of negligence? - Held, where the consignor establishes loss or damage or non- delivery of goods, it is deemed that negligence on the part of the carrier is established - Fact that in pursuance of a contract of insurance, the assured received from the insurer, the value of the goods lost, either fully or in part, does not erase or reduce the liability of the wrongdoer responsible for the loss - Therefore, the assured as a consumer, could file a complaint under the Act, even after the insurer had settled its claim in regard to the loss - Having regard to the presumption regarding negligence u/s. 9 of Carriers Act, it was not necessary for the complainants to prove further that the loss/damage was due to the negligence of the appellant or its driver - Presumption regarding negligence was not rebutted - Therefore, the District Forum was justified in allowing the complaint brought by the assured represented by the insurer and the insurer - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Carriers & Transportation - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Carriers Act, 1865, ss. 8 and 9 - (A) Difference between 'subrogation' and 'assignment' - Held, equitable assignment of the rights and remedies of the assured in favour of the insurer, implied in a contract of indemnity, known as 'subrogation' - An 'assignment' refers to a transfer of a right by an instrument for consideration - (B) Whether the document executed by the assured in favour of the insurer is a subrogation simpliciter, or a subrogation-cum-assignment? - Held, when the insurer obtains an instrument from the assured on settlement of the claim, whether it will be a deed of subrogation, or subrogation-cum-assignment, would depend upon the intention of parties as evidenced by the wording of the document - Title or caption of the document, by itself, may not be conclusive - If the terms clearly show that the intention was to have only a subrogation, use of the words "assign, transfer and abandon in favour of" would in the context be construed as referring to subrogation and nothing more - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Where the insurer pays the amount of loss to the assured, whether the insurer as subrogee, can lodge a complaint under the Act, either in the name of the assured, or in the joint names of the insurer and assured as co- complainants? - Held, insurer, as subrogee, can file a complaint under the Act either in the name of the assured (as his attorney holder) or in the joint names of the assured and the insurer for recovery of the amount due from the service provider - Insurer may also request the assured to sue the wrong doer (service provider) - Even if the letter of subrogation executed by the assured in favour of the insurer contains in addition to the words of subrogation, any words of assignment, the complaint would be maintainable so long as the complaint is in the name of the assured and insurer figures in the complaint only as an attorney holder or subrogee of the assured - Appeal dismissed.

(E) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Filing of complaint - Rights of insurer under a letter of subrogation-cum-assignment - Held, insurer cannot in its own name maintain a complaint before a consumer forum under the Act, even if it's right is traced to the terms of a Letter of subrogation-cum-assignment executed by the assured - Appeal dismissed.

(F) Consumer Protection - Insurance - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Principles relating to subrogation - Held, equitable right of subrogation arises when the insurer settles the claim of the assured, for the entire loss - Subrogation does not terminate nor puts an end to the right of the assured to sue the wrong-doer and recover the damages for the loss - Subrogation only entitles the insurer to receive back the amount paid to the assured, in terms of the principles of subrogation - Where the assured executes a Letter of Subrogation, reducing the terms of subrogation, the rights of the insurer vis-'-vis the assured will be governed by the terms of the Letter of Subrogation - Subrogation enables the insurer to exercise the rights of the assured against third parties in the name of the assured - Where the assured executed a subrogation-cum- assignment in favour of the insurer (as contrasted from a subrogation), the assured is left with no right or interest - But as the instrument is a subrogation- cum-assignment, and not a mere assignment, the insurer has the choice of suing in its own name, or in the name of the assured, if the instrument so provides - Appeal dismissed.
