Justice (Retd.) Markandey Katju v Lok Sabha and another

Supreme Court of India

15 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Uday Umesh Lalit, T.S. Thakur, R. Banumathi

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 906; (2017) 2 SCC 384; AIR 2017 SC 1379; 2017 (1) AWC 485; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 300; 2017 (1) OLR 466; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 849A; 2016(12) SCALE 912

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Limitation, Speaker, Government of India Act, 1935, Members Of Parliament, Privileges, Rules Of Procedure, Indian Independence Act, 1947, Parliamentary Privilege, Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, Constitution of India, 1950, Rajya Sabha Rules, Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act, 2004, Indian Dominion Legislature. India (Provisional Constitution) Order, 1947, Imperial Laws Application Act, 1988, Legislature Act, 1908, Defamation Act, 1996

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 121, 122, 105(2), 194, 19(1)(a) - Rajya Sabha Rules, rr. 156, 187, 203 - Defamation Act, 1996, s. 3 - Defamation - Claim for Cancellation of Resolution - Petitioner former judge of SC published post on his face book page in respect of father of nation - Such post evoked immediate response and resolution was moved by upper house of parliament - Petitioner does not received any response from either chairman of upper house or speaker of lower house - Hence, instant writ petition - Whether exercise of power u/art. 105(1) of Constitution was subjected to restriction.

Held, art. 105 of Constitution confers immunity inter alia in respect of 'anything said in Parliament'. Thus 'freedom of speech in Parliament' is absolute, unfettered and is subject only to such of the provisions of Constitution which relate to regulation of procedure in Parliament; this is recognition of the fact that Members need to be free of all constraints of what they say in Parliament; that art. 105(2) of Constitution puts negatively what art. 105(1)states affirmatively. Both clauses must be read together to determine their content; that a vote, whether cast by voice or gesture is an extension of speech or a substitute for speech, that if the impugned speech amounts to libel or becomes actionable or indictable under any provision of law, immunity has been conferred from any action in any Court; and that the Constitution makers attached so much importance to the absolute freedom in debates that they thought it necessary to confer complete immunity on the legislators from any action in any Court in respect of their speeches. The only restriction in the Constitution as regards subject matter of any debate or discussion in parliament is to be found in art. 121 of Constitution. It is entirely left to the discretion of the Presiding Officer to permit discussion so long as it is within the confines of Rules of Procedure. R. 156 of the Rules states that a resolution could relate to a matter of general public interest and u/r. 155 of the Rules, a resolution could be in the form of a declaration of opinion by Rajya Sabha. No separate law is required to confer jurisdiction to deal with the opinions expressed by individuals and citizens during debates. It would certainly be within the powers of the House to have a discussion or debate concerning such opinions. Hence, petition is devoid of any merit and the same is dismissed. Petition dismissed.
