S. Rangarajan and another v P. Jagjivan Ram

Supreme Court of India

30 March 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Jagannatha Shetty, K.N. Singh, Kuldip Singh

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 549; (1989) 2 SCC 574; JT 1989 (2) SC 70; 1990 (1) MLJ(SC) 17; 1989(1) SCALE 812; [1989] 2 S.C.R. 204

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Media & Entertainment

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Examining Committee, Board Of Film Censors, Film Censor

Summary: Media & Entertainment - Constitution - Cinematograph Act, 1952 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(a) and 19(2) - 'U' certificate - Grant - Revoke - Legality - Appellant producer of film applied for certificate for exhibition of film 'Ore Oru Gramathile' (In one Village) - Chairman of Censor Board however, referred film to Second Revising Committee for review and recommendation - Revising Committee majority opined that theme of film was on reservation policy of Govt. suggesting that reservation could be made on basis of economic backwardness - Such a view could be expressed in a free country like India, and it did not violate any guideline - On 7-12-1987, 'U' certificate was granted for exhibition of film - Respondent challenged the 'U' certificate before HC, which was dismissed by Single Judge - On appeal, DB allowed the appeal and revoked the certificate - Hence, instant appeal - Whether DB was justified in revoking the certificate - Held, it was difficult to appreciate the observations of HC - It was failed to understand how expression in film with criticism of reservation policy or praising colonial rule would affect security of State of sovereignty and integrity of India - There was no utterance in film threatening to overthrow Govt. by unlawful or unconstitutional means - Freedom of expression was the rule and it was generally taken for granted - Everyone had a fundamental right to form his own opinion on any issue of general concern - Film had given National Award by Directorate of Film Festival of Govt. of India - If film was unobjectionable and could not constitutionally be restricted u/art. 19(2) of Constitution, freedom of expression could not be suppressed on account of threat of demonstration and processions or threats of violence - That would tantamount to negation of rule of law and a surrender to black mail and intimidation - In instant case, two Revising Committees had approved the film - Members thereof come from different walks of life with variegated experiences - Committee represented cross section of community - Committees judged the film in light of objectives of the Act - Producer or as a matter of fact any other person had a right to draw attention of Govt. and people that existing method of reservation in education institutions overlooks merits - Appellant producer had a right to state that reservation could be made on basis of economic backwardness to benefit of all sections of community - Freedom of expression which was legitimate and constitutionally protected, could not be held to ransom, by an intolerant group of people - Fundamental freedom u/art. 19(1)(a) of Constitution could be reasonably restricted only for purposes mentioned in art. 19(2) of Constitution and restriction should be justified on anvil of necessity and not quicks and of convenience or expediency - Impugned order passed by DB of HC was set aside - Appeals allowed.
