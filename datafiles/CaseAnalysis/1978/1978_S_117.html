Â Pathumma and Others v State of Kerala and Others

Supreme Court of India

16 January 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Bhagwati, V.R. Krishna Iyer, V.D. Tulzapurkar, P.N. Shingal, Jaswant Singh

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 332; (1978) 2 SCC 1; AIR 1978 SC 771; [1978] 2 S.C.R. 537

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Agriculture & Agri. Products

Keywords: Constitutionality, Kerala Agriculturists Debt Relief Act, 1958, Indebtedness, Constitution (Thirtieth Amendment) Act, 1972

Summary: Syed M. Fazal Ali, M. H. Beg, P. N. Bhagwati, V. R. Krishna Iyer, V. D. Tulzapurkar, JJ.

(A) Agriculture & Agri Products - Constitution - Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Act, 1970, s. 20 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(f) - S.20 of the Act - Constitutionality - Appellant obtained a decree on the basis of a registered deed of mortgage against the agriculturist/judgment-debtor - Possession of the properties was taken from the judgment-debtor by the appellant and in the meantime, 1970 Act came into force - Judgment-debtor filed a petition u/s. 20(7) of the Act for setting aside the sale and redelivery of the properties - Appellant challenged the constitutional validity of s. 20 of the Act before the HC - HC upheld the validity of s. 20 of the Act - Whether the provisions of s. 20 of the Act is violative of art. 19 (1 ) (f) of the Constitution inasmuch as they sought to deprive the appellants of their right to hold property - Held, Act does not take away the property of the appellant/purchaser without paying him due compensation - S. 20(2) (b) provides for payment of the purchase money by instalments in view of the poverty of the debtor as it is not possible for him to pay the debt in a lump-sum and as the legislation is, for a particular community the provision for payment by instalments cannot be said to work serious injustice to the decree-holder purchaser - Stranger auction purchaser has been treated differently because he had nothing to do with the decree and is enjoined to return the property to the agriculturist debtor on payment of entire amount - Act does not provide for any drastic or arbitrary procedure as the property is restored to the debtor only on payment of the purchase money - Considering the social purpose sough to be achieved by the Constitution, it cannot be said that the invasion of the right of the appellants is so excessive as to be branded unreasonable - Restrictions are in no way arbitrary or excessive or beyond the requirements of the situation prevailing in the State prior to the enactment of the Act - Act is a valid piece of legislation and amounts to a reasonable restriction within the meaning of art. 19(5)(6) and the impugned provisions passes the test of constitutionality and hence valid -Appeals dismissed.

(B) Agriculture & Agri Products - Constitution - Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Act, 1970, s. 20 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Stranger auction purchaser and bona fide auction purchaser - Discrimination - Whether s. 20 of the Act is discriminating stranger auction purchaser as against the bona fide auction purchaser who bought the property prior to the enactment of impugned Act - Held, with regard to the nature of the rights acquired by the stranger auction purchaser and the bona fide alienee it cannot be said that they are similarly situated or happen to be in exactly the same position - Stranger auction purchaser has a clear notice of the circumstances under which the decree was passed as also the fact that the property sold was the property of the debtor - Stranger auction purchaser knows that he has purchased the property at a distress sale and the element of innocence is completely eliminated - Further, even if the property is restored to the stranger auction purchaser unlike the decree-holder the purchaser is entitled to get the entire purchase money in lump-sum including the cost before parting with the possession of the property - However, a bona fide alienee does not purchase the property under a distress sale but under sale which is negotiated with the vendor on the terms acceptable to the purchaser - Bona fide alienee has absolutely no notice of the debt or the debtor or the circumstance under which the decree was passed and the property was purchased by the vendor - Bona fide alience acquires a new title under a negotiated and completed sale and in case the sale is allowed to be re- opened by the Act it will lead to complicated questions which may cloud the real issues, and frustrate the object of the Act -Therefore, there is no any discrimination meted out by s. 20 of the Act as against the stranger auction purchaser with respect to the bona fide auction purchaser - Impugned order of HC is upheld - Appeals dismissed.

P. N. Shinghal & Jaswant Singh, JJ.

(C) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Agriculture & Agri Products - Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Act, 1970, s.20 - Constitution of India, 1950, List II Entry 30 - Whether the Act was beyond the legislative competence of the State legislature and did not fall within entry 30 of the State List - Held, Act which provides for 'the relief of indebted agriculturists in the State of Kerala' is within the competence of the State Legislature - Act gives an extended meaning to the expression 'agriculturist' so as to include a person who would have been an agriculturist but for the sale of his immovable property - Subject-matter of the Act is therefore clearly within the purview of Entry 30 of List II - Further, a look at Entry 30 of List II does not show that it would not enable the State Legislature to make a law simply because the debt of the agriculturist had been paid off under a distress sale - Subject-matter of the entry is 'relief of agricultural indebtedness' and there is no justification for the contention that it is confined only to subsisting indebtedness and would not cover the necessity of providing relief to those agriculturists who had lost their immovable property by court sales in execution of the decrees against them and had been rendered destitute - If the Legislature could provide relief to agriculturists against their subsisting debts, by legislation under Entry 30, there is no reason why it should find itself disabled from doing so in the case of the said agriculturists who had lost their immovable properties in the process of the liquidation of their debts by court sales even though their case called for greater sympathy and speedier relief - Therefore, impugned legislation is within the competence of the State Legislature and falls within the Entry 30 of List II - Appeals dismissed.

(D) Agriculture & Agri Products - Constitution - Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Act, 1970, s. 20 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1) (f) - Whether the provisions of s. 20 of the Act is violative of art. 19 (1 ) (f) of the Constitution inasmuch as they sought to deprive the appellants of their right to hold property - Held, HC has made a reference to the history of the debt relief legislation and the facts and circumstances which led to the passing of the Act - Thousands of suits were pending against indebted agriculturists in various courts and immovable properties of a large number of agriculturists had been sold rendering them completely helpless - So if the State Legislature passed the Act, in the interest of the general public, to provide relief of the nature mentioned in s. 20 of the Act in view of the rampant agricultural indebtedness in the State, and the urgency of the malady, it cannot be held that the restriction provided by that section was clearly 'unreasonable' - Further, s. 20 makes provision for the repayment of the purchase money, the costs of the execution and the improvements made by the purchaser and therefore, restriction provided u/s. 20 is therefore reasonable in every sense - Appeals dismissed.

(E) Agriculture & Agri Products - Constitution - Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Act, 1970, s. 20(6) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Whether s. 20 (6) of the Act is meted out any discrimination to the post-enactment auction purchasers with respect to the bona fide auction purchasers prior to the enactment - Held, decree-holder purchaser has been treated as a class by himself and that classification obviously has the object of benefiting the agriculturist judgment-debtor by permitting him to deposit only half the purchase money and paying the balance in instalments - Further, there is also justification for treating an auction-purchaser at a court sale differently from a bona fide alienee of the auction purchaser who derived his rights before the date of publication of the Kerala Agriculturists' Debt Relief Bill 1968, in the State Gazette - Such an alienee of the auction-purchaser could not possibly have been aware of the hazards of purchasing the property of an indebted agriculturist at the time of the purchase - Therefore, there is no hostile discrimination if the Legislature has protected interest of bona fide auction purchaser prior to the Act by an express provision in s. 20(6) - Appeals dismissed.
