Mayawati v Markandeya Chand and Others

Supreme Court of India

09 October 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M.M. Punchhi, K.T. Thomas, M. Srinivasan

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 922; (1998) 7 SCC 517; AIR 1998 SC 3340; 1998 (4) CLT 157; JT 1998 (7) SC 36; 1998(5) SCALE 517; 1999 (1) SCJ 82; 1998 (8) SLT 236; [1998] Supp2 S.C.R. 204; 1998 (8) Supreme 16

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Disqualification On Grounds Of Defection

Summary: SRINIVASAN, J.

Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Disqualification - In the general elections to the U.P. Legislative Assembly which took place in 1996 no political party obtained absolute majority - There was an agreement between the Bhartiya Janata Party (BJP) and the Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP) to support each other for running the Govt. - BSP had 67 MLAs who were elected on its fold - Pursuant to the agreement the appellant belonging to BSP became the Chief Minister of the State on 21-3-1997 - On 21-9-1997 X belonging to the BJP became the Chief Minister - On 19-10-1997 the appellant announced withdrawal of participation and support of BSP to the coalition govt. - All the BSP Ministers resigned from the Govt. - 12 MLAs of Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP) voted in favour of a motion of confidence moved by the Chief Minister of the State - Soon thereafter all those 12 MLAs were made Ministers in the State cabinet headed by Chief Minister - Appellant, leader of BSP Legislature Party, complained that the 12 MLAs (respondents) have incurred disqualification for membership of the Assembly - Speaker of the Assembly, by the impugned order exonerated the respondents from the tentacles of disqualification envisaged in the 10th schedule to the Constitution - Speaker passed the impugned order holding that (1) respondents are not liable to be disqualified under Para 2(1)(b) of the 10th Schedule because they belong to a faction which came into being as sequel to a split which arose in the BSP on 21-10-1997 consisting of not less than 1/3rd of the total members of the Legislature Party of the BSP - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the disqualification envisaged in Para 2(1)(b) of the 10th Schedule should have visited the respondents because it is admitted by the respondents themselves that they ceased to be members of BSP from 21-10-1997 as they had formed a new political party (JTBSP).

Held, all the circumstances referred to and relied on by the Speaker are relevant and germane for deciding the issue whether there was a split on 21-10-1997 and whether the group led by M had not less than one third members of the BSP legislature party. The speaker has not left out any relevant material from consideration; nor has he referred to any irrelevant matter. In the facts and circumstances of instant case it cannot be said that no reasonable or sensible person who had applied his mind to the question to be decided could have arrived at the findings given by the Speaker. Further, when the Tenth Schedule has expressly constituted the Speaker or the Chairman as the case may be to decide the question of disqualification and attach finality thereto, it is not for SC to consider the facts and decide the said question by substituting itself in the place of the Speaker. Also, Speaker has found on the basis of the records that the appellant instructed the members of the BSP to indulge in violence and disrupt the proceedings in the Assembly on 21-10-1997. It is also found that the allegations made by the respondents that the members of the JBSP were kept under threat by the appellant and prevented from entering Lucknow. In view of such finding also which is supported by records, the discretionary jurisdiction u/art. 136 of the Constitution should not be exercised in favour of the appellant. Appeal dismissed.

THOMAS, J.

Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950 - Disqualification on ground of defection - Case of split - Scope of judicial scrutiny on matters pertaining to the decision of a Speaker passed under Para 6 of the 10th Schedule.

Held, two conditions are sine qua non for avoiding the disqualification when any member of the House voluntarily gives up membership of his original political party. First is that the member concerned should have made a claim that the split in the original Political Party has arisen resulting in the Constitution of a group in its Legislature Party representing a faction thereof. Second is that such group should consist of not less than 1/3rd of the members of such Legislature Party. Speaker has to consider the repercussions of non-compliance of a particular rule in the Disqualification Rules to ascertain how far it has affected the credibility of the case of a claimant who seeks protection under Para 3 of the 10th Schedule. In the instant case, finding of the Speaker that a split arose in the BSP on 21-10-1997 forming a group representing a faction consisting of not less than 1/3rd of the members of the Legislature party of BSP is vitiated by perversity. The corollary of it is that the 12 respondents who have defected from the BSP on the said date cannot escape from the consequence provided in Para 2(1)(b) of the 10th Schedule. All the twelve respondents stand disqualified under Para 2(1) of the 10th Schedule of the Constitution. The twelve respondents stand disqualified to be members of the U.P. Legislative Assembly under Para 2(1) (a) of the 10th Schedule of the Constitution. Appeal allowed.

M. M. Punchhi, J.

Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950 - Disqualification on ground of defection - Case of split - Scope of judicial scrutiny on matters pertaining to the decision of a Speaker passed under Para 6 of the 10th Schedule.

Held, matter be referred to the Constitution Bench for decision. Order accordingly.
