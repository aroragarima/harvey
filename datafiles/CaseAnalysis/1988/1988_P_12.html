P.M. Ashwathanarayana Setty And Ors. Etc. Etc. v State Of Karnataka And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

22 September 1988

Case Analysis

Bench

M.N. Venkatachaliah, R.S. Pathak, S. Natarajan

Where Reported

1988 Indlaw SC 639; (1989) Supp1 SCC 696; AIR 1989 SC 100; JT 1988 (4) SC 639; 1988(2) SCALE 844; [1988] 3 S.C.R. 155; [1988] Supp3 S.C.R. 155

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Bombay Court Fees Act, 1959 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 39-A - Court Fees Act, 1870 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Karnataka Court Fees and Suits Valuation Act, 1958 - Rajasthan Court Fees and Suits Valuation Act 1961 - Policy and legality of the levy of court fees - (A)Whether levies of court fee under the "Karnataka Act" and the "Rajasthan Act" do not satisfy the requirements of the concept of a 'fee' but partake the character of a 'tax', inasmuch as that the co-relationship between the fee and the value of services by way of quid pro quo, is not established? - Held, if the essential character of the impost is that some special service is intended or envisaged as a quid pro quo to the class of citizens which is intended to be benefited by the service and there is a broad and general co-relation between the amount so raised and the expenses involved in providing the services, the impost would partake the character of a 'fee' notwithstanding the circumstance that the identity of the amount so raised is not always kept distinguished but is merged in the general revenues of the State and notwithstanding the fact that such special services, for which the amount is raised, are, as they very often do, incidentally or indirectly benefit the general public also - (B) Whether levy of court fees on ad valorem basis, without an upper limit, renders the impost a tax, inasmuch as having regard to the very nature of the services, which consists of adjudication of disputes, a stage is inevitably reached after the above which an ad valorem levy, the proportionate increase in the value of the subject matter, ceases to be 'fee' and becomes a 'tax'- Held, test of the co-relation is not in the context of individual contributors - The test is on the comprehensive level of the value of the totality of the services, set off against the totality of the receipts and if the character of the 'fee' is thus established, the vagaries in its distribution amongst the class, do not detract from the concept of a 'fee' as such, though a wholly arbitrary distribution of the burden might violate other constitutional limitation - This idea that the test of the co-relation is at the 'aggregate' level and not at the 'individual' level is expressed thus fee must be equal, in the aggregate to the cost of production of the service - That is the aggregate amount of the fees which the State collects from individual consumers must equal the aggregate expenses of production - Appeal dismissed.
