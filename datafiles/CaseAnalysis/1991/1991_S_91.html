Kalawatibai v Soiryabai And Others

Supreme Court of India

01 May 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Sahai, T.K. Thommen

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 449; (1991) 3 SCC 410; AIR 1991 SC 1581; 1991 (4) Bom.C.R. 115; 1991 (2) DMC 343; 1992 (1) HLR 25; JT 1991 (2) SC 385; 1991(1) SCALE 852; [1991] 2 S.C.R. 599; 1991 (2) UJ 230

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Practice & Procedure; Women & Children

Keywords: Female Heir, Declaratory Suit, Limited Heir, Succession & Inheritance, Marriage & Divorce

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Women's Rights to Property Act, 1937 - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, ss. 4 and 14 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 41 - (A) Whether a Hindu widow could alienate by gift entire estate inherited from husband, in favour of one of female reversioners prior to enforcement of Hindu Succession Act? - Held, a Hindu widow succeeding or inheriting any property from her husband or as widow of predeceased son, held limited interest known as Hindu women's estate, prior to coming into force of 1956 Act, under Hindu Women's Right to Property Act, 1937 - Hindu widow prior to 1956 held property fully with right to enjoy or even destroy or dispose it of or alienate it but such destruction or alienation should have been impressed with legal necessity or for religious or charitable purposes or for spiritual welfare of husband - (B) Whether donee become an owner of a widow's estate, a limited owner, an owner with some right or title, so as to acquire rights of absolute ownership u/s. 14 or a trespasser? - Necessary consequences that flowed from an alienation for legal necessity was that property vested in transferee or alienee, and reversioners were precluded from assailing its validity, hence if prior to 1956 any alienation was made by a Hindu widow of widow's estate prohibited by law or being beyond permissible limits, it stripped widow of her rights and she could not acquire any rights u/s. 14 and so far as alienees were concerned it could utmost create temporary and transitory ownership precarious in nature and vulnerable in character open to challenge if any attempt was made to cloud reversioner's interest; her possession may be good against world, her right in property may not be impeachable by widow but her interest qua reversioners was to continue in possession at maximum till lifetime of her donor or transferor; if alienation was valid i e it was for legal necessity or permitted by law then donee became an owner of it and right and title in property vested in her, but if it was contrary to law, as in this case gift being of entire widow's estate, then it did not bind reversioner who could file a suit after death of widow - (C) Whether as a trespasser, did she acquire rights by adverse possession by perfecting her rights against donor only or it was essential to prescribe rights against reversioners as well? - Possession under a gift deed which was found to be invalid, as it was not permitted under Hindu law was on general principle contrary to law, and as such could be adverse; in Radha Rani case [1965 Indlaw SC 492] it was held that in case of an alienation by a Hindu widow without legal necessity, reversioners were not bound to institute a declaratory suit during lifetime of widow, they could wait till her death and then sue that alienee for possession of alienated property treating alienation as a nullity - Held, appellant could not acquire any right by adverse possession against reversioner during lifetime of her mother and her claim was rightly negatived - Appeal dismissed.
