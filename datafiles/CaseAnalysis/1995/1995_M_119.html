International Airports Authority of India Etc v Messrs Grand Slam International and Om Etc

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Sahai, S.P. Bharucha

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 371; (1995) 3 SCC 151; AIR 1995 SCW 1802; 1995 (57) ECR 209; 1995 (77) E.L.T. 753; JT 1995 (2) SC 452; 1995(1) SCALE 859; [1995] 2 S.C.R. 149

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Customs

Keywords: Adjudication Proceedings, Warehoused Goods, Warehouse, Warehousing Corporations Act, 1962, International Airports Authority Act, 1971, Aircraft Rules, 1920

Summary: Customs - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136, 226 - International Airports Authority Act, 1971 - Customs Act, 1962 - Detention Certificate - Demurrage - Public notice - Respondents appeals pertaining to seeking clearance of goods, importing restricted item of wool waste without a valid import licence and payment of duty for import of multi-cable-transit/cable sealing system - Customs Department had issued Detention Certificate and informed the appellants not to charge any demurrage when the goods were in their custody - Appellants calculated demurrage much higher than the value of goods - Respondents filed W.P.s and passed order in their favour - Appellants aggrieved - Hence, instant appeals - Whether, the order of the HC to release the imported goods without payment of demurrage charges for the period for which detention certificate had been issued by the Customs Authorities, was just and proper - Held, that the appellants being only custodian of the Customs Department could not ignore the detention certificate issued in exercise of this power - After the issuance of Public Notice, in exercise of statutory power, the appellants could not set up the claim that the intimation issued by the Customs Department could not be taken into account for determination of free days - The time to calculate demurrage commenced from the date of landing after excluding free days - When Public Notice required the appellants not to charge any rent for the period detention certificate was issued, then by operation of law the expression 'within the time allowed' in the policy had to be read along with the detention certificate issued by the Customs Department - HC was right in holding the view that clauses of the Public notice issued by the Customs Department were binding on the appellants - The right and power of appellants to sell the goods for non payment of demurrage was statutorily regulated by the issuance of Public notice - It did not interfere with their rights to frame their rate schedule and charge demurrage but held that where the goods had been detained or seized at the instance of the Customs Department the dues had to be calculated minus that detention period - Appeals dismissed.
