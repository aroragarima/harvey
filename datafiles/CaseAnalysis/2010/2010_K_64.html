State of Uttar Pradesh v Krishna Master and others

Supreme Court of India

03 August 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Panchal, H.S. Bedi

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 570; (2010) 12 SCC 324; AIR 2010 SC 3071; 2010 CRLJ 3889; 2010 (4) JhrCR(SC) 18; JT 2010 (8) SC 240; 2011 (1) MLJ(Crl) 110; 2010 (3) RCR(Criminal) 843; 2010(7) SCALE 597; [2010] 9 S.C.R. 563

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34 and 302 - Murder - Acquittal - Challenged - Three respondents were charged sheeted for commission of offences punishable u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC - On the basis of testimony of the two eye-witnesses, the Trial Court convicted respondents u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC - Trial Court awarded capital punishment to respondents and a reference was made to HC for confirmation of the capital punishment imposed on the respondents - Respondent also filed appeal before HC challenging their conviction - HC acquitted the respondents and rejected the reference made by the trial court, for confirmation of the death sentence - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether HC was justified in acquitting respondents? - Held, HC acquitted respondents in a casual and careless manner - Approach of HC in appreciating evidence was not only contrary to the well settled principles of appreciation of evidence but contrary to ground realities of life - HC recorded reasons for acquittal of the respondents which were not borne out from the record and were contrary to the evidences adduced by the reliable eye- witnesses - HC was not justified in upsetting well reasoned conviction of the respondents recorded by the Trial Court which after observing demur of the eye-witnesses had placed reliance on their testimony - Appreciation of evidence by the HC was cursory and has done injustice to the prosecution - It was firmly established by the prosecution that respondents were persons who had committed six murders and, therefore, liable to be convicted u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC - (B) Sentence - Capital punishment - Held, killing six persons of family on flimsy ground of saving honour of the family would fall within the rarest of rare case evolved by SC and, therefore, the Trial Court was perfectly justified in imposing capital punishment on the respondents - However, to sentence the respondents to death would not be justified on the facts and in the circumstances of the case - Interest of justice would be served if each of the respondent sentenced to RI for life - Appeal allowed.

(B) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34 and 302 - Criteria for appreciation of oral evidence - Held, while appreciating the evidence of a witness, the approach must be whether the evidence of witness read as a whole appears to have truth - Minor discrepancies on trivial matters not touching the core of the case, hyper-technical approach by taking sentences torn out of context here or there from the evidence, attaching importance to some technical error committed by the investigating officer not going to the root of the matter would not ordinarily permit rejection of the evidence as a whole - Minor omissions in the police statements are never considered to be fatal - Statements given by the witnesses before the Police are meant to be brief statements and could not take place of evidence in the court - Small/trivial omissions would not justify a finding by court that the witnesses concerned are liars - Main thing to be seen is whether those inconsistencies go to the root of the matter or pertain to insignificant aspects thereof - Therefore, it is the duty of the Court to separate falsehood from the truth - Appeal allowed.
