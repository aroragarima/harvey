Mangala Kunhimina Umma and Others v Puthiyaveettil Paru Amma and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 January 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

A.N. Ray, G.K. Mitter

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 741; (1971) 1 SCC 562; AIR 1971 SC 1575; [1971] 3 S.C.R. 582

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Suit for redemption, Mortgage transaction

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - The Malabar Tenancy Act, 1929 - Kerala Land Reforms Act, 1964 - Suit for redemption - Mortgage transaction - Legality - Appellants filed instant appeal against impugned judgment of HC confirming the decree of the Lower Appellate Court declaring that the sum of Rs.1000/- was due to defendants no. 10 to 17 as LRs of defendant no. 2 on the mortgage mentioned in plaint and that plaintiffs having deposited the said sum of Rs.1000/- on the file of Trial Court, the defendants nos. 10 to 17 do surrender quiet and peaceable possession of the property described in plaint to the plaintiff no. 7 with all documents related to property in their possession and further that defendant nos. 10 to 17 do pay to the plaintiff no. 7 half of the mesne profits till the date of surrender of possession. Whether appellants were protected against eviction by reason of their contention that Ex.P6 (kanam-kuzhikanam) created a tenancy or whether respondents were entitled to possession of properties, by reason of their rival contention that Ex.B6 was a mortgage transaction and respondents were entitled to redeem the mortgage on the expiry of the stipulated period.

Held, HC correctly held that a mere direction to pay the revenue of the property by the grantee, particularly when no payment is stipulated to be made to the grantor or when the payment is not directed to be made out of anything which is due or payable to the grantor, cannot be construed as a payment or rent or Michavaram to the grantor. The proportion between the amount advanced and the value of the property is one of the important tests to be taken into consideration in deciding the nature of the transaction. Where the amount advanced bears a substantial proportion to the value of the property it is an important element indication that the intention was the creation of a mortgage and not a tenancy. In instant case, the amount for which the properties included in Ex.B-6 were sold to plaintiff no. 1 under Ex.A2 was Rs.5000/- out of which Rs.2500/- was to go in discharge of the amount under Ex.B6. The advance, therefore, bore a substantial proportion to the value of the property. This feature when considered along with the fact that the document did not provide payment of any annual purapped to the jenmi and that the annual amount was directed to be paid as revenue of the property which came to Rs. 10-4-0, a paltry recurring annual liability, would be an additional reason to support the intention of the parties that the transaction was a mortgage and not a tenancy. In instant case the features which favour the construction of the transaction to be a mortgage and not a lease are : first, that there is no provision for renewal; secondly, there is no provision for payment of customary dues; thirdly, the property was to be enjoyed by the defendants by way of interest on their advance after payment of land tax to the State, fourthly, the payment of land tax is not a deduction from rent or perquisites; fifthly, there is a provision for surrendering the property with a registered release at the cost of the transferees on the receipt of the consideration of kanam and the balance amount; sixthly, when the consideration is paid back the counter-pattam deeds and prior deeds would be returned; and finally, there is liability to pay interest on the advance and possession and enjoyment of profits of the property is in lieu of interest. HC was correct in its conclusion as to the nature of the transaction being a mortgage and not a lease. Appeal dismissed.
