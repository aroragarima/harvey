Ram Sunder Sen and another v Narender @ Bode Singh Patel

Supreme Court of India

15 October 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 713; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 4511; 2016 CRLJ 353; 2015 (3) JabLJ 344; JT 2015 (9) SC 301; 2015(10) SCALE 710; [2015] 12 S.C.R. 898

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Indian Evidence Act, 1972, Indian Penal Code, 1873

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 376(2)(f) and 201 - Rape - Circumstantial evidence - Charges were framed against accused for offences punishable u/ss.302, 376(2)(f) and 201 of IPC and upon perusal of evidence, Trial Court convicted accused and awarded capital punishment - Later, Criminal Reference was filed for confirmation of death sentence and accused also filed appeal before HC aggrieved by judgment and order of Trial Court - HC vide impugned judgment allowed appeal of accused and set him at liberty on ground that prosecution failed to prove chain of circumstances sufficient to connect accused with alleged offences - Hence, instant appeals, one by complainant, who was father of deceased and another by State - Whether acquittal of accused could be upheld.

Held, prosecution has the responsibility to present a chain of events but it has failed at the foremost to link the accused with the incident. HC went into each and every material aspect of the case, examined at length the deposition of the witnesses and rightly held that the links which are collected by the prosecution have not at all been proved by any cogent evidence and hence, it is difficult to hold that it was the accused who committed rape upon the deceased and thereafter killed her. Hence, impugned judgment of HC is upheld and appeals dismissed. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - Circumstances through which inference of guilt is sought to be drawn, should be cogent and firm; and of definite tendency unerringly pointing towards guild of accused; Further, circumstances taken cumulatively should form a chain so complete that there is no escape from conclusion that within all human probability the crime was committed by accused and none else; and circumstantial evidence in order to sustain conviction should be complete and incapable of explanation of any other hypothesis than that of the guilt of the accused and such evidence should not only be consistent with the guilt of the accused but should be inconsistent with his innocence.
