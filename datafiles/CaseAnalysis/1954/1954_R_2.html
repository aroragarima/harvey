Â R. S. Seth Shanti Sarup v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

26 November 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 178; AIR 1955 SC 624

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - U. P. Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 3(f) - Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946, s. 3(f) - Partnership firm of which petitioner was a partner, was started in year 1921 - In 1944 differences arose between partners and one of them instituted a suit for dissolution of partnership - Suit was dismissed - Appeal thereagainst still pending - During pendency of suit and appeal, Receivers were appointed - Receiver reported that mills could thenceforth be run only at a loss and thereupon Court ordered mills to be closed - Government expressed their desire of taking over these mills and running same themselves under Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Government of U. P. passed an order purporting to be made u/s. 3(f) of U. P. Act by which they appointed respondent no. 3 one of partners of firm, as "authorised controller" of undertaking apparently in terms of provision of that sub-section - A petition u/art. 226 of Constitution praying for appropriate writs to quash order of U. P. Government - During pendency of proceeding UOI (Ministry of commerce and industry) passed an order purporting to be made u/s. 3(4) of Act, 1946 by which Central Government appointed same person, namely respondent no. 3, as an authorised Controller - HC dismissed writ petition, being of opinion that in circumstances of case discretionary powers u/art. 226 of Constitution should not be exercised in favour of petitioner who should be left to his remedy in civil suit already filed by him - Present petition u/art. 32 of Constitution praying for quashing of orders mentioned above and restoration of properties and damages, alleging inter alia that suit which is pending, cannot afford him adequate, effective and expeditious remedy - It was contended that both orders complained against are illegal and inoperative by reason of their not being warranted at all by provisions of law under which they respectively purport to have been passed - They are invalid also as conflicting with fundamental rights of petitioner u/art. 31 of Constitution as they in substance had effect of depriving petitioner of his properties without complying with provisions of that article - Held, arguments prima facie well founded - Further, u/s. 3(4) of Act, 1946 Central Government is empowered to do is to authorise any person to exercise, with respect to any undertaking engaged in production and supply of essential commodity such functions of control as may be provided by order and which are necessary for maintaining or increasing production and supply of commodity - Exercise of control, it is said connotes issuing of directions under which management is to do or refrain from doing something - It cannot under any circumstance amount to divesting management or owners of property and, taking it over from them - Order of Central Government must therefore, be deemed to have deprived petitioner of his property within meaning of Art. 31 of Constitution as construed by SC - Petitioner is quite within his rights in making this application u/art. 32 of Constitution - Order of Central Government as well as earlier order passed by U. P. Government quashed - Petition allowed.
