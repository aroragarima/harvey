Bhushan Power & Steel Limited v S. L. Seal ADDL. Secretary (Steel & Mines) Government of Odisha and others

Supreme Court of India

15 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 893; (2017) 2 SCC 125; JT 2016 (12) SC 132; 2017 (1) MLJ 68; 2016(12) SCALE 801

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Grant Of Mining Lease, Minerals, Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, Mining Rules, Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2015, Mining Ordinance, 2015

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, ss. 10A, 10A(2)(c), 11A, 5(1) - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2015, ss. 10A, 11 - Mining Rules, r. 59(1) - Predecessor-in-interest of petitioner had proposed setting up of plant in some identified villages in respondents/State Govt. - MoU was entered into between parties containing the commitment of State Govt. to recommend to Central Govt. grant of iron ore mines for its use in the proposed plant - Subsequently, Govt. made a recommendation by letter dt. 24-5-2014 to Central Govt. to grant mining lease in favour of another licensee in relaxation of r. 59(1) of the Rules for a period of 30 years - Petitioner filed writ petition before HC thereagainst, which was dismissed - On appeal, SC allowed appeal and directed respondents to take appropriate steps to act in terms of MoU - Respondents did not comply with the directions of SC - Hence, instant contempt petitions.

Held, in order to enable State Govt. to enter into any lease agreement/contract with the prospecting licensee, 'previous approval' of Central Govt. was essential. Unless such approval came, State Govt. could not communicate to the prospecting licensee/lessee its intention to enter into any contract as the pre-requisite prior approval would be lacking. Therefore, no promise could be held by State Govt. to any applicant showing its intention to enter into a contract in future. Position would have been different had letter dt. 24-5- 2014 been issued after receiving previous approval of Central Govt. however, that was not so. That letter to Central Govt. was only recommendatory in nature and ultimate decision rested with Central Govt. Thus, SC was of view that it was not a Letter of Intent. The application of petitioner would not be covered by s. 10A(c) of the 1957 Act. Petitioner had originally succeeded in appeal giving direction to State Govt. to recommend the case of petitioner, in terms of MoU entered into between the parties, to Central Govt. which was not done. It was possible that had State Govt. promptly and sent the recommendations earlier, Central Govt. might have accorded its approval. However, whether it could have done so or not would be in the realm of conjectures. Insofar as Central Govt. was concerned no direction was ever given by SC. It would be for Central Govt. to consider the recommendations of State Govt. on its own merits and in accordance with law. If that has not been done by Central Govt., it cannot be the subject matter of instant contempt petition. Order accordingly.
