Ajay Singh v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

06 June 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 610; (2007) 12 SCC 341; (2008) 1 SCC (Cr) 371; AIR 2007 SC 2188; 2007 (3) JCC 1706; JT 2007 (8) SC 638; 2007 (3) RCR(Criminal) 348; 2007(8) SCALE 461; [2007] 7 S.C.R. 983; 2007 (4) UC 157

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Police Record, Tape-Record, Extra Judicial Confession, Exact Words, Charge- Sheet

Summary: Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Appeal against conviction and sentence - Held, extra judicial confession can form the basis of conviction if persons before whom it is stated to be made appear to be unbiased and not even remotely inimical to the accused - Where there is material to show animosity, Court has to proceed cautiously and find out whether confession just like any other evidence depends on veracity of witness to whom it is made - A conviction based on the accused's failure to explain what he was never asked to explain is bad in law - It is not sufficient compliance to string together a long series of facts and ask the accused what he has to say about them - He must be questioned separately about each material substance which is intended to be used against him - Prosecution has failed to establish the accusations - Appeal allowed.
