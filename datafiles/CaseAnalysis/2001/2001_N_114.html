Satish Chandra Jain v National Small Industries Corporation Ltd. and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 October 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Kirpal, N. Santosh Hegde, P. Venkatarama Reddi

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 1507; AIR 2003 SC 623; 2002 (2) ALD(SC) 12; 2002 (46) ALR 293; JT 2001 (10) SC 416; 2002 (131) PLR 829

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Banking & Finance - U.P. Public Moneys (Recovery of Dues) Act, 1972 - Agreement - Non payment - Recovery from previous surety - Maintainability - Appellant's son entered into a hire-purchase agreement with respondent No. 1 whereby the said respondent financed a business venture Y - At the time of signing of the hire-purchase agreement, the appellant stood as guarantor as per the deed of guarantee - Appellant's son then incorporated a private limited company called X and the said company took over the business of the proprietorship concern - Terms of the original agreement did not permit such a change without the consent of respondent No. 1 - Consent to the new entity taking over the business of the proprietorship concern was given by respondent No. 1 and a new agreement was entered into - By virtue of this agreement, the private limited company became the hirer and defendants became the first and the second surety respectively - Company failed to make the payments as contemplated by the agreement - Appellant, who was not a party to the suit, was not mentioned in the Certificate of Recovery - However, in Annexure-D which contained the details of the immovable property sought to be attached and sold that the house belonging to the appellant was mentioned - Hence instant appeal - Held, it was clear that originally when the hire-purchase agreement had been entered into between the parties in June, 1983, the appellant had stood as one of the guarantors - Agreement, however, was superseded by a new one on 2-4-1986 - Latter agreement did not say that the earlier agreement of guarantee stood terminated nor did it state that the earlier agreement of guarantee would continue - In fact, in the latter agreement of 2-4-1986, there was no mention of the earlier agreement entered into by the appellant as a guarantor - Conduct of the parties, however, showed that respondent No. 1 after 2-4-1986 did not regard the appellant as one of the guarantors - Apart from the recital in the agreement of 2-4-1986, inter alia, to the effect that a fresh agreement had been entered into and appellants had agreed to stand as guarantors, respondent No. 1 when it filed the suit in HC chose not to implead the appellant as one of the defendants - There was no reference in the plaint to the earlier deed of guarantee dt. 7-6-1983 executed by the appellant nor was there any prayer in the plaint for a decree against the appellant or in respect of the agreement of 1983 - Even in the Certificate of Recovery which was sent by respondent No. 1 to the Collector, the arrears were stated to be due from defendants in the capacity as guarantors apart from the company and its Directors - Appellant was admittedly not a Director of the company and his name did not find mention in the Certificate of Recovery Without going into the question whether the provisions of Act could be invoked on the facts of the case against the appellant, SC found that in fact there was no such invocation in instant case - Further, the second agreement of 2-4-1986 amounted to novation of contract as a result of which the earlier guarantors stood discharged and fresh guarantees of defedants were accepted by respondent No. 1 - It was for such reason that respondent No. 1 chose not to file any suit against the appellant thereby clearly indicating that it did not regard the appellant as a guarantor or a person who was still bound by the deed of guarantee executed on 7-6-1983 - Argument of continuing guarantee, therefore, could not be applied in the instant case - Appeal allowed.
