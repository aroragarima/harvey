Premier Breweries Limited, Karnataka v Commissioner of Income Tax, Cochin

Supreme Court of India

10 March 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Prafulla C. Pant, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 159; [2015] 372 ITR 180; JT 2015 (3) SC 127; 2015 (3) MLJ 101; 2015(3) SCALE 330; [2015] 3 S.C.R. 365; [2015] 230 TAXMAN 575

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Civil Procedure - Income-tax Act, 1961, s. 37 - Reframing of Questions - Jurisdiction of HC - Appellants are engaged in manufacture and sale of beer and other alcoholic beverages - Certain States had established marketing corporations which were exclusive wholesalers of alcoholic beverage for concerned State whereby all manufacturers had to compulsorily sell their products to State Corporations which, in turn, would sell liquor so purchased, to retailers - Appellants stated that manufacturers of beverages containing alcohol have to engage services of agents who would co-ordinate with retailers and State Corporations to ensure continuous supply of goods to ultimate consumers, hence, on that ground they sought deduction u/s. 37 of Act - AO dismissed claim made by assessee which was confirmed by appellant authority - Tribunal held that assessee was entitled to claim for deduction, however, view of Tribunal was reversed by HC - Questions were reframed by HC and it was held that Tribunal has committed grave error in not properly understanding transaction entered into between assessee and others, set aside order of Tribunal and upheld order of appellant authority and answered questions in favour of revenue by holding that assessee had not discharged burden so as to entitle it to deduction u/s. 37 of Act - Whether order passed by HC was within its jurisdiction and was in accordance with law.

Held, reading of questions initially framed and subsequently reframed show that what was done by HC is to retain three out of twelve questions, as initially framed, while discarding rest. Some of questions discarded by HC were actually more proximate to question of perversity of findings of fact recorded by Tribunal, than questions retained. From reading of order of HC, it is clear that HC examined entitlement of appellant assessee to disallowance by accepting agreements executed by assessee with commission agents; affidavits filed by husbands of two lady partners and also payments made by assessee to partnership firm. SC held that true effect of Govt. Circulars along with agreements between assessee and commission agents and details of payments made by assessee to commission agents and affidavits filed were considered by HC. Further, basis of doubts regarding very existence of partnership firm as entertained by AO was also weighed by HC to determine entitlement of assessee for deduction u/s. 37 of Act. SC held that in performing said exercise, HC did not disturb or reverse primary facts as found by Tribunal. Rather, exercise performed is one of correct legal inferences that should be drawn on facts already recorded by Tribunal and questions reframed were to said effect. Legal inference that should be drawn from primary facts, as consistently held by SC, is eminently question of law. No question of perversity was required to be framed or gone into to answer issues arising. Hence, HC found no fault with questions reframed by HC or answers provided. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Order passed by HC cannot be questioned on ground on perversity when same is passed within its jurisdiction.
