Smt. Sohbatdei v Deviplal and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 February 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

C.A. Vaidyialingam, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 713; (1972) 3 SCC 495; AIR 1971 SC 2192; 1971 UJ 395

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Void Contract, Suit for declaration

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Indian Contracts Act, 1872, s. 29 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 41 - Suit for declaration - Void contract - Legality - Appellant (plaintiff) filed suit for declaration that she was owner of suit properties mentioned in list A of plaint and that defendants be directed to execute a sale deed in her favour and to injunction restraining the defendants from interfering with her peaceful possession and enjoyment of suit properties - Respondent (defendants) filed another suit for a declaration of title and possession of same properties - Trial Court by common judgment and decree allowed the suit of appellant and dismissed the suit of respondents - Aggrieved respondents filed 2 appeals before Lower Appellate Court, which held that oral agreement was void u/s. 29 of 1872 Act and dismissed the suit of appellant - On appeal, HC concurred with the view of Lower Appellate Court - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, Lower Appellate Court has given no reason whatsoever as to why any or all the circumstances make the contract void. The HC also has in a rather summary manner accepted the views of the Lower Appellate Court in that regard. The learned Judge has stated that the amount of sale consideration was not certain and the terms of the agreement set out in the plaint show that there is no mutuality between the parties. The learned Judge has further stated that the purchase to be made by the appellant under the contract was dependent upon the sale of her properties enumerated in list B and, as it was highly doubtful as to whether she would be able to find any purchaser at all, the agreement pleaded by her cannot be considered to be certain. According to the HC the cumulative effect of all these circumstances make the agreement uncertain and vague and as such the agreement was void. The approach made both by Lower Appellate Court and HC is erroneous. None of the circumstances relied on by the Lower Appellate Court and HC will attract s. 29 of 1872 Act to the agreement in question. The fallacy underlying the reasoning of the HC is in construing the recitals in the plaint as if a document of title was being interpreted or construed by a Court. There is absolutely no ambiguity or uncertainty in any of the terms pleaded by the appellant. Consideration amount is fixed, namely, Rs.10, 000/- the view of the HC that the amount of sale consideration was not certain, is erroneous. Nowhere has appellant pleaded that the payment of the sale price to the first defendant depends upon her being able to sell her properties in list B, because it is made clear by her that by January, 1956, she will pay the entire amount of consideration and have the sale deed executed by the first defendant. Facts establish that the agreement pleaded by the appellant cannot be considered to be one the meaning of which is not certain or capable of being made certain. S. 29 of 1872 Act has no application to the facts of instant case. Trial court and Lower Appellate Court, have concurrently found on all other points in favour of appellant, and the HC has not differed from these findings. Impugned judgments of Lower Appellate Court and HC were set aside. Appeals allowed.
