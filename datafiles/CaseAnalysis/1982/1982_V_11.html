Khemchand Shankar Choudharyand Another v Vishnu Hari Patil And Others

Supreme Court of India

03 December 1982

Case Analysis

Bench

E.S. Venkataramiah, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali

Where Reported

1982 Indlaw SC 113; (1983) 1 SCC 18; AIR 1983 SC 124; 1983 (2) Bom.C.R. 294; 1982(2) SCALE 1120; [1983] 1 S.C.R. 898; 1983 UJ 20

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1860, O. 22 r. 10, s. 54 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 52 - Locus standi - For equitable partition - Whether transferees during pendency of a suit for partition of parts of an estate assessed to payment of land revenue to Govt. which was subject matter of the suit had locus standi to appear before the Revenue authorities in proceedings u/s. 54 of CPC and ask for an equitable partition of the lands even though they had not been impleaded as parties to the suit in the civil court - Held, s. 52 of Act no doubt laid down that transferee pendente lite of interest in an immovable property which was subject matter of a suit from any of parties to suit would be bound in so far as that interest was concerned by the proceedings in suit - Such transferee was representative in interest of party from whom he had acquired that interest - O.22 r. 10 of CPC clearly recognized the right of a transferee to be impleaded as a party to the proceedings and to be heard before any order was made - It might be that if transferee did not apply to be impleaded, he might suffer by default on account of any order passed in the proceedings - But if he applied to be impleaded as a party and to be heard, he had got to be so impleaded and heard - Transferee could also prefer an appeal against an order made in the said proceedings but with leave of appellate court where he was not already brought on record - Position of person on whom any interest had devolved on account of a transfer during the pendency of any suit or a proceeding was somewhat similar to the position of an heir or a legatee of a party who died during pendency of suit or proceeding, or official receiver who took over the assets of such party on his insolvency - Heir or legatee or official receiver or transferee could participate in the execution proceedings even though their names might not have been shown in the decree, preliminary or final - If they applied to court to be impleaded as parties they could not be turned out - Collector who had to effect partition of an estate u/s. 54 of CPC had no doubt to divide it in accordance with decree sent to him - But if party to such a decree died leaving some heirs about whose interest there was no dispute should he fold up his hands and return the papers to the civil court - Collector was not required do so - Collector might proceed to allot the share of deceased party to his heirs - Similarly collector might, when there was no dispute, allot the shares of a deceased party in favour of his legatees - In case of insolvency of a party, official receiver might be allotted share of insolvent - In case of transferees pendente lite also, if there was no dispute, Collector might proceed to make allotment of properties in an equitable manner instead of rejecting their claim for such equitable partition on the ground that they had no locus standi - Transferee from a party of a property which was the subject matter of partition could exercise all rights of the transferor - There was no dispute that a party could ask for equitable partition - Transferee from him, therefore, could also do so - Such construction of s. 54 of CPC advanced cause of justice - Otherwise in every case where party died, or where party was adjudicated as insolvent or where he transferred some interest in suit property pendente lite matter had got to be referred back to the civil court even though there might be no dispute about succession, devolution or transfer of interest - In any such case where there was no dispute if Collector made equitable partition taking into consideration the interests of all concerned including those on whom any interest in the subject matter had devolved, he would neither be violating decree nor transgressing any law - Collector's action would not be ultra vires - On the other hand, it would be in conformity with intention of Legislature which had placed the work of partition of lands subject to payment of assessment to the Govt. in collector's hands to be carried out 'in accordance with the law (if any) for the time being in force relating to partition or the separate possession of shares' - Appeals allowed.
