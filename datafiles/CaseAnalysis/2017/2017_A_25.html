Dagadabai (Dead) by Lrs. v Abbas @ Gulab Rustum Pinjari

Supreme Court of India

18 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Sapre, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 282; 2017(4) ALL MR 448; 2017 (122) ALR 887; 2017 (3) ALT(SC) 17; 2017 (3) Bom.C.R. 790; 2017 (4) MLJ 150; 2017 (3) RCR(Civil) 246; 2017 (2) RLW 1140; 2017(5) SCALE 22

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Adverse Possession, Adopted Son, Second Appeal, Adoption, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Family & Personal

Summary: Land & Property - Findings of facts - Interference by HC - Plaintiff filed suit against defendant contending that she inherited suit land upon death of her father-Rustum and that defendant was in unlawful possession of said suit land and sought decree for possession of suit land - Upon perusal of evidence, Trial Court held that plaintiff is the owner of suit land and that defendant is in illegal and unauthorized possession of suit land and decreed suit - Aggrieved, defendant filed appeal but First Appellate Court affirmed judgment and decree of Trial Court and dismissed appeal - However, on second appeal, HC had set aside judgment and decree of Trial Court and First Appellate Court and dismissed suit - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned judgment and decree of HC is sustainable.

Held, when Trial Court and First Appellate Court concurrently decreed plaintiff's suit by recording all the findings of facts against the defendant, then, such findings of facts are binding on HC. Further, in issue framed by HC, there was no question of law much less substantial question of law involved within the meaning of s.100 of CPC enabling HC in interfering with judgment of First Appellate Court. Further, when lower courts held that defendant failed to prove the plea of adverse possession in relation to suit land then such concurrent findings of fact was unimpeachable and binding on HC and observations of HC that it was not necessary for defendant to first admit ownership of plaintiff before raising plea of adverse possession is fundamentally wrong and are against the law of adverse possession. Defendant had not admitted plaintiff's ownership over the suit land and hence, the issue of adverse possession could not have been tried successfully at the instance of defendant as against the plaintiff. Further, defendant claimed ownership over suit land by inheritance as an adopted son of Rustum and having failed to prove said ground, was not entitled to claim the title by adverse possession against the plaintiff. As such, plea taken by defendant about the adoption for proving his ownership over the suit land as an heir of Rustum was rightly held against him. For the reasons detailed, HC erred in not only admitting second appeal but further erred in allowing it by answering the question framed in defendant's favour. Hence, impugned judgment and decree of HC is not sustainable and the same is set aside. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - Any person, who claims title over the property on the strength of adverse possession and thereby wants the Court to divest the true owner of his ownership rights over such property, is required to prove his case only against the true owner of the property. Further, such person is required to first admit the ownership of the true owner over the property to the knowledge of the true owner and secondly, the true owner has to be made a party to the suit to enable the court to decide the plea of adverse possession between the two rival claimants.
