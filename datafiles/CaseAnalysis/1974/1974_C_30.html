International Cotton Corporation Private Limited and Others v Commercial Tax Officer and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 October 1974

Case Analysis

Bench

A. Alagiriswami, A.N. Ray, Y.V. Chandrachud, A.C. Gupta

Where Reported

1974 Indlaw SC 503; (1975) SCC (Tax) 78; (1975) 3 SCC 585; AIR 1975 SC 1604; 1974 (3) CTR 265; [1975] 2 S.C.R. 345; 1975 (35) STC 1; 1975 TAXLR 1826; 1974 UJ 702

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956 - Central Sales Tax (Amendment) Act, 1969 - Inter-State sales - Tax evasion - W.P.s filed by a number of dealers in the State of Mysore (now Karnataka) questioning the levy of sales tax under the 1956 Act on certain inter-State sales - Goods dealt with were all declared goods and under the Mysore Sales Tax Act, 1957 they were taxable at the point of purchase at a single point - Assessing authorities assessed all these transactions of inter-State sales to tax - Appellants argued that the fixation of rate of tax was a legislative function and as the Parliament u/s. 8(2)(b) of the 1956 Act, had not fixed the rate of central sales tax but had adopted the rate applicable to the sale or purchase of goods inside the appropriate State in case such rate exceeded 10 per cent, the Parliament has abdicated its legislative function - Whether, Parliament in not fixing the rate itself and in adopting the rate applicable to the sale or purchase of goods inside the appropriate State had not laid down any legislative policy and has abdicated its legislative function - Held, the adoption of the rate of local sales tax for the purpose of the Central Sales tax as applicable in a particular State did not show that the Parliament had in any way abdicated its legislative function - Where a law of Parliament provided that the rate of central sales tax should be 10 per cent or that of the local sales tax, whichever be higher, a definite legislative policy could be discerned in such law, the policy being that the rate of central sales tax should in no event be less than the rate of local sales tax - In such a case, it was not possible to mention the precise figure of the maximum rate of central sales tax in the law made by the Parliament because such a rate was linked with the rate of local sales tax which was prescribed by the State Legislatures - Parliament in making such a law could not be said to have indulged in self-effacement - On the contrary, Parliament by making such a law effectuated its legislative policy, according to which the rate of central sales tax should in certain contingencies be not less than the rate of local sales tax in the appropriate State - Appeals dismissed.
