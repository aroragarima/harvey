Keshav (deceased) through Lrs. and others v Devi Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

14 October 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

R.C. Lahoti, Ashok Bhan

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 1783; (2003) 12 SCC 774

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Hindu Succession Act, 1956 - Suit for recovery of possession - Maintainability - X executed a registered deed of sale in favour of appellant/K and another/Y - Under the sale deed alienation was to the extent of 3/4 in favour of appellant and to the extent of 1/4 in favour of Y - K continued in possession of land as before - On 17-6-1956, Act came into force - On 9-8-1956 the reversioners filed a suit for declaration that the sale deed dt. 7-12-1950 executed by X in favour of K and Y was void inasmuch as what vested in X on 7-12-1950 was only life estate of a widow and she was not entitled to alienate ownership in the suit property to anyone - Trial Court dismissed the suit but the suit was decreed in first appeal by the District Judge vide judgment and decree dt. 26-11-1958 whereby the sale deed dt. 7-12-1950 was adjudged to be null and void - In 1969, X died - On 4-5-1970 the reversioners filed another suit for recovery of possession over the suit land from K - K defended himself by pleading all the relevant facts including the plea that in the event of the sale deed dated 7-12-1950 falling to the ground, he should continue to hold the land as tenant from husband of X which tenancy would continue to bind the legal heirs and successors of husband of X - Trial Court decreed the suit for possession - First appeal preferred by K was allowed and the suit was directed to be dismissed holding that K would continue to remain in possession of the suit land as tenant - Reversioners preferred second appeal to HC which was allowed - HC had restored the decree for possession setting aside the judgment of the first appellate court - Singular reasoning which prevailed with the HC was that by virtue of sale dated 7-12-1950 the pre-existing tenancy of K was extinguished by the ownership and tenancy having come to vest in the same person at the same time and by the doctrine of merger the tenancy would stand wiped out and merge into the ownership - HC held that once the tenancy had come to an end by merger, it would not revive and, therefore, the tenant K was liable to be evicted - No other aspect of the case was considered by HC - Whether HC rightly restored the decree for possession in favour of revisioners - Held, X possessed only a life estate in the suit property which was not disputed - X could not have, therefore, transferred full ownership rights - Similarly, the transfer was not in favour of K alone; he had purchased only 3/4 of the alienable interest in the property and remaining 1/4 was purchased by Y - HC was, therefore, not right in holding that the ownership and tenancy had come to vest fully in favour of one person and at the same time; more so, when the sale deed dt. 7-12-1950 by X came to be annulled subsequently in the suit filed by the next reversioners - Tenancy of Keshav did not come to an end and was not extinguished - Suit property was an agricultural land - Several tenancy laws were enacted having material bearing on legal effect of several factual events - Question which would arise for decision would be what would be the effect on tenancy rights of K - Whether by effect of tenancy laws the tenancy rights would stand enlarged into full ownership or other higher rights and whether the successors of the owners creating the tenancy would be entitled to restoration of possession from the tenant. So also, a question might arise, which would be the proper forum for initiating such legal proceedings and seeking adjudication rights - All these aspects were not gone into by the HC - Appeal allowed.
