David Patrick Ward and Another v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

26 August 1992

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Venkatachala, L.M. Sharma, S. Mohan

Where Reported

1992 Indlaw SC 578; (1992) 4 SCC 154; (1992) SCC (Cr) 814; 1992 (3) CCR 279; 1992 (3) Crimes 253; 1992 CRLR 648; 1992 CrLR(SC) 648; JT 1992 (5) SC 163; 1992(2) SCALE 442; [1992] Supp1 S.C.R. 26; 1992 (2) UJ 757

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Foreigners

Keywords: Defence Regulations

Summary: Criminal - Foreigners - Practice & Procedure National Security Act, 1980 - Possession of illegal weapons - Detention order - Legality - Petitioners were British Nationals - Petitioners along with convoy of insurgents and associates were moving in motor vehicles armed with modern and sophisticated weapons, as Chinese Live Machine Gun (LMG), three Chinese rifles together with large quantity of ammunition, they also had been used to avert their arrest by 14 Assam Rifles patrol party - They were necessary equipment's for preparing documentary of struggle of insurgents and their associates for establishment of independent Nagaland State, two VHF radio sets, one video camera charger - Halt signal was given to convoy, occupants opened fire and caused injury to one of the patrol party - Petitioner no. 2 surrendered and petitioner no. 1 was arrested only after chase - Seizure of large amount of money consisting of both Indian and foreign currency to fund insurgent activity - Detention order was passed by respondents against petitioners u/s. 3. (1)(2) of 1980 Act - Hence instant petition challenging legality of detention orders - Whether impugned detention order passed by respondents was justified - Held, if petitioners were those determined to fight for cause of Naga insurgents, as members of a U.K. based organization known as 'Naga Vigil' - If authorities concerned had taken conscious decision, as was disclosed that need of security required that petitioners should be kept in Naini Jail at Allahabad and had come forward to offer all facilities to officers of British High Commission desirous of visiting them at Naini Jail, to have easy access to petitioners, we find it difficult to grant prayer in petitions seeking shifting of petitioners from Naini Jail to Tihar Jail, Delhi - When authorities had come forward to afford all facilities to officers of British High Commission who desired to visit petitioners in Naini Jail, it could not be said that they were acting contrary to view expressed by SC in A. K. Roy 1981 Indlaw SC 381 as to need of affording facilities to those who desired to meet detenus in jail - However, we were not to be understood as saying that Court was powerless to examine conditions of preventive detention, where case warrants such examination - Regard to its facts and conscious decision taken by respondents, from point of view of security, to detain petitioners in Naini Jail, SC did not think that it could concede to their request to shift them to Tihar Jail more so, when counter-affidavit indicates that whenever visits were desired by officers of British High Commission, proper arrangements would be made in that behalf - Petitions dismissed.
