Shamsher Singh and others v Rajinder Kumar and others

Supreme Court of India

16 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 271; (2015) 5 SCC 531; AIR 2014 SC 2253; 2014 (5) ALD(SC) 95; 2014 (104) ALR 905; 2014 (4) AWC 3274; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 26; 2015 (4) CTC 441; JT 2014 (5) SC 319; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2517; 2014 (2) RCR(Civil) 865; 2014 (124) RD 293; 2014(5) SCALE 292

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: (A)Civil Procedure - Specific Relief Act, 1963, 20(2) (a) and (b) - Suit for specific performance - Decreed - Maintainability - Respondents/plaintiffs) filed a suit for specific performance against the appellants alleging that an agreement to sell was entered between them with respect to the suit land - It was alleged that out of Rs. 3,00,000/- (total agreed consideration), Rs. 2,00,000/- had already been paid by respondents and as per agreement, the balance amount were to be paid at the time of execution of sale deed - It was further alleged that later, the defendant/appellants became dishonest and wanted to sell the land to some other persons - Therefore, respondents had to file the suit in Trial Court - Defendants filed their written statement contending in that they had never entered into any agreement to sell the suit land to respondents - It was also contended that the alleged agreement to sell was a product of forgery and fabrication and so, it was null and void - Trial Court having considered the contention of both the parties, partly decreed the suit of respondents and directed the defendants to pay Rs. 3,00,000/- to respondents along with simple interest @9% per annum from the date of execution of the agreement to sell till the date of payment - Being aggrieved by the Judgment and Decree, appellants filed first appeal on the ground that the alleged agreement to sell was not valid and legal and the appellants never intended to sell the suit land - Appellants further contended that there was no signature of any of the attesting witness on the receipt of the earnest money and the land was already under mortgage with the banks and so there was no question of any agreement to sell and urged other grounds as well - Respondents also filed appeal against the judgment and decree of Trial Court with prayer for direction of execution of sale deed and transfer of possession of the suit land - Both the appeals were disposed of by First Appellate Court - Appellate Court while dismissing the appeal preferred by appellants, allowed the appeal preferred by respondents and directed appellants to get the sale deed executed and registered in favour of respondents - Second appeals preferred by appellants against the common judgment passed by the First Appellate Court were dismissed by impugned common judgment - Whether the concurrent finding of the second appellate court in reversing the finding of the Trial Court in its judgment to exercise discretionary power not to grant decree of specific performance as provided u/s.20(2) (a) and (b) of Act, after referring to Exs.P-2 and P-8 and other relevant facts, was legal and valid -

Held, HC had erred in law in dismissing the Second Appeal filed by appellants questioning the correctness of the judgment and decree of the First Appellate Court in reversing the judgment of the Trial Court wherein it had decreed a sum of Rs. 3,00,000/- with 9% interest to be paid to respondents in lieu of granting the relief of specific performance in relation to the property in question in favour of the respondents by exercising its sound discretionary power u/s.20(2) (a) and (b) of Act - That judgment and decree of the First Appellate Court was challenged by appellants urging various legal grounds - HC should have examined the case of appellant Nos. 1 to 3 keeping in view the discretionary power conferred upon the courts below while examining the respective claim and counter claims of the parties particularly in the light of the covenants in the agreement of sale, Ex.P-2 and also with reference to exhibit P-8, the compromise arrived at between the parties at the Police Station - Appellate courts had erred in holding that the agreement of sale was proved by respondent though the defendant Nos. 1 to 3 in their statement denied the execution and pleaded that it was a mortgage and not an agreement of sale - Said finding of fact recorded by the Trial Court was annulled by the First Appellate Court in exercise of its appellate power without examining the pleadings and substantiated evidence on record particularly the recitals of the agreement of sale and the compromise deed between the parties - Therefore, the said finding of fact recorded by the First Appellate Court in reversing the finding of the Trial Court recorded on the contentious issue is not only erroneous but also sufferred from error in law - Therefore, the substantial question of law that would arise in the Second Appeal before the HC namely, whether the finding of the Trial Court in exercise of its discretionary power u/s.20(2) (a) and (b) of Act should be reversed, keeping in view the Exhibit P-8, the compromise deed and awarding monetary decree of Rs. 3,00,000/- though the advance amount paid was Rs. 2,00,000/- was contrary to the legal evidence and the statutory provisions of s.20(2) (a) and (b) and also s.21(2) of Act - Trial Court's decree insofar as the amount was concerned and order the defendant Nos.1 to 3 to return Rs. 2,00,000/- with interest and not an amount of Rs. 3,00,000/- as directed by Trial Court - Appeals allowed.

(B)Civil Procedure - Specific Relief Act, 1963, 20(2) (a) and (b) - Suit for specific performance - Decreed - Maintainability - Whether the appellate courts were right in ignoring the relevant legal aspect of the case viz. whether the vendors, who had mortgaged the schedule property to the banks, had the right to enter into agreement and whether such agreement was valid and in that case, do the respondents then have the right of enforcing such agreement without asking the vendor to redeem the mortgage in relation to the suit schedule property and obtaining a valid discharge -

Held, important aspect of the case that had slipped the eyes of the courts below was the question of the mortgage of the suit property, as pleaded by appellant Nos. 4 and 5 - Appellants placed the confirmation of mutation in favour of the Bank, Ex. D-1 and the Mortgage deed, Ex. D-2 as evidence - Respondents had referred to the same in para 2 of their pleadings and appellants, in reply had admitted that in para 2 of their written statement wherein they had stated that it was a matter of record that they have mortgaged their portion of the land in favour of appellant Nos. 4 and 5 - More importantly, respondents had admitted the same in para 2 of their plaint, therefore it was in their knowledge that the land in question was already mortgaged with appellant Nos. 4 and 5 - There was no finding recorded on the same by either the appellate courts or the Trial Court and as per s.13(c) of the Act, when the vendor/appellant professed to sell unencumbered property but the same was mortgaged, then the vendor had only a right to redeem it and the purchaser might compel him to redeem the mortgage and to obtain a valid discharge - That aspect of the matter, too, was not dealt with by the Trial Court or the appellate courts - Appeals allowed.

(B)What order/decree are the appellants entitled to -

Held, judgment and order of the Trial Court should be modified and the judgments and orders of the appellate courts should be reversed - There were many discrepancies on the face of the case and the Trial Court would have done well to examine all the evidence with care and diligence, which were not done in the instant case - In spite of the same, it would be prudent to uphold the judgment and decree of the Trial Court by modifying it instead of the judgments and orders of the appellate courts which have decreed the original suit for specific performance of the agreement to sell the suit schedule property - Appeals allowed.
