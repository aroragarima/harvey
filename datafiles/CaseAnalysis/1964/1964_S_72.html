FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Ram Ratan Alias Ratan Ahir and others v State of Bihar and another

Supreme Court of India

22 September 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Raghubar Dayal, A.K. Sarkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 465; AIR 1965 SC 926; [1965] 1 S.C.R. 293

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Cattle-trespass Act,1871

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Cattle Trespass Act, 1871, s. 10 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 302 and 326 - Illegal seizures and impounding of cattle - Whether person who seizes cattle illegally, purporting to act u/s. 10 of the Act commits the offence of theft or robbery? - Held, s. 10 of the Act authorizes certain persons having interest in the crops grown on any land to seize or cause to be seized cattle trespassing on it and doing damage thereto or to any crop or produce thereon - It requires them to send the cattle so seized, within 24 hours, to the pound established for the village in which the land is situate - The Act does not make the illegal seizure of cattle punishable as an offence - Person seizing cattle illegally is made liable to pay compensation for the loss caused to the owner of the cattle on account of the illegal seizure of cattle or of detaining the cattle in contravention of the Act - Therefore, when a person seizes cattle on the ground that they were trespassing on his land and causing damage to his crop or produce and gives out that he was taking them to the pound, he commits no offence of theft however mistaken he may be about his right to that land or crop - Remedy of the owner of the cattle so seized is to take action u/s. 20 of the Act and he has no right to use force to rescue the cattle so seized - Appeal allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Cattle Trespass Act, 1871, s. 10 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 302 and 326 - Whether illegal seizures and impounding of cattle constitute the offence of theft under the IPC? - Held, a person is said to do a thing dishonestly when he does anything with the intention of causing wrongful gain to one person or wrongful loss to another person - Till the property is moved, no offence of theft can be committed even if the alleged offender had intended to take dishonestly the property out of the possession of any other person without his consent - Therefore, mere seizure of cattle found trespassing on land does not amount to moving the cattle - Act of moving the cattle would be subsequent to seizing them - It follows therefore that the seizure of cattle, though illegal, cannot amount to the offence of theft - Further, in the case of illegal seizures and impounding of cattle, the person seizing the cattle does not gain anything - He simply takes the cattle to the pound - He does not use them for his purpose - He, in fact, exercises no greater dominion over those cattle than that of being in their custody on their journey to the pound - It causes wrongful loss to the owner of the cattle inasmuch as he keeps the owner out of possession of the cattle as he was wrongfully deprived; of the property for the time being, it being not necessary that the deprivation of property be of a permanent character - In such circumstances, the owner of the cattle cannot be said to be deprived of his property - Owner can get back the cattle from the pound on payment of the fine and expenses - Owner of the cattle, seized illegally, suffers no loss and that therefore the act of illegal seizure of cattle does not cause any wrongful loss to the owner of the cattle - Appeal allowed.
