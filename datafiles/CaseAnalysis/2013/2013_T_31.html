A. Savariar v The Secretary, Tamil Nadu Public Service Commission And Another

Supreme Court of India

15 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 92; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 898; JT 2013 (3) SC 170; 2013(2) SCALE 348; 2013 (2) SLR 259; 2013 (3) WLN(SC) 93

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Tamil Nadu Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules

Summary: Service - Tamil Nadu Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, 1988, r. 17 - Dismissal - Challenged - Sustainability - Appeals wedre directed against judgments of HC whereby the appellant's challenge to the order of Single Judge was negatived and his dismissal from service was upheld - Whether the appellant's dismissal from service was legally correct and the HC rightly declined to interfere with the orders passed by the punishing authority and appellate authority - Held, r. 8 of Rules specified various penalties including dismissal from service which could be imposed on member of the Civil Service of the State or person holding Civil Post under the State - R. 17(b) of Rules, which contained the procedure for holding inquiry - analysis of the above reproduced rule made it clear that holding of oral inquiry was sine qua non for recording finding by the inquiring authority and the report of inquiry should contain sufficient record of evidence and statement of the findings together with grounds thereof - Substratum of the main allegation leveled against the appellant was that he had unauthorisedly issued order of appointment to X who was on leave, to act as Invigilator at Arts College for Women and appointed Y and Z as substitute Invigilators despite the fact that their names were not included in the list furnished by the Collector - Two other allegations leveled against the appellant were that he had unauthorisedly produced office note in the Court and went to the examination hall by neglecting his office work - On behalf of the Commission oral evidence was said to have been adduced to substantiate the allegations leveled against the appellant but neither the report of the Enquiry Officer nor the orders passed by the Controller of Examinations or the Chairman of the Commission made mention of that evidence and none of them relied upon the same for recording finding that the appellant had arrogated to himself the powers of his superiors and unauthorisedly appointed Invigilators - As matter of fact, Enquiry Officer simply referred to the statement of the appellant, analysed the same and concluded that Charges No. 1, 2, 4 and 6 were proved against him - It was thus evident that the inquiry report was not prepared in consonance with r. 17(b)(i) of Rules - Controller of Examinations went step further and recorded finding that the actions of the appellant had resulted in compromising the integrity of the Commission as whole - Concerned officer did so despite the fact that the appellant had not been charged with the allegation that he had by his action/omission compromised with the integrity of the Commission and no evidence was produced to substantiate the same - Appellate Authority, i.e., the Chairman recorded detailed reasons but the order passed by him also did not make reference to the evidence produced for proving the charges leveled against appellant - Single Judge and DB of the HC failed to notice fatal flaw in the orders passed by the Controller of Examinations and the Chairman and decided the matter by assuming that even though the Commission had not adduced any tangible evidence to prove the charges against the appellant, same stood proved because of the weakness of his defence - SC in one case held that it was the primary duty of the person making the allegations to establish the same by producing evidence and not for the delinquent to produce negative evidence to prove his innocence - Delhi Cloth and General Mills Company v. Ludh Budh Singh, 1972 Indlaw SC 402, relied on - SC was satisfied that the punishment of dismissal imposed on the appellant was legally unsustainable - Controller of Examinations and the Chairman of the Commission did not consider the impact of the alleged unauthorized action of the appellant in nominating/deputing substitute Invigilators at the particular examination centre - One could appreciate the Commission's concern about mixing of the question papers of afternoon examination with the question papers of morning examination, but in the absence of any evidence to show that "P" Section of the Commission, where the appellant was posted, had anything to do with the question papers or that he had custody of the question papers, Commission was not justified in holding him guilty of the incident which occurred at the examination centre - Indeed, it was nobody's case that the appellant was, in any way, responsible for mixing of the question papers - Therefore, the findings recorded by the Inquiry Officer and the two Authorities that the appellant was guilty of serious misconduct could not be sustained - Appeal allowed.
