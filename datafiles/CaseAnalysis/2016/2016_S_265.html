Ramesh and others v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

22 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 842; (2017) 1 SCC 529; AIR 2016 SC 5554; 2016 ALL MR (Cri) 5348; 2017 CRLJ 352; JT 2016 (11) SC 232; 2016 (4) RCR(Criminal) 955; 2016(12) SCALE 246; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 936

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Dying Declaration, Rape, FIR, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Appreciation Of Evidence, Review, Endorsement, Appreciation, Kerosene, Version, Doctor, Dead Body, Criminal Justice, Reply, Fear, Dealing, Medical Officer, Autopsy, Pressure, Rigorous Imprisonment, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 34, 498A - Murder - Conviction Challenged - Appellant/accused nos. 1-4 were charged for murder of deceased 'X' and Trial Court acquitted all accused - Further, HC in appeal reversed judgment of Trial Court on basis of dying declaration of deceased and convicted accused u/s. 302 of IPC - HC further convicted accused u/s. 498A/34 of IPC - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, it is trite that dying declaration is a substantive piece of evidence and can be made the basis of conviction once the Court is convinced that dying declaration is made voluntarily and is not influenced by any extraneous circumstances. A very vital distinction which the Court has to keep in mind while dealing with such appeals against the order of acquittal is that interference by the court is justifiable only when a clear distinction is kept between perversity in appreciation of evidence and merely the possibility of another view. It may not be quite appropriate for the HC to merely record that the judgment of the trial court was perverse without specifically dealing with the facets of perversity relating to the issues of law and/or appreciation of evidence, as otherwise such observations of the HC may not be sustainable in law. Thus, on reconsideration of the evidence on record, if the appellate court finds the verdict of acquittal to be perverse or against the settled position of law, it is duly empowered to set aside the same. In instant case, the HC has interfered on the ground that the very approach of the trial court in appreciating the evidence on record was legally unsustainable. If such observations of the HC are correct, it was fully justified in interjecting with the verdict of the trial court. Appeal dismissed.
