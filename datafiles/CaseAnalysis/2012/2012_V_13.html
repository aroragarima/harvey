National Council for Teacher Education and another v Venus Public Education Society and others

Supreme Court of India

01 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 389; (2013) 1 SCC 223; AIR 2012 SC 941; 2013 (1) ALD(SC) 79; 2013 (96) ALR 669; 2013 (1) AWC 331; 2012 (6) CTC 458; 2013 (136) FLR 217; 2013 (1) JabLJ 1; JT 2012 (11) SC 120; 2013 (2) MahLJ 565; 2012 (8) MLJ(SC) 521; 2013 (2) MPLJ 29; 2012(10) SCALE 622; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 919; 2012 (6) SLR 512

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Education; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Directions

Summary: Education - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - National Council for Teacher Education Act, 1993, ss. 14(3)(a), 14(3)(b), 18 - National Council for Teacher Education (Recognition, Norms and Procedure) Regulations, 2009, regn. 7(11), cl. 7(9) - Recognition - Directions - Legality - Respondent (society) submitted an application to Western Regional Committee (WRC) of National Council for Teacher Education (NCTE/appellant) for grant of recognition for the purpose of conducting D.El.Ed. course from the academic session 2010-11 - On receipt of the said application the WRC, after scrutiny of the same, issued a communication to respondent institution was advised to remove the deficiencies within a span of 60 days and It was also required to submit a reply pertaining to deficiencies pointed out by WRC - Thereafter, visiting team carried out inspection and submitted its report to WRC which, in its 136th meeting, decided to issue a show cause notice u/s. 14(3)(b) of the 1993 Act, requiring the respondent to file its representation within 21 days - However, reply to show cause notice was received and WRC considered the same and took decision to refuse recognition on the ground that the approved building plan submitted by college showed a square building with ground and 2 floors, whereas the video graph showed the building was rectangular and having ground and one floor - Said decision was communicated vide order dt.3-8-2010 where under the WRC refused recognition in exercise of power u/s. 14(3)(b) of 1993 Act - Aggrieved respondent filed an appeal u/s. 18 of the Act before Appellate Authority, and after the appeal was disposed of, WRC decided to constitute a visiting team - In the meantime respondent preferred writ petition for issue of writ of mandamus to NCTE to grant recognition for academic session 2010-11 - During the pendency of petition, WRC decided to conduct further inspection and its report indicated that a functionary of Society told the team that as the matter was subjudice, WRC had no authority to inspect - However, team went to the institution and took photographs of building - When the matter came up before HC, HC quashed the decision of inspection of institution and petitioner was directed to consider the case of respondent for grant of recognition - Thereafter, WRC considered the matter and issued a 'letter of intent' for grant of recognition for D.El.Ed. course u/cl. 7(9) of 2007 Regulations and directed to comply the certain requirement before formal recognition was considered u/regn. 7(11) of the Regulations u/s. 14(3)(a) of the Act - Meantime respondent filed a contempt petition, and petitions to command the NCTE to grant recognition for academic session 2011-12 - However, DB disposed of contempt petition and writ petition by directing that in recognition order dt.27-10-2011 it should be added that the institution was entitled for recognition for the D.El.Ed. course with an annual intake of 50 students for academic session 2011-12 also - aggrieved NCTE filed instant appeal - Whether impugned order passed by HC was justified -

Held, HC clearly erred in misconstruing its earlier order passed in writ petition 4541 of 2011 - True it was, there was some delay and, thus, HC was moved in another writ petition wherein the it had granted liberty to file a contempt petition expecting that the directions in the earlier order would be duly complied with - Thereafter, as was manifest, letter of intent was issued but the institution instead of complying with the same moved HC for grant of recognition - HC in the initial order had directed to consider the case of institution for grant of recognition without further inspection - Issuance of letter of intent was necessary prior to grant of formal letter of recognition - However, HC being moved directed for issuance of formal letter of recognition which was issued with a postulate that the institution should only grant admission after obtaining affiliation from the examining body in terms of cl. 8(12) of 2009 Regulations - Order of recognition clearly mentioned that it was meant for the academic session 2012-13 - HC could not have directed the recognition to be retrospectively operative because certain formalities remained to be complied with - It could not have put the clock back - It needs no special emphasis to state that HC did not keep itself alive to the conceptual difference between 'letter of intent' and 'formal recognition' - True it was, there was delay but that could not have enabled the HC to issue a writ for treating the recognition to be effective for the year 2011-12 with intake of fifty students - That apart, institution had not obtained affiliation from the university - Thus, direction of HC was contrary to the provisions of law and interpretation of 1993 Act and the Regulations made by SC - In instant case, basic motto should have been 'transparency' - Unfortunately, institution betrayed the trust of students and students, in a way, atrophied their intelligence - Institution decidedly exhibited characteristics of carelessness - It seems that they had forgotten that they were accountable to law - Students, while thinking 'vision of hope', chose to play possum - Law did not countenance either of the ideas - Thus, plea propounded with anxiety, vehemence and desperation on behalf of the appellant was not acceptable and, accordingly Court unhesitatingly repel the same - NCTE should have acted in quite promptitude, for a statutory authority which was conferred with the power, was required to act within the parameters of law and the directions given by Court and further not to create a feeling among the educational institutions that they were re harassed - Court expected that NCTE should function with propriety regard being had to the statutory responsibility bestowed on it by Parliament - Its actions neither should show arbitrariness nor should it reflect any indulgence. - Objectivity, reliability and trust were to be the motto of NCTE and the committees working under it - Impugned order passed by HC was set aside and that of NCTE was restored - Appeal allowed.
