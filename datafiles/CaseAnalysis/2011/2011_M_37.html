M/S. Delhi Airtech Services Private Limited And Another. v State Of Uttar Pradesh And Another

Supreme Court of India

18 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 638; (2011) 9 SCC 354; JT 2011 (9) SC 440; 2011(9) SCALE 201; [2011] 12 S.C.R. 191

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Emergency Provisions, Metro Railways (Construction of Works) Act, 1978

Summary: Swatanter Kumar J.

Constitution - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 17(1), 17(4), 9(1), 6, 17(3A), 11A, 16, 17 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.19(1)(f), 31A,300A - Acquisition of Land - Issuance of notification - Payment of compensation - Acquisition proceedings - Applicability of provision - Land of appellant as abadi land was sought to be acquired by appropriate Govt. under notification issued u/s. 4(1) r/w. ss.17(1), 17(4) of 1894 Act - Land was acquired for planned industrial development through NOIDA - Appellants alleged that they did not receive any notice u/s. 9(1) of 1894 Act but possession of land was nevertheless taken - According to appellants, even after lapse of more than three and half years after publication of declaration u/s. 6 of 1894 Act, award had not been made and published - Despite inordinate delay, they were neither paid estimated compensation in terms of s. 17(3A) of 1894 Act at time of taking of possession, nor had Collector passed award within two years of making declaration u/s. 17(1), as required by s.11A of 1894 Act - Non- payment of compensation and conduct of Govt. compelled petitioners to file writ petition in HC - HC dismissed Petition holding that provisions of s. 11A of the Act were not attracted to proceedings for acquisition taken by Govt. u/s. 17 of 1894 Act - Hence, instant Petition - Whether provisions of s. 11A of 1984 Act would apply to acquisition u/ss. 17(1) r/w. 17(4) of 1894 Act - Held, very purpose and object of 1984 Act would stand defeated if provisions of s. 11A of 1894 Act were applied to acquisitions u/s.17 of 1894 Act - Where lands were acquired in exercise of emergent powers of State u/s. 17 of 1894 Act, notification u/s.4(1) of 1894 Act was issued and notification itself refers to provisions of s.17(1) as well as s.17(4) of 1894 Act - S. 11A of 1894 Act had no application to acquisition proceedings conducted under provisions of s. 17 of 1894 Act - Once acquired land vested in Govt. in terms of ss. 16 or 17(1) of 1894 Act, possession of which had already been taken, such land was incapable of being re-vested or reverted to owners/persons interested therein, for lack of any statutory provision for same under 1894 Act - Therefore, default in complying with provisions of s. 17(3A) of 1894 Act could not result in invalidating or vitiating entire acquisition proceedings, particularly when possession of acquired land was taken and it had vested in Govt. free from all encumbrances - However, according to scheme of 1894 Act, provisions of s. 17 of the 1894 Act could be construed strictly but such interpretation must be coupled with doctrine of literal and contextual interpretation, while ensuring that object of legislation was not defeated by such interpretation - Thus, once right to property ceased to be Fundamental Right after omission of art. 19(1)(f) of the Constitution, addition of arts. 31A, 300A by 44th 1978 Act, could not place legal right to property at same pedestal to that of fundamental right falling under Chapter III of the Constitution - Hence, provisions of the 1894 Act were not violative of art. 14 of the Constitution and rights of citizens and interest of State could be balanced under provisions of 1894 Act, without any violation of Constitutional mandate - Appeal allowed.

A.K. Ganguly, J.

(B) Constitution - Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4(1), 17, 17(1), 17(2), 17(3), 17(3A), 17(4) -Constitution of India, 1950, arts.19(1)(f), 31A, 300A - Exercise of emergency provisions - Compliance of provision - Whether provisions of s.17(3A) of 1894 Act were mandatory or directory and invoking of emergency provisions which had effect of dispensing with provision and Court was entitled to construe emergency provisions strictly to safeguards inbuilt in s.17(3A) of the Act against such drastic provisions as conditions precedent and mandatory for a valid exercise of emergency provisions - Held, emergency provisions were invoked u/s.17 of Act, provisions of s. 11A of the Act should not apply - However, reason behind enacting s. 17 (3A) of the Act were incorporated in order to strike balance between rights of State and those of land owner - Legislative intent in s.17(3A) of the Act was thus expressed that before taking possession of any land u/s.17(1), (2) of the Act, Collector should tender payment of 80% of estimated compensation for such land to persons interested and entitled thereto and this was clear mandate of law - Therefore, emergency provisions of statute which empowered State to acquire land by dispensing with provisions of making enquiry, it was clear that said provision was drastic provision - Provisions of said Act were expropriatory in nature and must be strictly construed - Though, s. 17(1) and 17(2) and 17(3A) of 1894 Act must be read together and could not be worked out in isolation - Therefore, eminent domain concept was subject to aforesaid statutory condition and must be read subject to due process concept introduced in constitutional law - S. 17(3A) of the Act as consistently with Constitutional doctrine of due process as articulated in expression 'authority of law' u/art. 300A of the Constitution which constitutionally protected deprivation of right to property, save by authority of law - S. 17(3A) of the Act was enacted for protecting rights of deprived land-loser in emergency acquisition - Thus, emergency acquisition u/s. 17 of the Act, requirement of payment u/s. 17(3A) of the Act must be complied with and provision of ss. 17(1), 17(2) of the Act could not be worked out which was in nature of condition precedent - Hence, emergency acquisition without complying with s. 17(3A) of the Act was illegal - Appeal allowed.
