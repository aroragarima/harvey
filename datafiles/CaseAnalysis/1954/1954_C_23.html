Â Dhakeswari Cotton Mills Limited v Commissioner of Income Tax, West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

29 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Ghulam Hasan, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 9; AIR 1955 SC 65; 1955 (27) CTR 126; [1954] 26 ITR 775; 1955 (1) MLJ(SC) 60; 1955 SCJ 122; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 941

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Constitution - Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 22(2) and 28(3) - (A) Whether power conferred on SC by art. 136 of Constitution of India, 1950 being an extraordinary power, should not be exercised for purpose of reviewing findings of fact when law dealing with subject had declared those findings as final and conclusive? - Held, when Court reaches conclusion that a person had been dealt with arbitrarily or that a court or tribunal within territory of India had not given a fair deal to a litigant, then no technical hurdles of any kind like finality of finding of facts or otherwise can stand in way of exercise of this power because whole intent and purpose of this article was that it was the duty of SC to see that injustice was not perpetuated or perpetrated by decisions of courts and tribunals because certain laws have made decisions of these courts or tribunals final and conclusive - (B) Whether finding given by ITO and affirmed by Appellate Asst. Commr. and Tribunal was based on material and it could not be said that these bodies had acted arbitrarily in this matter? - Held, in making assessment u/s. 23(3) of Act, ITO was not entitled to make a pure guess and make an assessment without reference to any evidence or any material at all - There must be something more than bare suspicion to support assessment u/s. 23(3) of the Act - Tribunal violated certain fundamental rules of justice in reaching its conclusions - Firstly, it did not disclose to assessee what information had been supplied to it by departmental representative - Next, it did not give any opportunity to assessee to rebut material furnished to it by him, and, lastly, it declined to take all material that assessee wanted to produce in support of its case - Assessee had not had a fair hearing - Both ITO and Tribunal in estimating gross profit rate on sales did not act on any material but acted on pure guess and suspicion - It was thus a fit case for exercise of power u/art. 136 of Constitution of India, 1950 - Appeal allowed.
