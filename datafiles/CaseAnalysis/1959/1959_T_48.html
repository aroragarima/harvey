Â Sardar Sarup Singh and Others v State of Punjab and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 April 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

S.K. Das, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 55; AIR 1959 SC 860; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 499

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Family & Personal

Keywords: Religious Denomination, Sikhs, Sikh Gurdwaras (Supplementary) Act, 1925

Summary: Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950 arts. 26(b), 26(d) and 245 - Punjab Sikh Gurdwaras Act, 1925, ss. 43, 45, 46, 133, 134 and 148 - (A) Whether Supreme Court is competent to look into questions of public policy or motives of legislature in enacting law ? - Held, in determining constitutionality of a statute, court is not concerned with motives of legislature and whatever justification some people may feel in their criticisms of political wisdom of a particular legislative or executive action, Supreme Court cannot be called upon to embark on an enquiry into public policy or investigate into questions of political wisdom or even to pronounce upon motives of legislature in enacting a law which it is otherwise competent to make - (B) What distinction lies between cls. (b) and (d) u/art. 26 of Constitution? - Further held, distinction between cls. (b) and (d) strikes one at once - Administration of its property by a religious denomination has thus been placed on a different footing from right to manage its own affairs in matters of religion - Cl. (d) is a fundamental right which no legislature can take away, whereas cl. (b) can be regulated by laws which legislature can validly impose u/art. 26 of Constitution - (C) Whether s. 148 B of Punjab Sikh Gurdwaras Act, 1925 is ultra vires art. 26 of Constitution? - Further held, not violative - Contention that a direct election of members of board by entire Sikh community is itself a matter of Sikh religion and therefore part of content of right guaranteed u/art. 26(b) cannot be accepted as correct - Principle of direct election on universal denominational suffrage cannot be raised to pedestal of religion within meaning of art. 26(b) of Constitution - Petition dismissed.
