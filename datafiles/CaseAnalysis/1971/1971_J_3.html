J. K. Synthetics Limited v J. K. Synthetics Mazdoor Union

Supreme Court of India

09 September 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Jaganmohan Reddy, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 241; (1971) 3 SCC 509; AIR 1972 SC 1954; 1971 (23) FLR 367; 1972 LabIC 689; 1971 (2) LLJ 552; [1972] 1 S.C.R. 651

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Labour & Industrial Law; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Payment of bonus - Appellant filed instant appeal against award of Tribunal directing the payment of bonus of rs.1,21,000/- apart from an amount of Rs. 90,000/- already disbursed to workmen of appellant for the year 1962-63 - Dispute for bonus year beginning 1-7-1962 and ending 30-6-1963 was raised by workmen because Company which had admittedly made profits, did not pay them a bonus though a gratuity of one month was given to them - There was no dispute with regard to any of 8items which comprised computation of gross profits amounting to Rs. 62.16 lakhs - Union also did not dispute deduction of interest on debentures of Rs. 0.06 lakhs; share transfer fee of Rs. 0.05 lakhs the notional normal depreciation of Rs. 30.57 lakhs and return on share capital of Rs. 7.50 lakhs - It had however challenged deduction of Rs. 4.1 lakhs received as dividend on shares as extraneous income which was being claimed as a deduction by management - There was 2items of controversy were urged (1) relating to extraneous income of Rs. 4.10 lakhs and (2) relating to rehabilitation requirement amounting to Rs. 75.89 lakhs, first of which Tribunal disallowed while in respect of second it only admitted Rs. 4.23 lakhs - With respect to first item, the disallowance of Rs. 4.10 lakhs, management not only claimed this amount but also Rs. 7.5 lakhs as return on paid up capital of Rs. 125 lakhs @ 6% per annum - It appeared to Tribunal that if Company wanted to exclude income from investments it could not also be allowed 6% return on that part of share capital which was invested elsewhere and at the same time be allowed to treat the income of Rs. 4.10 lakhs earned therefrom as extraneous income, because apart from deducting income tax on this amount the Company also meets expenses of administration and management in respect of said investments - In this view it sustained objection of Union - Whether in any particular case return on investments amounts to an extraneous income would depend on facts and circumstances of each case - In instant case, there could be no doubt that return from investments was a return on a part of paid up capital of Company which was invested for the purpose of earning an income - It could not therefore be construed as extraneous income - Tribunal was justified in disallowing deduction of Rs. 4.10 lakhs and in fact on behalf of Appellant it was frankly conceded before SC that claim in respect of said item could not be pressed on any tenable or valid grounds - Whether investments of Appellant amount to Rs. 85.6 lakhs was available for rehabilitation which in turn would depend upon whether these investments were made in course of business of Company or were unconnected with its business and only invested with a view to earning extraneous income - Held, it was clear that trading investments were made prior to 1960 when Company Was an Investment Company, as such these investments were not connected with activities of Company were extraneous to its business and did not form part of the reserves available for rehabilitation - In the circumstances Tribunal was not justified in including this amount in the amounts available for rehabilitation purposes - Result of non-exclusion of Rs. 85.60 lakhs would result in a negative balance, respondents were entitled to challenge claim for rehabilitation on the ground that essential requisites were not established by any cogent or sufficient evidence - In computing requirements for rehabilitation as was stated often, regard should be had, to 2 imponderables out of 3main elements because one of them namely original cost of the asset was specifically ascertainable while other 2have to be established as near as possible which might to some extent involve an estimate based on evidence deducible therefrom - These 2imponderables were multiplier and deviser - Unless all these elements were determined amount required for rehabilitation could not be ascertained - Further, it was apparent that management was relying only on a few sample invoices which they said they had produced while depending heavily only on Parrv's Handbook for ascertaining life of machinery and probable cost - It was quite probable that price of indigenous industry as appearing from bulletin of RBI had gone up but that did not furnish a basis for arriving at any specific multiplier or deviser for Appellant's plant - All that invoices produced before Tribunal establish was only probable cost of machinery of 2 1/2 lakhs, in an attempt to prove the cost of replacement of plant and machinery worth Rs. 825 lakhs -Therefore, Tribunal was amply justified in saying that only evidence given was of few invoices value of which was only 2 1/2 % of requirement of replacement cost which in SC view was not sufficient to establish, how many machines in each Department of industry were required, what was nature of those machines and what was probable cost of each of those machines - SC was far from satisfied that management had placed before Tribunal any satisfactory evidence much less sufficient evidence to arrive at a multiplier and deviser nor had Tribunal any bases for arriving at its own multiplier and deviser except it be on a pure conjecture and guess work - Order accordingly.
