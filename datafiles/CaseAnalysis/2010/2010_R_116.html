Reliance Natural Resources Limited v Reliance Industries Limited

Supreme Court of India

07 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, B. Sudershan Reddy, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 347; (2010) 7 SCC 1; [2010] 156 Comp Cas 455; 2010 (2) CompLJ 649; JT 2010 (5) SC 413; 2010(5) SCALE 223; [2010] 5 S.C.R. 704

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Corporate; Petroleum & Gas

Keywords: Demerger, Companies Act, 1913, Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and Other Maritime Zones Act, 1976, Companies (Amendment) Act, 2001, Companies (Amendment) Act, 1965, Petroleum and Natural Gas Rules, 1959

Summary: Hon'ble Justice B. Sudershan Reddy

(A) Corporate - Petroleum & Gas - New Exploration and Licensing Policy, 1999 - In 1973, RIL was set up by Dhirubhai Ambani with his two sons Mukesh Ambani and Anil Ambani - In April 2000, a consortium of companies, Reliance Industries Limited (RIL) and NIKO, together forming Contractor, entered into a Production Sharing Contract (PSC) with UOI to explore for and produce Petroleum, which includes both crude oil and natural gas - In 2002, founder of RIL, passed away - Differences started between Mukesh Ambani and Anil Ambani over management and control of group companies - Settlement was arrived between Ambani Brothers - Both brothers arrived at a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU)/family arrangement dated 18.06.2005 - Mukesh Ambani took over the responsibility for RIL and IPCL and Anil Ambani took over the responsibility for Reliance Infocomm Ltd., Reliance Energy Ltd. and Reliance Capital Ltd - On 11.08.2005, "Reliance Natural Resources Limited (RNRL) was acquired by RIL for purpose of de-merger - RIL (de-merged company) moved a petition in Bombay HC to obtain a sanction of Scheme of Arrangement (Scheme) between RIL and four other companies ((i) Reliance Energy Ventures Limited, (ii) Global Fuel Management Services Limited, (iii) Reliance Capital Ventures Limited and (iv) Reliance Communication Ventures Limited) - Scheme became effective from 21.12.2005 - Drafts of GSMA (Gas Sale Master Agreement) and GSPA (Gas Sale Purchase Agreement) were approved by Board of RIL - Board of RNRL was re-constituted in order to hand over management and control of resulting companies to Anil Ambani - On 12.04.2006, RIL made an application to Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas (MoPNG) for approval of gas price at which sale of 28 MMSCMD of gas was agreed with RNRL under GSMA, which was refused - RNRL filed a Company application u/s. 392 of Companies Act, 1956 seeking directions from HC to order RIL to change gas supply agreements in a certain specific manner - According to RNRL, gas supply agreements were not bankable in international financial markets, did not demerge business of supply of gas to gas based energy producing companies within ADA Group and thereby very purpose for which RNRL had been set up was negated - Company Judge ordered parties to renegotiate - Company Judge held that company application u/s. 392 of Companies Act is maintainable - However, u/s. 392 of Companies Act company court cannot direct or dictate to maintain or amend or modify and/or insist for a particular clause or clauses of such gas supply agreement or such other commercial agreement/contract - It further held that MoU and its content are binding to both parties RIL and RNRL - On appeal, DB held that Company Application to be maintainable and it further held that as far as fixation of price is concerned, Government has power to fix price, but only for its "take" of gas, and although Government could lay down Gas Utilization Policy, such Utilization Policy would apply only to gas available for allocation after certain quantity of gas which according to Division Bench, "stood allocated" to RNRL as per MoU - Further, DB also ordered parties to renegotiate with respect to issues regarding identity, definition of affiliate and limitation of liability to make gas supply agreements bankable - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether MoU be binding on company? - Held, MoU is a private pact between members of Ambani family which is not binding on RIL - Courts below having rightly directed parties to negotiate, and further having rightly refused to grant prayers in Company Application, however, fell into error directing MoU to be binding and basis for further negotiations between parties - However, both Single Judge and Division Bench committed a serious error in exercising jurisdiction in manner they did u/s. 392 of Companies Act, 1956, for such interference has resulted in provisions of a document (MoU) which was not before shareholders supersede Scheme of Arrangement - Such a document could not have been read into and incorporated in Scheme propounded by Board, approved by shareholders and sanctioned by Company Court - (B) Empowered Group of Ministers framed a utilization policy and also approved price formula/basis submitted by RIL - EGOM decisions - Held, EGOM decisions, regarding utilization of natural gas and price formula/basis etc. do not suffer from any legal or constitutional infirmities - They shall apply to all supplies of natural gas under PSC - Parties are bound by governmental policy and approvals regarding price, quantity and tenure for supply of gas - (C) Ownership - Held, under PSC in issue Contractor (RIL) does not become owner of natural gas, and there is nothing like specified physical quantities of natural gas to be shared by GoI and Contractor - Parties directed to renegotiate as to suitable arrangements for supply of gas de-hors MoU - Such renegotiations shall be within framework of governmental policy and approvals regarding price, quantity and tenure for supply of gas - Judgments of Single Judge and DB of Bombay HC set aside - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Constitution - Corporate - Petroleum & Gas - New Exploration and Licensing Policy, 1999 - Whether Government of India (GoI) have right to frame a Utilisation Policy under PSC? - RNRL submitted that GoI should not have any right to interfere in this contractual commitment - Held, art. 21.1 of PSC makes it very clear that sales of Natural Gas have to be in accordance with a Government Utilisation Policy and to Indian Domestic Market - Art. 21.1 clearly contemplates that pool of eligible buyers of natural gas extends to whole of Indian domestic market - Under provisions of art. 21.1 in PSC, Board Room of RIL or its internal divisions do not constitute Indian domestic market - Under PSC, unless Government actually sets out a policy regarding utilization of natural gas produced, it cannot be committed or sold to anyone - Freedom to market can only be exercised subject to utilization policy of GoI - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Corporate - Petroleum & Gas - Whether under PSC, does title get transferred to Contractor (RIL) on account of it expending monies on exploration, development and production? - Held, title pursuant to art. 27.1 of PSC can pass from sovereign owner, people of India, at Delivery Point upon a sale, and not as a matter of offset against any incurred expenditure by RIL - Transfer of title can be only to entities within a class of buyers specified by a utilization policy - In as much as title passes only upon sale at Delivery Point, true owner, people of India acting through Union of India have a sovereign right, that is tempered by public law, in determining manner in which that sale is effectuated - Appeals disposed of.

(D) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 297 - Purport and meaning of art. 297 of Constitution - Held, u/art. 297 of Constitution, Union of India can indeed enter into contracts for identification, development and extraction of resources in geographic zones specified therein - However, such activities can only be premised on key therein to unlock those resources, for purposes of Union - In present case, natural gas, under dispute in these proceedings, is being mined from deep beneath sea bed, off eastern shore of India - Thus, it is a resource that falls squarely within purview of art. 297 of Constitution of India and is explicitly noted so in PSC - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Corporate - Petroleum & Gas - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 297 - Interpretation of PSC (Production Sharing Contract) - Whether PSC only means a sharing of physical quantity of natural gas as contended by RNRL? - Held, word "value" is being used, in PSC, to mean monetized value of physical quantity that is a resultant of multiplying quantity of Petroleum (crude oil or natural gas) produced, saved and sold in market (as discussed below) at a "price" - Change in wording in PSC clearly implies that under PSC by making "value" of natural gas produced, saved and sold as what is to be shared, intention of Government was to ensure that "volume" i.e., physical quantities remain outside purview of what is to be shared between Contractor and Government - Consequently, under this PSC, RIL has no rights whatsoever to take physical quantities/volume of natural gas as a part of Profit Petroleum or Cost Petroleum, in as much as contractor's right to take anything under PSC can only be from total value i.e., total revenue received from sale of natural gas - Appeals disposed of.

(F) Constitution - Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 392 and 391 - Maintainability - It was contended that powers of Court, u/s. 392 of Companies Act, are of widest amplitude, and courts must exercise such powers to fully implement Scheme to effectuate scheme one way or other - Held, however wide powers of courts may be, they cannot be so wide as to order supply of gas in contravention of government policies, constitutional obligations that Government of India must bear in mind when formulating such policies and in contravention of broader public interest - DB erred by holding that certain quantum of natural gas stood allocated to RNRL - Error is on account of both a misinterpretation of PSC and also public law - Appeals disposed of.

Hon'ble Justice P. Sathasivam

(G) Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 391 and 392 - Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) - Binding - Whether MoU entered into amongst family members of Promoter was binding upon corporate entity - RIL? - Held, MoU was signed as a private family arrangement or understanding between two brothers, Mukesh and Anil Ambani, and their mother - Contents of MoU were not made public, and even in present proceedings, they were revealed in parts - Clearly, MoU does not fall under corporate domain, it was neither approved by shareholders, nor was it attached to scheme - Therefore, technically, MoU is not legally binding - Nevertheless, cognizance can be taken of fact that MoU formed backdrop of Scheme, and therefore, contents of Scheme have to be interpreted in light of MoU - Appeals disposed of.

(H) Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 391 and 392 - Considerations to determine "suitable arrangement" under Clause 19 of Scheme - Whether challenge raised by RNRL to GSMA, that it is not a "suitable arrangement" was maintainable particularly in view of fact that on merits, Company Judge had found, these objections to be unsustainable? - Held, "suitable arrangement" under clause 19 of Scheme must not be merely suitable for RIL - It has a broader meaning - Such an arrangement must be suitable for interests of shareholders of RNRL as reflected by MoU, and RIL; obligation of RIL under PSC; national policy on gas including decisions of EGOM and Gas Utilization Policy; and broader national and public interest - Appeals disposed of.

(I) Corporate - Interpretation of PSC (Production Sharing Contract) - Held, objective of PSC inter alia is to regulate supply and distribution of gas - Keeping this objective in mind, art. 21 of PSC must be interpreted to give power to Government to determine both valuation and price of gas - It is not feasible to restrict power of Government in such matters of national importance, especially when governing contract, PSC, also provides for it - Appeals disposed of.

(J) Constitution - Petroleum & Gas - Pricing of gas - Role of Government - Whether it is open to RNRL to contend that Government approval for supply of gas is not required and further that provision requiring Government approvals should be deleted from GSMA/GSPA? - Held, in a constitutional democracy national assets belong to people - Government holds such natural resources in trust - Legally, therefore, Government owns such assets for purposes of developing them in interests of people - In present case, Government owns gas till it reaches its ultimate consumer - It is not permissible for RIL to enter into a contract with RNRL to supply fixed quantity of gas as gas continues to be property of government till time it reaches delivery point and thus, RIL has no right to dispose of same without express approval of Union of India - Further, in terms of Gas Utilization Policy and Production Sharing Contract, Government in capacity as an Executive of Union can regulate and distribute manner of sale of Natural Gas through allotments and allocation which would sub-serve best interest of country - Broader constitutional principles, statutory scheme as well as proper interpretation of PSC mandates Government to determine price of gas before it is supplied by contractor - Appeal disposed of.

(K) Constitution - Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 392 and 391 - Maintainability - Whether Company Petition filed by RNRL u/s. 392 of Companies Act, was maintainable? - It was argued that jurisdiction of Court can only be found u/s. 394 of Act and s. 392 is completely inapplicable - Held, s. 392 creates a duty to supervise carrying out of compromise or arrangement - This power and duty was created to enable Court to take steps from time to time to remove all obstacles in way of enforcement of a sanctioned scheme - While sanctioning, it shall anticipate some hitches and difficulties which it can remove by order of sanction itself but clause 1(b) makes it clear that this power can also be exercised after scheme has once been sanctioned - So long as basic nature of arrangement remains same power of modification is unlimited, only limit being that modification should be necessary for working arrangement - S. 392 is applicable to Company Application filed by RNR - Court does have jurisdiction over present matter - However, it is made clear that power of Court does not extend to re-writing Scheme in any manner - Appeal disposed of.

(L) Corporate - Relief - Held, though Contractor (RIL) has marketing freedom to sell product from contract area to other consumers, this freedom is not absolute - Price at which produce will be sold to consumer would be subject to government's approval - Tenure of such contracts can't be such that it vitiates development plan as approved by government - Therefore, GSMA and GSPA entered into with RNRL should fix price, quantity and tenure in accordance with PSC - EGOM has already set price of gas for purpose of PSC - Parties must abide by this, and other conditions placed by Government policy - RIL is directed to initiate renegotiation with RNRL within six weeks terms of GSMA so that their interests are safeguarded and finalize same within eight weeks thereafter and resultant decision be placed before Company Court for necessary orders - Appeals disposed of.
