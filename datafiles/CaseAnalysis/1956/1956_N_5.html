New Prakash Transport Company Limited v New Suwarna Transport Company Limited

Supreme Court of India

30 September 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 69; AIR 1957 SC 232; 1957 (1) MLJ 157; 1957 SCJ 236; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 98

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation; Constitution

Keywords: Departmental Action, Rule Of Natural Justice

Summary: Constitution - Carriers & Transportation - Motor Vehicles Act,1939, ss.64 and 47 - Motor Vehicles Rules,1940, r.73 - Constitution of India, 1950, art.226 - Code of Civil Procedure,1908, o.17 r.1 - Permits for running buses on Buldana-Malkapur route-1 - Right of accused to be heard - Correctness of police reports - (A) Whether Motor Vehicles Act and rules framed thereunder with particular reference to Regional Transport Authority and Appellate Authority contemplate anything like a regular hearing in a Court of justice? - S.64 of Motor Vehicles Act while creating right of appeal, does not in terms create any right in a respondent to be heard - Neither sections nor rules framed under Act contemplate anything like recording oral or documentary evidence in usual way as in courts of law - Held, Motor Vehicles Act and rules framed thereunder with particular reference to Regional Transport Authority and Appellate Authority, do not contemplate anything like a regular hearing in a court of justice - (B) Whether in present case there was failure of natural justice u/art. 226 of Constitution? - Applications was filed by S and P for permit for certain route - P's application rejected on ground of adverse police report on appeal of P second report was prepared by police, this report had only withdrawn some of adverse comments against conduct of P which had been found to have been made under a misunderstanding - Neither party raised any objection to use of that document nor asked for an adjournment on ground either that it had been taken by surprise or that it had materials to offer in opposition to report - Second police report did not directly contain any allegations against S, hence, there was nothing in that report which S could be called upon to meet - Held, fact that appellate Authority had read out contents of police report was enough compliance with rules of natural justice and allowance of appeal was right - Hence, there was no failure of natural justice u/art. 226 of Constitution (C) Whether u/ss. 47 and 64 of Motor Vehicles Act 1939, police report in any way assist Regional Transport Authority or Appellate Authority? - Police report submitted to Regional Transport Authority or to Appellate Authority, if it, requires police authority to do so, is not intended to be anything more than an expression of opinion by an authority interested in maintenance of law and order - Such a report is mean more for use of authority in making or refusing a grant than for use of several applicants or any one of them - Held, report is in nature of information supplied by police in order to assist authority in making up its mind - (D) Whether appellate authority is to grant an adjournment suo motu without application from parties? - Appellate Authority under Motor Vehicles Act does not administer justice as a court of law, though while deciding as between rival claims of applicants for a permit it has to deal with them in a fair and just manner - Held, tribunal even acting 'judicially' is not obliged to grant an adjournment suo motu without any application on behalf of any of parties interested - (E) Whether under CPC, 1908, o.41, r.1 a new plea by parties can be entertained at appellate stage? - Held, in a Court of law a party is not entitled to raise question at appellate stage that he should have been granted an adjournment which he did not pray for in Court of first instance - Far less, such a claim can be entertained in an appeal from a tribunal which is not a Court of justice, but a statutory body functioning in a quasi-judicial way - Appeal allowed.
