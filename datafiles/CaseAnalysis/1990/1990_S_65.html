British India Steam Navigation Co., Ltd. v Shanmughavilas Cashew Industries And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

13 March 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

K.N. Saikia, P.B. Sawant

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 991; (1990) 3 SCC 481; 1990 (2) CCC 159; 1990 (2) CompLJ 1; 1990 (48) E.L.T. 481; JT 1990 (1) SC 528; 1990(1) SCALE 462; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 884; 1990 (2) UJ 47

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Excise; Indirect Tax; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Constitution Of India, 1950 - Indian Bills of Lading Act, 1856 - Indian Contract Act, 1872 - Bills of Lading Act, 1855 - Carriage of Goods by Sea Act, 1932 - First respondent purchased from East Africa 350 tons of raw cashew nuts which were shipped in vessel SS Steliosm chartered by appellant - Out of 4445 bags containing nuts carried in said vessel only 3712 bags were delivered at Cochin, there being thus short-landing of 733 bags - It is submitted, as carrier under cl. 3 of bill of lading, only appellant has an option either to sue or be sued in England, or in Cochin, which is a port of destination but shipper had no option to sue at Cochin - (A) Whether shipper could or could not have right to sue at Cochin under bills of lading ? - Held, in Memo of Appeal before lower Appellate Court no specific ground as to jurisdiction was taken though there were grounds on non-maintainability of suit - Even in Special Leave Petition before this Court no ground of lack of jurisdiction of courts below has been taken - Appellant has to be held to have either waived objection as to jurisdiction or to have submitted to jurisdiction in facts and circumstances of case - (B) Whether appellant would be liable for suit claim ? - Held, on a review of bill of lading, it cannot therefore be said that shipper, whose knowledge will be attributed to first respondent, did not know of conditions of carriage printed on reverse, there being no other conditions printed elsewhere in bills of lading - None of parties having repudiated bills of lading in this case, High Court ought not to have accepted submission of first respondent that cl. 4 of bills of lading offended provisions of Carriage Of Goods By Sea Act, 1924 and therefore bad - As in instant case goods were shipped in Africa and carried to Cochin, this Act obviously was not applicable - Dispute could not have been decided partly according to municipal law and partly according to English law - Appeal allowed.
