FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

T. Ravi and another v B. Chinna Narasimha and others

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Arun Mishra, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 199; (2017) 7 SCC 342; 2017 (123) ALR 305; 2017 (2) CLT(SC) 185; JT 2017 (3) SC 317; 2017(3) SCALE 740

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Immovable Property, Res Judicata, Adverse Possession, Lis Pendens, Mohammedan Law, Jurisdiction, Transfer Of Property Act, 1882, Limitation, Review, Second Appeal, Receiver, Joint Family, Restoration, Hindu Law, Joint Tenancy, Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976, Mortgagee, Mortgagor, Alienate, Andhra Pradesh (Telangana Area) Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act, 1950, Effects, Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982, Enforcing, Lis, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Administrative, Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act, 1996, Hyderabad Record of Rights in Land Regulations, 1948

Summary: (A) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982 - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976 - Suit for partition - Dismissal - Revival - Plaintiffs filed suit for partition of properties comprised in Schedules 'A', 'B' and 'C' which was dismissed - Plaintiff moved an application for revival of suit - Trial Court by an order dt. 1-12-1955 directed plaintiff to deposit Rs.50 towards costs and if costs were not paid suit should stand dismissed - Meanwhile, defendant no.1 sold Schedule 'B' property in area 68 acres 10 guntas on to purchaser vide registered sale deed dt. 23-11-1959 and sold share inherited by other co-heirs also to purchaser - Legal representatives of purchaser initiated land grabbing proceedings under the 1982 Act which was dismissed by Special Court - On appeal, HC held that final decree passed by Trial Court with respect to plaint 'B' schedule property was impracticable - Hence, instant appeals - Whether the decision in suit operates as res judicata, if yes to what extent.

Held, in view of the categorical findings recorded by Trial Court and Appellate Court it was apparent that sale deed was hit by doctrine of lis pendens and on the basis of the said sale deed, L.Rs. of purchaser could have claimed only to the extent of the share of his vendor and not the entire land, i.e. only to the extent of 14/104th share of defendant no. 1. Further, there was clear inability to grant injunction and contention of plaintiffs that they were having title on entire land on the basis of sale deed was negatived. It was found that purchaser could have purchased only the share of his vendor and not the entire disputed property and purchase was affected by lis pendens. Therefore, the finding with respect to purchase being made during lis pendens had attained finality and was not open to question in instant proceedings. Besides, the validity of the sale deed to the extent of the share of vendor which was sought to be re-agitated in final decree proceedings, was also not open to be raised in view of clear findings recorded in suit. However nothing turns on the aforesaid finding as to res judicata as SC propose to examine both aspects on merits afresh, in view of the conclusions proposed to record. Appeals allowed.

(B) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982 - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976 - Sale deed - Executability - Whether sale deed dt. 23-11-1959 executed by defendant no.1 in favour of purchaser was hit by doctrine of lis pendens.

Held, when suit itself was not been dismissed vide order dt. 8-1-1955, the events subsequent thereto i.e. Trial Court vide order dt. 1-12-1955 treated it as having been dismissed or that plaintiff also was left under a wrong impression that suit was dismissed in default and cost was imposed on 1-12-1955 and it was not paid up to 15-12-1955, would make no difference. Due to non-payment of costs, by order dt. 1-12-1955 suit stood dismissed cannot be accepted, as the order was non est in the eye of law. It was an illegal order of treating a pending suit as having been dismissed. Subsequent order or imposition of costs for its restoration was non est and illegal and was rightly set aside by HC. When suit had not been dismissed at all in the eye of law, it is to be treated as pending only. No legal fiction could be created in favour of respondents that suit itself was dismissed due to non-payment of costs for restoration. HC has also held that the order dt. 1-12-1955 was without jurisdiction. Thus, sale deed in question dt. 23-11-1959 was executed during lis pendens and HC has erred in law in holding otherwise in impugned judgment. Appeals allowed.

(C) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, ss. 2, 52 - Extent of share - Void - Whether s. 52 of the Act renders a transfer pendente lite void.

Held, sale deed was not void but only valid to the extent of the share of vendor of purchaser i.e. it was valid to the extent of 14/104th share which was found in the preliminary decree and affirmed in the final decree. The sale deed was subject to the outcome of suit which was to the aforesaid effect. Appeals allowed.

(D) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Decree for partition - Binding - What is the effect of preliminary decree for partition and the extent to which it is binding.

Held, in instant case determination of shares as per preliminary decree has attained finality, shares of the parties was crystallised in each and every property. Purchaser pendente lite was bound by the preliminary decree with respect to the shares so determined and it cannot be re-opened and whatever equity could have been claimed in the final decree proceedings to the extent of vendor's share has already been extended to purchasers. It was apparent that the matters which are concluded by preliminary decree cannot be re-agitated in an appeal against the final decree. No appeal was preferred by purchasers or by defendant no.1 as against preliminary decree. Appeals allowed.

(E) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, ss. 2, 52 - Sale deed - Cancellation - Validity - Whether it was necessary to file a suit for cancellation of sale deed dt. 23-11-1959.

Held, when sale deed was executed during the pendency of suit purchaser pendente lite was bound by the outcome of suit. The provisions of s. 52 of the Act prevent multiplicity of proceedings. It was not at all necessary to file a suit for cancellation of sale deed as vendor had no authority to sell land of other co-sharers. As such the right, title and interest of purchaser were subject to the pending suit for partition in which a preliminary decree was passed in the year 1970 which had attained finality in which vendor of purchaser, defendant no.1 was found to be having share only to the extent of 14/104th. The preliminary decree was not based upon fraud or collusion. The sale deed was not under the authority of Court and pendency of suit u/s. 52 of the Act commenced from the date of presentation of plaint and continued until suit or proceedings were disposed of by a final decree and on a complete satisfaction of discharge of such decree, an order was obtained. The lis pendens operates during execution. Purchaser cannot circumvent the jurisdiction of Court and wriggle out of decree. The transfer remained valid subject to the result of suit and pendente lite purchaser was subject to the legal rights and obligations of his vendor as decided by Court. Appeals allowed.

(F) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, ss. 2, 52 - Adverse possession - Perverse - Whether purchaser, his heirs and purchasers had perfected their right, title and interest by virtue of adverse possession.

Held, there was no question of perfecting the title by adverse possession during pendency of suit. S. 52 of the Act negates the very plea of adverse possession. Trial Court and Appellate Court have rightly held that there was no question of adverse possession. HC has simply without any discussion held that the title was perfected by adverse possession. Merely a bald statement that there was adverse possession was not enough to set up a plea of adverse possession. It has to be clearly set out from which date it commenced, and became hostile when there was repudiation of the title and no such plea was raised. There were 3 classic requirements of plea of adverse possession i.e. "nec vi, nec clam, nec precario" i.e., peaceful, open and continuous. No such pleading was raised much less there was question of any proof and such plea was not available to be raised in view of doctrine of lis pendens. Possession never became adverse in instant case as the property was purchased subject to the outcome of litigation. HC has erred in law in holding that plaintiffs perfected their title by virtue of adverse possession. The finding was perverse and has no foundational basis. Appeals allowed.

(G) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Disputed property - Alienation - Challenged - Whether under the Muslim law, defendant no.1 being a co-sharer could have alienated the share of other co-sharers in disputed property.

Held, sale beyond 14/104th share by defendant no. 1 to purchaser was void. The Mohammedan Law does not recognize the right of one of shareholders being tenants-in-common for acting on behalf of others. While discharging debt also they act as independent debtors. A co-sharer cannot create charge on property of co-heir. The right of Muslim heir was immediately defined in each fraction of estate. Notion of joint family property was unknown to Muslim law. Co-heir does not act as agent while discharging debt but was an independent debtor not as co-debtor or joint debtor. Co-sharers were not defined as joint contractors, partners, executors or mortgagees. Appeals allowed.

(F) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Equity for allotment - Applicability - Whether purchaser has a right to claim equity for allotment of Schedule 'B' property in final decree proceedings in suit for partition.

Held, in instant case, equitable right of allotment of some land other than which was purchased out of some other properties allotted to the share of vendor, defendant no. 1 has not been claimed in the objections filed during the final decree proceedings filed by purchasers. The property admittedly has exchanged hands a number of times during the pendency of suit from 1935 till date and how the equity was to be worked out is always a question of fact in every case, how much share was allotted to the share of one vendor and how much property he had already alienated till that time and what are the debts or charges on property that were legally permissible, would be some of the relevant considerations. Though in the absence of claiming equitable right in the share of vendor's other properties, it cannot be worked out and it was doubtful when undivided share has not been sold and specific property was purchased, such equitable right could be enforced. Even assuming that purchaser could work out the equity, however in the absence of pleadings and evidence required for the purpose, it would amount to misadventure. SC considered the provisions of Mohammedan Law that sale beyond the extent of the share of vendor in specific property was void. Appeals allowed.

(G) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Property sold - Legal necessity - Validity - Whether sale was for legal necessity and binding.

Held, it was contended that payment of land revenue has enured for the benefit of entire estate and sale would be valid and binding on co-heirs. Except making the aforesaid bald statement, nothing was placed on record to indicate that sale was for payment of land revenue. Even otherwise under Mohammedan Law, it was not open to defendant no.1 to act in fiduciary capacity to sell the property and bind shares of others. It was not mentioned in sale deed that defendant no. 1 had sold for any legal necessity or for the benefit of entire estate. The recital in sale deed has evidentiary value and purchaser and his successors were bound by what was mentioned therein. Thus, no case was made out to make interference. Appeals allowed.

(H) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Sale conferred - Right on purchaser - Validity - What was the effect of decision of SC and HC with respect to final decree proceedings in Schedule 'B' property.

Held, perusal of judgment of HC indicates that in Mohammedan Law there was no recognition for a sale by a co-sharer of the entire estate and that the other co-sharers were not bound by such sale and said decision even went to the extent of saying that even when the sale was meant for discharging the debts of ancestor, whose property had devolved upon the sharers, the said sale without the consent of other co-sharers was invalid and does not confer any right on purchaser with regard to such co-sharers who do not join the said sale. Appeals allowed.

(I) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Tenancy Act, 1950 - Lis pendens - Waiver - Whether there was waiver of right by appellants.

Held, during the pendency of suit with respect to ancestral property defendant no. 1 had alienated the property treating it as his own whereas it was obviously subject to the right of other co-shares finally declared in preliminary decree. Thus, the stand which was taken by appellants under the Act was not at all inconsistent and did not amount to approbation and reprobation on the part of the heirs. The conduct of purchasers makes it clear that they instituted multifarious proceedings, and took inconsistent stands which were not accepted by SC. However, appellants or their predecessors had not taken inconsistent stands and were clearly protected by doctrine of lis pendens. Appeals allowed.

(J) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Final decree proceedings - Delay - Legality - Whether appellants were guilty of delay or laches.

Held, The preliminary decree passed in 1970 was clearly against the interest of purchasers as their vendor was not found to have the rights which were not assailed by them. The preliminary decree attained finality in the year 1976 and proceedings for final decree taken in 1984 were within the period of limitation. There was no delay on the part of appellants defeating their rights. It was respondents who having lost in proceedings one after the other, raised objection in the year 2004 in final decree proceedings. What prevented them from doing so in the year 1993, has not at all been explained. Thus, it was respondents who were responsible to delay in the final decree proceedings in a partition case instituted in the year 1935 and the matter was still pending in the shape of instant appeals before SC. Appeals allowed.

(K) Family & Personal - Land & Property - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976 - Right in property - Effect of - What was the effect of the decision of Court under the Act.

Held, orders passed in urban land ceiling case have not been placed on record. That apart, it was stated that proceedings lapsed due to repeal of the Act. Respondents were purchasers from branch of purchaser whose vendor was defendant no.1 and property has further exchanged hands. Since orders have not been placed on record, in the aforesaid factual scenario, SC decline to examine the aforesaid proposition further and not apprised how purchasers could claim a better right than the one possessed by their vendor. SC leave it open to State Govt. to examine the question of ceiling and effect of decision. Impugned judgment and decree passed by HC was set aside. Appeals allowed.
