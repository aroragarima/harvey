Daya Ram and Others v Shyam Sundari

Supreme Court of India

08 September 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 165; AIR 1965 SC 1049; 1965 (2) ALT(SC) 147; 1965 (1) ILR(All) 827; 1966 (1) SCJ 6; [1965] 1 S.C.R. 231

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Father of Shyam Sundari was owner along with his two brothers - Three brothers were separated in interest and were each entitled to a third share - Babu Har Charan Lal died, his widow Tulsa Kunwar died later leaving behind her only daughter, respondent - Even before widow's death, two brothers of her husband claiming a full interest in those plots, sold them to Lala Mata Din, father of appellants - Before this suit was filed, an agreement was entered into between Mata Din and Kanpur Improvement Trust as a result of which, Mata Din agreed to convey to Trust 895.35 sq yds of land free of cost, in lieu of betterment contribution and also agreed to construct on remaining part of premises, shops and houses in accordance with plans approved by Improvement Trust - (A) Whether in circumstances, agreement was one which was entered into bona fide and that he could claim an equity based on constructions erected in pursuance thereof? - Held, agreement with Trust could not justify Mata Din's action because Trust could not agree with a person who was not owner of property to construct buildings on another's property - It would have been open to Mata Din to have informed Trust immediately he got notice of claim of Shyam Sundari that only a 2/3 rd share in site belonged to him, but he did not do so but completed constructions ignoring claims of Shyam Sundari - They could not therefore, take advantage of their own acts and conduct and plead an equity based upon their wrongful acts - Learned Judges were justified in treating acts of Mata Din as those of a trespasser who, with notice of claim of true owner, had effected constructions on property - In those circumstances he could claim no special equity based upon his having bona fide put common property to use and effected improvements on it - (B) Whether when an appellant has impleaded heirs of deceased respondent so far as known to him within time allowed by law, but has omitted to bring on record some of heirs, this omission results in abatement of appeal? - Further held, almost universal consensus of opinion of all High Courts is that where a plaintiff or an appellant after diligent and bona fide enquiry ascertains who LRs. of a deceased defendant or respondent are and brings them on record within time limited by law, there is no abatement of suit or appeal, that impleaded LRs. sufficiently represent estate of deceased and decision obtained with them on record will bind not merely those impleaded but entire estate including those not brought on record - This correctly represents law - Appeal dismissed.
