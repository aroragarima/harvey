Dy. Chief Controller of Imports & Exports v Roshanlal Agarwal and others

Supreme Court of India

05 March 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

G.P. Mathur, S. Rajendra Babu

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 253; (2003) 4 SCC 139; (2003) SCC (Cr) 788; AIR 2003 SC 1900; 2003 (2) CCR 11; 2003 (2) Crimes 43; 2003 CRLJ 1698; 2003 (155) E.L.T. 216; 2003 (1) JCC 438; JT 2003 (2) SC 370; 2003 (3) KHCACJ 208; 2003 (2) RCR(Criminal) 110; 2003(2) SCALE 636; [2003] 2 S.C.R. 621; 2003 (2) SLT 640; 2003 (2) Supreme 483

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Import and Export (Control) Act. 1947

Summary: Customs - Criminal - Practice and Procedure - Imports, Exports (Control) Act, 1947, s.5 - IPC - ss. 420, 467, 468, 471, 120-B - Prosecution under - Cr P C - ss. 26 and 482 - Petitions under - HC allowed the petitions on the ground that the Special Court (Economic Offences) could only try offences under the Acts mentioned in the Schedule but had no jurisdiction to try offences under the IPC - Held, in view of specific provision contained in s.3(4)(a) of the Code, it is only a Judicial Magistrate who can try an offence under the Imports and Exports (Control) Act and u/s.26 of the Code such a Magistrate has also the jurisdiction to try those offences under the IPC, which the respondents are alleged to have committed - There is absolutely no scope for holding that the Special Court (Economic Offences), presided by a Judicial Magistrate of the First Class lost the jurisdiction to try offences under the IPC - Further held, the process issued to accused cannot be quashed merely on the ground that the Magistrate had not passed a speaking order - View taken by the HC is wholly unsustainable in law and must be set aside - Appeals Disposed Of.
