Colgate Palmolive India Limited v Commissioner of Customs, Patna

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 616; AIR 2016 SC 4045; 2016 (339) E.L.T. 161; JT 2016 (12) SC 145; [2016] 5 S.C.R. 1013

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Central Excise Act, 1944, Customs Act, 1962, Customs Tariff Act, 1975, Issued, Time, India, TRADE, INTERNATIONAL

Summary: Customs - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944 - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 12, 25, 8B, 9, 9A - Customs Tariff Act, 1975, ss. 2, 3, 3(1), 3(3), 3A - Notification - Amendment - Clarificatory - Govt. of India issued a Notification No. 37 of 1996 dt. 23-7-1996 whereby specified goods imported into India from Nepal were exempted "from the whole" of customs duty leviable under the 1975 Act - Appellant who was importing various dental hygiene products from Nepal was availing exemption under notification - Notification No. 18/2000-Customs was issued on 1-3-2000 prescribing the rates of special duty and appellant was asked to pay Special Additional Duty (SAD) and same was paid under protest - Thereafter, Notification No. 124/2000-Customs was issued on 29-09-2000 amending Notification No. 37/96-Customs - Aggrieved, appellant filed appeal, Member (Judicial) dismissed appeal holding that exemption notification could not be considered to be having retrospective effect and any exemption provision which enlarges the scope of earlier notification cannot be considered to be clarificatory - Member (Technical) expressed his dissent and opined that once a Treaty had been entered by Central Govt. issue of notification under the provisions of the 1962 Act is a ministerial act and notification dt. 29-09-2000 was a belated response to effectuate the terms of Treaty - Matter was placed before third Member who opined that there was no ambiguity in the earlier notification or in the subsequent Notification No.124/2000 and both the notifications operate in special fields he agreed with the Member (Judicial) and dismissed appeal - Hence, instant appeals - Whether amendment in such cases could be read retrospectively or not.

Held, it is not possible to accept the position that Cl. 1 of the protocol had included and had embraced the "special additional duty", which was introduced in the form of s. 3A of the 1975 Act enacted in 1998. The exemption which was granted by notification dt. 29-09-2000 was, therefore, in the nature of specific and new exemption from payment of special additional duty, which was otherwise payable in view of introduction of s. 3A to the 1975 Act. It is difficult to appreciate that exemption granted vide notification dt. 20-09-2000 to special additional duty was clarificatory or to give effect to the existing protocol. SC thinks so as protocol appended to the Treaty could not have conceived of future levy by way of proposition. Therefore, notification of 20-09-2000 conferred a new benefit which was not earlier stipulated or the subject matter of protocol. The instant case is not suggestive of any mistake or error or even inadvertence. The plea that there was delay in issue of notification, exempting special additional duty is not acceptable. It is because, what was earlier exempted under the protocol was basic customs duty and also additional customs duty equal to the duty of excise in some cases and on satisfying the conditions stipulated and it did not deal and relate to special additional duty chargeable u/s. 3A of the 1975 Act, which had introduced a new duty altogether. Therefore, SC repel the submission that exemption notification issued on 29-09-2000 is clarificatory. It was intended to be applied prospectively. It cannot be said the issue of notification was a formal ministerial act which got delayed for administrative reasons. It was a conscious act and a deliberate decision which came into existence after due deliberation when it was decided to grant exemption u/s. 3A of the 1975 Act. Appeals dismissed.
