Sita Ram v Radhabai and Others

Supreme Court of India

16 October 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

J.C. Shah, S.M. Sikri, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 386; AIR 1968 SC 534; [1968] 1 S.C.R. 805

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Debt Laws

Summary: Civil Procedure - Civil Procedure Code, 1908 - Father of appellant was brother of respondent - Respondent' entrusted gold, pearl and diamond jewellery to father of appellant - After his death, appellant was called upon by respondent to return that jewellery - Appellant replied that his father had during his life time returned jewellery to respondent - Respondent filed suit - Trial Court dismissed action upholding case of appellant - On appeal, High Court reversed decree passed by Trial Court and passed a decree directing that jewellery be restored to respondent - Hence, present appeal - (A) Illegal agreement - Enforcement of - Principle that courts will refuse to enforce an illegal agreement at instance of a person who is himself a party to an illegality or fraud is expressed in maxim in pari deucto portior est conditio defendentis - There are exceptional cases in which a man will be relieved of consequences of an illegal contract into which he has entered cases to which maxim does not apply (a) where illegal purpose has not yet been substantially carried into effect before it is sought to recover money paid or goods delivered in furtherance of it (b) where respondent is not in pari delicto with defendant (c) where respondent does not have to rely on illegality to make out his claim - (B) Whether parties of present case are Pari delicto? - Held, parties were not 'in pari delicto' - It is settled law that 'where parties are not in pari delicto, less guilty party may be able to recover money paid, or property transferred, under contract - Possibility may arise in three situations - First, contract may be of a kind made illegal by statute in interests 'of a particular class of persons of whom respondent is one - Secondly, respondent must have been induced to enter into contract by fraud or strong pressure - Thirdly, there is some authority for view that a person who is under a fiduciary duty to respondent will not be allowed to retain property, or to refuse to account for moneys received, on ground that property or moneys have come into his hands as proceeds of an illegal transaction - (C) Liability of appellant - Held, Hindu son governed by Mitakshara law is liable to pay debts of his father even if they are not incurred for purposes of legal necessity or for benefit to estate, provided debts are not avyavaharika or illegal - But there is no evidence that 'appellant is sought to be rendered liable for a debt which is avyavaharika or illegal - Granting that appellant was, after death of his father, unable to trace jewellery entrusted by respondent, it cannot be inferred that jewellery was misappropriated by his father - Burden of proving that there was a debt and that debt was avyavaharika or illegal lay upon appellant - No evidence to prove that debt was avyavaharika or illegal - Appeal dismissed.
