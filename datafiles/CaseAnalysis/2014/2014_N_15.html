Vijay Dhanuka Etc. v Najima Mamtaj Etc.

Supreme Court of India

27 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 247; (2014) 14 SCC 638; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 1924; 2015 (3) CalLT(SC) 65; 2014 CRLJ 2295; 2014 (3) JCC 1557; JT 2014 (14) SC 548; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1614; 2014 (2) OLR 68; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 793; 2014(4) SCALE 413B; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 171

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal intimidation, Voluntarily causing hurt

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 323, 380, 506 r/w s. 34 - Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 2005, s. 19 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 200, 202 - Criminal intimidation - Voluntarily causing hurt - Summons - Territorial Jurisdiction - Legality - Appellants accused were charged for offence punishable u/ss. 323, 380, 506 r/w s. 34 IPC - Magistrate took cognizance of offence and transferred the case to Court of another Magistrate for inquiry and disposal - Transferee Magistrate directed for issuance of summons against accused persons for offence u/ss. 323, 380 and 506 r/w s. 34 IPC - Appellants challenged order in four separate applications before HC - HC rejected all applications - Hence instant appelas - Whether in case, where accused resided at place beyond area in which Magistrate exercises his jurisdiction, inquiry was mandatory or not -

Held, in cases where accused was residing at place beyond area in which he exercises his jurisdiction was inserted by s. 19 of 2005 Act amendment, in opinion of legislature, was essential as false complaints were filed against persons residing at far off places in order to harass them - Use of expression 'shall' prima facie made inquiry or investigation, as case might be, by Magistrate mandatory - It was aimed to prevent innocent persons from harassment by unscrupulous persons from false complaints - Hence, use of expression 'shall' and background and purpose for which amendment had been brought, there was no doubt that inquiry or investigation, as case might be, was mandatory before summons were issued against accused living beyond territorial jurisdiction of Magistrate - It was evident from provision, every inquiry other than trial conducted by Magistrate or Court was inquiry - No specific mode or manner of inquiry was provided u/s. 202 CrPC- In inquiry envisaged u/s. 202 CrPC, witnesses were examined whereas u/s. 200 CrPC, examination of complainant only was necessary with option of examining witnesses present, if any, this exercise by Magistrate, for purpose of deciding whether or not there was sufficient ground for proceeding against accused, was nothing but inquiry envisaged under s. 202 CrPC - Hence, Magistrate examined complainant on solemn affirmation and two witnesses and only thereafter he directed for issuance of process - There was no error in impugned order and there was no merit in instant appeals - Udai Shankar Awasthi v State of Uttar Pradesh and another 2013 Indlaw SC 13, followed on - Appeals dismissed.
