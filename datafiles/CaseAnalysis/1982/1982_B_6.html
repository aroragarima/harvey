Â Bachan Singh and Others v State of Punjab and Others

Supreme Court of India

16 August 1982

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, P.N. Bhagwati, Y.V. Chandrachud, A.C. Gupta, N.L. Untwalia

Where Reported

1982 Indlaw SC 195; (1982) 3 SCC 24; (1982) SCC (Cr) 535; AIR 1982 SC 1325; 1982 CRLJ 193; 1982 CRLR 337; 1982(1) SCALE 713; [1983] 1 S.C.R. 145

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; International; Media & Entertainment

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Sovereignty And Integrity Of India, Freedom Of Movement, White Collar Crime, European Convention On Human Rights, Separation Of Powers

Summary: Chandrachud, CJ, Sarkaria, A. C. Gupta, N. L. Untwalia, JJ. (Majority view)

(A) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 354 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Death penalty - Constitutionality - Appellant/Accused was tried and sentenced to death u/s. 302 IPC for multiple murders - HC confirmed the death sentence - Appellant filed appeal before the SC - Appellant contended that in view of the ratio of Rajendra Prasad v. State of U.P., 1979 INDLAW SC 277, the courts below were not competent to impose the extreme penalty of death on appellant, especially, neither the circumstances that the appellant was previously convicted for murder and committed present murders after he had served out the life sentence in an earlier case, nor the fact that present three murders were extremely heinous and inhuman, constitutes a 'special reason' for imposing death sentence within the meaning of s. 354(3) CrPC - Hence the Two Judges Bench referred the matter to the Constitutional Bench - Whether the death penalty provided for the offence of murder in s. 302 IPC is unconstitutional - Held, six fundamental freedoms guaranteed u/art. 19(1) are not absolute rights, which are subject to the power of the State to impose reasonable restrictions, which may even extend to prohibition, on the exercise of those rights - No rights mentioned in art. 19(1) of the Constitution confers the freedom to commit murder or freedom to commit any offence, therefore, laws which define offences and prescribe punishment for the commission of offences do not attract the application of art. 19 of the Constitution - If the direct and inevitable effect of the penal law is such as to abridge or abrogate any of those rights mentioned in art. 19 of the Constitution, art. 19(1) should have to be attracted, however, deprivation of freedom consequent upon an order of conviction and sentence is not a direct and inevitable consequence of the penal law but is merely incidental to the order of conviction and sentence, which may or may not be passed the test of art. 19(1) of the Constitution - Thus, s. 302 IPC does not have to stand the test of art. 19(1) of the Constitution - 35th Law Commission suggested the retention of death sentence and the Parliament, while replacing the 1898 CrPC with 1973 CrPC, considered the reasonableness of the provision of death penalty - Therefore, impugned provision s. 302 violates neither the letter nor the ethos of art. 19 - Art. 21 recognizes the right of the State to deprive a person of his life or personal liberty in accordance with fair, just and reasonable procedure established by valid law - Further, it cannot be said that death penalty for the offence of murder violates the basic structure of the Constitution - IPC prescribes death penalty as an alternative punishment only for heinous crimes which are not more than seven in number - S. 354(3) CrPC in keeping with the spirit of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, has further restricted the area of death penalty - India's penal laws, including the impugned provision s. 302 and their application are, thus entirely in accordance with its international commitment and constitutional validity - Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 353 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Whether the sentencing procedure provided in s. 354(3) of the CrPC is unconstitutional on the ground that it invests the Court with unguided and untrammelled discretion and allows death sentence to be arbitrarily or freakishly imposed on a person found guilty of murder or any other capital offence punishable under the IPC with death or, in the alternative, with imprisonment for life - Held, procedure provided in CrPC for imposing capital punishment for murder and some other capital crimes under the IPC cannot be said to be unfair, unreasonable or unjust for the reason that sentencing discretions are vested with the Courts, amounts to delegation of its power of legislation by Parliament - Impugned provision s. 354 (3), hence does not violate arts. 14, 19 and 21 of the Constitution - Further, s. 235(2) of the CrPC makes not only explicit, what according to the decision of Jagmohan Singh v. State of Uttar Pradesh, 1972 INDLAW SC 736, was implicit in the scheme of the CrPC, but also bifurcates the trial by providing two hearings, one at the pre-conviction stage and another at the pre-sentence stage - S. 354(3) CrPC marks a significant shift in the legislative policy underlying the 1898 CrPC, as in force immediately before 1-4-1974, according to which both the alternative sentences of death or imprisonment for life provided for murder and for certain other capital offences under the IPC, were normal offences - However, according to changed legislative policy (1973 CrPC) which is patent on the face of s. 354(3), imprisonment for life and death penalty is an exception - Although s. 235(2) CrPC does not contain a specific provision as to evidence and provides only for hearing of the accused as to sentence, yet it is implicit in the said provision that if a request is made in that behalf by either the prosecution or the accused, or by both, the Judge should give the party or parties concerned an opportunity of producing evidence or materials relating to the various factors bearing on the question of sentence - Expression 'special reasons' in the context of s. 354(3) CrPC means 'exceptional reasons' founded on the exceptionally grave circumstances of the particular case relating to crime as well as criminal - Thus, the legislative policy now writ large and clear on the face of s. 354 (3) is that on conviction of murder and other capital offences punishable in the alternative with death under IPC, the extreme penalty should be imposed only in extreme cases - Further, s. 235 (2) CrPC provides for a bifurcated trial and specifically gives the accused personal right of pre-sentence hearing, at which stage, he can bring on record material or evidence, which may be strictly relevant to or connected with the particular crime under inquiry, but nevertheless have, consistently with the policy underlined in s. 354(3), a bearing on the choice of sentence - Court should not confine its consideration 'principally' or 'merely' to the circumstances connected with the particular crime, but also give due consideration to the circumstances of the criminal - Therefore, in order to qualify for inclusion in the category of 'aggravating circumstances' which may form the basis of 'special reasons' in s. 354(3), circumstances found on the facts of a particular case, must evidence aggravation of an abnormal or special degree - What is the relative weight to be given to the aggravating and mitigating factors, depends on the facts and circumstances of the particular case - In many cases, the extremely cruel or beastly manner of the commission of murder is itself a demonstrated index of the depraved character of the perpetrator - That is why, it is not desirable to consider the circumstances of the crime and the circumstances of the criminal in two separate water-tight compartments - Hence, it is only when the culpability assumes the proportion of extreme depravity that 'special reasons' can legitimately be said to exist - Therefore, Courts aided by the broad illustrative guidelines indicated by the SC, will discharge the onerous function with evermore scrupulous care and humane concern, directed along the highroad of legislative policy outline in s. 354(3), i.e., for persons convicted of murder, life imprisonment is the rule and death sentence is an exception - Hence, sentencing procedure provided in s. 354(3) of the CrPC is constitutionally valid - Order accordingly.

Bhagwati, J. (Minority view)

(C) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 19, 72 - Death penalty - Constitutionality - Whether death penalty provided for the offence of murder in s. 302 IPC is unconstitutional - Held, Court may refuse to entertain an issue like the constitutional validity of death penalty because it is satisfied that the previous decision is correct but it cannot decline to consider it on the ground that it is barred by the rule of adherence to precedence - Two supervening circumstances which justify the reconsideration of the decision in Jagmohan's case are; enactment of new CrPC, 1973, which by s. 354(3) has made life sentence the rule, in case of offences punishable with death or in the alternative imprisonment for life and provided for imposition of sentence death only in exceptional cases for special reasons and new dimension of arts. 14 and 21 unfolded by the SC in Maneka Gandhi v Union of India, 1978 INDLAW SC 212 - Constitutional validity of the death penalty provided as an alternative punishment in s. 302 IPC r/w s. 354(4) CrPC cannot be sustained - Death penalty does not serve any social purpose or advance any constitutional value and is totally arbitrary and unreasonable so as to violative of arts. 14, 19 and 21 of the Constitution - Constitution does not in so many terms prohibit capital punishment, in fact, it recognizes death sentence as one of the penalties which may be imposed by law - Apart from art. 21, art. 72(C) also recognizes the possibility of a sentence of death being imposed on a person convicted of an offence in as much as it provides that the President shall have the power to suspend, remit or commute the sentence of any person who is convicted of an offence and sentence to death - Therefore, the imposition of death sentence for conviction of an offence is not in all cases forbidden by the Constitution but that does not mean that the infliction of death penalty is blessed by the Constitution or that it has the imprimatur or seal of approval of the Constitution - Constitution is not a transient document but it is meant to endure for a long time to come and during its life, situation may arise where death penalty may be found to serve a social purpose and its prescription may not be liable to be regarded as arbitrary or unreasonable and therefore to meet such situation, the Constitution had to make a provision and this it did in art. 21 and art. 72(C), so that even where death penalty is prescribed by any law and it is otherwise not unconstitutional, it must still comply with the requirement of art. 21 and it would be subject to the clemency power of the President u/art. 72(c) of the Constitution - Legislature has not indicated what are the special reasons for which departure can be made from the normal rule and death penalty may be inflicted - Legislature has not given any guidance as to what are those exceptional cases in which, deviating from the normal rule, death sentence may be imposed, which left entirely to the unguided discretion of the court, a feature, which has lethal consequences so far as the constitutionality of death penalty is concerned - Death sentence is irrevocable and it cannot be recalled - Where a person has been wrongly convicted and sentenced to death and put out of existence in pursuance of the sentence of death and if any mistake is subsequently discovered, he cannot be brought back to life - Execution of the sentence of death in such a case makes miscarriage of justice irrevocable - Howsoever careful may be the procedural safeguards erected by the law before the death penalty can be imposed, it is impossible to eliminate the chance of judicial error - Judicial error in imposition of death penalty would indeed be a crime beyond punishment - Death penalty cannot be regarded as proportionate to the offence of murder, merely because the murder is brutal, heinous or shocking - Nature and magnitude of the offence or the motive and purposes underlying it or the manner and extent of its commission cannot have any relevance to the proportionality of death penalty to the offence - No evidence suggested that the death penalty has any special deterrent effect than life imprisonment - Therefore, death penalty has no rational penological purpose and it is arbitrary and irrational and hence violative of arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(D) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 353 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Whether the sentencing procedure provided in s. 354(3) of the CrPC is unconstitutional on the ground that it invests the Court with unguided and untrammeled discretion and allows death sentence to be arbitrarily or freakishly imposed on a person found guilty of murder or any other capital offence punishable under the IPC with death or, in the alternative, with imprisonment for life - Held, s. 354(3) CrPC, 1973 makes the exercise of discretion more difficult and uncertain and it is left to the Judge to grope in the dark for himself and in the exercise of his unguided and unfettered discretion decide what reasons may be considered as 'special reasons' justifying award of death penalty and whether in a given case any such special reasons exist which should persuade the Court to depart from the normal rule and inflict death penalty on the accused - No legislative policy or principle to guide the Court in exercising its discretion in the delicate and sensitive area of life and death, the exercise of discretion of the Court is bound to vary from judge to judge - What may appear as special reasons to one judge may not so appear to another and the decision in a given case whether to impose the death sentence or to let off the offender only with life imprisonment would, to a large extent, depend upon who is the judge called upon to make the decision - Reason for this uncertainty in the sentencing process is that it involves a highly delicate task calling for skills and talents very much different from those ordinarily expected of lawyers and the choice of death sentence is bound to be influenced by the subjective philosophy of the judge called upon to pass the sentence and on his value system and social philosophy will depend whether the accused should live or die - No doubt the judge will have to give 'special reasons' if he opts in favor of inflicting the death penalty, but that does not eliminate arbitrariness and caprice, firstly because there being no guidelines provided by the legislature, the reasons which may appeal to one judge as 'special reasons' may not appeal to another, and secondly, because reasons can always be found for a conclusion that the judge instinctively wishes to reach and the judge can bona fide and conscientiously find such reasons to be 'special reasons' - Further, the various decisions in which special reasons have been given singly and cumulatively indicate not merely that there is an enormous potential of arbitrary award of death penalty by the HC and the SC but that, in fact, death sentence have been awarded arbitrarily and freakishly.- Only yardstick which may be said to have been provided by the legislature is that life sentence should be the rule and it is only in exceptional cases for special reasons that death penalty may be awarded, but it is no where indicated by the legislature as to what should be regarded as 'special reasons' justifying imposition of death penalty - Death penalty in its actual operation is discriminatory, for it strikes mostly against the poor and deprived sections of the community and the rich and the affluent usually escape from its clutches - Such circumstance also adds to the arbitrary and capricious nature of the death penalty and renders it unconstitutional as being violative of arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution - Further, burden must lie upon the State to show that death penalty is not arbitrary and unreasonable and serves a legitimate social purpose, despite the possibility of judicial error in convicting and sentencing an innocent man and the brutality and pain, mental as well as physical, which death sentence invariably inflicts upon the condemned prisoner - State must place the necessary material on record for the purpose of discharging this burden which lies upon it and if it fails to show by presenting adequate evidence before the Court or otherwise that death penalty is not arbitrary and unreasonable and does serve a legitimate social purpose, the imposition of death penalty u/s. 302 of IPC r/w s. 354(3) of the CrPC would have to be struck down as violative of the protection of art. 21 - Order accordingly.
