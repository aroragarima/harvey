Adambhai Sulemanbhai Ajmeri and others v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

16 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 364; (2014) 7 SCC 716; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 2627; 2014 (2) MLJ(Crl) 670; 2014(7) SCALE 100; [2014] 7 S.C.R. 48

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Opinion Of Expert, Fax, Approver, Finding Of Facts, Retracted Confession, Admissibility, Coal Bearing Areas (Acquisition And Development) Act, 1957, Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, Violation of fundamental rights, Requirement of, Gujarat Government Business Rules, 1990, Jammu and Kashmir Code of Criminal Procedure, Illegally possessing arms, Death penalty (hanging by neck till death), Confessional statements, Non-compliance of procedure under the Act, Statements of accomplices, Recovery of letters, Retracted confessional statements, Evidence of accomplices

Summary: (A)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 452, 120B r/w ss. 121, 123, 124A, 153A, 302, 307 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25(1AA), 27, 29 - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 3, 4, 6 - Prevention of Terrorists Activities Act, 2002, ss. 3(1)(a), (b), 3(3), 4, 20, 21(2)(b), 32, 32(5), 50 - Bombay Police Act, 1951, s. 135(1) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 157, 366 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 20(3), 21, 136 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 114, 133 - Criminal conspiracy - Illegally possessing arms - Conviction - Death penalty (hanging by neck till death) - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) allegedly attacked on a Temple wherein 33 people were killed and more than 85 people were injured and thereby committed offences u/ss. ss. 452, 120B r/w ss. 121, 123, 124A, 153A, 302, 307 of IPC, u/ss. 25(1AA), 27, 29 of 1959 Act, u/ss. 3, 4, 6 of 1908 Act, u/ss. 3(1)(a), (b), 3(3), 4, 20, 21(2)(b) of POTA Act and u/s. 135(1) of 1951 Act - Special Court convicted A1 to under RI for 5 years, A3 to undergo imprisonment for life, A4 and 6 were convicted with death penalty u/s. 120B r/w s. 302 of IPC and A2 was convicted with death penalty u/s. 120B r/w s. 307 of IPC - However, A5 was already undergone 7 years of imprisonment and was released - Aggrieved appellants filed appeals before HC, which were dismissed by impugned judgment confirming the Special Court judgment - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that sanction required u/s. 50 of POTA Act was not obtained in a proper manner and there was no compliance of s. 32(5) of POTA Act and that conviction of accused persons on the basis of their confessional statements was erroneous, that there was no admissible or reliable evidence on record which connect them with the offences and that Chief Judicial Magistrate (PW99) violated the fundamental rights of accused persons guaranteed u/arts. 20(3) and 21 of Constitution and that there was serious infirmities in alleged confessional statements of accused persons and that provisions of 1872 Act were of no avail to prosecution case and that suppression of material witness draws an adverse inference against prosecution - Whether sanction given by State Govt. in instant case was in compliance with s. 50 of POTA Act -

Held, case did not show that the sanctioning authority had applied its mind to the satisfaction as to whether the instant case required granting of sanction - Prosecution had failed to prove that sanction was granted by the Govt. either on the basis of an informed decision or on the basis of an independent analysis of fact on consultation with the IO - That would go to show clear non-application of mind by the Home Minister in granting sanction - Thus, sanction was void on the ground of non-application of mind and was not a legal and valid sanction u/s. 50 of POTA Act - Appeals allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Terrorists Activities Act, 2002, ss. 3(1)(a), (b), 3(3), 4, 20, 21(2)(b), 32, 32(5), 50 - Confessional statements - Non-compliance of procedure under the Act - Legality - Whether confessional statements of accused persons were recorded as per the procedure laid down in s. 32 of POTA Act and CrPC and the principles laid down by SC -

Held, Court found stark discrepancies in the manner in which statements of accomplices and those of accused persons were recorded - While statements of accomplices in instant case, PW50 to 52 were preceded by written records of cautions in the same document, confessional statements of accused persons did not show such caution - On the other hand, intimation by DCP (PW78) appeared on a separate documents marked as separate Exhibits from the confessions - Intimation letters of caution written by PW78 fail to prove that process of intimation preceded the recording of confessional statements as a continuous process - On the other hand, letters of intimation and confessional statements exist as disjunctive evidence, failing to prove the required chain of procedure - Enough time was not given to accused persons to record their confessional statements, particularly in in instant case since they were making confessions after 11 months of the incident - In callous manner in which PW99 had discharged his duty in instant case - A2 and 4 made confessional statements on the same day, they were produced before PW99 the very next day - Neither police officer recording the confessional statements nor the CJM (PW9) followed the statutory mandates laid down in u/ss. 32 and 52 of POTA Act while recording the confessional statements of accused persons, and confessional statements made by A2, A3, A- and A6 u/s. 32 of POTA Act were not admissible in law - Appeals allowed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 114, 133 - Statements of accomplices - Admissibility - Legality - Whether statements of accomplices disclosing evidence of offences, and the connection of accused persons to the offence, could be relied upon to corroborate their confessional statements -

Held, Courts below have placed strong reliance upon the statements of accomplices PW50, PW51 and PW52 to establish the culpability of accused persons - Evidence of accomplices at the most, raises suspicion and conjectures but same could not be construed as legal evidence against the accused persons, relying solely on which they could be convicted, as was done by Courts below - Moreover, it was a settled principle of law that confessional statements of accomplices form a very weak form of evidence, to prove the culpability of accused persons if the guilt of accused could not be proved, independent of the statements of accomplices - Thus, same could not be used to corroborate the confessional statements of an accused - Instead, there should be independent evidence to corroborate the evidence of accomplice to establish the culpability of accused - There was an inordinate delay in recording of the statements of accomplices and that casts a grave suspicion on the reliability of the testimony of the accomplices - Evidence of accomplices could not be used to corroborate the confessional statements of the accused persons in absence of independent evidence and the delay of more than one year in recording their statements causes us to disregard their evidence - Appeals allowed.

(D)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Recovery of letters - Credibility - Whether 2 letters in 'Urdu (Ex.658), which were translated in English (Ex.775), were found from the pockets of the trousers of 'fidayeens' who were killed in the attack -

Held, post mortem report of fidayeens (Ex.492) stated that all their clothes were stained with blood and mud and all clothes bore multiple tears and holes due to perforation by bullets - In such a case, the fact that letters remained clean, without any tear, soiling or stains of blood and soil was highly unnatural and improbable - Thus, Court could not accept the recording of HC that secret behind the crease-free unsoiled and unstained letter lies in the divine philosophy of 'Truth was stranger than fiction' - 2 letters marked as Ex.658 could not be taken as evidence in order to implicate the accused persons in instant crime - Appeals allowed.

(E)Criminal - Practice & Procedure -Recovery of letters - Opinion of expert - Legality - Whether letters allegedly found from the pockets of the trousers of 'fidayeens' were written by A4 -

Held, certificate of the senior most official of FSL, was not admitted on record till a much later stage, after the charge sheet was prepared and handwriting expert (PW89) gave his statement before Court - It was at that stage that his evidence was admitted with protest from the defence - PW89 in his evidence had stated that he has basic knowledge of Urdu and could not differentiate between Urdu, Arabic and Persian - PW89 further stated that opinion of handwriting experts was not conclusive - Thus, prosecution had failed to establish beyond reasonable doubt that Urdu letters (Ex.658) were written by A4 - Appeals allowed.

(F)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Retracted confessional statements - Legality - Whether there was any evidence apart from the retracted confessional statement of A6 which connects him to offence -

Held, it was clear from the statement of IO (PW125) that neither the panchnama nor seizure memo of car, made during its alleged seizure was seen by PW125 since, 'he was engaged in other work' - However, without verifying the contents of panchnama and seizure memo of the car, the involvement of car was admitted in evidence on record by Courts below, merely on the basis of subsequent panchnama drawn by Gujarat Police, which was only for the transfer of possession of the car from police of Jammu and Kashmir to Gujarat police - Court was not inclined to give any weightage to the panchnama drawn by Gujarat police at Jammu and Kashmir for the seizure of car already in the possession of Jammu and Kashmir police at a Camp, in the absence of the original panchnama and seizure memo drawn by the police of Jammu and Kashmir - Prosecution had failed to prove that car was used by A6 to carry weapons from Jammu and Kashmir to 'B' for carrying out the attack on Temple - Appeals allowed.

(G)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Terrorists Activities Act, 2002, s. 32(2) - Evidence of accomplices - Independent evidence - Corroboration - Whether there was any independent evidence on record apart from confessional statements recorded by police, of the accused persons and accomplices, to hold them guilty of the crime -

Held, fact that confessional statements of accused persons were recorded by DCP, PW78 in extremely suspicious circumstances - Procedure of presenting them before the CJM PW99, and subsequently sending them to judicial custody mandatorily had been reduced to a mere, empty formality - That procedural lapse coupled with the fact that letters of caution to be given to them, before the making of such statements, mandated u/s. 32(2) of POTA Act, and the process of recording their confessional statements were done in an extremely casual manner which was not the conduct expected from such high ranking police officers of the State Govt. - Even if confessional statements of accused persons were made admissible, that alone could not have been made the only ground for convicting them, as it would amount to a violation of the legal principle laid down in Hari Charan Kurmi and Jogia Hajam v. State of Bihar, 1964 Indlaw SC 114 - Retraction of confessional statements of accused persons A2, A3, A4 and A6 and that of PW51 revealed that they were tortured by Police to extract their confessional statements - Thus, confessional statements of A2, A3, A4 and A6 could not be relied upon for reason i.e, they have been retracted vide Exs.779 (A2), 780 (A4), 33 (A3) and 32 (A6) - A2 had retracted his confessional statement vide Ex.779, wherein he had detailed the account of how he was detained on the charge of 'autorickshaw theft' and was brought to the Crime Branch and forced to confess regarding the crime of attack on the Temple - A2 had stated that he was put to intense physical and psychological torture and police threatened him and his family members with the motive of eliciting a confession out of him which he stated to be 'false' as he was not guilty of the same and had was falsely charged - Case of prosecution rests on the confessional statements on accused persons, the confessional statements of the accomplices and their evidence and 2 Urdu letters purportedly found in the pockets of the trousers of fidayeens and written by A4, and apart from that, it was very clear that there was absolutely no independent evidence to implicate the accused persons for the crime - Evidence of accomplices, PW50 to 52 were also rejected - Thus, there was no independent evidence on record which corroborates the confessions of accused persons which were subsequently retracted - Confessional statements of accused persons could not be relied upon and case of prosecution failed - There was no independent evidence on record to prove the guilt of accused persons beyond reasonable doubt in the face of retractions and grave allegations of torture and violation of human rights of the accused persons against the police - Appeals allowed.

(H)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120-B - Criminal conspiracy - Conviction - Sustainability - Whether A2 to A6 were guilty of criminal conspiracy u/s. 120-B of IPC -

Held, confessional statements of accused persons and accomplices did not complement each other to form a chain of events leading to offence - Rather, depositions of prosecution witnesses were contradictory and disrupt the chain of events and turn it into a confusing story with many discrepancies, defeating the roles of each of the accused persons which have been allegedly performed by them - None of the events of alleged criminal conspiracy was supported by independent evidence that inspires confidence to uphold the conviction and sentences meted out to accused persons - Prosecution failed to prove beyond reasonable doubt, the guilt against accused persons, for the offence of criminal conspiracy u/s. 120B of IPC - Appeals allowed.

(I)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Violation of fundamental rights - Finding of facts - Interference - Requirement of - Whether concurrent findings of Courts below on the guilt of accused persons could be interfered with by SC in exercise of its appellate jurisdiction u/art. 136 of Constitution -

Held, there was absolutely nothing in art. 136 of Constitution which prohibits SC from reversing the concurrent findings of fact by Courts below, if it was of the opinion on the basis of the evidence on record, that affirming the findings of Courts below would result in a grave miscarriage of justice - Perversity in conducting instant case at various stages, right from investigation level to the granting of sanction by State Govt. to prosecute the accused persons under POTA Act, the conviction and awarding of sentence to the accused persons by Special Court and confirmation of the same by HC - SC, being the apex court could not afford to sit with folded hands when such gross violation of fundamental rights and basic human rights of citizens of country were presented before Court - A vital witness, DGP, who discovered the link to accused persons, was not examined by Special Court - Courts below accepted the facts and evidence produced by Police without being suspicious about the extreme coincidences with which the chain of events unfolded itself immediately that was, within 24 hours of the case being transferred to Crime Branch - Courts below did not examined the evidence with 'more than ordinary care' - Courts below had ignored basic legal principles while admitting the statement of witnesses while weighing the case against the accused persons - Instant case was a fit case for interference by Court u/art. 136 of Constitution, that concurrent findings of fact of Special Court and HC were erroneous in fact but also suffers from error in law - Impugned judgment passed by Special Court and HC were set aside - Appeals allowed.
