Supreme Court of India

30 September 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Syed Jaffer Imam, A.K. Sarkar, Vivian Bose

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 87; AIR 1958 SC 86; 1958 MLJ 197; 1958 SCJ 242; [1958] 1 S.C.R. 595

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Police Act, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949

Summary: (Majority Order)

S.R. Das, CJ. (for himself and on behalf of Venkatarama Ayyar, Jafer Imam and Sarkar, JJ)

Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226, 132(1) and 133(1) - Jurisdiction - Quashing the departmental proceedings - Respondent, a Head Constable was dismissed from service vide order dt. 20.04.1948 pursuant to a departmental enquiry - Respondent went up on appeal to the Deputy Inspector General of Police who dismissed said appeal vide order dt. 7.06.1949 - Respondent filed revision application to the Inspector General of Police who also dismissed said application on 22.04.1950 - Subsequently, respondent filed writ petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution before HC praying for the setting aside of the order of the dismissal on the ground that a departmental enquiry against the respondent was held by the District Superintendent of Police who himself became a witness and gave evidence at two stages against the respondent - Respondent further submitted that the District Superintendent of Police then found the respondent guilty and passed an order of dismissal against him - HC quashed the departmental proceedings initiated against respondent on the ground that the rules of natural justice and fair-play had been disregarded - Appellant filed appeal against said order contending that the HC had no power u/art. 226 of the Constitution, to deal with the order of dismissal which had been passed at a time when the Constitution of India had not come into force? - Whether HC had power u/art. 226 of the Constitution to deal with the order of dismissal which had been passed at a time when the Constitution of India had not come into force? - Held, original order of dismissal passed on 20.04.1948, was not suspended by the presentation of appeal by the respondent nor was its operation interrupted when the Deputy Inspector General of Police simply dismissed the appeal from that order or the Inspector General simply dismissed the application for revision - The original order of dismissal, was operative on its own strength and it did not gain any greater efficacy from the subsequent orders of dismissal of the appeal or the revision except for the specific purposes - That order of dismissal having been passed before the Constitution and rights having accrued to the appellant and liabilities having attached to the respondent before the Constitution came into force, the subsequent conferment of jurisdiction and powers on the HC can have no retrospective operation on such rights and liabilities - Even if the order of dismissal of the respondent was a nullity on the ground that it was passed by disregarding the rules of natural justice, the HC could not properly be asked to exercise its newly acquired jurisdiction and powers u/art. 226 of the Constitution to correct errors, irregularities or illegalities committed by the inferior departmental tribunal before the commencement of the Constitution, for then there will be no limit to its going backward and that will certainly amount to giving the provisions of art. 226 a retrospective operation - Hence, HC could not exercise its powers u/art. 226 of the Constitution to quash the order of the dismissal passed before the commencement of the Constitution - Appeal allowed.

(Minority Order)

Vivian Bose, JJ.

Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226, 132(1) and 133(1) - Jurisdiction - Quashing the departmental proceedings - Whether HC had power u/art. 226 of the Constitution to deal with the order of dismissal which had been passed at a time when the Constitution of India had not come into force? - Held, HC had jurisdiction to quash all the orders as the proceedings should be regarded as still pending till the order in revision was passed on 22.04.1950 - Moreover, there was no doubt that the District Superintendent of Police, who conducted the departmental trial and found the respondent guilty, acted in a judicial capacity - Appeal dismissed.
