Parry and Company Limited v P.C. Pal and Others

Supreme Court of India

27 November 1968

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Shelat, Vishishtha Bhargava, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1968 Indlaw SC 176; AIR 1970 SC 1334; 1970 (21) FLR 266; 1970 LabIC 1071; 1970 (2) LLJ 429; 1970 (2) SCJ 433; [1969] 2 S.C.R. 976

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 25F - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 227 - Policy decision - Industrial award - Writ direction - Maintainability - Appellant/company had taken policy decision in consequence of which some of employees would have to be retrenched - On 23-6-1961 respondent/union sent to company its demands, State Govt. by its order dt. 31-7-1961 referred for adjudication to Industrial Tribunal - Tribunal ordered reinstatement and payment to 52 employees of back wages as from 1-7-1961 - Appellant filed writ petition for certiorari before HC which was granted - In appeal DB concluded that HC was not competent to interfere with Tribunals' findings - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned order of Tribunal and DB was justified - Held, finding that company had failed to establish its policy was not only beyond scope of enquiry before Tribunal but totally invalid - It was well established that it was within managerial discretion of employer to organise and arrange his business in manner he considers best, so long as that was done bona fide it was not competent of Tribunal to question its propriety - If scheme for such reorganisation results in surplus age of employees no employer was expected to carry burden of such economic dead weight and retrenchment had to be accepted as inevitable, however unfortunate it was - Legislature realised such position and therefore provided by s. 25F of the Act compensation to soften blow of hardship resulting from employee being thrown out of employment through no fault of his, it was not function of Tribunal, therefore, to go into question whether such scheme was profitable or not and whether it should have been adopted by employer - These considerations which Tribunal took into account were totally extraneous to issue before it and Tribunal ought not to have allowed its mind to be influenced by such considerations and thereby disabling itself from viewing issue from proper perspective - Therefore, its finding that company had failed to establish that it was profitable was incompetent, it was for employer to decide whether particular policy in running his business will be profitable, economic or convenient and SC know of no provision in industrial law which confered any power on Tribunal to inquire into such decision so long as it was not actuated by any consideration for victimisation or any such unfair labour practice - Therefore, some of findings arrived at by Tribunal and which influenced its verdict were beyond its competence, rest were either speculative or contrary to evidence on record and were consequently liable to be set aside in writ petition for certiorari - DB, therefore, was not correct in its view that HC could not interfere with those findings or that such findings did not fall under one or other recognized grounds justifying HC's interference - Thus, SC set aside order passed by DB and restored order passed by Single Judge including his order of remand to Tribunal to prepare list of 52 persons liable to be retrenched in accordance with principle of 'last come first go' - Appeal allowed.
