Colour-Chem Limited v A.L. Alaspurkar & Ors

Supreme Court of India

05 February 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Majmudar, M. Jagannadha Rao, A.P. Misra

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 307; (1998) 3 SCC 192; (1998) SCC (L&S) 771; AIR 1998 SC 948; 1998 (3) Bom.C.R. 644; 1998 (1) CLR 638; 1998 (78) FLR 625; JT 1998 (1) SC 455; 1998 LabIC 974; 1998 (1) LLJ 69; 1998 (1) LLJ 694; 1998 (2) LLN 84; 1998(1) SCALE 432; [1998] 1 S.C.R. 663; 1998 (2) SLJ 87; 1998 (1) SLR 757; 1998 (2) SLT 497; 1998 (2) Supreme 127

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Practice & Procedure - Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Union & Prevention of Unfair Labour Practices Act, 1971, Schedule IV, Item 1, Cls. (a), (b), (d), (f), (g) - Misconduct - Dismissal order - Reinstatement with back wages - Maintainability - Respondent nos.3, 4/employees were working with appellant/employer as operators - Respondents nos.3, 4 and 10 mazdoors as well as shift spervisor were found sleeping in night shift, though machine was kept working - For said misconduct domestic enquiry was held and both respondents no. 3, 4 were dismissed from service by order dt. 4-6-1983 - Respondent nos. 3, 4 field complaints before respondent no. 2/authority under provisions of the Act - Labour Court passed order of reinstatement with appropriate back wages - Industrial Court dismissed revision applications and confirmed order of Labour Court - Writ petition filed by appellant before HC was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned order of HC was justified - Held, if punishment of dismissal or discharge was found shockingly disproportionate by the Court regard being had to particular major misconduct and past service record of delinquent or was such as no reasonable employer could ever impose in like circumstances, it would be unfair labour practice by itself being instance of victimisation in law or legal victimisation independent of factual victimisation, if any - Thus, on facts of instant case there was clear finding reached by Labour Court and as confirmed by Industrial Court that charges levelled against respondent-delinquents which were held proved even though reflecting major misconducts, were not such in light of their past service record as would merit imposition of punishment of dismissal - This factual finding would obviously attract conclusion that by imposing such punishment appellant had victimised respondent - Imposition of such shockingly disproportionate punishment by itself, therefore, had to be treated as legal victimisation apart from not being factual victimisation as on latter aspect Labour Court had held against respondent and that finding had also remained well sustained on record - Further, it was pertinent to note that so far as 10 mazdoors were concerned they were let off for this very misconduct by mere warning while respondents were dismissed from service, it was of course, true that respondents were assigned more responsible duty as compared to mazdoors - punishment of dismissal imposed on them for such misconduct was grossly and shockingly disproportionate, as rightly held by Labour Court and as confirmed by Revisional court and HC and therefore, it should be held that appellant was guilty of unfair labour practice - Thus, respondent nos. 3, 4 would be entitled to reinstatement and continuity of service but so far as back wages were concerned, even after order of Labour Court instead of 100% of back wages, respondent no. 3 would be entitled to 40% back wages till reinstatement and respondent no. 4 would be entitled to 50% back wages till actual reinstatement pursuant to instant order - Appeal dismissed.
