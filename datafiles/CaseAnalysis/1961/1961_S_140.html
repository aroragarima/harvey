Â Ujjam Bai v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

28 April 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Mohammad Hidayatullah, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 465; AIR 1962 SC 1621; [1963] 1 S.C.R. 778; 1963 (14) STC 188

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Trader, Sales Tax Authorities, Right To Carry On Business, Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950, Tax Levied, Collected, Change Of Opinion, Prohibition Act, Import and Export (Control) Act. 1947, Board Of Revenue Act, 1913, Import Trade Control Policy

Summary: (S. K. Das. J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Petitioner was a partner in a firm carried on the business of manufacture and sale of handmade biris - The Government of Uttar Pradesh on 14.12.1957 issued a notification u/s. 4(1)(b) of the Act exempting from tax, sales of certain goods including biris provided that the additional Central Excise duties leviable thereon had been paid - In partial modification of notification dt. 14.12.1957, the Govt. issued another notification on 25.11.1958, exempting from tax unconditionally, sales of biris, both machine made and handmade, w.e.f. 01.07.1958 - The effect of the said two notifications was that while for the period, 14.12.1957 to 30.06.1958, the exemption of biris from tax was unconditional and absolute - Petitioner claimed exemption granted under the notification dt. 14.12.1957 which was denied by the Sales Tax Officer - On appeal, HC dismissed the appeal filed by petitioner - Petitioner filed petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution for the enforcement of its fundamental right u/art. 19(1)(g) and 31 of the Constitution - Respondent contended that a law imposing a tax enacted by a competent legislature was not open to attack under the provisions of Part III of the Constitution - Is an order of assessment made by an authority under a taxing statute which was intra vires, open to challenge as repugnant to art. 19(1)(g), on the sole ground that it was based on a misconstruction of a provision of the Act or of a notification issued thereunder or can the validity of such an order be questioned in a petition u/art. 32 of the constitution? - Held, an order of assessment made by an authority under a taxing statute which was intra vires and in the undoubted exercise of its jurisdiction cannot be challenged on the sole ground that it was passed on a misconstruction of a provision of the Act or of a notification issued thereunder - Nor can the validity of such an order be questioned in a petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution - The proper remedy for correcting an error in such an order was to proceed by way of appeal, or if the error was an error apparent on the face of the record, then by an application u/art. 226 of the Constitution - It was necessary to observe here that art. 32 of the Constitution does not give SC an appellate jurisdiction such as was given by arts 132 to 136 of the Constitution - Article 32 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a constitutional remedy and relates only to the enforcement of the rights conferred by Part III of the Constitution - Unless a question of the enforcement of a fundamental right arises, art. 32 of the Constitution does not apply - Petition dismissed.

(J. L. Kapur, J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Held, since the statute was constitutionally valid every part of it must be so and the determination by the Sales Tax Officer, acting within his jurisdiction under the Act, even though erroneous, was valid and legal - An order of assessment under a statute that was ultra vires could not be equated with one passed under another that was intra vires, even though erroneous - If the Sales Tax Officer, acting quasi-judicially, misconstrued the notification, which it had jurisdiction to construe, and imposed a tax, there could be no infringement of art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution and validity of such an order cannot be questioned in a petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution - Petition dismissed.

(Subba Rao, J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Held, it was settled law that art. 32 of the Constitution confers a wide jurisdiction on SC to confer the fundamental rights and right to enforce a fundamental right was itself a fundamental right - Though the legislature can make a law imposing reasonable restrictions on a fundamental right in the interest of the public, the Constitution does not empower the legislature to make an order of an executive authority final so as to deprive the SC of its jurisdiction u/art. 32 of the Constitution - Whether relief can be given u/art. 32 of the Constitution against the order of a court or not, it was clear that administrative tribunals were only the limbs of the Executive, though they exercise quasi-judicial functions, and therefore, were clearly comprehended by the expression 'other authorities' in art. 12 of the Constitution - If the Sales-tax officer, by a wrong construction of the provisions of the Act, made an illegal order imposing a tax on the petitioner's fundamental right, it was liable to be quashed - In the instant case, on a plain reading of the impugned notification it was clear that handmade biris were exempted from sales tax under the Act and therefore the Sales-tax Authorities have no power to impose sales tax thereon - Petition dismissed.

(Hidayatullah, J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Held, laws which do not offend Part III of the Constitution and were not otherwise ultra vires were protected from any challenge whether u/art. 265 or under the Chapter on Fundamental Rights - Where the laws were ultra vires but do not per se offend fundamental rights, they were capable of a challenge u/art. 265 of the Constitution, and the executive action, u/art. 32 of the Constitution - Where they were intra vires otherwise but void being opposed to fundamental rights, they can be challenged u/arts. 265 and 32 of the Constitution - But where the law was made validly and in conformity with the fundamental rights and the officer enforcing it acts with jurisdiction, other considerations arise - If, in the course of his duties, he has to construe provisions of law and miscarries, it gives a right of appeal and revision, where such lie, and in other appropriate cases, resort can be had to the provisions of arts. 226 and 227 of the Constitution, and the matter brought before SC by further appeals - This was because every erroneous decision does not give rise to a breach of fundamental rights - Every right of appeal or revision cannot be said to merge in the enforcement of fundamental rights - Such errors can only be corrected by the processes of appeals and revisions - Article 32 of the Constitution does not, confer an appellate or revisional jurisdiction on SC - Petition dismissed.

(Ayyangar, J. )

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Held, from the fact that a statute was competently enacted and did not violate fundamental rights, it did not necessarily follow that quasi-judicial authorities created by it could not violate fundamental rights - Legislative competence covered only such action as could on a proper interpretation of the statute be taken under it - If a law did riot create a liability an authority acting under it could not do so by a misinterpretation of it, for Legislative backing for the imposition of such a liability would be plainly lacking - The answer to the question should, therefore, be that an action of a quasi-judicial authority would violate a fundamental right where by a plain and patent misconstruction of the statue such an authority affected fundamental rights - In the instant case, since the construction put upon the notification by the Sales Tax Officer was reasonable possible, it was a case of mere error of law and not a patent error or an error apparent on the face of the record - Petition dismissed.

(Mudholkar, J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) (g) and 32 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948, s. 4(1) - Enforcement of fundamental right - Held, the question of enforcement of a fundamental right could arise if a tax was assessed under a law which was (i) void u/art. 13 of the Constitution or, (ii) was ultra vires the Constitution or, (iii) where it was subordinate legislation, it was ultra vires the law under which it was made or inconsistent with any other law in force - Where a tax was assessed bona fide by a competent authority under a valid law and under the procedure laid down by it, no question of infringement of any fundamental right could arise, even though it was based upon an erroneous construction of law unless the tax imposed was beyond the competence of the Legislature or violated any of the fundamental rights or any other provisions of the Constitution - Mere misconstruction of a provision of law did nor render the decision of a quasi-judicial tribunal void as being beyond jurisdiction - Petition dismissed.
