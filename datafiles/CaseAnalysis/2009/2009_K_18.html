K. Ramachandran v V.N. Rajan & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

07 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 911; (2009) 14 SCC 569; AIR 2009 SC (Supp) 1970; 2009 CRLJ 4413; JT 2009 (9) SC 344; 2010 (2) MLJ(Crl) 120; 2010 (5) RCR(Criminal) 237; 2009(9) SCALE 369; [2009] 10 S.C.R. 216

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Private Party, Private Complainant, Crimes Act, 1861

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 378(2)(5)(6) and 174 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 201 - Appeal in case of acquittal - Appellant/accused was charged u/ss. 302 and 201 - For killing his wife - Strained relationship; appellant/accused always suspected his wife as to her fidelity; ill treatment and harassment meted out - Trial Court acquitted the appellant/accused - No appeal preferred by the State; but father of deceased filed a criminal revision which was admitted by the Single Judge of the High Court - During pendency of revision, State Govt. filed an appeal against acquittal, belatedly, by 801 days - Appeal was filed with condonation of delay application, which was rejected by the Division Bench of the High Court, and thus the appeal had failed - Court observed emergence of awkward situation as, an appeal against the judgment had failed, though only on the question of limitation, yet, a revision against the same judgment continued and was allowed also and all this happened because of casual attitude on the part of the State Govt. as also the appellant/accused in not pointing out the proper facts to the Courts, both to the Division Bench as well as the Single Judge of HC - In revision, Single Judge remitted the matter to the Trial Court for reconsideration, on the basis of evidence already on record - Which is impugned in the instant case - Revisional judgement, whether proper and legal? - Held, No, from point of law and also, 'No', on merit - P /&gt;(A) Dismissal of the statutory appeal preferred by the State and its effect on the pending revision, or continuance of pending criminal revision when statutory appeal was dismissed - Court observed that such question is being raised for the first time before it and should not ordinarily be allowed, but considering the circumstance of the case, Court proceeded to decide the same - Provisions of s. 378, esp. cls. (1), (2), (3) and (6) perused - Court observed that Division Bench in not allowing the condonation of delay has effectively dismissed the appeal in the sense that it has not allowed the State Govt. to proceed with the appeal for which there was a provision(cl. (1)) - Instant is the case prosecution of which was based on police report and not private complaint, Court noted; therefore, the State Govt. had a right u/s. 378(2) CrPC to file appeal and very conspicuously the private party did not have that right; private complainant, thus could only excite the general powers of revision by the HC - In facts of case, Court clarified that when DB considered the question of condonation of delay in filing the appeal against acquittal, though technically, it was deciding the application u/s. 378(3), CrPC, it was actually the whole appeal itself which was before it; limitation for filing such appeal at the instance of the State Govt. against acquittal is provided by art. 114 of Limitation Act; and for filing appeal under cls. (1) and (2), leave has to be sought from HC by moving an application therefor under cl. (3); limitation for filing the appeal is 90 days from the date of the order, while art. 114 provides for 30 days of limitation from the date of grant of special leave - Finding, what was before the HC was the appeal itself and the petitioner prayed the condonation of delay in filing appeal against acquittal; thus when the HC declined to grant that permission, it, in effect refused to entertain the appeal against the order of the Trial Court, thus, making it final - Conclusive finding, if the judgment was rendered final by the DB of the HC then there could not be any subsequent order to the contrary by the Single Judge even if the effect of the pendency of the revision was not brought to the notice of the DB; there is no review power under CrPC to the Criminal Court including the HC; such review power exists only in the Supreme Court - Trial Court's judgment thus attained finality, and it can only be upset by the present Court on a proper appeal having been filed before it by the State Govt. - Final opinion, the revision against the acquittal order could not have been entertained, much less allowed, upsetting the finality of the Trial Court's judgement, which finality was confirmed by the order of the HC by refusing to condone the delay in filing the appeal against the same Trial Court's judgment - True import of the appellate powers of the High Court, thus laid down - P /&gt;(B) Revisional judgment, on merits - Single Judge had observed that the appreciation of evidence by the Trial Court was not correct - Reading of evidence by the Single Judge examined - Found to have mis-read the evidence of doctor; speculative observations found to have made - Nowhere in the judgment it was pointed as to how and where the Trial Court had gone wrong - On merit also impugned judgment, unsustainable - Judgment of revisional Court, set aside; of Trial Court restored - Appeal allowed.
