Swaran Singh & Ors v State Through Standing Counsel & Anr

Supreme Court of India

18 August 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

Markandey Katju, Altamas Kabir

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2087; (2008) 8 SCC 435; (2008) 3 SCC (Cr) 527; AIR 2008 SC (Supp) 441; 2009 ALL MR (Cri) 260; 2008 CRLJ 4369; 2008 (4) JCC 2297; 2008 (4) JhrCR(SC) 37; JT 2008 (9) SC 60; 2009 (1) MPLJ 503; 2008 (4) RCR(Criminal) 74; 2008(11) SCALE 346; [2008] 12 S.C.R. 132; 2008 (3) UC 1866

Case Digest

Subject: Human Rights; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Revenue Record, Economic Development

Summary: (A) Human Rights - Practice & Procedure - Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989, s. 3(l)(x) - Framing of charges - Using word 'Chamar' - Intent to insult - Prima facie offence - On basis of FIR, investigation was done and charge-sheet was filed against appellants u/s. 3(1)(x) of the - Against said order of framing charges criminal revision petitions were filed by appellants in HC were dismissed by impugned judgment - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether there was prima facie offence had been committed u/s. 3(1)(x) of the Act - Held, calling member of Scheduled Caste 'Chamar' with intent to insult or humiliate him in place within public view was certainly offence u/s. 3(1)(x) of the Act - Thus, intent to insult or humiliate by using word 'Chamar' would of course depend on context in which it was used - Perusal of FIR clearly shows that, prima facie, offence was made out against appellant nos. 2 and 3 - Therefore, treating allegations in FIR to be correct offence u/s. 3(1)(x) of the Act was prima facie made out against appellant nos. 2 and 3 because it prima facie seems that intent of appellants was to insult or humiliate first informant, which was done within public view - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Human Rights - Practice & Procedure - Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989, s. 3(1)(x) - Commitment of offence - Public place - Applicability of provision - Whether act was committed in public place and come within purview of s. 3(1)(x) of the Act - Held, it was certainly place within public view, since gate of house was certainly place within public view - It could have been different matter had alleged offence been committed inside building, and was not in public view - However, provided that offence was committed outside building which could be seen by someone from road or lane outside boundary wall, lawn would certainly be place within public view - Even if remark was made inside building, but some members of public were there then also it would be offence since it was in public view - Therefore, there was no confusion with expression 'place within public view' with expression 'public place' - Thus, if word 'Chamar' was not regarded offensive at one time in our country, today it was certainly highly offensive word when used in derogatory sense to insult and humiliate person and it should never be used with that intent - Hence, use of word 'Chamar' would certainly attract s. 3(1)(x) of the Act, if from context it appears that it was used in derogatory sense to insult or humiliate member of SC/ST - Appeal dismissed.
