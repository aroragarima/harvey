Â Collector of Malabar and Another v Erimmal Ebrahim Hajee

Supreme Court of India

11 April 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Syed Jaffer Imam, P. Govinda Menon, S.K. Das, Sudhi Ranjan Das, A.K. Sarkar

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 74; AIR 1957 SC 688; 1957 CRLJ 1030; [1957] 32 ITR 124; 1957 KLT 1010; 1957 (2) MLJ 115; 1957 SCJ 640; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 970

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, s. 491 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Income-tax Act, 1961 - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, s. 46(2) - Madras Revenue Recovery Act, 1864. ss. 48, 5 - Tax Default - Warrant - Arrest - Sustainability - Respondent had been guilty of fraudulent conduct in evading payment of tax - As certificate had already been issued to by Income Tax Officer u/s. 46(2) of 1961 Act, Collector proceeded u/s. 48 of Act to issue warrant of arrest against respondent in consequence of which he was arrested and lodged in Central - Respondent had filed petition in HC u/s. 491 of the CrPC praying that directions in nature of habeas corpus may be issued for his production before Court to be dealt with according to law and for his release from imprisonment - HC, allowed petition and ordered that respondent be set at liberty as his arrest was illegal - Further stated that s. 48 of Act was ultra vires Constitution as it offended art. 22, and further opined that s. 46(2) of 1922, Act was ultra vires as it offended art. 14 of Constitution - Hence instant appeal - Whether respondent was deprived of his personal liberty in accordance with procedure established by law, and whether section of Act was ultra vires - Held, that there was nothing in s. 48 of Act which required Collector to give defaulter an opportunity to be heard before arresting him - Collector should have reason to believe that defaulter was willfully withholding payment or had been guilty of fraudulent conduct in order to evade payment - Collector, therefore, should have some material upon which he based his belief, which must be rational belief-and court may look into that material in appropriate cases in order to find out if conditions laid down in section had been fulfilled or not - From affidavits filed in HC by Collector and Income Tax Officer it was quite clear that there was material upon which Collector could base his belief that respondent was willfully withholding payment of arrears of tax and had been guilty of fraudulent conduct in order to evade payment - Collector was, therefore, justified in arresting the respondent - Appeal allowed.
