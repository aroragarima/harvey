Sunil Bharti Mittal v Central Bureau of Investigation

Supreme Court of India

09 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, H.L. Dattu, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 10; (2015) 4 SCC 609; AIR 2015 SC 923; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 812; [2015] 191 Comp Cas 177; 2015 (4) CompLJ 31; 2015 CRLJ 1130; JT 2015 (1) SC 258; 2015 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 417; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 231; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 1; 2015 (3) RLW 1894; 2015(1) SCALE 140; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 377

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure; Telecom

Keywords: Mens Rea

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Telecom - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 136 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, s. 13 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Spectrum Case - Order of cognizance - Legality - CBI registered case with regard to alleged irregularities in grant of additional spectrum in the year 2002 during the tenure of X as Minister of Communications - On completion of the investigation, charge-sheet was filed by the CBI in the Court of Special Judge - Case was registered for the offences punishable u/s. 120-B of IPC r/w ss. 13(2) and 13(1)(d) of the Act against accused persons (Deputy Director General (VAS) and three Cellular Companies) - Special Judge passed orders recording his satisfaction to the effect that appellants, prima facie, could be treated as controlling the affairs of the respective companies and represent the directing mind and will of each company and they were, thus, 'alter ego' of their respective companies and the acts of the companies could be attributed and imputed to them - On this premise, the Special Judge felt that there was enough material on record to proceed against appellants as well - Thus, while taking cognizance of the case, he decided to issue summons not only to the four accused named in the charge-sheet but appellants as well - Hence, instant appeals filed challenging the order insofar as it proceeds to implicate them as accused persons in the said charge-sheet.

Held, an individual who has perpetrated the commission of an offence on behalf of a company can be made accused, along with the company, if there is sufficient evidence of his active role coupled with criminal intent. Second situation in which he can be implicated is in those cases where the statutory regime itself attracts the doctrine of vicarious liability, by specifically incorporating such a provision. Further, the words 'sufficient grounds for proceeding' in s. 204 of CrPC suggest that an opinion is to be formed only after due application of mind that there is sufficient basis for proceeding against the said accused and formation of such an opinion is to be stated in the order itself. The order is liable to be set aside if no reason is given therein while coming to the conclusion that there is prima facie case against accused, though the order need not contain detailed reasons. However, there has to be a proper satisfaction in this behalf which should be duly recorded by the Special Judge on the basis of material on record. No such exercise is done in the instant case. The company is the accused person and the Special Magistrate has observed in the impugned order that since the appellants represent the directing mind and will of each company, their state of mind is the state of mind of the company and, therefore, on this premise, acts of the company is attributed and imputed to the appellants. It is difficult to accept it as the correct principle of law. The impugned order insofar as it relates to implicating the appellants and summoning them as accused persons set aside. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - A fortiori, the order would be bad in law if the reason given turns out to be ex facie incorrect.
