State Of H.P. And Ors v Raj Kumar Brijender Singh And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

22 April 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

Brijesh Kumar, Arun Kumar

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 1881; (2004) 10 SCC 585; AIR 2004 SC 3218; 2004 (3) RCR(Civil) 252; 2004(5) SCALE 807; [2004] Supp1 S.C.R. 618

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Himachal Pradesh Ceiling on Landholdings Act, 1972

Summary: Land & Property - Himachal Pradesh Ceiling on Landholdings Act, 1972, s. 20(3), s. 20 - Appeal withdrawn - Suo- motu - Order - Ceiling proceedings were initiated and order was passed by Collector in accordance with provisions of the 1972 Act - As a result of which land belonging to original owner, whose heirs were respondents in instant appeal, was declared surplus and 1000 bighas was left as land which they were found entitled to hold within permissible limits on basis of number of units in family - Aggrieved by order passed by Collector State preferred appeal before Commissioner and later withdrew it - As result of withdrawal of appeal order passed by Collector attained finality - Financial Commissioner, exercised suo motu power u/s. 20(3) of the Act and set aside order of Collector - Against order of commissioner respondent filed writ petition on that power of Financial Commissioner to take up matter suo motu after appeal preferred by State was withdrawn - HC observed that it was not open to Financial Commissioner to exercise those powers and bare reading of provision, s. 20, shows that exercise of suo motu power was not available once appeal preferred by State was withdrawn - Hence, instant appeals - (A)Whether order of HC was sustainable - Held, Financial Commissioner while exercising power u/s. 20 (3) of the Act acts as a statutory authority - Financial Commissioner was an appellate authority against order of Commissioner - While exercising such powers vested in him u/s. 20 (2)(3), no such restrictions could be placed on exercise of power of the Financial Commissioner on ground that he was part of machinery of State Government or officer of State - It was in a different statutory capacity under which Financial Commissioner exercises his power and was free to pass any order in accordance with law which may or may not be against State Government - Therefore, merely for the reason that one of the parties to the appeal, maybe the State Government, chose to withdraw an appeal filed before lower appellate authority, would be treated as bar for the Financial Commissioner to exercise his power under sub-s. (3) of the Act - Hence, order of HC was not sustainable - (B) Whether Commissioner was justified in delayed in exercising power- Held it was true that sub-s. (3) provides that such power might be exercised at any time but such expression did not mean there would be no time-limit or it was in infinity - All that was meant such powers should be exercised within reasonable time - Further held, that it depends on facts and circumstances of each case as to what was reasonable time within which power of suo motu action could be exercised - Special facts and circumstances by reason of which it could be said that exercise of suo motu power after 15 years of order interfered with was within reasonable time - Hence, order of Financial Commissioner stands vitiated having been passed after a long lapse of 15 years of order which has been interfered with - Therefore, while holding that the Financial Commissioner would have power to proceed suo motu in a suitable case even though an appeal preferred before the lower appellate authority is withdrawn, maybe, by the State - Thus view taken by HC was not sustainable - But order of Financial Commissioner suffers from vice of exercise of power after unreasonable lapse of time and such delayed action on his part nullifies order passed by him in exercise of power u/s. 20(3) of the Act - Appeals disposed of.
