R. K. Mohammed Ubaidullah and Others v Hajee C. Abdul Wahab (D) and Others

Supreme Court of India

18 July 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

Shivaraj V. Patil, D.P. Mohapatra

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 360; (2000) 6 SCC 402; AIR 2001 SC 1658; 2000 (5) ALD(SC) 5; 2000 (40) ALR 572; 2000 (4) AWC 2693; JT 2000 (8) SC 1; 2000 (3) MLJ(SC) 192; 2000 (3) RCR(Civil) 595; 2000(5) SCALE 186; 2000 (5) SLT 578; [2000] Supp1 S.C.R. 524; 2000 (5) Supreme 147

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 19(b) - Bonafide purchasers - Without notice of prior agreement existing exemption of - (A) Whether defendants 2 to 5 are bona fide purchasers of suit property in good faith for value without notice of original contract? - Held, s. 19(b) protects bona fide purchaser in good faith for value without notice of original contract - This protection is in nature of exception to general rule - Hence onus of proof of good faith is on purchaser who takes plea that he is an innocent purchaser - Good faith is a question of fact to be considered and decided on facts of each case - It may be actual where party has actual knowledge of fact or constructive 'A person is said to have notice' of a fact when he actually knows that fact, or when, but for willful abstention from an inquiry or search which he ought to have made, or gross negligence, he would have known it - It is clear that defendants 2 to 5 were not bona fide purchasers for value without prior notice of original contract - (B) Whether defendants 2 to 5 were not required to make any inquiry as to equitable or further interest plaintiff had in suit property at time of execution of sale deed (Exhibit B-1) in their favor, on ground that they were already aware of nature of possession of plaintiff as a tenant? - Held, it is not disputed that plaintiff and defendants 2 to 4 carry on business in hardware in adjoining premises; only a common wall separates them - Normally landlord or owner of property would also be interested in selling property to a person in possession if a reasonable price is given to avoid litigation and to have smooth transaction - In certain statutes provisions are even made to give first option to a tenant to purchase property - In such situation defendants 2 to 4 would have made inquiry with plaintiff about nature of his possession and title under which he is in possession on date of sale deed (Exhibit B-1) executed in their favor - If they had made enquiry plaintiff would have certainly revealed about Exhibit A-3, prior agreement in his favor - If such enquiry was not made it only means that defendants 2 to 5 willfully abstained from making such inquiry or they grossly neglected to do so - Defence of defendants 2 and 4 is not consistent with regard to contacting plaintiff and informing of their intention to purchase property - Once they took a stand that they directly contacted plaintiff seeking his permission to inspect suit property and in evidence of DW 1 it is stated that they sent their clerk to plaintiff seeking permission to inspect suit property - Further held, defendants 2 and 4 were required to make inquiry as to nature of possession or title or further interest, if any, of plaintiff over suit property at time when they entered into sale transaction notwithstanding they were already aware that plaintiff was in possession of property as tenant - Appeal dismissed.
