Arnesh Kumar v State of Bihar and another

Supreme Court of India

02 July 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 425; (2014) 8 SCC 273; AIR 2014 SC 2756; 2014 CRLJ 3707; 2014 (210) DLT 599; 2014 (2) G.L.R. 1848; 2014 (3) JCC 1529; JT 2014 (7) SC 527; 2014 (4) KarLJ 177; 2014 (3) KLT 143; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2289; 2014 (3) MLJ(Crl) 353; 2014(4) M.P.H.T. 81; 2014 (2) OLR 562; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 527; 2014 (3) RLW 2171; 2014(8) SCALE 250; [2014] 8 S.C.R. 128; 2014 (3) WLN(SC) 28

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 2008, Dowry harassment, Denied

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 498-A - Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, s. 4 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 41A, 41(1)(b), 41(1)(b)(ii) - Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 2008, s. 6 - Dowry harassment - Anticipatory bail - Denied - Justifiability - Petitioner (accused) was husband of respondent no. 2 - Petitioner allegedly committed offence u/s. 498-A of IPC and u/s. 4 of the Act - Petitioner filed an application for anticipatory bail before Session Judge, which was dismissed against which he approached HC, which was also rejected - Petitioner apprehending his arrest and his attempt secure anticipatory was failed had filed instant SLP along with appeal -

Held, fact that s. 498-A of IPC was a cognizable and non-bailable offence had lent it a dubious place of pride amongst the provisions that were used as weapons rather than shield by disgruntled wives - In a quite number of cases, bed-ridden grand-fathers and grand-mothers of the husbands, their sisters living abroad for decades were arrested - Law Commissions, Police Commissions and SC in a large number of judgments emphasized the need to maintain a balance between individual liberty and societal order while exercising the power of arrest - Police officers make arrest as they believe that they possess the power to do so - As the arrest curtails freedom, brings humiliation and casts scars forever, Court feel differently - Court believe that no arrest should be made only because the offence was non-bailable and cognizable and thus, lawful for the police officers to do so - The existence of the power to arrest was one thing, the justification for the exercise of it was quite another - Apart from power to arrest, police officers should be able to justify the reasons thereof - No arrest could be made in a routine manner on a mere allegation of commission of an offence made against a person - It would be prudent and wise for a police officer that no arrest was made without a reasonable satisfaction reached after some investigation as to the genuineness of the allegation - If the provisions of s. 41 of CrPC which authorises the police officer to arrest an accused without an order from a Magistrate and without a warrant are scrupulously enforced, the wrong committed by police officers intentionally or unwittingly would be reversed and the number of cases which come to Court for grant of anticipatory bail would substantially reduce - Court emphasise that practice of mechanically reproducing in the case diary all or most of the reasons contained in s. 41 of CrPC for effecting arrest be discouraged and discontinued - Police officers do not arrest accused unnecessarily and Magistrate do not authorise detention casually and mechanically - Thus, State Govts. were directed to instruct its police officers not to automatically arrest when a case u/s. 498-A of IPC was registered but to satisfy themselves about the necessity for arrest under the parameters laid down above flowing from s. 41 of CrPC - All police officers be provided with a check list containing specified sub-clauses u/s. 41(1)(b)(ii) of CrPC - Police officer shall forward the check list duly filed and furnish the reasons and materials which necessitated the arrest, while forwarding/producing the accused before the Magistrate for further detention - Magistrate while authorising detention of accused should peruse the report furnished by police officer in terms aforesaid and only after recording its satisfaction, the Magistrate would authorise detention - Decision not to arrest an accused, be forwarded to Magistrate within 2 weeks from the date of the institution of the case with a copy to Magistrate which might be extended by Superintendent of police of district for the reasons to be recorded in writing; Notice of appearance in terms of s. 41A of CrPC be served on the accused within 2 weeks from the date of institution of the case, which might be extended by Superintendent of Police of District for the reasons to be recorded in writing; Failure to comply with the directions aforesaid should apart from rendering the police officers concerned liable for departmental action, they should also be liable to be punished for contempt of court to be instituted before HC having territorial jurisdiction - Authorising detention without recording reasons as aforesaid by Judicial Magistrate concerned should be liable for departmental action by the appropriate HC - Said directions should not only apply to cases u/s. 498-A of IPC or s. 4 of the Act, instant case, but also such cases where offence was punishable with imprisonment for a term which might be less than 7 years or which may extend to 7 years; whether with or without fine - Court by order dt.31-10-2013 had granted provisional bail to appellant on certain conditions, which was made absolute - Appeal allowed.
