Ishwarlal Girdharlal Joshi v State of Gujarat and Another

Supreme Court of India

16 November 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, Vishishtha Bhargava, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 413; AIR 1968 SC 870; 1968 (9) G.L.R. 634; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 267

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Defence Regulations

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Land acquisition - Public purpose - Authority - Under Secretary - Govt. issued notifications u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act that certain lands were needed for a public purpose and that Govt. was satisfied that it was arable land - Govt. further directed u/s. 17(4) of the 1894 Act that as the acquisition of the said lands were required urgently, the provisions of s. 5A the 1894 Act would not apply - Another notification was subsequently issued u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act and it contained a direction u/s. 17(1) of the 1894 Act, enabling the Collector to take possession of all arable lands - Both notifications were signed by the Under Secretary to Govt. and were shown to be by order and in the name of the Governor - Hence the instant appeal - Whether the Under Secretary was duly authorised to sign the notifications under the 1894 Act - Held, the Under Secretary was competent to sign the declaration as a Secretary - Word 'Secretary' was not defined either in the 1894 Act or the General Clauses Act, 1897 so as to exclude Additional, Joint, Deputy, Under or Assistant Secretaries - Moreover r. 13 of the Rule of Business specifically placed all Secretaries on equality for authentication of orders and instruments of Govt. and r. 15 of the Rule of Business further authorised supplemental instructions which were in fact issued - And, even if the Under Secretary did not possess the power as a Secretary he would undoubtedly have been competent as an officer duly authorised by virtue of r. 13 of the Rules of Business and that was all that s. 6 of the 1894 Act required - No further special authorisation under the Act was necessary - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Land acquisition - Arable land - Public purpose - Authority - Under Secretary - Whether formation of opinion by the Govt. as regards urgency or that the lands were arable was required - Held, it was obvious that the executive action of the Govt. was in fact expressed to be taken in the name of the Governor, and that the orders were authenticated in the manner required by r. 13 of the Rules of Business already quoted - Validity of the order could not be called in question that it was not an order made by the Governor - In addition to the Constitutional provision there was also the presumption of regularity of official acts - Orders of Govt., whether at ministerial or gubernatorial level, were all issued in the same form and the Constitutional protection as well as the presumption both covered the instant case - Govt. was not called upon to answer the kind of affidavit which was filed with the petition because bare denial that Govt. had not formed an opinion could not raise an issue - HC, having accepted the affidavits of the Under Secretary and Deputy Secretary had formed the necessary opinion, was only required to see if they had the competence, which it held that they had - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Land acquisition - Arable land - Public purpose - Authority - Under Secretary - Deputy Secretary - Jurisdiction - Whether the directions u/ss. 17(1) and 17(4) of the 1894 Act of the deputy secretary and the under secretary were valid - Held, Secretaries were given the jurisdiction to take action on behalf of Govt. and had satisfied themselves about the need for acquisition u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act, the urgency of the matter and the existence of waste and arable lands for the application of ss. 17(1) and 17(4) of the 1894 Act - In view of the Rules of Business and the Instructions their determination became the determination of Govt. and no exception could be taken - Of course, if Govt. had relied upon the provisions of Art. 166(2) of the Constitution and the presumption of regularity of official acts, all this enquiry would have become unnecessary since the appellants had not originally pleaded any facts leading to any enquiry - However, on a review of the affidavits, the provisions of the Act and the Business Rules and instructions the instant court was satisfied that the directions u/ss. 17(1) and 17(4) of the 1894 Act were not invalid - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Land acquisition - Public purpose - Arable land - Waste land - Whether the land was waste or arable land - Held, arable land meant not only land capable of cultivation but also actually cultivated - It was not arable not because it was cultivated demonstrated its nature as arable land - There was no question of crops on waste land for the crops could only be on arable lands because if crops could grow or were actually grown the land would hardly be waste - Appeal dismissed.
