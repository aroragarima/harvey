Regional Provident Fund Commissioner v Hooghly Mills Company Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

18 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 396; (2012) 2 SCC 489; 2012 (2) AWC 1920; 2012 (3) CalLT 34; JT 2012 (2) SC 51; 2012 (2) LLJ 1; 2012 (4) MahLJ 52; [2012] 1 S.C.R. 363; 2012 (2) SLR 481

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Acquisition of Certain Area At Ayodhya Act, 1993, Statement of Objects and Reasons of Act, Amending Act 37

Summary: Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Employees' Provident Funds and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1952, ss. 17(1)(a), 17(1A)(a), 6, 7, 8 and 14B - Exemption - Interpretation - Validity - Challenged - By notification dated 23-11-1967, the Central Govt. in exercise of its power u/s. 17(1) (a) of the Act granted exemption to respondent/company - After grant of exemption respondent framed scheme and created Trust and appointed Board of Trustees from the Management of the said Trust fund and was thus enjoying exemption u/s. 17(1A) (a) of the Act - There were defaults on the part of respondent in making timely payment of dues towards provident fund - Proceedings were initiated against respondent - Commissioner directed respondent to remit an amount of Rs. 32,62,153/- by way of damages - Aggrieved respondent filed Writ petition before Single Judge of HC and was allowed - On appeal DB affirmed the judgment of Single Judge - Hence instant appeal - Whether the employer of an establishment which was an 'exempted establishment' under the Act was subject to the provisions of s. 14B of the said Act whereby in cases of default in the payment of contribution to the provident fund, proceedings for recovery of damages could be initiated against the employer of such an 'exempted establishment' - Held, instant case calls for interpretation of certain statutory provisions - It was not disputed, and possibly could not be disputed, that the Act was a social welfare legislation - Act was one of the earliest Acts after the Constitution came into existence - SC had occasion to expressly hold that the said Act was a beneficial social welfare legislation to ensure benefits to the employees - In the case of Regional Provident Fund Commissioner v. S.D. College, Hoshiarpur and others 1996 indlaw SC 345 SC while interpreting s. 14B of the Act held that the Act envisaged the imposition of damages for delayed payment - SC also held that the Act was a beneficial social legislation to ensure health and other benefits of the employees and the employer under the Act was under a statutory obligation to make the deposit - In construing the provision of s. 17(1A)(a) where it was provided that when an exemption had been granted to an establishment s. (1)(a), the provision of ss. 6, 7, 8 and 14B of the Act should, 'so far as may be' apply to the employer of exempted establishment in addition to such other condition as may be specified in the notification granting such exemption - S. (a) which had been set out hereinbefore, would find that cl. (a) of s. 17(1A) was divided in two parts - The second part is more specific in as much as it had been clearly stated that where an employer contravenes and makes default in compliance with any of the said conditions and provisions or any other provisions of the Act, (this would obviously include s. 14B), he should be punishable u/s. 14 as if the said establishment had not been exempted u/cl. (a) - Therefore, there was a deeming provision giving clear indication of application of s. 14B of the Act to the 'employer' of an 'exempted establishment' - In instant case, the HC failed to discern the correct principle of interpretation of a social welfare legislation - Unfortunately, the HC missed well settled principle of interpretation of social welfare legislation while construing the expression 'so far as may be' in interpreting the provision of s. 17 (1A)(a) of the Act and unduly restricted its application to the employer of an exempted establishment - HC's interpretation also was in error for not considering another well settled principle of interpretation - It was not uncommon to find legislature sometime using words by way of abundant caution - HC by adopting, if we may say so, a rather strait jacket formula in the interpretation of expression 'so far as may be' has in judgment, misinterpreted the intent and scope and the purpose of the Act - Impugned judgment of Single Judge and DB were overruled and set aside - Appeal allowed.
