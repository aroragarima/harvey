Joseph M. Puthussery v T. S. John and others

Supreme Court of India

01 December 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Panchal, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1014; (2011) 1 SCC 503; AIR 2011 SC 906; 2011 (3) ALD(SC) 40; 2011(1) ALL MR 475; JT 2010 (13) SC 213; 2010 (2) MPLJ 488; 2011 (1) OLR 614; 2011 (1) RCR(Civil) 388; 2010(12) SCALE 633

Case Digest

Subject: Election

Keywords: Statement Of Fact

Summary: Election - Representation of People Act, 1951, ss. 116A and 123(4) - Disqualification of MLA - Corrupt Practices - Respondent no. 1 filed election petition u/s. 100(1)(b) of 1951 Act assailing the election of the appellant submitting that the election of the appellant was vitiated by corrupt practice defined u/s. 123(4) of 1951 Act for the reason that pamphlets which allegedly contained false statements regarding the respondent No. 1 were distributed by the appellant, his election agent and workers of the United Democratic Front, i.e., the party to which the appellant owe allegiance, with his consent as well as with the consent of his election agent - Appellant filed written statement resisting the election petition contending that neither he nor his election agent or any one with his and/or their consent had distributed said pamphlets and distribution of said pamphlets was done by the Youth Wing of the party to which the respondent No. 1 belongs and that the distribution of pamphlets does not amount to any publication - Single Judge allowed said Election Petition filed by the respondent no. 1 and set aside the election of the appellant - Whether Single Judge rightly allowed the election petition filed by the respondent no. 1? - Held, one of the important ingredients in proving the offence of corrupt practice u/s. 123(4) of the 1951 Act is that it has to be established that the returned candidate believed the statement that was published, to be an untrue statement - SC in Dr. Jagjit Singh vs. Giani Kartar Singh and others 1965 INDLAW SC 499, held that the onus of proving that the maker believed the statement to be false rests with the election petitioner - In the present case, HC does not explain how on the basis of evidence led by the respondent no. 1 it stands proved that the appellant believed that the contents of the pamphlets were false - On the contrary, the defence of the appellant that he believed the statements made in said pamphlets to be true because of their prior publication in 'Crime' magazine and failure of the respondent no. 1 to initiate any legal action against the Crime magazine, if tested on preponderance of probability stands proved - Hence, appellant has rightly proved that he believed the contents of the pamphlets to be true - Impugned order of HC set aside - Appeal allowed.
