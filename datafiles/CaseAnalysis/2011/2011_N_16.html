State Of U.P. v Naresh & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

08 March 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 169; (2011) 4 SCC 324; 2011 CRLJ 2162; JT 2011 (3) SC 508; 2011 (2) RCR(Criminal) 364B; 2011(3) SCALE 425; [2011] 4 S.C.R. 1176

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, 34, 307 and 379 - Acquittal - Challenged - Trial Court convicted and sentenced all the four accused u/ss. 302/34, 307/34 and 379/34 of the IPC - Appeal filed thereagainst was allowed - HC reversed the order of Trial Court and acquitted all accused - HC held that the FIR was ante-timed and ante-dated without giving any reason whatsoever - HC held that the FIR was subject to doubt, though such a finding did not get any support from any material on record - HC held that testimony of PW.5 injured witness was not worth believing - Hence, instant appeal - Whether HC erred in reversing the well reasoned judgment of the Trial Court giving unwarranted attention to minor contradictions on trivial matters and taking into consideration non-existent facts? - Held, in all criminal cases, normal discrepancies were bound to occur in the depositions of witnesses due to normal errors of observation - However, minor contradictions, inconsistencies, embellishments or improvements on trivial matters which did not affect the core of the prosecution case, should not be made a ground on which the evidence can be rejected in its entirety - The court had to form its opinion about the credibility of the witness and record a finding as to whether his deposition inspires confidence - Therefore, mere marginal variations in the statements of a witness could not be dubbed as improvements as the same may be elaborations of the statement made by the witness earlier - The omissions which amount to contradictions in material particulars i.e. go to the root of the case/materially affect the trial or core of the prosecution's case, render the testimony of the witness liable to be discredited - In the instant case, FIR was lodged most promptly considering the distance between the place of occurrence and the police station - Therefore, the finding that the FIR was ante-timed and ante-dated was erroneous and contrary to the documents on record - Further, HC without giving any cogent reason held that testimony of PW.5 was not worth believing - Such a view was contrary to the consistent and persistent view taken by SC time and again that the presence of injured witness could not be doubted and his version of events could, even in exceptional circumstances, be relied upon with care and caution - Hence, HC erred in giving significance to a trivial issue - Findings recorded by the HC were perverse and accordingly, set aside - Appeal allowed.
