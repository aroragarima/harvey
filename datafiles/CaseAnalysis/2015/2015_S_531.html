Sujasha Mukherji v Hon'ble High Court of Calcutta, Through Registrar and others

Supreme Court of India

19 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 135; JT 2015 (2) SC 567; 2015(2) SCALE 563; [2015] 2 S.C.R. 480; 2015 (3) SLR 260; 2016 (1) WLN(SC) 18

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges

Keywords: Sustainable, Moderation of marks

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Moderation of marks - Sustainable Appellant was placed in second position in written test conducted for recruitment to cadre of District judge - However, after moderation of marks was carried out in Paper No. II, appellant stood disqualified for further consideration as she scored only 37 % in said Paper No. II, against minimum marks of 40% which was prerequisite for being called for interview - Appellant approached HC and Single Judge of HC held that ratio laid in Sanjay Singh vs. U.P. Public Service Commission, Allahabad, 2007 INDLAW SC 656, had not been comprehensively followed and that appellant was unjustifiably excluded from zone of consideration and was hence, entitled for relief - However, on appeal before DB of HC, clarified that ratio laid in Sanjay Singh case is not be read and interpreted like statute and allowed appeal - Hence, instant appeal by appellant - Whether the order recorded by DB was sustainable.

Held, respondents have neither asseverated nor established that Head Examiner had been appointed for conducting moderation. Further, no meeting was convened in which examiners were present so as to discuss the substance of questions and reach consensus as regards suitable or model answers thereto. Furthermore, there was no justification for the junior-most Judge or Examiner to have been given the task of moderation and there were no additional criteria to support his candidature as the Moderator. Further, moderation has been carried out in respect of assessment or marking of 2nd Examiner and only in Paper No. II. Most of candidates whose answer scripts had been reassessed afresh, the reduction averages 10 marks, which, hence, constituted the 'mean'. Hence, deduction of as many as 18 marks to appellant is not logical or justified as a consequence of moderation. Further, in Sanjay Singh case has been misconstrued and hence misapplied in impugned judgment by DB of HC while it has been correctly applied by Single Judge of HC. Further, as effort of moderation been carried out, it is directed that 10 marks being appropriate mean arrived at, be deducted from initial marks of 55 obtained by appellant in Paper II and also respondent is directed to conduct interview of appellant and declare the result. Further, CJ of HC was to ensure that Interview Committee is not comprised of any of three Examiners. Hence, impugned judgment is set aside. Appeal allowed.
