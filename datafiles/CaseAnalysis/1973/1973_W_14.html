Western India Match Company Limited v Workmen

Supreme Court of India

20 August 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Dwivedi, P. Jaganmohan Reddy

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 315; (1973) SCC (L&S) 531; (1974) 3 SCC 330; AIR 1973 SC 2650; 1973 (27) FLR 288; 1973 LabIC 1602; 1973 (2) LLJ 403; [1974] 1 S.C.R. 434

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Practice & Procedure - Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1946, ss. 4, 6-B, 10, 13(2) and 18 - Workmen was a watch man on probation for a period of six months which was extended by two months - Discharged - Whether discharge was legal and/or justified? - According to Standing Order, a workman shall not be kept on probation for more than two months, if he has worked during these two months to satisfaction of Company, ' he becomes permanent - But as a result of special agreement, even though he has worked during these two months to satisfaction of Company, he will not be a permanent workman - Terms of employment specified in Standing Order would prevail over corresponding terms in contract of service in existence on enforcement of Standing Order - M/s J K Cotton Manufacturers Ltd Kanpur v J N Tewari (A 1 R 1959 All 639) and Banaras Electric Light and Power Co Ltd Berhlupura v Govt. of Uttar Pradesh and others (1961 Indlaw ALL 19) were overruled - It plainly follows from ss. 4, 10 and 13(2) that inconsistent part of special agreement cannot prevail over Standing Order - Held, employer cannot enter into an agreement with a workman which is inconsistent with Standing Orders of company - As long as Standing Order is in force, it is binding on Company as well as workmen - Further held, that it is settled law now that Labour Court may interfere with order of discharge where it is satisfied that it was made mala fide or was a measure of victimization or unfair labour practice - No evidence to show that work of workmen was unsatisfactory - Order of discharge is arbitrary - Appeal allowed.
