Sidhartha Vashisht @ Manu Sharma v State (Nct Of Delhi)

Supreme Court of India

19 April 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 296; (2010) 6 SCC 1; AIR 2010 SC 2352; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 1627; 2010 (168) DLT 739; JT 2010 (4) SC 107; 2010 (2) MLJ(Crl) 1104; 2010 (2) RCR(Criminal) 692; 2010(4) SCALE 1

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Media & Entertainment; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Comments, Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970, Refresh, Interception of Communications Act, 1985, Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act, 1996, Bar Council of India Rules, 1975, Criminal Appeal Act, 1988

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Scope of Appellate Court in reversing order of acquittal by Trial Court - Held, appellate Court has all necessary powers to re-evaluate evidence led in before trial Court as well as conclusions reached - It has a duty to specify compelling and substantial reasons in case it reverses order of acquittal passed by trial Court - In present case, HC by adhering to all ingredients and by giving cogent and adequate reasons reversed order of acquittal - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 154 - What constitutes First Information Report (FIR) - Whether three telephonic messages received by Police or statement made by PW2 recorded at Hospital constitute FIR? - Held, phone calls made immediately after an incident to police constitutes an FIR only when they are not vague and cryptic - Calls purely for reason of getting police to scene of crime do not necessarily constitute FIR - In present case, phone calls were vague and therefore could not be registered as FIR - FIR was properly lodged as per statement of PW-2 - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 162 - Implication of delay in recording statements - Whether delay in recording statements of witnesses is fatal to case of prosecution? - Held, delay in recording statement of witnesses do not necessarily discredit their testimonies - Court may rely on such testimonies if they are cogent and credible - In present case, consequent to large number of witnesses, their interrogation also consequently took a substantial amount of time - Furthermore, identity of appellant as a suspect in present case was not consequence of any delay - Thus, delay, if any, in recording evidence of witnesses in present case cannot be considered as an infirmity in prosecution case - Appeals dismissed.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Nature of duty of disclosure on public prosecutor in ordinary cases of criminal trial - Held, CrPC imposes a statutory obligation on public prosecutor to disclose certain evidence to defense - Public prosecutor is under a duty of disclosure under CrPC, Bar Council Rules and relevant principles of common law - Nevertheless, a violation of this duty does not necessarily vitiate entire trial - A trial would only be vitiated if non-disclosure amounts to a material irregularity and causes irreversible prejudice to accused - In present case, no such prejudice was caused to accused person, and therefore trial is not vitiated - Appeals dismissed.

(E) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860,ss.302, 201, 120B - Arms Act, 1959, s. 27 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - In a private party on 29/30-4-1999 at Tamarind CafÃ© people were invited - Liquor was being served by bartenders - Deceased (Jessica Lal) and 'S' were in-charge of bar - On refusal to serve liquor as party was over appellant (Manu sharma) shot at deceased - Deceased died - Charge u/ss. 302/201/120B IPC and also u/s. 27 Arms Act framed against accused - Charge u/ss. 120B/201 IPC were framed against other accused persons - Prosecution in order to support their case, examined 101 witnesses - Trial Court came to conclusion that all links in chain of evidence produced by prosecution are either missing or broken and prosecution had miserably failed to bring home guilt of accused and thereby acquitted accused persons of all charges - Appeal was filed before HC against acquittal - HC reversed order of acquittal and convicted appellant u/s. 302, 201/120B of IPC and s. 27 of Arms Act - HC also convicted other accused persons u/s. 201/120B of IPC - Hence, present appeal - (A) Presence of accused at place of occurrence - Held, presence of accused at scene of crime is proved through ocular testimonies of PWs 1, 2, 6, 20, 23, 24 and 70 and is also corroborated by other evidence - (B) Report of Ballistic expert - Held, laboratory reports in present case are vague and ambiguous and, therefore, they cannot be relied upon to reach any specific conclusion regarding incident - (C) Fair Trial - Held, right of accused with regard to disclosure of documents is a limited right but is codified and is very foundation of a fair investigation and trial - Accused cannot claim an indefeasible legal right to claim every document of police file or even portions which are permitted to be excluded from documents annexed to report u/s. 173(2) as per orders of Court - In present case, no prejudice had been caused to right of appellant to fair trial and non-furnishing of copy of one of ballistic reports had not hampered ends of justice - Right of appellant to disclosure has not received any set back in facts and circumstances of case - (D) Conviction of co-accused - Held, HC rightly convicted other two accused after appreciation of evidence of PWs 30 and 101 - (E) Conviction of appellant - Held, prosecution established that appellant was holder of a.22" bore Pistol; he was witnessed by 'B' as perpetrator of crime; a mutilated .22" lead was recovered from skull of deceased; two empties of .22" make with mark 'C' were found at spot; a .22" live cartridge with mark 'C' was found in Tata Safari of appellant which was found abandoned; that his prior and subsequent conduct of having got Tata Safari removed from spot, of absconding; refusal to TIP without having any basis; that he even denied his presence at spot, clearly prove beyond reasonable doubt leaving no manner of doubt that he is guilty of offence of murdering deceased by using firearm and destroying evidence thereafter - Prosecution established its case beyond doubt against appellants - Appeals dismissed.

(F) Practice & Procedure - Remarks made by Trial Judge against prosecution and by Division Bench against Trial Judge - Held, a judicial consensus would require that judgment should be set aside or affirmed as case may be but preferably without offering any undesirable comments, disparaging remarks or indications which would impinge upon dignity and respect of judicial system - Despite exercise of such restraint, if, in a given case, Court finds compelling reasons for making any comments in that event it will be in consonance with basic rule of law and adherence to principles of natural justice that view point of concerned learned Judge should also be invited - In present, DB could have avoided making such observations which directly or impliedly indicates towards impropriety in functioning of Court, appreciation of evidence by learned Judge and/or any other ancillary matter - Content and merit of judgment would have remained unaffected even if such language or comments were not made against learned trial Judge - Expunction of all remarks made by Trial Judge against prosecution and by Division Bench against Trial Judge directed - Appeals dismissed.

(G) Criminal - Media & Entertainment - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(a) - Role of the Media and Press - Appellant submitted that the appellant had been specifically targeted and maligned before and during the proceedings by the media, who proclaimed him as guilty despite even after his acquittal by the Trial Court - Held, every effort should be made by the print and electronic media to ensure that the distinction between trial by media and informative media should always be maintained - Trial by media should be avoided particularly, at a stage when the suspect is entitled to the constitutional protections - Invasion of his rights is bound to be held as impermissible despite the significance of the print and electronic media - Freedom of speech protected u/art. 19(1)(a) of the Constitution has to be carefully and cautiously used, so as to avoid interference in the administration of justice and leading to undesirable results in the matters sub judice before the Courts - Appeal dismissed.
