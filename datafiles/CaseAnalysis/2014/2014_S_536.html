Sundeep Kumar Bafna v State of Maharashtra and another

Supreme Court of India

27 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 191; AIR 2014 SC 1745; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 4113; 2014 CRLJ 2245; 2014 (2) JCC 1264; JT 2014 (4) SC 486; 2014 (2) KLT 809; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1923; 2014 (2) MLJ(Crl) 454; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 416B; 2014 (2) RLW 1232; 2014(4) SCALE 215; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 486

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss.82, 167 and 439 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 288, 304, 308, 336, 388 read with 34 and Section 120-B - Bail - Denied - Sustainability - In the impugned Judgment, Single Judge has opined that when the appellant's plea to surrender before the Court was accepted and he was assumed to be in its custody, the police would be deprived of getting his custody, which was not contemplated by law, and thus, the appellant was required to be arrested or otherwise he had to surrender before the Court which could send him to remand either to the police custody or to the Magisterial custody and this could only be done u/s. 167 of CrPC by the Magistrate and that order could not be passed at the HC level - Appellant had fervidly assailed the legal correctness of that opinion - It was contended that the Magistrate was not empowered to grant bail to appellant, since he could be punished with imprisonment for life, as statutorily stipulated in s. 437(1) CrPC; case stood registered with police for offences punishable u/ss.288, 304, 308, 336, 388 r/w. 34 and s.120-B of IPC - Appellant further contended that since the matter stands committed to Sessions, the Magistrate was denuded of all powers in respect of the said matter, for the reason that law envisaged the commitment of a case and not of an individual accused - While accepting the Preliminary Objection, the dialectic articulated in the impugned order was that law postulated that a person seeking regular bail should perforce languish in the custody of the concerned Magistrate u/s. 167 CrPC - Petitioner had not responded to the notices/summons issued by the concerned Magistrate leading to the issuance of non-bailable warrants against him, and when even these steps proved ineffectual in bringing him before the Court, measures were set in motion for declaring him as a proclaimed offender u/s. 82 CrPC - Since it was not the position obtaining in the case, i.e. it was assumed by the HC that the petitioner was not in custody, the application for bail u/s. 439 of CrPC was held to be not maintainable - That conclusion was reached even though the petitioner was present in Court and had pleaded in writing that he be permitted to surrender to the jurisdiction of the High Court -

Held, SC was unable to locate any provision in the CrPC which prohibited an accused from moving the Court of Session for such a relief except, theoretically, s. 193 which also only prohibited it from taking cognizance of an offence as a Court of original jurisdiction - That embargo did not prohibit the Court of Session from adjudicating upon a plea for bail - It appeared to SC that till the committal of case to the Court of Session, s.439 of CrPC could be invoked for the purpose of pleading for bail - If administrative difficulties were encountered, such as, where there were several Additional Session Judges, they could be overcome by enabling the accused to move the Sessions Judge, or by further empowering the Additional Sessions Judge hearing other Bail Applications whether post committal or as the Appellate Court, to also entertain Bail Applications at the pre-committal stage - Since the Magistrate was completely barred from granting bail to a person accused even of an offence punishable by death or imprisonment for life, a superior Court such as Court of Session, should not be incapacitated from considering a bail application especially keeping in perspective that its powers were comparatively unfettered u/s. 439 of the CrPC - In the case in hand, appellant had filed an application praying, firstly, that he be permitted to surrender to the HC and secondly, for his plea to be considered for grant of bail by the HC - SC said that because there were no provisions in the CrPC contemplating the committal of a case to the HC, thereby logically leaving its powers untrammeled - There were no restrictions on the HC to entertain an application for bail provided always the accused was in custody, and that position would be obtained as soon as the accused actually surrenders himself to the Court - Further singularity of the instant case was that the offence had already been committed to Sessions, albeit, the accused/Appellant could not have been brought before the Magistrate - Therefore, the HC was not justified in directing the appellant to appear before the Magistrate - In conclusion, therefore, SC was of the opinion that the Single Judge erred in law in holding that he was devoid of jurisdiction so far as the application presented to him by the appellant before SC was concerned - Conceptually, Single Judge could have declined to accept the prayer to surrender to the Courts' custody, although, SC was not aware of any reason for that option to be exercised - Once the prayer for surrender was to be accepted, appellant before SC would come into the custody of the Court within the contemplation of s.439 CrPC - Sessions Court as well as the HC, both of which exercised concurrent powers u/s.439 CrPC, would then have to venture to the merits of the matter so as to decide whether the appellant had shown sufficient reason or grounds for being enlarged on bail. - Impugned Order was, accordingly, set aside - Single Judge should consider the appellant's plea for surrendering to the Court and dependent on that decision, Single Judge should, thereafter, consider the appellant's plea for his being granted bail - Appeal allowed.
