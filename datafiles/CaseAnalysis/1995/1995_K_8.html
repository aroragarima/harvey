Trustees of The Port of Madras Through Its Chairman v K. P. V. Sheikh Mohd. Rowther and Company Private Limited and Another

Supreme Court of India

29 March 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

Kuldip Singh, K. Venkataswami, S. Saghir Ahmad

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 970; (1997) 10 SCC 285; AIR 1995 SC 1922; 1995 (52) ECC 6; 1996 (82) E.L.T. 174

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Stores

Summary: Customs - Customs Act, 1962, s. 39 - Port Trust Act - Whether demurrage charges, harbour dues etc. payable to Port Trust of Madras were to be recovered from steamer agent or consignee? - High Court on facts of case came to conclusion that consignee was liable to pay charges - Even though consignee is not a party to contract of carriage once property in goods had passed to him, he becomes liable to pay storage or demurrage charges as owner of goods to shipowner - Relied on International Airports Authority of India v. Grand Slam International 1995 Indlaw SC 371 wherein it was held that an authority created under a statute even if is custodian of imported goods because of provisions of Customs Act, 1962, would be entitled to charge demurrage for imported goods in its custody and make importer or consignee liable for same even for periods during which he/it was unable to clear goods from customs area, due to fault on part of Customs authorities or of other authorities who might have issued detention certificates owning such fault - (B) Whether customs duty was leviable in respect of stores supplied out of bonded stock to what are called daughter vessels? - Vessels, which brought wheat and milo, indisputably were foreign vessels owned or operated by foreign shipping companies, but because of defects they could not be berthed in port premises themselves for purpose of unloading - They, therefore, employed daughter vessels - These daughter vessels could not be treated in any way different from foreign vessels from which cargo had to be discharged - Daughter vessels were only a means adopted or used in order to enable discharge of cargo on land - Supplies made from bonded stock should, therefore, in substance and in truth, be taken to be supplies made to foreign vessels - No customs duty would be leviable - Held, reasoning and conclusions reached by HC correct - Appeals dismissed.
