D. Sudhakar & Ors. v D.N. Jeevaraju & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

25 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Cyriac Joseph

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 513; (2012) 2 SCC 708; 2012 (5) AWC 4618; JT 2012 (1) SC 568; 2013 (4) KarLJ 74; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 350; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 330

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Karnataka Legislative Assembly (Disqualification of Members on Ground of Defection) Rules, 1986

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136,32,75,226,227 - Karnataka Legislative Assembly (Disqualification of Members on Ground of Defection) Rules, 1986, rr. 3,4,6,7 - Withdrawal of Support - Disqualification as Member - Principles of Natural Justice - Sustainability - Appellants herein were elected to Legislative Assembly as independent candidates in elections, they were sworn in as Ministers in Cabinet - Appellants submitted separate letters to Governor stating that having become disillusioned with functioning of Govt., in which there was widespread corruption and nepotism, a situation had arisen where governance of State could not be carried on in accordance with provisions of Constitution - Governor was also requested to intervene and institute Constitutional process, on basis of letters written by Appellants and others, Governor asked Chief Minister to prove his majority on Floor of House - Respondent Nos.1,3 filed Complaint with Speaker u/r. 6 of Disqualification Rules, to declare that Appellants had incurred disqualification on ground of defection - Speaker issued Show-Cause Notices to Appellants informing them of Disqualification Application filed by respondent No. 1and 3 indicating that despite having got elected as independent candidates, they became members of Party and also became Ministers and thereby they violated Paragraph 2(2) of Tenth Schedule to Constitution - Appellants submitted interim reply that notice was in clear violation of Disqualification Rules, 6 and 7, it was mentioned that r. 7(3) required copies of petition and annexures thereto to be forwarded along with show-cause notice, notice which was pasted on doors of MLA quarters in MLA hostels, which were locked and used by legislators only when House was in session, called upon Appellants to reply to notice was against principles of natural justice, further appellants explained that it was not intention of Appellants to withdraw support to government, but only to Government headed CM - Speaker disqualified appellants, which was concurred by HC in writ petition filed by appellants - Aggrieved instant appeal - Appellants contended that by not allowing Appellants sufficient time to reply to Show-Cause Notices, Appellants had been deprived of a valuable opportunity to meet allegations, further Appellants had always been treated as a separate group and it was only in connection with this case that Respondents had attempted to show that Appellants had joined Party and by withdrawing support from Govt. they had incurred disqualification and also that Whip issued by Chief Whip of Party did not form part of documents produced before Speaker - Whether disqualification of appellants by Speaker which was concurred by HC, was sustainable - Held, apart from fact that Appellants were not given 7 days' time to file their reply to Show-Cause Notices, HC, did not give serious consideration to fact that even service of Show-Cause Notices on Appellants and 13 MLAs had not been properly effected - It was obvious from procedure adopted by Speaker that he was trying to meet time schedule set by Governor for trial of strength in Assembly and to ensure that Appellants and 13 MLAs stood disqualified prior to date on which Floor test was to be held, Speaker passed detailed orders, in which various judgments, both of Indian Courts and foreign Courts, and principles of law from various authorities were referred to, on same day, holding that Appellants and other MLAs stood disqualified as Members of House - Vote of Confidence took place in which disqualified Members could not participate, and in their absence CM, was able to prove his majority in House - Unless it was to ensure that Trust Vote did not go against CM, there was hardly any reason for Speaker to have taken up Disqualification Applications in such a great haste - Therefore, SC, held that impugned order of Speaker was vitiated by mala fides and was unsustainable - Appeal allowed.
