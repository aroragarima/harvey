Southern Motors v State of Karnataka and others

Supreme Court of India

18 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 37; (2017) 3 SCC 467; AIR 2017 SC 476; JT 2017 (1) SC 346; 2017(1) SCALE 604; [2017] 98 VST 207

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; VAT

Keywords: Central Excise Act, 1944, Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, Karnataka Value Added Tax Act, 2003, Jharkhand Value Added Tax Act, 2005, Kerala General Sales Tax Rules, 1963, United State of Travancore and Cochin General Sales Tax Act, 1125, Tamil Nadu Value Added Tax Act, 2006, Karnataka Value Added Tax Rules, 2005, Constitution of India, 1950, Income-tax Act, 1961, Travancore-Cochin General Sales Tax Amendment Act, 1951, Travancore Cochin General Sales Tax Rules, 1950

Summary: Indirect Tax - VAT - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 42 - Karnataka Value Added Tax Rules, 2005, rr. 3, 3(2), 3(2)(c ), 31, 32 - Trade Discount - Disallowance of Deduction Challenged - Appellant nos. 1-2 registered dealers in motor vehicles and electronic goods respectively sold goods at price after allowing discount and filed returns of AY's 2007 - 08 & 2008-09 - Respondent no.3/AO initially allowed trade deduction of discount and later passed rectification order thereby deduction was disallowed - HC dismissed appeals of dealers and confirmed findings of authorities - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, it is well-recognised rule of construction that a statutory provision must be so construed, if possible, that absurdity and mischief may be avoided. R. 3(2) of the Rules, in particular lists the array of deductions conditioned on variety of situations as scheduled therein to ascertain the taxable turnover. Allowance of discount is one of the several other permissible deductions contingents on the melange of determinants referred to therein. Further, r. 3(2)(c) of the Rules, would reveal that all amounts allowed as discount would qualify for deduction from the total turnover to ascertain the taxable turnover and thus the extent of exigibility under this statute. The first proviso prescribes that a discount to be eligible for deduction has to be one which is allowed in accordance with the regular practice of the dealer or is in accordance with the terms of any contract or agreement entered into in a particular case and the tax invoice or bill of sale issued in respect of the sales relating to such discount shows the amount allowed as discount. The second proviso enjoins further, that the accounts should show that the purchaser had paid only the sum originally charged less the discount. Hence, to insist on the quantification of trade discount for deduction at the time of sale itself, by incorporating the same in the tax invoice/bill of sale, would be to demand the impossible for all practical purposes and thus would be ill-logical, irrational and absurd. Hence, the contrary interpretation accorded by the HC being in defiance of logic and the established axioms of interpretation of statutes is thus unacceptable and is negated. Appeals allowed.
