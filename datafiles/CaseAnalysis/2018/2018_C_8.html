Susme Builders Private Limited v Chief Executive Officer, Slum Rehabilitation Authority and others

Supreme Court of India

04 January 2018

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Gupta, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2018 Indlaw SC 1

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Constitution

Keywords: accommodation, applicant, arbitration, arrangement, completion, elections, false, Government Company, jurisdiction, laches, migration, minority, obstruction, occupiers, public interest litigation, review, Slum Areas, Societies, verification

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, 13(2) - Development Control Regulations for Greater Bombay, 1991, regn.33(10) - Development agreement - Termination - Challenged - Appellants/developers entered into an agreement with respondent no. 3/society to develop land in question and provide accommodation to slum dwellers - However, no work was done as per the terms of the agreement and nothing was constructed during that period - Managing committee of respondents in a meeting terminated agreement with appellants and decided to appoint capable developer - Aggrieved, appellants filed writ petition before HC challenging impugned order, which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - What was the scope, ambit and effect of the order of SC dt. 27-03-2015.

Held, words in praesenti only mean that Court wanted the verification of consent of eligible slum dwellers as on date of passing the order. In praesenti cannot be read to mean present and voting. It only means eligible slum dwellers as on dt. 27-03-2015. Out of 867 total eligible voters only 651 voted and appellant secured 423 votes, which would mean 64.98% or roughly 65% of the votes polled. In case SC exclude 79 votes which are doubtful, then the total eligible voters would be 788 and appellant secured 413 i.e. 52.41% of the total eligible slum dwellers, well below the magic figure of 70%. Court only wanted to find out who had the majority which was not the essence of the order dt. 27-03-2015. It is true that 70% was not reflected in the direction given however, directions have to be understood in view of the intention of Court, which was to find out that which of the builders had the support of 70% of slum dwellers. Appeal disposed of.

(B) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, 13(2) - Development Control Regulations for Greater Bombay, 1991, regn. 33(10) - Slum development - Notice issued - Validity - Whether in the notice issued u/s. 13(2) of the Act the issue of 70% consent was raised.

Held, when a notice was issued to a party it should be clearly told what werethe allegations which it shouldmeet. The notice should be clear and unambiguous. There was no allegation in the notice(s) that the right to develop granted in favour of appellants was liable to be revoked because it had not obtained consent of 70% of slum dwellers. The reference to regn. 33(10) of the Regulations also did not specifically raise the issue of 70% consent. Appellants were never put to notice by Slum Redevelopment Authority (SRA) that its right to develop the land might be cancelled because of not having consent of 70% slum dwellers which was confined to the issue of delay. However, while considering the issue of delay SRA was justified in making reference to the various communications made by appellants and its architects seeking time to obtain consent of 70% slum dwellers and while dealing with the issue of delay SC should take into consideration all those matters. Appeals disposed of.

(C) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, 13(2) - Development Control Regulations for Greater Bombay, 1991, regn. 33(10) - Slum dwellers consent - Permissibility - Whether support of 70% of slum dwellers was mandatory and slum dwellers are entitled to withdraw their consent.

Held, while granting migration, SRA could lay down conditions and such conditions could also be laid down during the course of the Scheme. SRA envisaged and appellants clearly understood, that it had to obtain consent of 70% of the slum dwellers. The slum dwellers are interested with the rehabilitation buildings and not with the free sale buildings. Later on, when applying for permission to trade their development rights, appellants clearly understood and undertook that it would furnish the consent forms of 70% of slum dwellers. The agreements with 70% slum dwellers should have been provided within a reasonable time and, though almost 20 years have elapsed since the second letter of intent was granted in favour of appellants it has till date failed to submit such agreements. Thus, appellants delayed the project because it failed to get consent from 70% of the occupiers. Appeals disposed of.

(D) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, 13(2) - Development Control Regulations for Greater Bombay, 1991, regn. 33(10) - Scheme implementation - Delayed - Whether appellants delayed the construction of the Scheme and was not entitled to any relief.

Held, finding given by SRA that appellant was responsible for the delay, is a finding based on appreciation of material on record. It cannot be said to be a perverse finding. It is a finding of fact and HC was justified in coming to the conclusion that it could not set aside this finding of fact in writ jurisdiction. Appellants were itself guilty of delaying the construction for no reason at all and was rightly held responsible for the delay in implementation of the rehabilitation scheme and as such SC found no error in the impugned order. Appeals disposed of.

(E) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, 13(2) - Development Control Regulations for Greater Bombay, 1991, regn. 33(10) - Scheme applicability - Entitled to - Whether appellants were entitled to continue with the Scheme.

Held, since the notice by SRA to appellants did not make any specific allegation with regard to appellants not having 70% consent, that portion of the order of SRA setting aside the right to develop the land on ground of lack of 70% consent might have been beyond the scope of the notice. The law and the conditions of the letter of intent as well as the conditions imposed in the various letters issued by SRA clearly required appellants to produce agreements with at least 70% of slum dwellers which appellants has miserably failed to do. Though appellants might have remained the same entity in name, there have been, at least, three changes in the promoters of appellants and those transfers of shareholdings should have been done for consideration. It was more than obvious that appellants as a legal entity was treating the slum dwellers only as a means of making money and was not entitled to any relief. SC direct SRA that it should within 3 weeks invite letters of interest from renowned builders/developers, who have the capacity and experience to take up such a large project by issuing advertisements in not less than three newspapers having wide circulation in State. Appeals disposed of.
