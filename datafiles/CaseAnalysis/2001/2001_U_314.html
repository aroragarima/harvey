Clarence Pais and Others v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

22 February 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Rajendra Babu, R.C. Lahoti

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20265; (2001) 4 SCC 325; AIR 2001 SC 1151; 2001 (2) ALD(SC) 87; 2001 (43) ALR 249; JT 2001 (3) SC 82; 2001 (1) KLT 860; 2001 (3) MLJ(SC) 43; 2001 (2) RCR(Civil) 184; 2001 (92) RD 272; 2001(2) SCALE 184; 2001 (1) SCJ 465; [2001] 2 S.C.R. 43; 2001 (2) SLT 263; 2001 (2) Supreme 127

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Hindu Wills Act, 1870, Indian Succession (Amendment) Act, 1962, Indian Christian Estates Administration Act, 1901

Summary: Family & Personal - Indian Succession Act, 1925 - Indian Succession Act, 1865, ss. 211 and 213, - Indian Christian Estates Administration Act, 1901 - Indian Succession (Amendment) Act, 1962 - Hindu Wills Act, 1870 - Probate and Administration Act, 1881 - Validity of s. 213 - Whether s. 213 of Indian Succession Act, 1925 is unconstitutional and its provisions can be enforced against Indian Christians? - S. 213(1) requires that no right as executor or legatee under a will can be established in a court of justice without obtaining probate or letters of administration of will under which such right is sought to be established - S. 57 of Act makes it clear that provisions of that part which are set out in sch. III, subject to restrictions and modifications specified therein, shall apply to all wills and codicils made by any Hindu, Buddhist, Sikh or Jaina after 01-09-1870, who are originally residents in jurisdiction of High Courts of judicature at Madras and Bombay or subject to jurisdiction of Lieutenant Governor of Bengal and to all such wills and codicils made outside those territories and limits so far as relate to immovable property situate within those territories or limits and to all wills and codicils made by any Hindu, Buddhist, Sikh or Jaina on or after 01-01-1927 to which those provisions are not applied by cls. (a) and (b), provided that marriage shall not revoke any such wills or codicils - Thus compulsory requirement of probating a will to establish such a right by virtue of provisions of s. 213 which is made applicable and is restricted to Indian Christians and certain other categories of persons professing Hindu and other faiths - Thus it is applicable to Parsis after amendment of Act in 1962 and to Hindus who reside within territories which on 01-09-1870 were subject to Lt. Governor of Bengal or to areas covered by original jurisdiction of HCs of Bombay and Madras and to all wills made outside those territories and limits so far as they relate to immovable property situate within those territories and limits - If that is so, it cannot be said that section is exclusively applicable only to Christians and, therefore, it is discriminatory - Whole foundation of case is thus lost - Differences are not based on any religion but for historical reasons that in British Empire in India, probate was required to prove right of a legatee or an executor but not in part 'B' or 'C' states - That position has continued even after Constitution has come into force - Historical reasons may justify differential treatment of separate geographical regions provided it bears a reasonable and just relation to matter in respect of which differential treatment is accorded - Uniformity in law has to be achieved, but that is a long - drawn process - Undoubtedly, states and Union should be alive to this problem - Only on basis that some differences arise in one or other States in regard to testamentary succession, law does not become discriminatory so as to be invalid - If Christians alone had been discriminated against by treating them as a separate class argument could have been understood and merited consideration - No merit - Petition dismissed.
