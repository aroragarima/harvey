Karunanidh v Seetharama Naidu and others

Supreme Court of India

27 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Abhay Manohar Sapre, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 214; (2017) 5 SCC 483; AIR 2017 SC 1632; 2017 (4) ALD(SC) 1; 2017 (122) ALR 494; 2017 (3) Bom.C.R. 31; 2017 (2) CLT(SC) 317; JT 2017 (6) SC 82; 2017 (2) RCR(Civil) 594; 2017 (2) RLW 1347; 2017(4) SCALE 1

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Second Appeal, Hindu Succession Act, 1956, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Family & Personal - Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 100 - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, ss. 15, 15(2) - Absolute interest in property - Testator bequeathed his immovable and movable properties including suit properties to his heirs viz, granddaughters from first wife and son-in-law; two daughters from second wife and to his 3rd wife - While so, two daughters of testator, Alamelu Ammal and Ramanujatha Ammal effected partition between them in relation to properties they got from their father through Will - Later, Alamelu Ammal executed Will and bequeathed some portion to her sister-Ramanujatha Ammal and remaining to appellant and later died - Aggrieved, grand children of testator from his first wife served legal notice to Ramanujatha Ammal stating that they were entitled to share in properties received by her from her father-testator and her sister-Alamelu Ammal and later, filed suit against Ramanujatha Ammal for declaration of their title and for possession in relation to suit properties contending that Ramanujatha Ammal and Alamelu Ammal had only life interest in properties they received from their father-testator and hence on death of Alamelu Ammal, properties held by her devolved on plaintiffs as reversioners by succession through testators first wife as father's heirs - However, Trial Court dismissed suit by holding that Alamelu Ammal and defendant-Ramanujatha Ammal had 'absolute interest' in properties received by them by Will from testator and that plaintiffs failed to prove that defendant and Alamelu Ammal had only life interest in suit properties - Aggrieved, plaintiffs filed appeal but the same was dismissed affirming judgment and decree of Trial Court and so, plaintiffs filed second appeal before HC and during pendency of appeal, defendant-Ramanujatha Ammal died and appellant was brought in as respondent to represent estate of defendant - Upon hearing parties, HC allowed appeal in part and while setting aside judgment, decreed suit in part - Further, HC held that since plaintiffs are son and daughter of a pre-deceased daughter of a pre-deceased daughter and are class I heirs as specified in Schedule and by virtue of s.15(2)(a) which has overriding effect on those categories of heirs specified in S.15(1) of the Act, would be entitled to claim 1/3rd share in suit properties along with defendant - Hence, instant appeal by appellants - Whether plaintiffs have right in suit properties.

Held, HC though formed three substantial questions of law, had not included any question regarding applicability of s.15(2) of the Act. HC was not right in suo moto applying the provisions of s.15(2)(a) of the Act without even framing any additional substantial question of law by taking recourse to s.100(5) of CPC as it is mandatory for HC to have first formulated the specific question on the applicability of s.15(2)(a) of the Act either at the time of admission of the appeal or at the time of final hearing of the appeal by assigning reasons for framing such question but the same was not done and the same was jurisdictional error committed by HC while deciding the second appeal. Further, HC was not right in placing reliance on s.15 of the Act for deciding the rights of the parties as plaintiffs had belonged to category of heirs - 'son of a pre-deceased daughter of a pre-deceased daughter and daughter of a pre-deceased daughter of a pre-deceased daughter' which was added in Schedule (Class I) only with effect from 9-9-2005 by amendment by Act No.39 of 2005. However, to claim interest by succession in the properties of Alamelu Ammal opened in plaintiff's favour as an heir from father's side in 1987 when Alamelu Ammal died and plaintiff's rights as an heir to claim shares in suit properties had to be worked out on the basis of law in force in 1987. Hence, plaintiffs had no right on the strength of succession/devolution to claim any interest in the properties of Alamelu Ammal in 1987 as father's heir. Furthermore, testator bequeathed his properties to all his heirs including his two daughters by conferring on them 'absolute interest' and not the life interest in the properties. As such, Alamelu Ammal and defendant acquired absolute ownership rights in the suit properties and rightly got their names recorded in revenue records in 1957 itself and continued to exercise their ownership rights till 1987 without any interference from anyone including plaintiffs or/and their predecessor-in-title. Further, Alamelu Ammal alienated her share by executing a Will in favour of defendant and the same is legally permissible. Hence, even though HC rightly upheld all the material findings of the two lower courts, committed legal error when it placed reliance on s.15(2)(a) read with Schedule appended to the Act for granting relief to the plaintiffs and by recognizing their right in the suit properties against the defendant. However, such a finding of HC is legally unsustainable and so, the same is set aside and judgment of Trial Court is restored. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - HC has jurisdiction to hear the second appeal only on the substantial question of law framed u/s.100(5) of CPC and HC has no jurisdiction to decide the appeal on the question which is not framed as required u/s.100(4) of CPC. Further, once a heir becomes the absolute owner of the property by virtue of a Will then as a necessary consequence, he/she is entitled to alienate such property by any mode permissible in law to anyone.
