Salig Ram v Munshi Ram and Another

Supreme Court of India

21 March 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 318; AIR 1961 SC 1374; 1962 (1) SCJ 130; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 470

Case Digest

Subject: Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Punjab Laws Act, 1872, Punjab Laws Act

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Punjab Laws Act, 1872 - Whether respondent was entitled to property under any personal law or custom prevailing in Punjab? - Held, in 1940 customary law of Amritsar district was again compiled and custom recorded is that an adopted son loses his right to inherit from his natural father but if latter dies without other sons adopted son cannot inherit as a son but may inherit collaterally as a successor of his adoptive father - Conclusion therefore at which we arrive is that amongst Brahmins and Khatris of Amritsar district, a son given away in adoption can succeed to property of his natural father only if there is no other son of natural father; if there is another son he cannot succeed - In this case Munshi Ram was claiming to succeed not to property of Hans Raj, his natural father, but, to property of Nanak Chand his natural grandfather - If case was for succession to property of natural father, namely, Hans Raj, custom might have favoured Munshi Ram, for Hans Raj had no other son and Munshi Ram would thus have succeeded to property of Hans Raj - But Hans Raj, having died in lifetime of his father (Nanak Chand), never succeeded to property of his father - Munshi Ram having been adopted away and there being another son of Nanak Chand, even custom recorded in para 48 would exclude Munshi Ram because then there would be a brother of Munshi Ram alive in family of Nanak Chand and this brother would succeed in exclusion of Munshi Ram who would be representing his father - Appeal allowed.
