Â Mc Dowell and Company Limited v Commercial Tax Officer

Supreme Court of India

17 April 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

O. Chinnappa Reddy, Y.V. Chandrachud, D.A. Desai, E.S. Venkataramiah, Ranganath Misra

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 426; (1985) 3 SCC 230; (1985) SCC (Tax) 391; AIR 1986 SC 649; 1985 (2) CompLJ 137; 1985 (47) CTR 126; [1985] 154 ITR 148; 1985(1) SCALE 788; [1985] 3 S.C.R. 791; 1985 (59) STC 277; 1986 TAXLR 2174; [1985] 22 TAXMAN 11

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Corporate; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Ranganath Misra, J. (for himself and on behalf of Y. V. Chandrachud, C.J. and D. A. Desai and E. S. Venkataramiah, JJ.)

Sales Tax - Excise - Indirect Tax - Andhra Pradesh General Sales Tax Act, 1947, s. s.2(s) - Andhra Pradesh Distillery Rules, 1981, r. 76 - Liability to pay excise duty - Allegedly buyers of Indian Liquor from the appellant's distillery obtain distillery passes for release of liquor after making payment of excise duty and presented the same at the distillery whereupon the bill of sale or invoice was prepared by the distillery showing the price of liquor excluding the excise duty - Appellant's books of account did not contain any reference to excise duty paid by the purchaser - Appellant paid sales tax payable by it under the Act, 1957 on the basis of its turnover which excluded excise duty - Said position was not accepted by the Sales Tax Authorities and the matter was contested upto the SC - SC in Mc Dowell & Company Ltd. etc. vs. Commercial Tax Officer 1976 INDLAW SC 601, held that the Sales Tax Authorities were not competent to include in the "turnover" of the appellant, the excise duty which was not charged by it, but was paid directly to the Excise Authorities of the buyers of the liquor, inasmuch as the excise duty did not become a part of the circulating capital - After said judgment of SC, rr. 76 and 79(1) of 1981 Rules were amended w.e.f. 4.10.1981 which provides that 'no spirit of liquor' manufactured or stored shall be removed unless excise duty specified in r. 6 of 1981 Rules had been paid by a holder of D-2 Licence before such removal and on payment of the excise duty by the holder of D-2 licence a distillery pass for the removal of spirit fit for human compensation may be granted in favour of any of the named persons therein - Subsequently, appellant, being a D-2 licence holder was served with a notice by a respondent proposing to include a sum of Rs. 4,49,09,532/- representing the excise duty paid directly by buyers of appellants' liquor in the appellants, turnover for a part of the year 1982-83 - Appellant filed writ petition before HC for quashing the said notice - HC dismissed said writ petition and held that the primary liability to pay excise duty was indisputably of the holder of the D-2 licence - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether excise duty paid by buyers of Indian liquors for obtaining distillery passes was includible in the turnover of the appellant? - Held, r. 76 of 1981 Rules had clearly affirmed that liability for payment of excise duty was of manufacturer - Payment of excise duty was primary and exclusive obligation of manufacturer and if payment be made under a contract or arrangement by any other person it would amount to meeting of obligation of manufacturer and nothing more - However, sale of liquor had now been made exigible to tax at every point other than point of last sale in State - In the instant case, though excise duty paid by the purchaser to meet the liability of the appellant, was a part of the consideration for the sale and was includible in the turnover of the appellant - Appeal dismissed.

Minority Order)

Chinnappa Reddy, J

Sales Tax - Excise - Indirect Tax - Andhra Pradesh General Sales Tax Act, 1947, s. s.2(s) - Andhra Pradesh Distillery Rules, 1981, r. 76 - Liability to pay excise duty - Held, the proper way to construe a taxing statute, while considering a device to avoid tax, was not to ask whether the provisions should be construed literally or liberally, nor whether the transaction was not unreal and not prohibited by the statute, but whether the transaction was a device to avoid tax, and whether the transaction was such that the judicial process may accord its approval to it - It was neither fair nor desirable to expect the legislature to intervene and take care of every device and scheme to avoid taxation - It was upto the Court to take stock to determine the nature of the new and sophisticated legal devices to avoid tax and consider whether the situation created by the devices could be related to the existing legislation with the aid of 'emerging' techniques of interpretation, to expose the devices for what they really were and to refuse to give Judicial benediction - Appeal dismissed.
