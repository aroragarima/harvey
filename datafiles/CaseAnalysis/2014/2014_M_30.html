Mahavir Singh v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

23 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 384; (2014) 6 SCC 716; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 2615; 2014 CRLJ 3228; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 433; 2014(7) SCALE 477; [2014] 6 S.C.R. 370

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Conviction

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 201, 120B r/w s. 34 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) and co-accused had illicit relationship with wife (accused) of deceased, who was brother of PW13 and appellant had grudge on account of marriage of deceased with his wife - Appellant and co-accused were charged with offence u/ss. 302, 201, 120-B of IPC and Trial Court convicted appellant and co-accused u/ss. 302, 120-B of IPC and sentenced them to under imprisonment for life - Aggrieved appellants filed separate appeals before HC which were dismissed by impugned judgment - Hence, instant appeals - Appellant contended that co-accused 'J' had developed illicit relations with the wife of deceased and that courts below committed an error in applying the last seen theory and that there was evidence on record to the extent that appellant and co-accused had been in the company of deceased on 21-6-1995, but missing person report was lodged on 25-6-1995, and when there was such a long gap in last seen and recovery of dead body, such a doctrine had no application whatsoever and no independent witness was examined - Whether impugned conviction of appellant was justified -

Held, it was a settled legal proposition that last seen theory comes into play only in a case where the time gap between the point of time when the accused and deceased were seen alive and when the deceased was found dead - Since the gap was very small there might not be any possibility that any person other than the accused might be the author of crime - In instant case, if examine the medical report minutely, it becomes evident that deceased had been murdered 1 week prior to the post mortem - Thus, it becomes evident that he had been killed in a very proximity of time when the deceased was seen alive with the appellant and 'J', co-accused - It was pointed out that there had been some discrepancies in the inquest report as well as in the depositions of witnesses - However, no material contradictions could be brought to notice - Minor discrepancies were bound to occur in every case - It was a settled legal proposition that in case question was not put to the witness in cross-examination who could furnish explanation on a particular issue, the correctness or legality of said fact/issue could not be raised - In instant case, Court had gone through the cross-examination of witnesses who could furnish an explanation for the discrepancies pointed out by appellant - However, defence had never put any question in those regards to the material witness who could furnish the explanation for the same - So the chain of all circumstantial evidence was complete and no link was missing and the accused persons had an opportunity to commit the murder of deceased - Both Courts below after appreciating the evidence on record held the appellant guilty of offences - Appeal dismissed.
