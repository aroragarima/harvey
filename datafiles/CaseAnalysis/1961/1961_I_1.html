Immani Appa Rao and Others v Gollapalli Ramalingamurthi and Others

Supreme Court of India

22 September 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Raghubar Dayal

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 194; AIR 1962 SC 370; 1962 (2) MLJ 101; 1962 (2) MLJ(SC) 101; 1962 (2) SCJ 437; [1962] 3 S.C.R. 739

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Court Fees Act, 1870 - Indian Trusts Act, 1882 - Provincial Insolvency Act, 1920 - Trusts Act - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Whether, where parties are equally guilty estoppel cannot be pleaded against appellants and estate must be allowed to remain where it rests? - Held, transaction in favour of respondent 1 is result of a fraudulent plan to which both he and respondent 2 agreed - It was effected with mutual consent of vendor and vendee to defraud creditors of vendor - That being so transfer is not supported by any consideration and transferee agreed to act as benamindar until transferor required him to reconvey properties to his sons - Object intended to be achieved and fraud initially contemplated by both parties have been achieved and creditors of respondent 2 have been defrauded - Thus, both parties are confederates in fraud and are equally guilty - Respondent 1 can be said to be guilty of a double fraud because first, he joined respondent 2 in his fraudulent scheme and participated in commission of fraud object of which was to defeat creditors of respondent 2, and then he committed another fraud in suppressing from court fraudulent character of transfer when he made out claim for recovery of properties conveyed to him - Conveyance in his favour is not supported by any consideration and is result of fraud and as such it conveys no title to him - Yet, if plea of fraud is not allowed to be raised in defence, court would in substance be giving effect to a document which is void ab initio - Paramount consideration of public interest requires that plea of fraud should be allowed to be raised and tried, and if it is upheld estate should be allowed to remain where it rests - Adoption of this course is less injurious to public interest than alternative course of giving effect to a fraudulent transfer - Appeal allowed.
