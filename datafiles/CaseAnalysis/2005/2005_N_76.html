State (N.C.T. of Delhi) and Others v Navjot Sandhu @ Afsan Guru and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 August 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Venkatarama Reddi, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1026; (2005) 11 SCC 600; (2005) SCC (Cr) 1715; AIR 2005 SC 3820; 2005 CRLJ 3950; 2005 (122) DLT 194; JT 2005 (7) SC 1; 2005(6) SCALE 177; [2005] Supp2 S.C.R. 79

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Comments, Joint Trial, Law Commissioners, Indian Telegraph Rules, Government Of National Capital Territory Of Delhi Act, 1991, TADA Act, Acquaintance, Constitution (Sixty-Ninth Amendment) Act, 1991, Geneva Conventions Act, 1960, Criminal Law Act, 1977, Human Rights Act, 1988, Prevention of Terrorism (2Nd) Ordinance, 2001, Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, Business Rules

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 239 and 239 AA - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 173 and 196 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 121, 121A, 122, 120B, 302, 307, 120B, 123, 186, 332, 353 and 196 - Prevention of Terrorism Act, 2002, ss. 52, 3(2)(3),(4) and (5), 4(b), 50, 34, 6, 2(h) and 45 - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 3, 4 and 5 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25 and 27 - Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 - Constitution (Sixty-Ninth Amendment) Act, 1991 - Govt. of National Capital Territory of Delhi Act, 1991 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Govt. of Union Territories Act, 1963 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Terrorists and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act,1987 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 17, 25, 26, 10, 63 and 65 - Police and Criminal Evidence Act, 1984 - Human Rights Act, 1988 - Criminal Law Act, 1977 - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 - Prevention of Terrorism (2nd) Ordinance, 2001 - Telegraph Act, s. 5 - Five heavily armed persons entered Parliament House complex - 9 persons including 8 security personnel and 1 gardener lost their lives in attack and 16 persons including 13 security personnel, received injuries - Five assailants were killed - Analysis of cell phones and SIM cards led to Afzal, Shaukat, Gilani, Navjyot Sandhu - Afzal and Shaukat confessed having been parties to conspiracy - Afzal, Shaukat and Gilani were convicted and sentenced to death - Navjot Sandhu was acquitted of all charges except one u/s. 123, IPC - High Court dismissed appeals of Afzal and Shaukat - Gilani and Navjot Sandhu acquitted - Appeal by Afzal and Shaukat - Appeal by State against acquittal of Gilani and Navjot Sandhu - (A) Whether sanctions were valid? - S. 50 of POTA enjoins that no Court shall take cognizance of offences under Act without previous sanction of Central Govt. or State Govt. - Also, s. 196 of CrPC enacts a bar against taking cognizance of any offence punishable under Chapter VI of IPC except with previous sanction of Central Govt. or State Govt. - Some offences charged are under Chapter VI - State Govt. u/s. 2(h) of POTA, the expression 'Administrator' finds place in art. 239 - U/art. 239 AA designation of Administrator gets merged into new designation of Lt Governor - Thus, Lt Governor who continues to be Administrator also derives his or her authority to grant sanction u/s. 50 of POTA by virtue of legislative fiction created by s. 2(h) r/w art. 239 - Administrator is deemed to be State Govt. for purpose of s. 50 of POTA - Lt. Governor acted in exercise of powers conferred by s. 196(1) CrPC - Held, sanctions u/s. 50 of POTA and s. 196 of CrPC were accorded by a competent authority - (B) Whether there was deliberate failure on part of investigating agency to invoke POTA initially in order to circumvent requirements of ss. 45 and 52? - Though POTO provisions could have been invoked on very first day having regard to nature and manifestations of this grave crime no justification to characterize action of concerned police officers as malafide or motivated - POTA contains drastic and stringent provisions - In view of this special feature of law, it is necessary to bestow sufficient care and thought before prosecuting an offender under this special law instead of proceeding under ordinary law - Held, that no fault with Investigating Officers in going slow in bringing POTA into picture - (C) Whether Charges were defective? - 'Fundamental defect' should be found in charges if Court has to quash it - S. 215 of CrPC makes it clear that no error or omission in stating either offence or particulars required to be stated shall be regarded as material unless accused was in fact misled by such error or omission and it has occasioned a failure of justice - Held, that accused not misled or prejudiced - Charges are often framed by way of abundant caution - No ground to set aside charges and invalidate trial - (1) Law regarding confessions - Further held, confession should have been made with full knowledge of its nature and consequences - If any reasonable doubt is entertained by court that these ingredients are not satisfied, court should eschew confession from consideration - In order to be assured of truth of confession court should look to corroboration from other evidence - However, there need not be corroboration in respect of each and every material particular - (2) scope of confessions u/s. 32 of POTA and u/s. 15 of TADA - Non obstante provisions - Use of confession under POTA against co-accused - Further held, that confession of a co-accused ought not be brought within sweep of s. 32(1) - (3) Whether confession of accused which cannot be proved against a co-accused either u/s. 32(1) of POTA or u/s. 30 of Evidence Act, would be relevant evidence against co- accused involved in conspiracy by reason of s. 10 of Evidence Act? - S. 10 of Evidence act is based on principle of agency operating between parties to conspiracy - If conditions laid down therein are satisfied, act done or statement made by one is admissible against co-conspirators - (4) S. 27 of Evidence Act - Interpretation of - S. 27 of POTA lifts ban against admissibility of confession/statement made to police to a limited extent by allowing proof of information of specified nature furnished by accused in police custody - In that sense s. 27 is considered to be an exception to rules embodied in ss. 25 and 26 - (5) Call records in doubt - S. 63 Evidence Act deals with secondary evidence - S. 65 enables secondary evidence of contents of a document to be adduced if original is of such a nature as not to be easily movable - Further held, may be that certificate containing details in s. 65B(4) is not filed in instant case, but that does not mean that secondary evidence cannot be given even if law permits such evidence to be given in circumstances mentioned in ss. 63 and 65 - (6) Interception of phone calls - Interception of communication is provided for by provisions contained in Chap. V of POTO/POTA which contains ss. 36 to 48 - Further held, evidence cannot be shut out by applying embargo contained in s. 45 when on date of interception, procedure under Chap. V of POTA was not required to be complied with as on relevant date POTA was not in picture and investigation did not specifically relate to offences under POTA - Procedural safeguards of POTA discussed - Held that with regard to evidence death sentence of Afzal confirmed, Shaukat convicted of lesser charge and Gilani and Navjot Sanvdhu stand acquited - Appeal by Afzal dismissed - Appeal by Shaukat partly allowed - Appeal by state dismissed.
