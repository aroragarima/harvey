Shyamal Saha and another v State of West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

24 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 108; (2014) 12 SCC 321; AIR 2014 SC 3701; 2014 CRLJ 4388; 2014 (2) JCC 1112; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1429; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 691; 2014 (1) OLR 932; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 751; 2014(2) SCALE 690; [2014] 3 S.C.R. 90; 2015 (2) WLN(SC) 116

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Acquittal, Interference by HC, Legality

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Murder - Acquittal - Conviction - Interference by HC - Legality - Appellants accused allegedly abducted and murdered deceased - Trial Court held that neither charge of abduction nor charge of murder was proved against accused, therefore they were acquitted - Aggrieved by accused acquittal State respondent filed appeal before HC - Appeal was allowed by HC and convicted and sentenced accused - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC ought not to have interfered in appeal with acquittal of accused by Trial Court -

Held, HC did not take into consideration view of Trial Court, based on evidence on record, HC did not seem to have looked at evidence from point of view of accused who had already secured acquittal - Merely because HC disagreed (without giving reasons why it did so) with reasonable and possible view of Trial Court on completely independent analysis of evidence on record, was not sound basis to set aside order of acquittal given by Trial Court - Fact arrived at or every reason given by Trial Court must be dealt with and all that it meant was that decision of Trial Court should not be ignored or treated as non-existent - Case demonstrated that first link in chain of circumstances was missing - It was only if this first link was established that subsequent links might be formed on basis of last seen theory - However, HC overlooked missing link as it were and directly applied last seen theory - Sc opined that this was rather unsatisfactory way of dealing with appeal - There was really no occasion for HC to have overturned view of Trial Court which was not only reasonable view but probable view of events - HC did not take into consideration view of Trial Court based on evidence on record, that it was doubtful if five persons boarded boat to cross river as alleged by prosecution - Hence, view taken by Trial Court was reasonable and probable view on facts of case - There was no occasion for HC to set aside acquittal accused - Accordingly, their conviction and sentence handed down by High Court was set aside - Appeal allowed.
