Collector, Distt. Gwalior And Another v Cine Exhibitors P. Ltd. And Another

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 504; (2012) 4 SCC 441; 2012 (1) JabLJ 364; JT 2012 (1) SC 307; 2012 (4) MahLJ 556; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 932

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Permanent Lease, Permanent Leases, Madhya Pradesh Nagar Tatha Gramin Adhiniyam, 1973, M.P. Town Improvement Trust Act, 1960

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Land & Property - Administrative - Madhya Pradesh Town Improvement Trust Act, 1960, s. 31 - Madhya Pradesh Nagar Tatha Gramin Adhiniyam, 1973, ss. 38 and 87 - Govt. land - Lease rights - Dispossession - Challenged - Respondent no. 1(Company) was awarded with a contract to construct a cinema hall - Respondent and appellant (Development authority) entered into lease deed - After completion of construction, respondent commenced business - However, director's respondent resolved to surrender licence and after closure of cinema hall, appellant terminated lease and directed for handing over of possession of land in question - Aggrieved respondent filed a petition before HC - Single Judge dismissed petition - Aggrieved respondent filed an appeal before DB - DB allowed appeal holding that there was no commission of breach of express conditions of lease and no notice of termination of lease was served - Hence, instant appeals - Appellant (State) contended that it had not transferred land in favour of Development authority and development authority had no authority to deal with land in any manner whatsoever - Whether respondent was liable to be evicted from land - Held, lands belonging to State Govt. were dealt with in Revenue Book Circular and 'nazul' lands were specifically adverted to in Part IV of said Circular - It dealt with management and disposal of nazul lands within limits of Municipal Corporation, municipal towns and notified areas - Schematic concept read with language employed in 1960 Act and 1973 Act would clearly reveal that 'nazul' land, unless notified, did not automatically get vested in any authority or trust - State Govt., from time to time, was issuing notifications to the effect of vesting or transferring of nazul land to be part of improvement trust and giving advance possession to Town Improvement Trust - State Govt. issued notifications framing guidelines for distribution of Nazul plots - Unless affirmative steps were taken by Govt. by issuing a notification changing character of land and transferring it in favour of any authority, corporation or municipality, it maintain its own character, viz., 'nazul' land - In instant case, admittedly land was recorded as nazul land for Public Works Department - Nothing was brought on record that it had ever notified for transfer in favour of Development authority and authority never became owner of land or had authority to deal with the land - Thus, authority could not have put land to auction for any purpose whatsoever - Respondent could not assert any right or advance any claim to remain in possession and run cinema hall and that too after cancellation of licence, solely on the basis of a lease granted by its lessor, a statutory authority, who had no right on land, as ownership still remained with State Govt. - No legal sanctity could be attached to lease executed by authority in favour of respondent - Grant was fundamentally ultra vires - Govt. was at liberty to proceed against respondent for eviction - Impugned orders were set aside - Appeals allowed.
