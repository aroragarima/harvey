P. Palaniswami v Shri Ram Popular Service Private Limited and

Supreme Court of India

03 December 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

D.G. Palekar, V.R. Krishna Iyer

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 370; (1974) 1 SCC 197; AIR 1974 SC 1117; 1974 (2) MLJ(SC) 61; 1974 UJ 21

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, ss. 43-A, 47 - Stage carriage permit - Denial - Justifiability - Applications were invited for the grant of a stage carriage permit on a route - Transport Authority by an order Authority opined that respondent obtained the highest number of marks and respondents were entitled to the grant - Appellant and others aggrieved by that order, went in appeal to Appellate Tribunal - By a process of elimination the only important contestors before the Tribunal were the appellant and respondents - Tribunal reversed the order of Transport Authority and made the grant in favour of appellant - Aggrieved respondents filed petition before HC, which were dismissed - On appeal, DB allowed appeal, the grant was quashed and matter was sent down for re-hearing by Tribunal - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, Tribunal noticed that appellant was entitled to 1/4 mark more than what was given to the appellant by the Transport Authority. But this addition did not make any difference because even with this addition to the appellant's marks the respondents' score would be still higher. It also considered the period of experience of the appellant, in the line and noticed that he had 2 or 3 permits on shorter routes. All these were matters which the Tribunal was entitled to take into consideration u/s. 47 and merely because they also find a place in the GO it would not be enough to show that the GO was decisive in the final order passed by the Tribunal. When there is a GO in existence and parties applying for permits come to know that the authorities under the Act, were disposing of their applications for permits in accordance with the GO, matters not referred to in the GO but which may be very germane for consideration u/s. 47 of the Act get automatically excluded during the hearings. The GO, instead of s. 47 of the Act, becomes the last word on the subject. That is the real vice of such Govt. instructions. The authorities feel bound by these instructions and the parties before them feel equally bound by them. They, naturally exclude from the controversy other matters which though relevant u/s. 47 do not find a place in the GO. Appeal dismissed.
