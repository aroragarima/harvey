Rabindra Kumar Pal @ Dara Singh v Republic of India

Supreme Court of India

21 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 53; (2011) 2 SCC 490; AIR 2011 SC 1436; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 673; JT 2011 (1) SC 350; 2011 (2) MLJ(Crl) 236; 2011 (1) OLR 818; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 641; 2011(1) SCALE 615; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 929

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 164 - Triple murder of Australian Christian Missionary and two minor sons - Conviction - Challenged - Deceased and his two sons (Christian Missionary from Australia) working among the tribal people were burnt to death - Charge sheet was filed by the CBI against 14 accused persons - Sessions Judge convicted all the accused and sentenced and death sentence was passed against appellant and others were awarded sentence of life imprisonment - On appeal, HC held that the witnesses were not trustworthy and no credence should be given to their statements and confessional statements were procured by the investigating agency under threat and coercion - HC, by the impugned judgment, modified the death sentence awarded to appellant into life imprisonment and confirmed the life imprisonment imposed on A3 and acquitted all the other accused persons - Present appeals - (A) Evidence of eye-witnesses - Creditability - Whether statements of eye-witnesses were contradictory to each other on all material points? - Held, evidence of witness given in the court as to the identification may be accepted only if he identified the same persons in a previously held TIP in jail - Further, absence of TIP may not be fatal to the prosecution - In the present case, in the statements most of the witnesses recorded by various IOs, particularly, the local police and State CID ,these eye witnesses except few claim to have identified any of the miscreants involved in the incident - Also, as rightly held by the HC, for a number of days, many of these eye-witnesses never came forward before the IOs and the police personnel visiting the village from time to time claiming that they had seen the occurrence - In these circumstances, no importance need to be attached on the testimony of these eye-witnesses about their identification of the accused persons except appellant and A3 before the Trial Court for the first time without corroboration by previous TIP held by the Magistrate in accordance with the procedure established - (B) Confessional statements of various accused persons - Whether confessions of accused persons u/s. 164 of the CrPC cannot be considered to be voluntary on account of the fact that all the co-accused persons were produced before the Magistrate from the police custody and were remanded back to police custody? - Held, s. 164 of CrPC requires strict and faithful compliance of sub-sections 2 to 4 of s. 164, the failure to observe safeguards not only impairs evidentiary value of confession but cast a doubt on nature and voluntariness of confession on which no reliance can be placed - In the present case, analysis of evidence of Judicial Magistrates who recorded the confessional statements of some of the accused shows that many of the confessional statements were recorded immediately after production of the maker after long CBI custody and in some cases after such statements were made and recorded by the Judicial Magistrate, the maker was remanded to police custody - Procedure provided u/s. 164 of CrPC was not fully adhered to by them - (C) Whether the conviction and sentence of life imprisonment imposed on appellant and A3 sustainable? - Held, yes - Witnesses have specified the role of appellant and A3 - (D) Sentence - Held, on conviction u/s. 302 IPC, the normal rule is to award punishment of life imprisonment and the punishment of death should be resorted to only for the rarest of rare cases - Question whether a case falls within the rarest of rare case or not, has to be examined with reference to the facts and circumstances of each case and the Court has to take note of the aggravating as well as mitigating circumstances and conclude whether there was something uncommon about the crime which renders the sentence of imprisonment for life inadequate and calls for death sentence - In the present case, all these aspects were correctly appreciated by the HC and death sentence was rightly modified to life imprisonment - (E) Appeals against acquittal - Held, presumption of innocence is a fundamental principle of criminal jurisprudence - Further, presumption of innocence is further reinforced, reaffirmed and strengthened by the judgment in his favour - In the absence of definite assertion from the prosecution side, about their specific role and involvement, as rightly observed by the HC, it was not safe to convict them - Reasoning and conclusion of the HC insofar as the order relating to acquittal of certain accused persons were correct - Appeals disposed of.
