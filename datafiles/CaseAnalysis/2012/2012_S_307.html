M. Sarvana @ K. D. Saravana v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

24 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 410; (2012) 7 SCC 636; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 2737; 2012 CRLJ 3877; 2012 (3) JCC 1880; 2012 (4) KarLJ 598; 2012 (3) MLJ(Crl) 786; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 399; [2012] 6 S.C.R. 592

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Endorsement of duty - Dying declaration - Voluntarily - Head Constable PW2, received intimation in night from doctor on duty at Hospital stating that badly injured person had been admitted - After receiving this information, PW2 proceeded to Hospital and approached duty doctor - Police officer found deceased in sound state of mind and duty doctor duly endorsed regarding fitness of deceased to make statement - Head Constable recorded statement of deceased and same was exhibited as Ex.P2 - When PW2 was examined as witness in HC, he identified MLC report, Ex.P3 and also identified endorsement of duty doctor on said dying declaration regarding fitness of injured as Ex.P2 (b) - Fast Track (Sessions) Judge-III passed ordered of conviction and sentence - Appeal before HC against ordered of Fast Track (Sessions) Judge-III - HC confirmed ordered of conviction and sentence - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether admissibility and evidentiary value of dying declaration made by deceased was truthful description of events - Held, dying declaration was last statement made by person at stage when he in serious apprehension of his death and expects no chances of his survival - At such time, it was expected that person will speak truth and only truth - However, in such situations Courts attached intrinsic value of truthfulness to such statement - Once such statement was made voluntarily, it was reliable and was not attempt by deceased to cover up truth or falsely implicate person, then Courts could safely rely on such dying declaration and it could form basis of conviction - Therefore, where version given by deceased as dying declaration was supported and corroborated by other prosecution evidence, there was no reason for courts to doubt truthfulness of such dying declaration - Though, dying declaration was made after due certification of fitness by doctor and was recorded by police officer in discharge of his normal functions - Thus, statement was made by deceased voluntarily and was truthful description of events and this version was fully supported by PW3, witness who had accompanied deceased at all relevant times, right from inflicting of injury till time of his death - Hence, complete chain of events, pointing unexceptionally towards guilt of appellant was established by prosecution thereby proving case of prosecution beyond any reasonable doubt - Appeal dismissed.
