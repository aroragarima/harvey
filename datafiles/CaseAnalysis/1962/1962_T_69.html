Â Raja Jagannath Baksh Singh v State of Uttar Pradesh and Another

Supreme Court of India

04 April 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, K.C. Das Gupta, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar, S.B. Majmudar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 407; AIR 1962 SC 1563; 1962 ALJ 799; [1962] 46 ITR 169; [1963] 1 S.C.R. 220

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Uttar Pradesh Agricultural Income Tax Act, 1948 - Uttar Pradesh Large Land Holdings Tax Act, 1957 - Constitution Of India, 1950 - (A) Whether Uttar Pradesh Legislature was competent to pass Act ? - Held, this contention raises question about construction of Entry 49 in List II of 7 th Schedule of Constitution - This Entry relates to taxes to lands and buildings - Argument is that 'Lands' in context does not include agricultural lands and so, Uttar Pradesh Legislature was not competent to levy tax - It would be out of place to put a narrow or restricted construction on words of wide amplitude in a Constitution - Fact that agricultural income having been specifically provided by Entry 46 cannot be deemed to be included in Entry 49, does not justify argument that word 'Lands' in latter Entry does not include agricultural lands - If agricultural lands are included in said Entry, validity of Act would be beyond challenge, as in substance and in fact, it imposes a tax on land holding and as such, is within competence of State Legislature - (B) Whether rates have been fixed in accordance with provisions of s. 5(1) of Act? - Further held, s. 5(1) has prescribed maximum limits of multiple which may be adopted and it has left to discretion of State Govt. to adopt such multiple for different districts or by reference to different classes of land as it may deem proper - Notification issued by State Govt. on 23-04-1958, shows that it has complied with provisions of s. 5(1) - In prescribing multiple, State Govt. has classified lands and has varied multiple accordingly - There can be no doubt that notification issued by State Govt. u/s. 5(1) has complied with statutory requirements prescribed therefore - (C) Whether said Act violates fundamental rights guaranteed by arts. 14, 19 and 31 and as such, is void? - Further held, if law is not valid because if offends against arts. 14 or 19 or some other fundamental right guaranteed by Part III, then imposition of tax levied by it cannot be said to meet requirements of art. 31(1) - It is clear that provisions of art. 31(2) cannot be invoked in impeaching validity of a taxing statute and so, we come back to position that a taxing law which does not offend against any of fundamental rights guaranteed by Part III, would justify imposition of a tax and would meet requirements of art. 31(1) - Therefore, in our opinion challenge to validity of Act on ground that if contravenes art. 31(1) is not well-founded - It is only big holders whose land holdings are subjected to tax by this Act - Even so, basis adopted for levying tax is ultimately rent payable for land or lands in question and taking basis of said rent, annual value of land is required to be determined by adopting a suitable multiple - Having laid down general policy in that behalf, Legislature naturally left adjustment of multiple to discretion of State Govt. - Therefore, we do not think that discretion left to State Govt. can be said to be unfettered or uncanalised so as to amount to an unreasonable restriction - Land holding is taxed on basis of its annual value and it is difficult to understand how Schedule can be successfully challenged as being inconsistent with arts. 14 and 19(1)(f) - Petition dismissed.
