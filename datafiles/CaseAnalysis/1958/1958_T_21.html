Â Sri Ram Ram Narain Medhi v State of Bombay

Supreme Court of India

18 November 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, K. Subba Rao, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 88; AIR 1959 SC 459; 1959 (61) BomLR 811; 1959 (2) MLJ(SC) 1; 1959 SCJ 679; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 489; [1959] Supp1 S.C.R. 489

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1879, Constitution (Fourth Amendment) Act, 1955, Bombay Prevention of Fragmentation and Consolidation of Holdings Act, 1947, Ajmer Tenancy And Land Records Act, 1950, Ajmer Tenancy and Land Records Act, Bombay Khoti Abolition Act, 1949, Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Amendment) Act, Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Amendment) Act, 1956, Constitution (Fifth Amendment) Act,1955

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution Of India (Fifth Amendment), 1955 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Bombay Industrial Disputes Act, 1938 - Ajmer Tenancy and Land Records Act - Bombay Hereditary Offices Act, 1874 - Bombay Khoti Abolition Act, 1949 - Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1879 - Bombay Land Revenue Code - Bombay Prevention of Fragmentation and Consolidation of Holdings Act, 1947 - Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Amendment) Act - Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Amendment) Act, 1956 - Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act, 1948 - Constitution Of India (Fourth Amendment) , 1955 - Constitution Of India (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Constitution Of India, 1950, 31-A - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - (A) Whether Bombay Tenancy of 1956 was protected by art. 31-A of Constitution? - Held, if definition of expression 'estate' in context of Code is thus clear and unambiguous as comprising both alienated and unalienated types of lands, there is no reason why a narrower construction as suggested by petitioners should be put upon expression 'estate' - Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1879, was an existing law relating to land tenures in force in Bombay at time when Constitution (Fourth Amendment) Act, 1955, was passed and art. 31 A in its amended form was introduced therein and expression estate had a meaning given to it u/s. 2(10) there, viz,any interest in land which comprised within its scope alienated as well as unalienated lands and covered holdings of occupants within meaning thereof - There is an extinguishment or in any event a modification of landlord's right in estate well within meaning of those words as used in art. 31 A(1)(a) - With advent of Constitution provisions contained in 1948 Act required to be tested on touch-stone of fundamental rights enshrined in Part III thereof and when Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951, was passed introducing arts. 31 A and 31 B in Constitution, care was taken to specify 1948 Act in Ninth Schedule so as to make it immune from attack on score of any provision thereof being violative of fundamental rights enacted in Part III of Constitution - 1948 Act was second item in that schedule and was expressly saved from any attack against constitutionality thereof by express terms of art. 31 B - Impugned Act is covered by art. 31 A and is protected from attack against its constitutionality on score of its having violated fundamental rights, enshrined in arts. 14, 19 and 31 of Constitution - Further, impugned Act being within legislative competence of State Legislature no question as to its being a piece of colourable legislation can arise - (B) Whether s. 7 of impugned Act is bad by reason of excessive delegation of legislative power? - Further held, legislature applied its mind to question of method and manner of payment of compensation - It settled its policy and broad principles - It gave State Govt. power to determine matters of detail after having settled vital matters of policy - It cannot be said in this case that any essential legislative power has been delegated to executive or that legislature did not discharge trust which Constitution had reposed in it - Petitions dismissed.
