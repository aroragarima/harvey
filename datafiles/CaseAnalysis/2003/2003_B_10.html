Dwarka Prasad Agarwal (D) By Lrs. And Anr. v B.D. Agarwal And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

07 July 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, AR. Lakshmanan

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 492; (2003) 6 SCC 230; AIR 2003 SC 2686; 2003 (5) ALD(SC) 14; 2003 (4) AWC 2982; [2003] 117 Comp Cas 1; JT 2003 (6) SC 398; 2003 (4) RCR(Civil) 258; 2003(5) SCALE 138; 2003 (4) SLT 378; [2003] Supp1 S.C.R. 336; 2003 (4) Supreme 513

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution; Media & Entertainment; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Press and Registration of Books Act, 1867 - ss. 6, 8B, 19B - Indian Registration Act, 1908 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - O. 23, r. 3, s. 141 - Constitution of India, 1950 - arts. 14, 19, 32, 225, 226 - European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms 1950 - art. 6(1) - Indian Contract Act, 1872 - Whether settlement of a private dispute between the parties to a writ proceeding is permissible in law - Held, the High Court derives its jurisdiction in terms of art. 226, Constitution of India, if an occasion arises therefore, to make judicial review of the order passed by a statutory authority. It is beyond any cavil that no writ can be issued if the disputes involve private law character. The writ court has also no jurisdiction to determine an issue on private dispute over a property or right under a partnership. While purporting to record a compromise, the writ court cannot enlarge its jurisdiction by directing that the suits pending in different courts filed or different causes of action would also stand compromised. By reason thereof the writ court would be entrenching upon the jurisdiction of the civil court indirectly which it could not do directly - Further held, the High Court did not have any jurisdiction to record the compromise for the reasons stated hereinbefore and in particular as no writ was required to be issued having regard to the fact that public law remedy could not have been resorted to, the impugned orders must be held to be illegal and without jurisdiction and are liable to be set aside.
