Yazdani International Private Limited v Auroglobal Comtrade Private Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

17 December 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 854; (2014) 2 SCC 657; JT 2014 (2) SC 72; 2014 (1) MLJ 879; 2013(15) SCALE 410

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, Indian Ports Act, 1908 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Allotment of plots - Renewal - Licence fee payable - Liability - Cancellation of allotment - Violation of substantive rights - Legality - Iron ore plots were allotted on payment of normal licence fees as per Port Scale of Rates and plot holders were required to pay the said fee - Respondent/Board invited applications for allotment of 20 manual iron ore plots - AG was the successful bidder for the plots - Board allotted plots to AG - After securing allotment AG about a year filed writ petition and challenged the act of demanding/imposing of licence fee on renewal - Along with the said petition an application was filed - Said application was allowed and HC opined that Board did not follow a uniform and consistent procedure in making allotment of various plots - Pursuant to the said order of HC, notices were issued by the Board that allotment order made earlier was cancelled and called upon the allottee to hand over vacant possession of the plot - Hence instant appeals - Whether SC was required to set aside the order under appeal and also the consequential notices issued by respondent/Board to the various appellants on the ground that the order under appeal was made in breach of the rule of audi alteram partem - Held, order under appeal was required to be set aside on the simple ground that the same was in breach of principles of natural justice - Most of the licences in favour of appellants came to be granted pursuant to a process of either an auction or tender, those allotments, in SC's view, could not be said to be inconsistent with the principles of law laid down by SC in Centre For Public Interest Litigation And Others, Dr. Subramanian Swamy v. Union Of India And Others, 2012 Indlaw SC 29, in the absence of any other circumstance vitiating the allotment - Insofar as the allotment of plots made on application (prior to 2005), the respondent came out with a clear explanation that there was hardly any competition at that point of time for the allotment of plots - Therefore, SC did not see any reason to find any fault with such allotment on the ground that the allotment was made without following the procedure of auction or tender - More particularly, in the absence of any dispute regarding the correctness of the assertion of the Board that there was hardly any demand at that point of time for allotment of plots - Opinion of HC that a renewal of the licence in dispute without following the procedure of auction was inconsistent with the principles laid down in the 2G case 2012 Indlaw SC 29, as an absolute proposition of law could be examined in appropriate case - Such a scrutiny was not required for the present as the Board did not propose to renew the licences - Appeals dimissed.

(B) Whether SC would be justified in law to decline granting relief in exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 136 of the Constitution either on the ground that appellants have no indefeasible right of renewal or on the ground that irrespective of the rights of the appellants, the purpose sought to be achieved by the Board in proposing to resume the disputed lands is a public purpose which should override the interest of any of appellants - Held, jurisdiction u/art. 136 of Constitution was discretionary - It was settled by catena of decisions of SC that the jurisdiction u/art. 136 of Constitution was purely discretionary only to be exercised in order to ensure that injustice was not perpetuated - One of the important attributes of discretionary jurisdiction was that such jurisdiction was not exercised where it was likely to be a futile exercise i.e. where the relief might be of no use to the applicant because it would still be open to the competent authority to achieve the result sought to be achieved by the disputed action leaving the defeat i.e. by following the proper procedure etc. - Though the statement of principle were made in the context of the jurisdiction of the Court to issue mandamus, the principle applies proprio vigore to the discretionary jurisdiction u/art. 136 of Constitution - Such being the principle of law, even if instant appeals were to be allowed, on the ground that the order under challenge was made in breach of the rule of audi alteram partem, nothing in law prevent the Board from calling upon appellants to desist from use of the land in dispute - Such a course of action was the declared intention of the Board - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Whether SC would be justified in declining to interfere with the order under appeal and deny relief to appellants - Held, Board desired to utilise the parcel of land in dispute for a purpose which was authorised by law and serves better the larger interests of nation - Including the interests of the exporters and importers such as appellants - Setting aside the order under appeal on the ground of breach of natural justice would be a futile exercise as explained earlier - Board/licensor could always terminate/revoke the licences - Proposed revocation of licences would not amount to an irrational or arbitrary decision, rejecting the new plea of the Board or any ground would only lead to protracted litigation consuming considerable time and delay in execution of the project, by the Board - Such a delay would not subserve public interest - SC should not contribute to such delay on the ground of some perceived procedural irregularity, particularly when appellants have no substantive right - Appellants were given a wholesome opportunity by SC to establish their legal right, to prevent the Board from terminating their licences - Interfering with the notices of termination of licences in each one of the cases on the ground that the notices were initially based on a decision of the HC to which appellants were not parties, would not be in the larger public interest - Appellants failed to place before SC any material to establish that the decision of the Board to terminate their licences was otherwise violative of any of the substantive right - Appeals dismissed.
