Narayan Dutt and another v State of Punjab and another

Supreme Court of India

24 February 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 125; (2011) 4 SCC 353; AIR 2011 SC 1216; 2011 (3) AWC 2953; JT 2011 (2) SC 532; 2011 (4) MLJ(SC) 861; 2011 (2) RCR(Civil) 209; 2011 (2) RCR(Criminal) 140; 2011(2) SCALE 712; [2011] 4 S.C.R. 983

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 161 - Grant of pardon by Governor - Additional Sessions Judge convicted accused persons - All the accused appealed before the HC - During the pendency of the appeals, three accused persons 'N', 'M' and 'P' also filed petitions u/art. 161 of the Constitution before the Governor of Punjab - Governor of Punjab granted pardon to them and they were directed to be released - Writ petition was filed thereagainst was allowed - HC set aside the order of pardon of the Governor of Punjab - HC acquitted 'N', 'M' and 'P' - However, the conviction and sentence of other accused persons was upheld by the HC - Instant appeals - (A) Whether the power u/art. 161 was subject to judicial review? - Held, art. 161 of the Constitution conferred on the Governor of a State the right to grant pardons, remissions, reprieves or commute the sentence of any person convicted of any offence against any law relating to a matter to which the executive power of the State extends - There was limited scope of judicial review on the exercise of power by the Governor u/art. 161 of the Constitution - (B) Whether the Governor had rightly exercised his power to pardon u/art. 161 of the Constitution? - Held, before the power of the Governor u/art. 161 of the Constitution was invoked by any person, the condition precedent was that such person or persons must be convicted of any offence against any law and would be subjected to undergo a sentence - Therefore, an omission of any reference to an order of conviction or sentence in the Governor's order in respect of the accused was of no consequence - In the instant case, before the Governor could pass the order of pardon, the accused persons filed appeals against the order of conviction and sentence and the same were pending before the HC - This was a relevant fact for the Governor to take into consideration before granting his power of pardon - However, it was not considered - Further, a Governor's power of granting pardon u/art. 161 of the Constitution being an exercise of executive function, was independent of the Court's power to pronounce on the innocence or guilt of the accused - The instant order of the Governor, by pronouncing upon the innocence of the accused, was therefore, exceeded the permissible constitutional limits u/art. 161 of the Constitution - Order of the Governor set aside the order - Matter remanded to the Governor for re-consideration - Appeals disposed of.
