V. Sriharan @ Murugan and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

18 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Shiva Kirti Singh, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 93; (2014) 4 SCC 242; AIR 2014 SC 1368; 2014 CRLJ 1681; 2014 (2) CTC 214; JT 2014 (3) SC 112; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1645; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 617; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 990; 2014(2) SCALE 505; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 1093

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Constitution - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161, 139A - Mercy petitions - Delay in disposal - Commutation of death sentence - Petitioner death-row convicts filed writ petition before HC seeking commutation of their death sentence to life imprisonment - Matter transferred to SC u/art. 139A of Constitution - Petitioners contended that there was an inordinate delay in deciding their mercy petitions u/art. 72/161 of Constitution and hence the sentence was unconstitutional - Whether nature of the delay caused was inordinate -

Held, petitioners mercy petitions were rejected by Governor on 25-4-2000 - After an unreasonable delay of 5 years 1 month, Ministry of Home Affairs submitted mercy petitions to President on 21-6-2005 - Ministry recalled mercy petitions from Office of President after a delay of 5 years and 8 months - President rejected mercy petitions on 12-8-2011, after a delay of more than 11 years - Respondent authority could not explain delay - Hence, delay ensued in the petitions were inordinate and unreasonable and the same was not caused at the instance of petitioners - Thus, unreasonable delay caused qualified as supervening circumstance, which warranted commutation of death sentence into life imprisonment as stipulated in Shatrughan Chauhan and another v. Union of India and others, 2014 Indlaw SC 34 - Petitioners death sentence commuted into life imprisonment - Petitions allowed.

(B) Constitution - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Delay in disposal of mercy petitions - Plea of commutation of death sentence - Proof of sufferings - Requirement - Whether SC in Shatrughan Chauhan's case laid down for actually proving dehumanizing effect on the accused or mere unreasonable and inordinate delay warranted commutation of death sentence to life -

Held, regardless of independent of the suffering it causes, delay made the process of execution of death sentence unfair, unreasonable, arbitrary and capricious - Delay violated procedural due process guaranteed u/art. 21 of the Constitution and dehumanizing effect was presumed in such cases - Argument that petitioners were under obligation to produce evidence of their sufferings and harm caused to them on account of prolonged delay was unknown to law and would be a misinterpretation of Shatrughan Chauhan's case - Such a prerequisite would render fundamental rights guaranteed under Part III of the Constitution beyond the reach of death row convicts and would make them nugatory and inaccessible for all intent and purposes - There was no requirement in Indian law as well as in International judgments for death-row convict to prove actual harm occasioned by the delay - There was no obligation on the convict to demonstrate specific ill effects of suffering and agony on his mind and body as a prerequisite for commutation of death sentence - Petitioners extensively pleaded their sufferings in petitions as well as in reminded letters to President - Petitions allowed.

(C) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161 - Mercy petition - Order of Executive - Challenge to SC - Nature of - Whether instant petitions would amount to review the order passed u/art. 72/161 of the Constitution -

Held, no - Relief sought for petitions was not per se review the order passed u/art. 72/161 of the Constitution on merits but on the ground of violation of fundamental rights guaranteed under Constitution to all citizens including the death-row convicts - Petitions allowed.
