N. R. GHOSE alias NIKHIL RANJAN GHOSE v STATE of WEST BENGAL

Supreme Court of India

27 October 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

J.L. Kapur, Syed Jaffer Imam, A.K. Sarkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 190; AIR 1960 SC 239; 1960 CRLJ 289; 1960 SCJ 532; [1960] 2 S.C.R. 58

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, Prevention of Corruption Act

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Penal Code, 1898, ss. 403, 409, 120-B and 439 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, s. 5(2) and 4(1) - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 226 and 227 - West Bengal Criminal Law Amendment (Special Courts) Amending Act, 1952 - West Bengal Criminal Law Amendment (Special Courts) Act, 1949 - Complaint instituted against Appellant and S K Bose- Appellant acquitted but S K Bose held guilty - In case of J K Gupta v State of West Bengal High Court held that s. 4(1) of West Bengal Criminal Law Amendment (Special Courts) Act, 1949 was ultra vires - Appeals therefore allowed - West Bengal Criminal Law Amendment (Special Courts Amending) Ordinance 1952 came into force - A petition of complaint was filed against both appellant and S K Bose - Special Judge summoned Appellant who pleaded bar of s. 403, CrPC, 1898 - Special judge overruled this plea on ground of want of jurisdiction of previous Special Judge to try offences because s. 4(1) of Act had been declared ultra vires by High Court - Appellant moved HC - Fresh complaint was filed - HC quashed proceedings in HC of special judge - So long as these proceedings have not been disposed of in accordance with law, fresh proceedings cannot be instituted against petitioner - Whether s. 403 is applicable? - U/s. 403(1) CrPC, 1898 a person once tried and acquitted for an offence is not liable to be tried again for same offence or on same facts - For an order of acquittal to be binding it must be pronounced by a court of competent jurisdiction - If trial court was not a court of competent jurisdiction acquittal would be no more than a discharge; but if it was by a court of competent jurisdiction it is binding unless lawfully set aside - Plea of appellant effectively falls within s. 403 - First trial of appellant was before a court of competent jurisdiction and verdict of acquittal was not a nullity; its efficacy was not impaired by any binding order of HC; and at this stage when matter is properly before this court and proceedings are a continuation of proceedings it is not precluded from rectifying any error or defect in order of HC and giving effect to plea set up u/s. 403 - Trial being a lawful one which resulted in acquittal and which has never been set aside, another trial would place appellant in jeopardy a second time which would contravene s. 403 - Appeal allowed.
