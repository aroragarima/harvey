Tandon Brothers v State of W. B. and Others

Supreme Court of India

03 April 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

U.C. Banerjee, A.P. Misra

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20184; (2001) 5 SCC 664; AIR 2001 SC 1866; JT 2001 (4) SC 454; 2001(3) SCALE 197; [2001] 2 S.C.R. 960; 2001 (3) SLT 599; 2001 (3) Supreme 218

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Defence & Security Forces; Land & Property; Service

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution of India, 1950 - West Bengal Estate Acquisition Act, 1953, ss. 5 and 6(3) - Defence of India Act, 1962 - Requisitioning and Acquisition of Immovable Property Act, 1952 - Central Govt. Act, s. 7(1) - On 01-11-1962, notice for vesting of 1451.40 acres of land was given and consequently Govt. intended to allow appellant to retain 3544.acres of land - Tea Garden Advisory Committee meeting held on 09-07-1962 recommended for resumption of 1451.40 acres of land - On 21-06-1967, State Govt. issued a second notice for resumption of 1451.40 acres of land - This notice seems to be one issued after requisition of said 2542 acres of land by Military Authorities - Several notices were issued by Military Authorities proposing to acquire said land - Appellant agreed to said acquisition and wanted compensation for same to be paid to appellant - On 26 - 08 - 1969, third notice was issued by State Govt. for resumption - Till this date no final order was passed by State Govt. u/s. 6(3) of said Act - On 22-7-1973, Calcutta High Court quashed notice u/s. 6(3) of said Act - On 06-04-1973, a final order u/s. 6(3) of said Act was passed - (A) Whether notice D/- 26-08-1969 does not bear any provision of law under which notice was issued? - Held, notice itself records in supersession of Notice No 13942 - L Ref, D/- 01-11-1962 - Notice D/- 26-08-1969 cannot possibly be issued otherwise than as a notice to reopen issue - Hence, notice does not bear any provision of law under which notice was issued - (B) Whether Govt. is bound by its own notice under doctrine of estoppel? - Further held, s. 6(3) notice was served upon due compliance with all requirements, orders passed thereon, but after a lapse of about 9 years notice itself stands superseded - Since there are existing no justifiable reasons for change of quantum of land as mentioned in s. 6(3) notice, State Govt. cannot but be said to be bound by its own notice - Learned Single Judge of HC had directed disposal of s. 6(3) notice which admittedly referred to 1151 acres - But order passed was for 3059 acres - Doctrine of estoppel has its fullest play in contextual facts - (C) Whether actions of Govt. in issuing fresh notices and orders u/s. 6(3) after appellant had received notices u/s. 7(1) of Central Act were tainted with motive? - Further held, Governor of State issues a notification, obviously upon consideration of all relevant materials, that notification stands superseded by another Governor of State without however, ascribing any reason whatsoever - Govt. action must be based on utmost good faith, belief and ought to be supported with reason on basis of state of law - Action in present context appears prima facie to be tainted with motive and thus not sustainable - Appeals allowed.
