M/S. Bajaj Hindustan Limited v Sir Shadi Lal Enterprises Limited And Another .

Supreme Court of India

29 November 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Markandey Katju, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1027; (2011) 1 SCC 640; 2011(2) ALL MR 946; 2011 (5) AWC 4794; JT 2010 (13) SC 585; 2010(12) SCALE 654

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution

Keywords: Sugarcane (Control) Amendment Order, 2006

Summary: Administrative - Constitution - Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951, ss. 11 and 29B - Press Note No. 12 dt. 31.8.1998 and Notification No. SO 808(E) dt. 11.9.1998 - Licence for establishing a sugar industry - Union Government issued press note dt. 31.8.1998 and notification dt. 11.9.1998 u/s. 29B of 1951 Act vide which the Sugar Industry was de-licensed - HC quashed the said press note and notification due to which petitioners were debarred from establishing a sugar industry without obtaining a licence u/s. 11 of 1951 Act - Petitioners filed appeal against said order submitting that the effect of the impugned judgment and order quashing of the Notification dt. 11.9.1998 and the Press Note dt. 31.8.1998 is that the sugar industry in India has virtually been thrown back into the era of 'License Raj', nullifying the efforts of the Government of India to open up the economy to prospective investors - Petitioners also submitted that due to said order all the sugar industries established throughout India after 11.9.1998 have became illegal - Whether HC rightly quashed the press note no. 12 dt. 31.8.1998 and notification no. SO 808(E) dt. 11.9.1998 vide which the Sugar Industry was de-licensed? - Held, HC has probably overlooked that the Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha together constitute Parliament in India - Also, the 1951 Act does not require a notification u/s. 29B(1) to be approved by Parliament - Hence there was nothing irregular about the impugned notification - Moreover, judiciary should never interfere with administrative decisions - The State should not be hampered by the Court in such measures unless they are clearly illegal or unconstitutional - The State must therefore be left with wide latitude in devising ways and means of imposing fiscal regulatory measures, and the Court should not, unless compelled by the statute or by the Constitution, encroach into this field - Appeal allowed.
