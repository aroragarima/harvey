Mahmadhusen Abdulrahim Kalota Shaikh v Union Of India And Others

Supreme Court of India

21 October 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

K.G. Balakrishnan, Dalveer Bhandari, R.V. Raveendran

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2527; (2009) 2 SCC 1; (2009) 2 SCC 47; (2009) 1 SCC (Cr) 620; (2009) 1 SCC (Cr) 659; AIR 2008 SC (Supp) 734; 2009 (4) G.L.R. 3054; JT 2008 (13) SC 207; 2009 (2) MLJ(Crl) 482; 2008 (4) RCR(Criminal) 764; 2008(13) SCALE 398; [2008] 14 S.C.R. 889

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Deem, Constitution (Thirty-Ninth Amendment) Act, 1975, Prevention Of Terrorism (Repeal) Ordinance, 2004, Ajmer-Merwara (Extension of Laws) Act, 1947, Prevention of Terrorism (Repeal) Act, 2004, Election Laws (Amendment) Act, 1975, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (as amended by Act No.25 of 2005 & Act No.2 of 2006.), Prevention of Terrorism (Amendment) Act, 2003, Prevention of Terrorism (Repeal) Bill, 2004, U.P. Private Forests Act, Village Panchayat Raj Act, Prevention of Terrorism (Amendment) Ordinance, 2003, Prevention of Terrorism Ordinance, 2001, Prevention of Terrorism (Second) Ordinance, 2001

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Prevention of Terrorism (Repeal) Act, 2004 - Prevention of Terrorism Act, 2002 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Criminal offence - Constitutional validity - Accused were charged under the 2004 Act for persons killed in the Godhra train accident and the terrorist attack on the Akshardham temple - Appellants were relatives of victims of said terror attacks - Whether, s. 2(3) and 5 of 2004 Act were constitutionally valid - Held, the allegations of misuse of the 2002 Act were directed by some of the State Govts which was relevant because it showed that the Parliament wanted a mechanism by which it could reverse the State Govt.s' alleged misuse of the 2002 Act - Subject to the Central Review Committee's decision to the will of the State Govt.'s Public Prosecutor, was done when s. 321 of CrPC applied, against the very objective of POTA (Repeal) 2004 - It was reiterated that those aggrieved by the Review Committee's decision had recourse u/art. 226 of the Constitution and the basic principle of separation of power were not violated - Since, no provisions of the Constitution were violated by repealing the Act by the Parliament the 2004 Act was constitutionally valid - Appeals disposed of.
