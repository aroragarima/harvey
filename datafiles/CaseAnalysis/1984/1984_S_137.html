Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

17 July 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A. Varadarajan, S. Mukharji

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 432; (1984) 4 SCC 116; (1984) SCC (Cr) 487; AIR 1984 SC 1622; 1985 (1) Bom.C.R. 208; 1984 (2) Crimes 235; 1984 CRLJ 1738; 1984 CRLR 296; 1984(2) SCALE 445; [1985] 1 S.C.R. 88

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Probable Consequence

Summary: Criminal - Dowry death - Circumstantial evidence - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120-B, 201 and 302 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 154, 174, 313 and 342 - Indian Evidence Act, ss. 8 and 32 - Deceased was found dead after four months of her marriage - Trial Court and High Court convicted appellant and awarded death sentence - (A) Whether death of deceased was suicidal? - Court must carefully scan evidence and determine four important circumstances which alone can justify a conviction (1) there is a clear motive for an accused to administer poison to deceased, (2) that deceased died of poison said to have been administered, (3) that accused had poison in his possession, (4) that he had an opportunity to administer poison to deceased - In instant case, while two ingredients have been proved but two have not - Held, yes - Deceased was a sensitive and extremely impressionable woman - Her mental state was badly disturbed when her marriage with appellant did not work out - She committed suicide out of sheer desperation and frustration - circumstance of appellant having been last seen with deceased has not been proved conclusively so as to raise an irresistible inference that death was a case of blatant homicide - Prosecution failed to prove that death of deceased was caused by administration of poison - Conclusion may be drawn that appellant may have murdered her but when two views are reasonably possible, benefit must go to accused - From her letters, there was a clear tendency that she may commit suicide - (B) S. 32 of Evidence Act - Clear language of s. 32(1) of Evidence Act - S. 32 is an exception to rule of hearsay and makes admissible statement of a person who dies, whether death is a homicide or a suicide, provided statement relates to cause of death, or exhibits circumstances leading to death - Test of proximity cannot be too literally construed and practically reduced to a cut-and-dried formula of universal application so as to be confined in a strait-jacket - Distance of time would depend or vary with circumstances of each case - S. 32 does not speak of homicide alone but includes suicide also, hence all circumstances which may be relevant to prove a case of homicide would be equally relevant to prove a case of suicide - Where main evidence consists of statements and letters written by deceased which are directly connected with or related to her death and which reveal a tell-tale story, said statement would clearly fall within four corners of s. 32 and, therefore, admissible - Distance of time alone in such cases would not make statement irrelevant - (C) Circumstantial evidence - Panchsheel of proof of a case based on circumstantial evidence - Held, where various links in a chain are in themselves complete, then a false plea or a false defence may be called into aid only to lend assurance to Court - (1) circumstances from which conclusion of guilt is to be drawn fully, established (2) facts so established should be consistent only with hypothesis of guilt of accused, (3) circumstances should be of a conclusive nature and tendency, (4) they should exclude every possible hypothesis except one to be proved, and (5) there must be a chain of evidence so complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for conclusion consistent with innocence of accused and must show that in all human probability act must have been done by accused - Discussed Hanumant's decision 1952 Indlaw SC 89 - Prosecution has not fulfilled essential, requirements of a criminal case which rests purely on circumstantial evidence - Fit case in which HC should have given at least benefit of doubt to appellant - Appeal allowed.
