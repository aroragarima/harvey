C. K. Jaffer Sharief v State through CBI

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 418; (2013) 1 SCC 205; AIR 2013 SC 48; 2013 CRLJ 341; JT 2012 (11) SC 283; 2013 (1) MLJ(Crl) 90; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 187; 2012(11) SCALE 71; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 1079

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 13(1)(d) and 13(2) - Pecuniary advantage - Illegal means - Dropping of proceedings - Challenged - Appellant had dishonestly made the managing directors of RITES and IRCON to approve journeys of stenographers in the railway cell and one to London - Pecuniary loss to the Public Sector Undertakings was, therefore, caused by the wrongful acts of appellant - An FIR was lodged against appellant alleged commission of the offence u/s. 13(2) r/w s. 13(1)(d) - Sanction for prosecution, u/s. 19 of the Act was refused by the competent authority, in the final report it was mentioned that the proceedings against appellant be dropped - Trial Court declined to accept the closure report filed by the investigating agency - Aggrieved on appeal HC dismissed the appeal - Hence instant appeal - Whether proceedings against appellant could be dropped - Held, it had already been noticed that appellant besides working as the Minister of Railways was the Head of the two Public Sector Undertakings in question at the relevant time - While in London 4 persons had assisted appellant in performing certain tasks connected with the discharge of duties as a Minister - It was difficult to visualise as to how in the light of the above facts, demonstrated by the materials revealed in the course of investigation, appellant could be construed to have adopted corrupt or illegal means or to have abused his position as a public servant to obtain any valuable thing or pecuniary advantage either for himself or for any of the aforesaid four persons - If the statements of the witnesses examined it show that the aforesaid four persons had performed certain tasks to assist appellant in the discharge of his public duties, however insignificant such tasks may have been, no question of obtaining any pecuniary advantage by any corrupt or illegal means or by abuse of the position of appellant as a public servant could arise - As a Minister it was for appellant to decide on the number and identity of the officials and supporting staff who should accompany him to London if it was anticipated that he would be required to perform his official duties while in London - SC did not find any reason to allow prosecution to continue against appellant - Such continuance, would be an abuse of the process of Court and therefore it would be the plain duty of the Court to interdict the same - Impugned judgment and order of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
