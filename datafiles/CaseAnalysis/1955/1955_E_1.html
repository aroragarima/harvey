Sangram Singh v Election Tribunal Kotah and Another

Supreme Court of India

22 March 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

Vivian Bose, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 126; AIR 1955 SC 425; 1955 (2) MLJ(SC) 13; 1955 MWN 844; 1955 SCJ 431; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Election - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 226 - Representation of People Act, 1951, s. 105 - Pending matter before Election Tribunal - Interference by HC - Permissibility - 2nd respondent filed an election petition assailing the election of appellant - Since appellant did not appear before the Election Tribunal, Election Tribunal proceeded ex parte - Election Tribunal examined witnesses of 2nd respondent - Appellant's counsel appeared subsequently, however, Election Tribunal did not allow him to cross-examine 2nd respondent's witnesses - Appellant made an application for setting aside the ex parte order - Election Tribunal dismissed the application - Appellant challenged the Election Tribunal order before the HC - HC dismissed appellant's writ petition - Whether HCs and SC had the jurisdiction to interfere on an election petition pending before the Election Tribunal - Held, notwithstanding the provision in s. 105 of the Act that every order of an Election Tribunal made under the Act should be final and conclusive, the HC and the SC have unfettered jurisdiction to examine whether the tribunal, in the exercise of its undoubted jurisdiction, had acted legally or otherwise - Jurisdiction of HC and SC could not be taken away by a legislative device that purports to confer power on a tribunal to act illegally - Legality of an act or conclusion was something that existed outside and apart from the decision of an inferior tribunal - It was a part of the law of the land which could not be finally determined or altered by any tribunal of limited jurisdiction - HC and SC alone could determine what the law of the land was vis-a-vis all other Courts and tribunals and they alone could pronounce with authority and finality on what was legal and what was not - All that an inferior tribunal could do was to reach a tentative conclusion which was subject to review u/arts. 226 and 136 of the Constitution - Jurisdiction of the HCs u/art. 226, with that of the SC above them, remained its fullest extent despite s. 105 of the Act -Limitations on the exercise of, such jurisdiction could only be imposed by the Constitution - Powers of the HCs u/art. 226 of the Constitution were discretionary and, though no limits could be placed upon that discretion, it should be exercised along recognized lines and not arbitrarily - In the exercise of their jurisdiction u/art. 226, the HCs should not act as Courts of Appeal or revision to correct mere errors of law which did not occasion injustice in a broad and general sense -It was a sound exercise of discretion to bear in mind the policy of the legislature to had disputes about special rights, as in election cases, decided as speedily as may be - HCs should not therefore entertain petitions for prerogative writs lightly in those classes of case - Order accordingly.

(B) Election - Practice & Procedure - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 7 r. 9, O.17 r. 2, O. 9 r. 6(1)(a) - Representation of People Act, 1951 - Discretion of the Court - Exercise of - Whether the Election Tribunal was right in refusing to allow the appellant's counsel to appear and take part in the proceedings after passing the order of ex parte - Held, u/s. 90(2) of the Act the procedure for the trial of election petitions was to be, as near as may be, the same as in the trial of suits under CPC - Under CPC there was no such thing as 'an ex parte order for non-appearance' which precluded further appearance at an adjourned hearing until the order was set aside - If a party appeared at an adjourned hearing the court had a discretion (which should be exercised judicially) either to allow him to appear on such terms as it thinks fit, or to disallow further appearance; but if he was allowed to appear then, unless good cause was shown under O. 9 r. 7 for the earlier non-appearance the proceedings should continue from the stage at which the later appearance was entered and the party so appearing should not be relegated to the position he would have occupied if he had appeared at the earlier hearing or hearings - In exercising its discretion the court should see that justice was done to all concerned, including the witnesses r. 6 (1) (a) of O. 9 of the CPC was confined to the first hearing of the suit and did not apply, per se to subsequent hearings - O. 9, r. 7 gave a party a right to be relegated to the position he would have occupied if he had appeared at the earlier hearing or hearings if he showed good cause - It did not per se prevent further appearance when no good cause was shown - O. 17 r. 2 applied at the adjourned hearing and there, the Court was given a wide discretion to make such order as it thinks fit - A code of procedure was a body of law designed to facilitate justice and further its ends, and should not be treated as an enactment providing for punishments and penalties - Laws of procedure were grounded on the principle of natural justice which required that men should not be condemned unheard, that decisions should not be reached behind their backs, that proceedings that affect their lives and property should not continue in their absence and that they should not be precluded from participating in them - Subject to clearly defined exceptions the laws of procedure should be construed wherever reasonably possible, in the light of that principle - Court was invested with the widest possible discretion to see that justice was done to all concerned - No hard and fast rule could be laid down; and the court in the exercise of its judicial discretion would have, in a given case, to determine what consequences had to follow from non-appearance - An order awarding costs, or an adjournment, or the consideration of the written statement and the framing of the issues on the spot, could in some cases meet the ends of justice - In other cases, more drastic action may be called for - Court should in every case exercise the discretion given to it - Its hands were not tied by a so-called 'ex parte order', and, if it thought they were tied by r. 7 of O. 9 of the CPC, then it was not exercising the discretion which the law says it should - In the instant case, Election Tribunal did not exercise the discretion given to it by law because of a misapprehension that it had none - Hence, direction issued to the Election Tribunal to exercise its discretion and to proceed with the further hearing in accordance with law - Order accordingly.
