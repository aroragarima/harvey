Smt. Gracy v State of Kerala and Another

Supreme Court of India

15 February 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

J.S. Verma, L.M. Sharma, B.C. Ray

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 510; (1991) 2 SCC 1; (1991) SCC (Cr) 467; AIR 1991 SC 1090; AIR 1991 SCW 559; 1991 (1) Crimes 552; 1991 CRLR 248; 1991 CrLR(SC) 248; 1991 (44) DLT 1; 1991 (33) ECC 125; 1991 (54) E.L.T. 161; JT 1991 (1) SC 371; 1991(1) SCALE 211; [1991] 1 S.C.R. 421; 1991 (1) ShimLC 324

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1988, s.3 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.22(5) and 32 - Illegal detention - Denial of representation - Sustainability - Writ petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution of India was by the mother of the passed u/ss.3 and 9(f) r/w.10(2) of Act, by the Central Govt. directing detention of the detenu for a period of two years - Only argument advanced in support of this writ petition was infraction of art.22(5) of the Constitution of India - Whether there was any infraction of the guarantee u/art.22(5) of the Constitution as a result of Central Govt's omission to consider the detenu's representation independent of its consideration by the Advisory Board - Held, it being settled that the dual obligation of consideration of the detenu's representation by the Advisory Board and independently by the detaining authority flows from art.22(5) of Constitution when only one representation was made addressed to the detaining authority, there was no reason to hold that the detaining authority was relieved of this obligation merely because the representation was addressed to the Advisory Board instead of the detaining authority and submitted to the Advisory Board during pendency of the reference before it - It was difficult to spell out such an inference from the contents of art.22(5) of Constitution in support of the contention of Solicitor General - Contents of art.22(5) of Constituion as well as the nature of duty imposed thereby on the detaining authority support the view that so long as there was a representation made by the detenu against the order of detention, the dual obligation u/art.22(5) of Constitution arose irrespective of the fact whether the representation was addressed to the detaining authority or to the Advisory Board or to both - Mode of address was only a matter of form which cannot whittle down the requirement of the Constitutional mandate in art.22(5) of Constitution enacted as one of the safeguards provided to the detenu in case of preventive detention - SC was therefore, unable to accept the only argument advanced by Solicitor General to support the detention - It was not disputed that there was a breach by the Central Govt. of its duty u/art.22(5) of the Constitution of India to consider and decide the representation independently of the Advisory Board's opinion - Petition allowed.
