Laxminarayan and Another v Returning Officer and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 September 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Dwivedi, P. Jaganmohan Reddy

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 131; (1974) 3 SCC 425; AIR 1974 SC 66; [1974] 1 S.C.R. 822

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election

Summary: (A) Election - Constitution - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, r. 63 - Recount - Votes - Legality - Appellants filed an election petition challenging election of X to Lok Sabha from Parliamentary Constituency - HC dismissed petition - Hence instant appeal - Whether appellants application in HC for inspection of all votes was valid - Held, recounting had no effect on result of election - Further, order of Returning Officer directing recount was not in contravention of r. 63 of the Rules - Returning Officer was examined by appellants and he had stated that count and recount was done in accordance with prescribed procedure - He had also explained missing 14 votes on recount - Returning Officer was of opinion that discrepancy of 14 votes might be due to mistake in counting votes and making them into bundles of 50 each - In recount they recounted only some of those bundles and not all - Thus, SC accept finding of HC in instant issue - Appeal disposed of.

(B) Election - Constitution - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961 - Statement - Validity - Whether Y was an agent of X and that he issued statement with consent of X was true - Held, it was true that according to evidence on record Y seems to have addressed several meetings in support of X and that in some of those meetings X had also delivered speeches - However, such circumstance alone would not prove consent of X - SC agree with HC that statement was not proved to have been made with consent of X - SC agree with HC that appellants had failed to establish that particular corrupt practice - Appeal disposed of.

(C) Election - Constitution - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961 - Pamphlet - Evidence - Denial - Validity - Whether appellants allegation in petition that document C attached to petition was pamphlet which was denied by X was correct or not - Held, it was highly improbable that a candidate would publish an offending document and show expenses incurred on its printing in his return of election expenses - Appellants had examined several witnesses to prove distribution of document C during election - HC had disbelieved those witnesses and held that from their demeanour they appear to be tutored witnesses - However, their evidence was not pressed in service before SC on behalf of appellants - Appeal disposed of.

(D) Election - Constitution - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961 - Offending statements - Legality - Whether Y and Z made offending statements with consent of X - Held, HC had exhibited not only extracts from reported speeches of X but had also admitted full reports of his speeches - Appellants contended that full reports establish other instances of pleaded corrupt practice against X which was not accepted by HC as well as SC - X got no opportunity to deny them or to disprove them and he did not even cross- examine police witnesses with respect to those instances - He confined his cross-examination mainly to instances pleaded in petition - Taking notice of new instances would cause serious prejudice to him - Hence, SC held that HC had not overlooked any material feature in evidence nor could its view be said to be perverse - Appeal disposed of.

(E) Election - Constitution - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961 - Publication - Consent - Validity - Whether X and his followers distributed copies of weekly Gram Sewak which was published by W with consent of X - Held, W was working on his own initiative - Further, copies of Gram Sewak were sold and not distributed free - However, it was hardly probable that if X had given his consent to publication of Gram Sewak, it would have been sold and not distributed free - Thus, sale suggests want of consent of X - Hence, SC agree with HC that appellants had failed to prove publication of Gram Sewak with consent of X - Appeal disposed of.
