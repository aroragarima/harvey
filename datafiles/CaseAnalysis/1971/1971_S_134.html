Percy Rustam Basta v State Of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

16 March 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

C.A. Vaidyialingam, A.N. Ray

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 147; (1971) SCC (Cr) 345; (1971) 1 SCC 847; AIR 1971 SC 1087; 1971 CRLJ 933; [1971] S.C.R. 35; [1971] Supp S.C.R. 35

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Customs; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Smuggle of goods

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Customs - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 193 and 228 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 135, 108 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 24 - Smuggle of goods - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant accused allegedly committed offences punishable u/s. 120B IPC r/w s. 135 of the 1962 Act - Trial Court convicted appellant u/s. 120B IPC and u/s. 135 of the 1962 Act - Aggrieved, appellant preferred appeal before HC, which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether s. 24 of the 1872 Act was a bar to admissibility in evidence of statement given by appellant to Customs Officers on a summons issued to him u/s. 108 of the 1962 Act - Held, it was not accepted the contention of appellant that in circumstances mentioned any threat had proceeded from a person in authority to appellant, in consequence of which statement was given - S. 108 of the 1962 Act gives power to a Customs Officer of a gazetted rank to summon any person to give evidence in any inquiry in connection with smuggling of any goods - A person summoned u/s. 108 of the 1962 Act was bound to appear and state truth when giving evidence - If he did not answer he should render himself liable to be prosecuted u/s. 228 IPC - Appellant was told that law requires him to tell truth and if he did not tell the truth, lie might be prosecuted u/s. 193 IPC for giving false evidence - It did not constitute a threat u/s. 24 of the 1872 Act - Appellant had no basis for supposing that by making statement he should gain any advantage or avoid any evil with reference to proceedings in respect of which an inquiry was conducted by Customs Officers - Therefore, even on that ground also s. 24 of the 1872 Act had no application - However, by mere fact that Customs Officer, who recorded statement, explained provisions of s. 193 IPC and informed appellant that he was bound to tell truth and that he was liable to be prosecuted if he made a false statement, there was no threat given to appellant - S. 24 of the 1872 Act had no application and statement was properly admitted in evidence in trial of appellant - Both Courts had found that there was also independent evidence to corroborate truth of statements - Appeal dismissed.
