Narayanan Rajendran And Another v Lekshmy Sarojini And Others

Supreme Court of India

12 February 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, H.S. Bedi

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 2104; (2009) 5 SCC 264; 2009 (4) AWC 3933; 2009 (2) JhrCR(SC) 196; JT 2009 (4) SC 62; 2009 (2) RCR(Civil) 286; 2009(2) SCALE 414; [2009] 2 S.C.R. 71

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Keywords: Civil Procedure Code, 1882

Summary: (A) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 100 - Second appeal - Scope of interference by HC - Held, HC would have jurisdiction to interfere u/s. 100 of CPC only in a case where substantial questions of law are involved and those questions have been clearly formulated in the memorandum of appeal - At the time of admission of the second appeal, it is the bounden duty and obligation of the HC to formulate substantial questions of law and then only the HC is permitted to proceed with the case to decide those questions of law - Further, scope of interference by the High Court in second appeal under section 100 CPC after 1976 Amendment is strictly confined to cases involving substantial questions of law - HC would not be justified in dealing with any second appeal without first formulating substantial question of law - Appeal allowed.

(B) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 100 - Rationale behind permitting second appeal on question of law - Held, rationale behind allowing a second appeal on a question of law is, that there ought to be some tribunal having jurisdiction that will enable it to maintain, and, where necessary, re-establish, uniformity throughout the State on important legal issues, so that within the area of the State, the law, in so far as it is not enacted law, should be laid down, or capable of being laid down, by one court whose rulings will be binding on all courts, tribunals and authorities within the area over which it has jurisdiction - Appeal allowed.

(C) Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 100 - Second appeal - Scope of interference by HC - Plaintiffs filed a suit contending that the property in question is a subtarwad property of defendant no.1 and, therefore, the members of the subtarwad including the plaintiffs and defendants no.1 to 3 are entitled to 1/11 share each for each member of the subtarwad under the customary law - Both the Trial Court as well as the first appellate court concurrently found that defendant no.1 has exclusive right and possession over the suit property and that the plaintiffs have failed to prove that the suit property is subtarwad property or that the parties are governed by the customary marumakkathayam law - On second appeal, HC on re-appreciation of the evidence in the case reversed the concurrent findings of the courts below and held that the suit property is the subtarwad property and the parties are governed by ezhava marumakkathayam customary law - Hence, present appeal - Held, courts below comprehensively re-evaluated and re-examined the entire evidence on record and came to the conclusion - Therefore, there is no reason to interfere with the reasoning and findings of the lower court - Entire basis of the finding of the HC was without any basis and unsustainable in law - Impugned judgment of the HC was wholly unsustainable in law and accordingly set aside and consequently the findings of the trial court was upheld by the first appellate court restored - Appeal allowed.
