Mohmed Amin @ Amin Choteli Rahim Miyan Shaikh and Another v C. B. I., through Its Director

Supreme Court of India

18 November 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, B.N. Agrawal

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2603; (2008) 15 SCC 49; (2009) 3 SCC (Cr) 693; AIR 2008 SC (Supp) 938; JT 2008 (12) SC 160; 2009 (1) MLJ(Crl) 562; 2009 (1) RCR(Criminal) 143; 2008(14) SCALE 240; [2008] 16 S.C.R. 155

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Terrorist And Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Amendment Act, 1985, Acquaintance, Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, 1987

Summary: (A) Criminal - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 15 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 r/w s. 120B - Bombay Police Act, s. 135 - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, 1987, r. 15 - Murder - Conviction - Appellants allegedly shot to death the deceased - Trial Court convicted appellants for murder on the basis of appellants' confession - Whether conviction on the basis of confession is sustainable - Held, concerned officers recorded their satisfaction that the appellants were making confessions voluntarily and that no threat or allurement was given to any of them and nobody had coerced them to make confession - Confessions were read over to the appellants who admitted that the recording contains full and true account of their statements and then signed it and the same were sent to the Magistrate for onward transmission to the Trial Court, therefore, there was total compliance of s. 15 of the Act and r. 15 of the Rules - Confessions of appellants cannot be held inadmissible on the premise that before recording of confessions they were in police custody and the statements were recorded by the officers supervising the investigation - When the appellants were produced before the concerned Magistrate, none of them gave out that he had not made confessional statement or that his signatures had been obtained on the blank paper or that he was made to sign on the prepared statement or that he had been subjected to torture, or any threat or allurement was given to him to make confession - While appellants were in jail, none of the confessing appellants made any application to the Trial Court that he wants to retract the confession - Therefore, appellants are guilty of offence u/s. 302 r/w s. 120B IPC and no independent corroboration is required for sustaining their conviction, hence Trial Court order is confirmed - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 15 - Confession recorded u/s. 15 - Effect of - Held, even if a person is acquitted of the charge under the Act, a confession recorded u/s. 15 of the Act can be used for convicting him for an offence under other enactments including Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 15 - Confession recorded u/s. 15 - Probative value of - Held, whether a confession recorded u/s. 15 requires corroboration depends on the facts of the given case - If the Court is convinced that the probative value of the confession is such that it does not require corroboration then the same can be used for convicting the maker and/or co-accused under the Act and/or other enactments without independent corroboration - Appeals dismissed.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 15 - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, 1987, r. 15 - Confession recorded u/s. 15 - Burden of proof - Held, if a person accused of committing an offence under the Act challenges his confession on the ground that it was not made voluntarily, then the initial burden is on the prosecution to prove that all requirements u/s. 15 of the Act and r. 15 of the Rules have been complied with - Once the nature of confession is proved by prosecution, the burden shifts on the accused person and it is for him to prove that the confession was not made voluntarily or that the same is not truthful and if he adduces evidence during the trial to substantiate his allegation that the confession was not voluntary then the Court has to carefully scrutinize the entire evidence and surrounding circumstances and determine whether or not the confession was voluntary - Appeals dismissed.

(E) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120B - Criminal conspiracy - Proof of - Held, for proving a charge of conspiracy, it is not necessary that all the conspirators know each and every details of the conspiracy so long as they are co-participators in the main object of conspiracy nor that all the conspirators should participate from the inception of conspiracy to its end - If there is unity of object or purpose, all participating at different stages of the crime will be guilty of conspiracy - Appeals dismissed.
