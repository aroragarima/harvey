Prakash v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

15 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 249; (2014) 12 SCC 133; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 2218; 2014 CRLJ 2503; JT 2014 (5) SC 239; 2014 (4) KarLJ 529; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 744; 2014(5) SCALE 83; [2014] 5 S.C.R. 242

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Identification of Prisoners Act, 1920, s. 5 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) allegedly murdered the deceased and was charge-sheeted for having murdered deceased and for having stolen her cash and ornaments - Trial Court by its judgment acquitted appellant - Acquittal was set aside in appeal by HC - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that the report of serologist was not put to him when he was examined u/s. 313 of CrPC and that HC dealt with that issue in a rather unsatisfactory manner - Whether impugned conviction of appellant was justified -

Held, given the facts of case, it would have been more appropriate for an identification parade to have been conducted, but its absence in instant case was not necessarily fatal, there being other reasons for not accepting the case set up by prosecution - However, absence of an identification parade certainly casts a doubt about appellant's presence at deceased's house on 5-11-1990 - It was very difficult to accept with certainty the case of prosecution that appellant alone was with deceased on the fateful night of 5-11-1990 - View took by Trial Court giving appellant the benefit of doubt was certainly a plausible view and in absence of any perversity in view taken, HC ought not to have upset the conclusion arrived at - There was a clear doubt whether appellant was with deceased; if he was, then it was at about 1.00 p.m on 5-11-1990; there was no evidence that appellant was with deceased thereafter and on the contrary there was evidence that some of her relatives (which might or might not include appellant) were with her at about 8.30 p.m - Assuming appellant's fingerprint was in fact obtained by IO (PW25), it was clearly not given voluntarily, but perhaps unwittingly and in what seems to be a deceitful manner - To avoid any suspicion regarding the genuineness of fingerprint so taken or resort to any subterfuge, the appropriate course of action for IO was to approach the Magistrate for necessary orders in accordance with s. 5 of the Act - Entire exercise of appellant's fingerprint identification was shrouded in mystery and Court could not give any credence to it - There was no fingerprint evidence worth it linking appellant to the murder of deceased - Court was not satisfied with the conclusion of HC that since the clothes of appellant were blood stained and the stains bore the same blood group as that of deceased, the circumstance could be used appellant - A serological comparison of the blood of deceased and appellant and the blood stains on his clothes was necessary and that was absent from the evidence of prosecution - In absence of any identification of the ornaments as belonging to deceased, HC could not have definitely concluded that they did belong to deceased - In any event, even assuming that ornaments belonged to deceased, at best, appellant would be guilty of having received stolen property but could certainly not be guilty of having murdered deceased - None of the circumstances relied upon by prosecution and accepted by HC point to the probability of appellant's guilt or involvement in the murder of deceased - Substantial issues raised in instant appeal, in which conviction was based on circumstantial evidence, primarily related to the presence of convict at the place and time of the murder of deceased, the analysis of fingerprint evidence recovered from the place of incident and recovery of blood stained clothes of the convict and ornaments of the deceased at his instance - On all issues, Court found in favour of the convict and conclude that that none of the circumstances that were found against appellant by HC and which have led to his conviction have been satisfactorily proved - Conviction should, thus, be set aside - Appeal allowed.
