Government of Andhra Pradesh v Thummala Krishna Rao and Another

Supreme Court of India

16 March 1982

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.V. Chandrachud, A. Varadarajan, Amarendra Nath Sen

Where Reported

1982 Indlaw SC 114; (1982) 2 SCC 134; AIR 1982 SC 1081; 1982(1) SCALE 180; [1982] 3 S.C.R. 500; 1982 UJ 259

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 6 and 7 - Andhra Pradesh Land Encroachment Act, 1905 - Eviction - Denied - Sustainability - Appellants wanted to evict respondents from three plot of lands which it had acquired for development of university - Whether the appellant had the power to evict the respondents summarily in exercise of the power conferred by Act - Held, view of DB of HC that the summary remedy of eviction provided for by s. 6 could not be resorted to unless the alleged encroachment was of 'a very recent origin', could not be stretched too far - That was also the view taken by Single Judge - It was not the duration, short or long, of encroachment that was conclusive of the question whether the summary remedy prescribed by the Act could be put into operation for evicting a person - What was relevant for the decision of that question was more the nature of the property on which the encroachment was alleged to had been committed and the consideration whether the claim of the occupant was bona fide - Facts which raised a bond fide dispute of title between appellant and the occupant should be adjudicated upon by the Ordinary courts of law - Appellant could not decide such questions unilaterally in its own favour and evict any person summarily on the basis of such decision - But duration of occupation was relevant in the sense that a person who was in occupation of a property openly for an appreciable length of time could be taken, prima facie, to have a bonafide claim to the property requiring an impartial adjudication according to the established procedure of law - Conspectus of facts in the instant case justified the view that the question as to the title to the three plots could not appropriately be decided in a summary inquiry contemplated by ss. 6 and 7 of the Act - Long possession of the respondents and their predecessors in title of these plots raised genuine dispute between them and the Govt. on the question of title, remembering especially that the property, admittedly, belonged originally to the family of Nawab from whom the respondents claim to have purchased it - Question as to whether the title to the property came to be vested in appellant as a result of acquisition and the further question whether the Nawab encroached upon that property thereafter and perfected his title by adverse possession should be decided in a properly constituted suit - Might be, that appellant might succeed in establishing its title to the property but, until that was done, the respondents could not be evicted summarily - Appeals dismissed.
