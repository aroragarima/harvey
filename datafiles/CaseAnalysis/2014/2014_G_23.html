Darius Rutton Kavasmaneck v Gharda Chemicals Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

28 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 737; [2015] 188 Comp Cas 291; 2014 (4) CompLJ 362; JT 2014 (12) SC 318; 2014(12) SCALE 403; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 1119

Case Digest

Subject: Capital Market; Corporate

Keywords: Shares, Companies (Amendment) Act, 2000, Prohibit invitation or acceptance

Summary: Capital - Corporate - Companies (Amendment) Act, 2000, ss. 43A(11), 43A(2A) - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 26, 27(3), 3(1)(iii), 43, 43(2A), 43(A), 43A(1A), 58A(1) - Shares - Deposits - Prohibit invitation or acceptance - Appellant filed Company Petition before Company Law Board against respondent no. 2 and 3 from committing breach of pre-emption agreement and the same was dismissed - Aggrieved appellants filed Company Appeal before HC and the same was dismissed - Hence instant appeal - Whether amendment made by the Act 53 of 2000 to ss. 3 and 43A destroys rights and obligations created by art. 57 of the Articles of Association of respondent no. 1 company.

Held, s. 3(1)(iii) of the Act stipulated to be private company a company's Articles of Association are required to contain certain stipulations with regard to matters specified in cl. (a),(b), (c). By virtue of the Act 53 of 2000 a private company's Articles of Association are required to contain additional stipulations relating to matter contained in cl. (d) also. Interpretation of sub-s. (11) creates 'two classes of private companies and would have discriminatory results' is required to be answered. Argument is based on a wrong premise. It proceeds on basis that HYBRID companies created prior to 13-12-2000 are private companies. SC have already held that HYBRID companies are public companies which in law are entitled to retain some features of private companies if shareholders choose to retain them. Question of discrimination does not arise. Failure of respondent no. 1 company to amend its Articles of Association to give effect to cl. (d) of s. 3(1)(iii) does not effect operation of its Art. 57. Both submissions are required to be rejected. Submission based on principle of estoppel is required to be rejected in view of my conclusion that HYBRID companies contemplated u/s. 43A(1C), which were in existence, would continue to be in existence. Requirement of amending Articles of Association pursuant to Amendment Act 53 of 2000, insofar as such companies are concerned, is only optional on part of shareholders. Fact that shareholders of HYBRID company exercised option not to amend Articles of Association thereby converting a HYBRID company into private company does not prevent such shareholders from advancing an argument that respondent no. 1 company is not a public company but still a HBRID company. Only other submission of respondent which requires to be dealt with is regarding transfer of five shares of appellant which, according to respondents, resulted in membership of respondent no. 1 company exceeding fifty thereby rendering respondent no. 1 a public company. Unfortunately, though HC noted submission at, it did not record any finding in this regard. SC, therefore, decline to examine this question. SC cannot be converted into a Court which enquires into questions of fact for first time. In view of fact the HC, though noted contentions of respondent herein, failed to record any conclusion thereon, SC deem it appropriate to remit matter to HC. Appeal allowed.
