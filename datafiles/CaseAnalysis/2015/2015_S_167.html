Nagaraj v State Represented by Inspector of Police, Salem Town, Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

10 March 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 169; (2015) 4 SCC 739; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 1638; 2015 CRLJ 2377; 2015 (2) JCC 1121; JT 2015 (3) SC 117; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 452; 2015(3) SCALE 396

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Alleged Murder - Conviction - Justification - Appellant accused had joined business of deceased on basis of commission - Further, both deceased and accused had gone out of station for business - Further, it was alleged that accused had committed murder of deceased in hotel room leaving his body in room which was recovered by hotel staff - Trial court convicted accused for offence u/s. 302 of IPC which was confirmed by HC - Whether conviction order passed against appellant accused was justified and in accordance with law.

Held, appellant accused denied that he voluntarily confessed to crime or that he accompanied police to any place. Further, there are number of inconsistencies in case of prosecution. Neither Register nor Receipt Book was produced by prosecution as evidence and no explanation has been given for their non-production. HC has held that failure to produce these does not damage case of prosecution, as there is no reason to doubt statement of PW1 according to which accused and deceased came to Lodge and stayed in that particular room. However, failure to produce them has resulted in prosecution relying on circumstantial evidence instead of direct evidence, thus weakening its case. Further, various witnesses have given contradictory statements regarding number of bags with accused and deceased. Further, there are inconsistencies in cross-examination of PW1 regarding his duty timings. Further, SC held that there was plenty of time for crime scene to have been tampered with before police arrived. There was also possibility of other parties, including PW1 and/or Manager to have perpetrated murder. Further, no explanation has been given for fact that accused was not arrested after investigation commenced and he was only taken into custody after he voluntarily surrendered. SC held that no suspicious or ulterior slant can be attributed to appellant accused for surrendering before Magistrate after one and half years, particularly given that there were no outstanding warrants for his arrest or even for participating in investigation. Further, no identification parade has been conducted and identification by PW3 and PW4 took place two and half years after incident, again without identification parade, and eventually in course of Court proceedings. SC held that conviction is predicated on circumstantial evidence alone. Fingerprints have not been lifted from scene, murder weapon has not been recovered, and any credible motive is absent. It cannot even be contended that accused was last person to be seen with deceased since several persons including Manager, PW1, and guests in adjoining rooms could have accessed room where deceased was eventually found. SC held that HC erred in concluding that complicity of accused in murder of Deceased had been proved beyond reasonable doubt. Hence, impugned order of HC is set aside and conviction of appellant accused is set aside. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - Conviction of accused under criminal law should be proved by prosecution beyond reasonable doubt.
