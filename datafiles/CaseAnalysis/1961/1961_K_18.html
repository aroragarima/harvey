Supreme Court of India

04 August 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Syed Jaffer Imam, S.K. Das, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, K.C. Das Gupta, Raghubar Dayal, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 144; AIR 1961 SC 1808; 1961 ALJ 936; 1962 (64) BomLR 240; 1961 CRLJ 856; [1962] 3 S.C.R. 10

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Indian Evidence Act, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, Prisoners Act

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - Arms Act, 1959, s. 19 (e) - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898, ss. 439, 94 and 96 - Indian Evidence Act, ss. 27, 47, 73, 119 and 139 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 302, 380 and 457 - Prisoners Act - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14, 20 (3),134 (1) (e),136 (1)and 227 - Identification of Prisoners Act, 1920, ss. 5 and 6 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Several questions for decision arising out of batch of cases - Interpretation of art. 20 (3) - Court did not intend to lay down that calling upon a person accused of an offence to give his thumb impression, his impression of palm or fingers or of sample handwriting or signature comes within ambit of a witness which has been equated to furnish evidence - To be a witness may be equivalent to furnishing evidence in sense of making oral or written statements, but not in larger sense of expression so as to include giving of thumb impression or impression of palm or foot or fingers or specimen writing or exposing a part of body by an accused person for purpose of identification - Taking of impressions or parts of body of an accused person very often becomes necessary to help investigate the crime - It is as much necessary to protect an accused person against being compelled to incriminate himself, as to arm agents of law and law courts with legitimate powers to bring offenders to justice - Must be assumed that Constitution-makers were aware of existing law, for example, s. 73 of Evidence Act ors. 5, 6 of Identification of prisoners Act - A person is said to be a witness by testifying to what he has seen, or something he has heard which is capable of being beard, is not hit by rule excluding hearsay or giving his opinion, as an expert, in respect of matters in controversy - Well-established that cl. (3) of art. 20 is directed against self-incrimination by an accused person - Self-incrimination must mean conveying information based upon personal knowledge of person giving information and cannot include merely mechanical process of producing documents in court which may throw a light on any of points in controversy, but which do not contain any statement of accused based on his personal knowledge - Production of such a document, with a view to comparison of writing or signature or impression, is not statement of an accused person, which can be said to be of nature of a personal testimony - Giving of finger impressions or of specimen writing or of signatures by an accused person, though it may amount to furnishing evidence in larger sense, is not included within expression to be a witness - In order to bring evidence within inhibitions of art. 20 (3) it must be shown not only that person making statement was an accused but also that he was compelled to make that statement - 'Compulsion' in context, must mean 'duress' - Compulsion is a physical objective act and not state of mind of person making statement - Mere asking by a police officer investigating a crime against a certain individual to do a certain thing is not compulsion within meaning of art. 20 (3) - An accused person cannot be said to have been compelled to be a witness against himself simply because he made a statement while in police custody,without anything more - Mere questioning of an accused person by a police officer,resulting in a voluntary statement, which may ultimately turn out to be incriminatory, is not compulsion - To be a witness is not equivalent to garnishing evidence in its widest significance - Giving thumb impressions or impressions of foot or palm or fingers or specimen writings or showing parts of body by way of identification were not included in expression to be a witness - To bring statement in question within prohibition of art. 20 (3), person accused must have stood in character of an accused person at time he made statement - Appeals listed to be heard on merits in accordance with these principles - Appeals disposed of.
