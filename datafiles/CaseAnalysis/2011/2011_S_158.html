Birender Poddar v State Of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

16 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, Deepak Verma

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 363; (2011) 6 SCC 350; AIR 2011 SC 2336; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 2061; 2011 CRLJ 3120; JT 2011 (6) SC 609; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 510; 2011 (4) RLW 3061; 2011(6) SCALE 354; [2011] 6 S.C.R. 873

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 302 and 498A - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appellant was convicted u/ss. 302 r/w s. 34 and s. 498A of IPC - HC on appeal, affirmed the conviction and the sentences - Hence, instant appeal - (A) Absence of direct evidence - Appellant contended that the there was no direct evidence in the case - Held, in cases where death takes place within the matrimonial home, it was very difficult to find direct evidence - For appreciating circumstantial evidences, the court had to be cautious and find out whether the chain of circumstances led by the prosecution was complete and the chain must be so complete and conclusive as to unmistakably point to the guilt of the accused - In the instant case, it was established from the evidence of PWs 5, 6, 7 and 8 on which the prosecution relied that there was consistent evidence of ill-treatment of the deceased - There was also evidence of beating and injury mark on the deceased - (B) identification of the dead body - It was submitted that there was substantial contradiction in the matter of identification of the dead body - Held, HC rightly held that there was positive evidence of identification, by the father of the deceased and by her brothers, PWs 5 and 7 - There was nothing in the postmortem report to suggest that the body was beyond identification - There was no inconsistency in the evidence about the identification of the dead body - (C) Interested witnesses - Held, the evidence given by PWs 5, 6, 7 and 8 was quite cogent and clearly established the prosecution case - HC had scrutinised the evidence of the relations with due care and caution - (D) Whether there was substantial contradiction between the medical evidence and the oral evidence? - (E) Appellant raised a defence that the deceased died a natural death as she was suffering from jaundice - Held, defence of the appellant that the deceased was suffering from jaundice was not been proved - There was no evidence on record that the deceased was treated for jaundice - There was no pathological report nor was there any medical subscription of any drug being administered on the deceased for treatment of jaundice - Conviction upheld - Appeal dismissed.
