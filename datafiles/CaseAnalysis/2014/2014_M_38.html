Union of India and others v Major S. P. Sharma and others

Supreme Court of India

06 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

M.Y. Eqbal, Balbir Singh Chauhan, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 150; (2014) 6 SCC 351; 2014 (4) AWC 3319; JT 2014 (3) SC 611; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 327; 2014 (3) SLR 543

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Defence & Security Forces; Service

Keywords: Legality, General Court Martial, Termination of service

Summary: Defence & Security Forces - Service - Constitution - General Court Martial - Termination of service - Dismissal - Legality - HC allowed appeals filed by respondents and quashed their termination orders and quashed their Court Martial proceedings held against two jawans - Hence instant appeals - Whether order recorded by HC was maintainable -

Held, violation of Fundamental Rights guaranteed under Constitution have to be protected, but at same time, it was duty of Court to ensure that decisions rendered by Court were not overturned frequently, that too, when challenged collaterally as that was directly affecting basic structure of Constitution incorporating power of judicial review of Court - There was no doubt that SC had extensive power to correct error or to review its decision but that should not be done at cost of doctrine of finality - Issue of law could be overruled later on, but question of fact or, as in instant case, dispute with regard to termination of services should not be reopened once it had been finally sealed in proceedings inter-se between parties up to SC way back in 1980 - Term 'dismissal' in original order was substituted by term 'termination' issued corrigendum to ratify mistake committed while issuing order - In fact, competent authority had taken decision only to terminate, and therefore it was found necessary to issue corrigendum - However, in view of such substitution of word 'dismissal' by term 'termination', did not tilt balance in favour of respondents - Further, proposed 5% deduction was withdrawn and therefore issue did not survive - Entire facts of case were analysed and material produced in Court and upon exhaustive consideration of matter, SC opined that power of pleasure exercised by President in terminating services of respondents did not suffer from any illegality, bias or mala-fide or based on any other extraneous ground, and same should not be challenged on ground that it was camouflage - Onus lay on respondent-officers who alleged mala-fides - No credible evidence or material produced before Court impelled SC to conclude that order of termination was baseless or mala-fide - Hence, judgment and order passed by HC was set aside - Appeals allowed.
