A. Neelalohithadasan Nadar v George Mascrene

Supreme Court of India

11 April 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

M.M. Punchhi, A.M. Ahmadi, K. Ramaswamy

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 2119; (1994) Supp2 SCC 619; AIR 1994 SCW 2198; JT 1994 (3) SC 181; 1994(2) SCALE 522; [1994] 3 S.C.R. 437; 1994 (Supp) ShimLC 347; 1995 (1) UJ 756

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Purity

Summary: Election - Practice & Procedure - Representation of People Act, 1951, ss. 17, 18, 62(4) and 66 - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, r. 94 A - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 73 - Whether election of respondent was liable to be declared void due to charges of double voting ? - Handwriting may be proved on admission of writer or by evidence of some witness in whose presence it was written - This is direct evidence - In absence of such direct evidence, opinion of handwriting expert or of some who is familiar with writing of person is relevant - A writing may be proved to be handwriting of a particular individual by evidence of a person familiar with handwriting of that individual or by testimony of an expert competent to compare handwritings on a scientific basis - A third method is also provided by Evidence Act in s. 73 - It is comparison by court with writing made in presence of court or admitted or proved to be writing of person - Court can apply its own observation to admitted or proved writings and to compare them with disputed one - This comparison depends on an analysis of characteristics in admitted or proved writings and of same characteristics in large measure in disputed writing - Even if there is opinion of expert on handwriting, it is subjected to scrutiny by court - Expert's opinion is not final word - Court must see for itself whether it can safely be held that two writings are of same person - To this extent, court may play role of an expert - Court can accept disputed signature to be that of witness when it is satisfied on its observation that it is safe to accept same - It is not necessary to have admitted signature of witness to be compared with signature in disputed counterfoils of ballot paper by any expert - This Court can scrutinise characteristics of signatures - If it finds that disputed signature has same characteristics in large measure with admitted signature, it can safely come to conclusion that both are of same person - High Court finally recorded its satisfaction or otherwise in case of signatures resulting in double voting and impersonation, and signatures and thumb impression not tallying at all - No meaningful argument on facts in regard thereto was addressed except to approach of employing s. 73 of Evidence Act - It was urged that HC should not have become an expert - However, when larger public interest is served by expeditious disposal of an election petition, then course adopted by HC is in conformity therewith - Appeal dismissed.
