Â Shashi Kumar Banerjee and Others v Subodh Kumar Banerjee Since Deceased and After Him His Legal Representatives and Others

Supreme Court of India

13 September 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K. Subba Rao, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 414; AIR 1964 SC 529

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Family & Personal

Summary: Family & Personal - Indian Succession Act, 1925 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Will - Signature of testator - Reliance on evidence of handwriting expert - Chemical test - Whether will was executed on date which appears on will or whether it was executed at a much later date and was thus not duly executed and attested? - Held, whole argument therefore based on theory of tremor put forward by handwriting expert appears to Court to be of no help to respondents for testator having retained full mental capacity and power of judgment till almost last moment of his life, it does not stand to reason that he would antedate will if he really signed it late in 1946 - Once therefore it is admitted that signature on will is that of testator, theory that it is antedated by him can be accepted only if expert's evidence is so convincing that extreme improbability attaching to said theory can be safely rejected - Expert admitted in his evidence that it was only by a chemical test that it could be definitely stated whether a particular writing was of a particular year or period - He also admitted that he applied no chemical tests in this case - So his opinion cannot on his own showing have that value which it might have had if he had applied a chemical test - Osborn on 'Questioned Documents' at p. 464 says even with respect to chemical tests that 'the chemical tests to determine age also, as a rule, are a mere excuse to make a guess and furnish no reliable data upon which a definite opinion can be based' - In these circumstances mere opinion of expert cannot override positive evidence of attesting witnesses in a case like this where there are no suspicious circumstances - Appeal allowed.
