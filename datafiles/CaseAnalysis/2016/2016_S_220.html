Usmangani Adambhai Vahora v State of Gujarat and another

Supreme Court of India

08 January 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 19; (2016) 3 SCC 370; AIR 2016 SC 336; 2016 (2) CalLT(SC) 119; 2016 (1) CTC 572; 2016 (3) G.L.R. 1959; 2016 (1) JCC 536; JT 2016 (1) SC 135; 2016 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 209; 2016 (1) MLJ(Crl) 379; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 731; 2016 (2) RLW 1321; 2016(1) SCALE 228; [2016] 1 S.C.R. 56

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Additional Sessions Judge, Presiding Officer, Conversation, Remarks

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Transfer of case - Reasonable apprehension - Respondent no.2, on overhearing conversation between informant and his son regarding trial, filed application for transfer of sessions case to any other court in same Sessions Division but the same was rejected - However, on appeal, HC observed that case was to be transferred to any other Additional Sessions Judge in same Sessions Division - Hence, instant appeal - Whether application for transfer from the court where the trial was pending to the court of another Additional Sessions Judge could be allowed.

Held, apprehension that was stated is absolutely mercurial and could not remotely stated to be reasonable. Hence, order of transfer passed by HC is not sustainable and so appeals are partly allowed and finding recorded as regards jurisdiction of Sessions Judge is sustained and direction to Principal Sessions Judge to transfer the case from 3rd Addl. Sessions Judge to other court being vulnerable and wholly unsustainable is set aside. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - An order of transfer is not to be passed as a matter of routine or merely because an interested party has expressed some apprehension about proper conduct of the trial but the power has to be exercised cautiously and in exceptional situations, where it becomes necessary to do so to provide credibility to the trial and there has to be real apprehension that there would be miscarriage of justice.
