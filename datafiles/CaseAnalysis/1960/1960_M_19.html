Management of Kairbetta Estate, Kotagiri v Rajamanickam and Others

Supreme Court of India

24 March 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K.C. Das Gupta

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 155; AIR 1960 SC 893; 1960 (1) FLR 107; 1960 (2) LLJ 275; 1961 (1) SCJ 103; [1960] 3 S.C.R. 371

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 33C(2), 2(1), 2(kkk), 25(C), (D) & (E) - Workmen - Pay lay-off compensation - Entitlement - Appellant's manager was assaulted by respondents/workmen and also threatened appellant's staff working in Kelso division - On receipt of threats and complaints from staff appellant notified to close said unit - After reconciliation and assurance from respondents, said unit was opened - Respondents filed complaint u/s. 33C(2) of the Act claiming lay-off compensation for the period of closure - Tribunal had directed appellant to pay lay-off compensation - Aggrieved appellant filed instant appeal - Whether closure of appellant's division during relevant period amounts to lock-out and falls within definition of lay-off u/s. 2(kkk) of the Act - Held, that concept of lockout was essentially different from concept of lay-off, and where closure of business amounts to lock-out u/s. 2(1) of the Act, it would be impossible to bring it within the scope of lay-off u/s. 2(kkk) of the Act - Broadly stated lay-off generally occurs in a continuing business, whereas lock-out was the closure of business - In case of lay-off, owing to reasons specified in s. 2(kkk) of the Act, employer was unable to give employment to one or more workmen and in case of lock-out employer closes the business and locks out the whole body of workmen for reasons which had no relevance to causes specified u/s. 2(kkk)of the Act - Thus, nature of two concepts was entirely different and so are their consequences - Hence in case of lay-off employer could be liable to pay compensation as provided u/s. 25(C), (D) and (E) of the Act, but this liability could not be invoked in case of lock-out - Liability of employer in cases of lock-out would depend upon whether lock-out was justified and legal or not but whatever the liability, provisions applicable to payment of lay-off compensation could not be applied to cases of lockout - Therefore, held that lock-out in instant case was not lay-off, and as such respondents were not entitled to claim any lay-off compensation from appellant - Hence, lock-out was fully justified and appellant's manager had been violently attacked and other members of staff working in lower division were threatened by respondents and no grievance could be made against appellant's conduct - Appeal allowed.
