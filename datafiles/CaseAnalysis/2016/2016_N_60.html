Nidhi Kaim v State of Madhya Pradesh and others

Supreme Court of India

12 May 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 505; (2016) 7 SCC 615; AIR 2016 SC 2865; JT 2016 (5) SC 531; 2016 (4) MLJ 684; 2016(5) SCALE 246; [2016] 7 S.C.R. 822

Case Digest

Subject: Education

Keywords: Unfair Means, Jurisdiction, Limitation Act, 1963, Election, Hindu Succession Act, 1956, Government of India Act, 1935, Juvenile Justice Act, 1986, Smugglers and Foreign Exchange Manipulators (Forfeiture of Property) Act, 1976, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act, 1908, Invigilator, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Madhya Pradesh Vyavsayik Pariksha Mandal Adhiniyam, 2007, M.P. Recogniltion Examination Act, 1937

Summary: (A) Education - Practice & Procedure - Madhya Pradesh Vyavsayik Pariksha Mandal Adhiniyam, 2007, ss. 24(2)(e), 3 - Examination - Conspiracy - Cancellation of admissions - Pre-Medical Entrance Test (PMT) for admissions into medical colleges was conducted by respondent no. 3/BOARD - FIR was registered against students and some employees of respondents/State alleging commission of various offences pursuant to a large scale conspiracy in context of examination - Parallelly, police investigated the crime, some officers of BOARD were arrested - Enquiry was conducted, admissions granted to appellants/students in various medical colleges stood cancelled by BOARD - Aggrieved, appellants filed writ petition before HC - HC upheld the stand taken by the State and dismissed writ petitions - Hence, instant appeals - Whether it is a case of mass copying.

Held, in a case where several candidates are found involved in "mass copying" or in other words, where vast majority of candidates were found to have resorted to use of unfair means in any examination then it is not necessary for the concerned Institute to give any show cause notice to any individual candidate before cancellation of his result. Firstly, neither HC nor SC could sit as an Appellate Court over the decision of Expert Committee and find fault in material relied on by Committee. Secondly, method evolved by experts was usually applied to find out as to whether two candidates had copied from each other and hence no fault could be noticed in it. Thirdly, decision to cancel the results was based on other contemporaneous material seized during the investigation by STF. Fourthly, decision to cancel the results was not taken in post-haste but was taken with full application of mind by Expert Committee which consists of experts in subjects. Therefore, this being a case of "mass copying", it was neither necessary to give any show cause notice to appellants and nor necessary to supply material to appellants. Thus, it is a clear case of what is called in ordinary parlance a "mass copying" and procedure adopted by State cannot be said to be unfair or arbitrary. The action impugned is not in breach of rules of natural justice which has no application to the facts of instant case as held in Bihar School Examination Board v. Subhas Chandra Sinha, & Ors., 1970 Indlaw SC 100 and Chief General Manager, Calcutta Telephones District, Bharat Sanchar Nigam Limited & Ors. v. Surendra Nath Pandey & Ors. 2011 Indlaw SC 769. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Education - Practice & Procedure - Madhya Pradesh Vyavsayik Pariksha Mandal Adhiniyam, 2007, ss. 24(2)(e), 3 - Equitable relief - Delay - Validity - Whether appellants were entitled to claim any equity in their favour on account of delay occurred on part of State in cancelling their result.

Held, appellants were not entitled to claim any equitable relief on ground that they have almost completed their course during interregnum period and hence no action on basis of their PMT Examination results is called for. State might consider permitting appellants and other candidates' alike appellants to appear in competitive examination whenever it is held and consider granting age relaxation to those candidates who crossed age limit, if prescribed. Such liberty, if granted, would not cause any prejudice to any one and at the same time would do substantial justice to all such candidates as was done in case of Bihar School Examination Board v. Subhas Chandra Sinha, & Ors., 1970 Indlaw SC 100. Therefore, appellants were not entitled to claim any indulgence. Appeals dismissed.
