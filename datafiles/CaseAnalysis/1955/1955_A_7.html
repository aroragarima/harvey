Director of Endowments, Government of Hyderabad and Others v Akaram Ali

Supreme Court of India

22 April 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

Vivian Bose, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Syed Jaffer Imam

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 99; AIR 1956 SC 60; 1979 LabIC 426

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Hereditary lands - Possession - Mandamus - Respondent filed a petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution asking for a 'mandamus' against the Director of Endowments of the Hyderabad Govt. - His prayer was that the Director be ordered to hand over the management and possession of a certain Dargah called the Dargah of Jehangir and Burhan-ud-din Piran and also the adjoining 'hereditary lands', together with the income and profits, to the respondent - Respondent's case was that the Dargah contained the tomb of one of his ancestors and that he and his ancestors had been the hereditary Sajjadas and Mutawallis of the Dargah for generations - In the year 1914, when the respondent's brother was in possession, the Ecclesiastical Department of the State stepped in and entrusted the supervision of the Dargah to his opponent - Respondent was removed in 1920 and the Ecclesiastical Department took over the supervision under a Firman of the Nazim which directed the Department to supervise the Dargah until the rights of the parties have been enquired into and decided - Respondent alleged that these rights were investigated by the Civil Courts - It was contended that the Firman of the Nazim ceased to be valid after the Constitution, therefore the possession of Govt. after the date was unlawful and that the Ecclesiastical Department took possession from the respondent and so it was bound to hand the Dargah back to him - HC granted respondent's prayer - Appellants alleged that the respondent sought to establish from them his possession and that of his ancestors for generations, and also that the Ecclesiastical Department took over possession from him - It was contended that as the respondent had no present right to possession, no 'mandamus' could be issued and that whoever was out of possession should go to the Courts to establish his rights - Whether the respondent was in possession of the property or held in abeyance - Held, even if it be assumed that the respondent was in possession, his rights to immediate possession, whatever they may have been, were taken away and held in abeyance till he could establish them in the Civil Courts - Position of the respondent was that at the date when the Constitution came into force he had no right to immediate possession, the utmost he had was right to be restored to possession if and when he established his rights in a Court a law - There was no decision of a Civil Court declaring the respondent's right to possession, and therefore, on that short ground he could not get the writ he sought - HC's order was set aside and the respondent's petition for a writ was dismissed - Appeal allowed.
