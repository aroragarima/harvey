In Re: Ramlila Maidan Incident Dt.4/5.06.2011 v Home Secretary, Union Of India & Ors

Supreme Court of India

23 February 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 54; (2012) 5 SCC 1; AIR 2012 SC (Supp) 266; 2012 CRLJ 3516; JT 2012 (3) SC 1; 2012 (2) MLJ(Crl) 32; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 443; 2012(2) SCALE 682; [2012] 4 S.C.R. 971

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Human Rights; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Fundamental Duties, Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Speech And Expression, Hunger Strike, Confrontation, U.P. Urban Planning and Development Act, 1973, Obedience, Imaginary, Constitution (Sixteenth Amendment) Act, 1963, Bombay Police Rules, Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2010, Constitution (44th Amendment) Act, 1979, MCD Rules

Summary: Swatanter Kumar, J.

(A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Human Rights - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 144 - Constitution of India, 1950, art 19 - Public Tranquillity - Prohibitory orders - Unlawful assembly - Validity - Baba Ramdev (Yoga guru) was involved in holding Anti-Corruption rally raising issue of black money where more than 1 lakh persons participated -Trust proposed to take Ramlila Maidan (RM) on rent for holding yoga training camp for 4- 5 thousand people between 1-6-2011 to 20-6-2011 - DCP granted permission subject to terms and conditions - Yoga Guru in continuation of his agitation for black money had been in serious talks with Government and threatened to go on fast unto death - Yoga guru made application for permission to hold dharna at Jantar Mantar (JM) which was granted with restriction - Government held talks with Yoga guru requesting not to hold dharna - Media reported that Yoga guru intended to hold fast unto death at Yoga camp - DCP clarified with organisers who informed that there would be no other program except residential yoga camp - RM swelled more than 50,000/- people and no yoga training was held entire day and late night yoga guru decided to march to JM - Authorities fearing JM would not accommodate massive gathering withdrew permission granted for holding dharna and imposed order u/s. 144 CrPC - Police reached dais/platform which was resisted by supporters of yoga guru - Yoga guru jumped and went in public making barricade of women supporters - Police shot teargas shells and water cannons, there was chaos and stampede where several persons sustained minor and major injuries, one person sustained injury to spine and ultimately died - SC suo motu took up issue in instant writ petition - Amicus curiae contended that neither withdrawal of permissions for RM and JM nor imposition of restriction by passing an order u/s. 144 CrPC was for valid and good cause/reason - Amicus curie further contended that imposition of restriction, passing of order u/s. 144 CrPC and force and brutality with which persons present at RM were dispersed was nothing but a show of power of State as opposed to a citizen's right - Respondent No. 4 contended that in earlier meetings, no untoward incident had occurred, which could, by any standard, cause an apprehension in mind of Police that there could occur an incident, communal or otherwise, leading to public disorder, in any way - Police contended that respondent No 4 had obtained permission to hold yoga camp was granted citing certain inputs, DCP issued a warning to respondent No. 4 expressing their concern about variance of purpose as well as that there should be a limited gathering, otherwise authorities would be compelled to review permission - -Police further contended that it had granted permission, and was fully competent to revoke it as well as to pass orders u/s. 144 CrPC -(a) Whether orders passed by the State including the order passed u/s. 144 CrPC were valid and legal - Held, State and Police could have avoided tragic incident in question by exercising greater restraint, patience and resilience, - Orders were passed by authorities in undue haste and were executed with force and overzealousness, as if an emergent situation existed - Decision to forcibly evict innocent public sleeping at RM in midnight by police, independently or in consultation with Ministry of Home Affairs was amiss and suffered from element of arbitrariness and abuse of power to some extent - Restriction imposed on right to freedom of speech and expression was unsupported by cogent reasons and material facts - It was an invasion of liberties and exercise of fundamental freedoms - Members of assembly had legal protections available to them even under provisions of CrPC - Thus, restriction was unreasonable and unwarrantedly executed - Action demonstrated might of State and was an assault on very basic democratic values enshrined in our Constitution - (b) Whether associating police while holding large meeting, dharna and protests would infringe fundamental rights - Held, requirement of associating police, which was an important organ of State for ensuring implementation of rule of law, while holding such large scale meetings, dharnas and protests, would not infringe fundamental rights enshrined u/arts. 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(b) of Constitution - It would squarely fall within regulatory mechanism of reasonable restrictions, contemplated u/arts. 19(2) and 19(3) of Constitution Further, it would help in ensuring due social order and would also not impinge upon rights of others, as contemplated u/art. 21 of Constitution - Petition disposed of.

Dr. B. S. Chauhan, J.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Human Rights - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 144 - Constitution of India, 1950, art 19 - Public Tranquility - Prohibitory orders - Unlawful assembly - Public Order - Validity - Whether orders passed by the State including the order passed u/s. 144 CrPC were valid and legal - Held, it was admittedly an assembly of followers, under a peaceful banner of Yogic training, fast asleep - Assembly was at least, purportedly, a conglomeration of individuals gathered together, expressive of a determination to improve material condition of human race - Aim of assembly was prima facie unobjectionable and was not to inflame passions - Such an assembly was necessarily illegal should not be presumed, and even if it was, individuals were all asleep who were taken by surprise altogether for a simultaneous implementation and action u/s. 144 CrPC without being preceded by an announcement or even otherwise, giving no time in a reasonable way to the assembly to disperse - Judicially and on strength of impartial logic, answer should be in negative as a sleeping crowd could not be included within bracket of an unlawful category unless there was sufficient material to brand it as such - Facts as uncovered and procedural mandate having been blatantly violated, was malice in law and also part played by police and administration showed outrageous behaviour which could not be justified by law in any civilized society - Petition disposed of.
