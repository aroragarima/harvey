Remdeo Chauhan @ Rajnath Chauhan v Bani Kant Das & Others

Supreme Court of India

19 November 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 973; 2011 (3) AWC 2384; 2011 CRLJ 985; 2011 (1) JhrCR(SC) 58; JT 2010 (12) SC 516; 2011 (1) MLJ(SC) 819; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 197; 2010(12) SCALE 184

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Human Rights

Keywords: Intervene

Summary: (A) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Juvenile Justice Act, 1986 - Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 137 - Murder - Conviction and death sentence - Confirmation by SC - Review Petition - Maintainability - Petitioner allegedly murdered four members of a family - Petitioner contended that he was below 16 years of age at the time of commission of crime, hence he would be tried under the Juvenile Justice Act - Subsequently, physical examination and radiological examination was done on petitioner and medical reports opined that petitioner was above 20 years of age - Trial Court, on the basis of medical evidence, held that petitioner could not be below 16 years of age at the time of offence and thereby, convicted the petitioner for murder and sentenced him to death - HC confirmed the conviction and death sentence in appeal - Petitioner filed a review petition before the SC - Two Judge Bench referred the matter to Larger Bench wherein two Judges upheld the HC order, however, one Judge gave dissenting judgment - Petitioner, in the meantime, filed a mercy petition before the Governor for commuting death sentence to life imprisonment - National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) also made a recommendation of petitioner's case to Governor for commutation on the basis of an article published - Governor, consequently, commuted the sentence - Relatives of the deceased challenged the Governor's order before the SC u/art. 32 of the Constitution - SC set aside the Governor's order and held that NHRC recommendation was not in accordance with the procedure established under the 1993 Act - SC, further, directed the Governor to reconsider petitioner's mercy petition - Hence the present second review - Whether a second review petition is maintainable in SC - Held, second review would be entertained and decided as it raised various questions of legal significance, touching the rights of the petitioner to seek commutation as also touching questions regarding the jurisdiction of the NHRC, as also various constitutional provisions relating to life and freedom - Various concerns of public law came up for consideration in the instant second review and in the context of those issues the constitutional provision of art. 137 for review of judgment and order of SC may have to be examined - Therefore, instant second review is maintainable - Review allowed.

(B) Human Rights - Practice & Procedure - Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993, ss. 2(d), 12(j) - Confirmation of death sentence by SC - Recommendation for commutation - Jurisdiction of NHRC - Whether the NHRC has the jurisdiction to make recommendation to the Governor for commuting death sentence which was confirmed by the SC - Held, while interpreting the powers and jurisdiction of NHRC, the Court construes s. 2(d) of the 1993 Act along with its long title and also the Statement of Objects and Reasons of the said Act - Jurisdiction of NHRC stands enlarged by s. 12(j) of the 1993 Act to take necessary action for the protection of human rights - Such action would include inquiring into cases where a party has been denied the protection of any law to which he is entitled, whether by a private party, a public institution, the government or even the Courts of law - If a person is entitled to benefit under a particular law, and benefits under that law have been denied to him, it will amount to a violation of his human rights - Thus, if a person has been guaranteed certain rights either under the Constitution or under an International Covenant or under a law, and he is denied access to such a right, then it amounts to a clear violation of his human right and NHRC has the jurisdiction to intervene for protecting it - Findings in the judgment under review about the absence of jurisdiction of NHRC to make some recommendations to the Governor is thus vitiated by errors apparent on the face of the record - Assumption in the judgment under review that there can be no violation of a person's human right by a judgment of SC is possibly not correct - Therefore, in the facts of the present case, NHRC has not committed any illegality by taking into consideration a published article and then making a mere recommendation to the Governor, for considering the petitioner's plea for commutation and hence, in doing so, NHRC acted within its jurisdiction - Review allowed.

(C) Human Rights - Practice & Procedure - Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993, ss. 12(6), 36(2) - Matter pending before SC - Interference by NHRC - Scope - Whether NHRC can interfere a matter pending before the SC - Held, NHRC cannot intervene in proceeding pending in Court without its approval u/s. 12(6) of the Act as it is assumed that Court will remedy any case of violation of human rights - NHRC cannot function as a parallel seat of justice to rectify or correct or comment upon orders passed by the SC or any other Courts of competent jurisdiction - For correcting an order in a judicial proceeding, the aggrieved party has to avail of the well established gamut of the corrective machinery of appeal, revision, review, curative petition and so on - However, in the present case, NHRC did not send any recommendation as long as the first review proceedings were pending in SC and made its recommendations to the Governor only after the judgment in first review was passed by SC - Further, SC, in Paramjit Kaur v. State of Punjab and Ors, 1998 INDLAW SC 1247, held that when in exercise of its power u/art. 32, SC gives any directions to NHRC, then like all other authorities in the country, NHRC is bound by such directions and in such situations, NHRC acts 'sui-generis' and statutory bar of limitation u/s. 36(2) of the Act would not stand in the way - Therefore, NHRC, a statutory body, in a given situation, may have to act under the order or direction given by SC in exercise of its constitutional power of judicial review - Review allowed.

(D) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 161 - Mercy Petition - Cryptic order - Legality - Whether non-disclosure of reason vitiates the order of the Governor u/art. 161 of the Constitution - Held, while exercising power of commutation u/art. 161 of the Constitution, the Governor is to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers - In the instant case, detailed consideration was made in the Chief Minister's Secretariat and notes in detail was put up by the Chief Minister's Secretariat, wherein the entire factual aspect of the case had been considered - Further, different judgments given in the first review petition were part of the note, and on a consideration of the detailed note, the Chief Minister approved the note put up before him by the Secretary, Judicial Department for commutation - Subsequently, the matter was placed before the Governor's Secretariat with the entire record and the Governor approved the proposal - Therefore, more than adequate reasons are available on the records of the case and upon a detailed consideration of the relevant facts, the Governor exercised his power of commutation - Such an exercise of power by the Governor does not call for any interference, in view of the law laid down in Maru Ram v. Union of India & Ors., 1980 INDLAW SC 278 and Kehar Singh & Anr. v. Union of India & Anr., 1988 INDLAW SC 47 - Review allowed.

(E) Civil Procedure - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Review Petition - Scope - Whether the petitioner is entitled to insist on a fresh look at his juvenility and a fresh consideration of his rights in view of the changes in the Juvenile Justice (Care & Protection of Children) Act, 2000 by 2006 amendment Act - Held, in the writ petition before the SC notice was served on petitioner and it was open to him to raise the point of changes of law in that proceeding as by that time the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000 as amended by the 2006 amendment Act had come into force - Further, petitioner did not plead the point of change of law in the first review petition also as a ground for review - Even though the ambit of the review petition has been widened by SC in P.N. Eswara Iyer and others v. Registrar, Supreme Court of India, 1980 INDLAW SC 100, it has to follow broadly the principles of review u/O. 47 r. 1 of the CPC - Following the discipline of O. 47 r. 1 or the concept of 'an error apparent on the face of the record', it cannot pronounce in a review petition on a question, which was open to be raised in the original proceeding, but was not raised - Therefore, it is refrained from pronouncing on the rights of the petitioner under Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection) Act, 2000 as amended by the 2006 Amendment - Review allowed.
