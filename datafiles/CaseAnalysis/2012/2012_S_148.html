Sangeet & Anr. v State Of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

20 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 466; (2013) 2 SCC 452; AIR 2013 SC 447; 2013 CRLJ 425; JT 2012 (11) SC 388; 2013 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 214; 2012 (4) MLJ(Crl) 792; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 114; 2012(11) SCALE 140; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 85

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Jail Manual, Karnataka Prison Rules, 1974

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302,307,148,449,149 - Arms Act, 1959, s. 25(1-B) - Murder - Conviction - Death Sentence - Mitigating and Aggravating Circumstances - Rarest of rare - Remission - Sustainability - Appellants-accused and other accused were convicted for offence u/ss. 302,307,148,449,149 of IPC and 25(1-B) of Act - Trial Court sentenced appellants to death and awarded life imprisonment to other accused - Prosecution case was that appellants believing that deceased performed some black magic which led to death of their son took revenge and committed crime - Aggrieved appellants field appeal before HC - HC, opined that the crime was committed in a pre-meditated, cold-blooded, cruel and diabolic manner while the victims were sleeping and held that act of appellants fell in category of rarest of rare cases and as such a death penalty was warranted - Hence instant appeal - Whether sentence of death penalty awarded by Trial Court confirmed by HC, was sustainable - Held, judgment does not, with respect, indicate material that led Court to conclude what aroused intense and extreme indignation of community, except nature of crime, it was not clear on what basis it concluded that criminal was a menace to society and 'shall continue to be so and he cannot be reformed' - Balance sheet of aggravating and mitigating circumstances approach invoked on a case-by-case basis had not worked sufficiently well so as to remove vice of arbitrariness from our capital sentencing system, it could be safely said that threshold of 'the rarest of rare cases' had been most variedly and inconsistently applied by various HC as also this Court - It does appear that in view of inherent multitude of possibilities, aggravating and mitigating circumstances approach had not been effectively implemented - In cases such as present, there was considerable uncertainty on punishment to be awarded in capital offences - whether it should be life imprisonment or death sentence, SC opined that due to this uncertainty, awarding a sentence of life imprisonment, in cases such as present was not unquestionably foreclosed, therefore, allowed these appeals to extent that death penalty awarded to appellants was converted into a sentence of life imprisonment - Appeals allowed.
