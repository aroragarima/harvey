Supreme Court of India

17 February 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, K.G. Balakrishnan, R.V. Raveendran, P. Sathasivam, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 133; (2010) 3 SCC 571; 2010 (4) ADJ 158; AIR 2010 SC 1476; 2010(2) ALL MR 941; 2010 (6) AWC 6073; 2010 (2) CTC 84; JT 2010 (2) SC 352; 2010 (2) MLJ(Crl) 169; 2010 (2) RCR(Criminal) 141; 2010 (1) RLW 822; 2010(2) SCALE 467; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 979; 2010 (1) UC 444

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Distribution Of Legislative Powers

Summary: (A) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Power of Judicial Review - Enforcement of fundamental rights - Held, in view of the constitutional scheme and the jurisdiction conferred on SC u/art. 32 and on the HCs u/art. 226 of the Constitution the power of judicial review being an integral part of the basic structure of the Constitution, no Act of Parliament can exclude or curtail the powers of the Constitutional Courts with regard to the enforcement of fundamental rights - Such a power is essential to give practicable content to the objectives of the Constitution embodied in Part III and other parts of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(B) Constitution - Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946, s. 6 - Judicial Review - Powers of the Constitutional Courts to issue direction to CBI to investigate a cognizable offence - HC, in exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 226 of the Constitution, directed the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI), established under the Special Police Act, to investigate a cognizable offence, which was alleged to have taken place within the territorial jurisdiction of a State - Whether course adopted by the HC without the consent of the State was incompatible with the federal structure as also the doctrine of separation of powers between the three organs of the State, embodied in the Constitution? - Held, when the Special Police Act itself provides that subject to the consent by the State, the CBI can take up investigation in relation to the crime which was otherwise within the jurisdiction of the State Police, the court can also exercise its constitutional power of judicial review and direct the CBI to take up the investigation within the jurisdiction of the State - Power of the HC u/art. 226 of the Constitution cannot be taken away, curtailed or diluted by s. 6 of the Special Police Act - Irrespective of there being any statutory provision acting as a restriction on the powers of the Courts, the restriction imposed by s. 6 of the Special Police Act on the powers of the Union, cannot be read as restriction on the powers of the Constitutional Courts - Therefore, exercise of power of judicial review by the HC, would not amount to infringement of either the doctrine of separation of power or the federal structure - Further, if in terms of Entry 2 of List II of the Seventh Schedule on the one hand and Entry 2A and Entry 80 of List I on the other, an investigation by another agency is permissible subject to grant of consent by the State concerned, there is no reason as to why, in an exceptional situation, court would be precluded from exercising the same power which the Union could exercise in terms of the provisions of the Statute - Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Power of Judicial Review - Enforcement of fundamental rights - Doctrine of separation of power - Can the doctrine of separation of powers curtail the power of judicial review, conferred on the Constitutional Courts even in situations where the fundamental rights are sought to be abrogated or abridged on the ground that exercise of such power would impinge upon the said doctrine? - Held, fundamental rights, enshrined in Part III of the Constitution, are inherent and cannot be extinguished by any Constitutional or Statutory provision - Any law that abrogates or abridges such rights would be violative of the basic structure doctrine - Actual effect and impact of the law on the rights guaranteed under Part III has to be taken into account in determining whether or not it destroys the basic structure - Order accordingly.

(D) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Violation of federal structure - Separation of power - Held, if the federal structure is violated by any legislative action, the Constitution takes care to protect the federal structure by ensuring that Courts act as guardians and interpreters of the Constitution and provide remedy u/arts. 32 and 226 of the Constitution, whenever there is an attempted violation - In the circumstances, any direction by the SC or the HC in exercise of power u/art. 32 or 226 to uphold the Constitution and maintain the rule of law cannot be termed as violating the federal structure - Order accordingly.

(E) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Restriction on power of the Judiciary - Held, restriction on the Parliament by the Constitution and restriction on the Executive by the Parliament under an enactment, do not amount to restriction on the power of the Judiciary u/art. 32 and 226 of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(F) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32 and 226 - Self-imposed limitations on the exercise of Constitutional powers - Issuing a direction to the CBI to conduct investigation - Held, extra-ordinary power must be exercised sparingly, cautiously and in exceptional situations where it becomes necessary to provide credibility and instil confidence in investigations or where the incident may have national and international ramifications or where such an order may be necessary for doing complete justice and enforcing the fundamental rights - Order accordingly.
