Sunil Kundu and others v State of Jharkhand

Supreme Court of India

09 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 207; (2013) 4 SCC 422; 2013 (3) CGLJ 583; 2013 CRLJ 2339; JT 2013 (6) SC 599; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 147; 2013(5) SCALE 433; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 924; 2013 (2) WLN(SC) 74

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Pelting Stones

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302/34 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability -Appellants allegedly committed offence of murder - Appellants were convicted u/ss. 302/34 of IPC - Hence instant appeal - Whether conviction of appellants was sustainable - Held, SC in one case had stated that all the eye-witnesses had categorically stated that the deceased was injured by the use of firearm, whereas the medical evidence specifically indicated that no firearm injury was found on the deceased - SC held that while appreciating variance between medical evidence and ocular evidence, oral evidence of eye-witnesses had to get priority as medical evidence is basically opinionative -

But, when the evidence of the eye-witnesses was totally inconsistent with the evidence given by the medical experts then evidence was appreciated in different perspective by the courts - It was observed that when medical evidence specifically ruled out the injury claimed to have been inflicted as per the eye-witnesses' version, then the court could draw adverse inference that the prosecution version was not trustworthy - Kapildeo Mandal & Ors. v. State of Bihar, 2007 Indlaw SC 1191, relied on - It was argued that the accused were absconding and, therefore, adverse inference needed to be drawn against them - It was well settled that absconding by itself did not prove the guilt of person - Person might run away due to fear of false implication or arrest - Sk. Yusuf v. State of West Bengal, 2011 Indlaw SC 385, relied on - It was also true that the plea of alibi taken by the accused had failed - Defence witnesses examined by them were disbelieved -When the prosecution was not able to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt it could not take advantage of the fact that appellants did not probablise their defence - It was well settled that the prosecution should stand or fall on its own feet - It could not draw support from the weakness of the case of appellants, if it had not proved its case beyond reasonable doubt - It was true that acquitting the accused merely on the ground of lapses or irregularities in the investigation of case would amount to putting premium on the deprecable conduct of incompetent investigating agency at the cost of the victims which might lead to encouraging perpetrators of crimes - SC had laid down that the lapses or irregularities in the investigation could be ignored subject to rider - They could be ignored only if despite their existence, evidence on record would bear out the case of the prosecution and the evidence was of sterling quality - If the lapses or irregularities did not go to the root of the matter, if they did not dislodge the substratum of the prosecution case, they could be ignored - In instant case, the lapses were very serious - PW-5 was pancha to the seizure panchnama under which weapons and other articles were seized from the scene of offence and also to the inquest panchnama - Independent panchas were not examined - Investigating officer (IO) had stated in his evidence that the seized articles were not sent to the court along with the charge-sheet - They were kept in the Malkhana of the police station - IO had admitted that the seized articles were not sent to the Forensic Science Laboratory (FSL) - No explanation was offered by him about the missing sanha entries - IO's evidence on that aspect was evasive - Clothes of the deceased were not sent to FSL - IO admitted that no seizure list of the clothes of the deceased was made - Blood group of the deceased was not ascertained - No link was established between the blood found on the seized articles and the blood of the deceased - It was difficult to make allowance for such gross lapses - Besides, the evidence of eye-witnesses did not inspire confidence - Grave suspicion was created about the involvement of the accused in the offence of murder - It was well settled that suspicion, however strong, could not take the place of proof - In such case, benefit of doubt should go to appellants - Appeals disposed of.
