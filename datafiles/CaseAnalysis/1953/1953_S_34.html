Â Smt. Kalawati and Another v State of Himachal Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

19 January 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Chandrasekhara Aiyar, M. Patanjali Sastri, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 132; AIR 1953 SC 131; 1953 CRLJ 668; [1953] S.C.R. 546

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 20(3), 132(1) and 134(1)(c) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 164, 237 and 342 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 14, 201 and 302 - Admissibility of confession - Held, a confession has to be voluntary before it can be used against a person making it, and a Magistrate is bound to satisfy himself that it is being made without any inducement threat or promise - No person accused of a crime is bound to make a confession, and if there is any compulsion or threat, it has to be ruled out as irrelevant and inadmissible - Art. 20(3) does not apply at all to a case where confession is made without any inducement, threat or promise - It is true that a retracted confession has only little value as basis for a conviction, and that confession of one accused is not evidence against a co-accused tried jointly for same offence, but can only be taken into consideration against him - This deals with its probative value and has nothing to do with any repugnancy to Constitution - Order accordingly.
