Upendra Pradhan v State of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

28 April 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 305; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 2444; 2015 CRLJ 2878; 2015 (3) JCC 1631; JT 2015 (4) SC 511; 2015 (2) MLJ(Crl) 547; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 907; 2015(5) SCALE 634; [2015] 5 S.C.R. 214

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Benefit Of Doubt, Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970, Juvenile Justice Model Rules, 2007

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss.307, 302, 34 - Murder - Conviction - Juvenility of accused - 'S' and his younger brother 'B' were having title deeds of their lands standing in their names jointly - Dispute arose between 'S' and his younger brother 'B' when 'B' did not yield to request of 'S' to hand over Patta of their lands to procure loan as Patta was with mother - 'S' and his family/accused persons each armed with axe and lathi assaulted 'B' and his family and 'B' and his three children died - On investigation, charges framed against appellants u/ss.307 and 302 r/w s.34 of IPC - Trial Court convicted and sentenced appellants as per charges and acquitted 'U'/appellant child of 'S' - On appeal, HC held that accusation against each of appellants had been proved beyond all reasonable doubt and in matter of death sentence it awarded sentence of imprisonment for life - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, in case there are two views which can be culled out from the perusal of evidence and application of law, the view which favors the accused should be taken. Argument of appellant that HC has erred in reversing the acquittal of accused appellant, stands good. Trial Court was right in granting him benefit of doubt. Testimonies of interested witnesses are of great importance and weightage. No man would be willing to spare real culprit and frame an innocent person. HC was wrong in interfering with order of acquittal of 'U' passed by Trial Court. True copy of certificate issued by School which shows that accused appellant was less than 18 years on date of occurrence. As per School Certificate, date of birth of appellant is 8-7-1976. Age of appellant on date of occurrence 28-8-1993, was 17 years, 1 month & 20 days. Juvenility of accused appellant is at time of commission of crime. As stated earlier, age of accused appellant was less than 18 years at time of incident. Appellant has undergone about 8 years in jail. Appellant falls within definition of 'juvenile' u/s.2(k) of Act 2000. On question of sentencing, accused appellant is to be released. In addition to fact that he was a juvenile at time of commission of offence, accused appellant is entitled to benefit of doubt. Therefore, conviction order passed by HC is not sustainable in law. Assuming without conceding, that even if conviction is upheld, appellant has undergone almost 8 years of sentence, which is more than maximum period of three years prescribed u/s.15 of 2000 Act. Appeal allowed.
