Krishnegowda and another v State of Karnataka by Arkalgud Police

Supreme Court of India

28 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 220; AIR 2017 SC 1657; 2017 CRLJ 2830; 2017 (2) JCC 1424; 2017 (4) KarLJ 385; 2017 (2) RCR(Criminal) 432; 2017(4) SCALE 42

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: FIR, Benefit Of Doubt, Reasonable Doubt, Place Of Occurrence, Version, Blood, Doctor, Medical Evidence, Arms Act, 1959, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer, Seizure

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 143, 147, 148, 324, 302 - Arms Act, 1959 - Contradiction between ocular and medical evidence - Lapses in investigation - IO registered case against accused no.1 and five others for offence punishable u/ss.143, 147, 148 and 324 read with s.149 of IPC and later, charge u/s. 302 of IPC was added against accused - However, upon conclusion of trial, Trial Court acquitted accused persons on ground that prosecution could not prove the guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt - Aggrieved, State filed appeals before HC and upon scrutinizing order of acquittal passed by Trial Court, HC convicted accused nos. 2 and 5 for offence u/s. 302/34 of IPC and accused nos. 1 to 5, 9 and 10 for offence u/s. 324/149 of IPC and accused nos. 6,7,9,10 and 13 for offence u/s.323 of IPC - Hence, instant appeals by accused - Whether HC was justified in reversing order of acquittal passed by Trial Court.

Held, evidence of eyewitnesses is only consistent on the aspect of injuries inflicted on the deceased but on all other factors there were lots of contradictions which go to the root of the matter. Further, even with regard to seizure of weapons, there were two versions though consistently it was stated that MOs were not sealed and doctor observed that those were not sent to her for opinion. Under such circumstances, it is doubtful whether MOs which were presented before Court were the ones seized from scene of offence. As such, adverse inference has to be drawn on prosecution case. Further, even with regard to exact time when the incident had happened, with regard to the people present at the scene of offence, the time of police reaching the scene of offence, place of registering the complaint, there were lot of variations. Further, eyewitnesses have not mentioned the names of accused nos.7 to 13 in FIR and subsequent addition of their names, clearly demonstrates that it was an afterthought, only to implicate them. Further, there were latches in investigation and one of the major lacunas in the case was non-mentioning of the names of accused nos. 2 and 5 by PW1 to police at the earliest point of time. Furthermore, when accused no.1 had sustained injuries and complaint was given by his father, prosecution has to explain the injuries and said lapses on part of IO assume greater importance and are fatal to case of prosecution. IO also suppressed the fact that there was a direct evidence to seize the gun used by deceased and register a complaint against the deceased under relevant provisions of the Act which is evident from endorsement made on Exhibit No.P22. Hence, considering the material on record, finding of HC that ocular evidence and medical evidence are in conformity with the case of prosecution to convict the accused could not be accepted. As such, appeals are allowed and judgment of HC is set aside and order of acquittal passed by Trial Court is re-affirmed. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - When there is clear contradiction between the medical and the ocular evidence coupled with severe contradictions in the oral evidence and clear latches in investigation, then accused has to get the benefit of doubt.
