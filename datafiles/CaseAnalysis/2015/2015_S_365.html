Choith Nanikram Harchandani and another v State of Maharashtra and others

Supreme Court of India

20 November 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Sapre, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 958; 2016 (3) CalLT(SC) 18; 2016 (1) JCC 198; JT 2015 (12) SC 223B; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 214; [2015] 11 S.C.R. 80

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Detention Order, Challenged, Detenu, Advisory Board, Detaining Authority, Conservation Of Foreign Exchange And Prevention Of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, s.3(1) - Detention order - Challenged - Petitioner filed instant petition and challenged the order of prevention detention issued by respondent against him u/s. 3(1) of the Act.

Held, since the Detaining Authority was represented by the officers at the time of hearing of the petitioner's case before the Advisory Board, the petitioner too was entitled to be represented through legal practitioner. Since no such opportunity was afforded to the petitioner though claimed by him, he was denied an opportunity of a fair hearing before the Advisory Board, which eventually resulted in passing an adverse order. It is not necessary for SC to examine other grounds though taken by the petitioner in these proceedings because one ground once held made out is enough to quash the impugned order of detention. Therefore, SC expresses no opinion on any other ground taken in the petition and raised in the arguments. Respondents submitted that the petitioner being a habitual offender and, therefore, he should not be shown any indulgence in this case is concerned, SC viewed, that SC cannot uphold the detention order on such submission and once the legal infirmity pointed out by the petitioner in the proceedings before the Advisory Board is held made out then the impugned detention order is rendered bad in law and cannot be sustained. If the petitioner is a habitual offender and has past criminal record, as alleged by the respondents, it was all the more necessary for the respondents to have followed in letter and spirit the procedure laid down in A. K. Roy, Etc. v Union of India and Anr. 1981 Indlaw SC 381, before passing the impugned order of detention. It was, however, not done. Hence, impugned order of detention is quashed by issuance of writ of certiorari. Petition allowed.
