Â Seth Banarsi Das v Wealth Tax Officer, Special Circle Meerut

Supreme Court of India

08 December 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, S.M. Sikri, R.S. Bachawat, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 166; AIR 1965 SC 1387; [1965] 56 ITR 224; [1965] 2 S.C.R. 355

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: 'Individualsâ, Meaning and scope, S.3 of the Act

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Wealth-tax Act, 1957, s. 3 - Constitution of India, 1950, Entry 86 - 'Individuals' - Meaning and scope - S.3 of the Act - Constitutionality - Appellants/Hindu undivided families (HUF) were charged levy of tax u/s. 3 of the Act - Appellants challenged the validity of said charge before HC on ground that s. 3 of the Act was ultra vires - HC rejected the appellants contention and upheld the validity of impugned provision - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contended that word 'individuals' used in Entry 86 of Constitution could not take in HUF - Further contended that taxes which Parliament was empowered to levy under Entry 86 of Constitution could be levied only on individuals and not on groups of individuals, and on companies - Whether s. 3 of the Act in so far as it purports to levy a charge of wealth tax in respect of net wealth of a HUF at specified rate, was valid - Held, if object of making Entry 86 of Constitution was to enable Parliament to levy taxes on capital value of assets, how could it be said to be reasonable to introduce a limitation on denotation of word 'individuals' and to say that taxes could not be levied on capital value of assets which belong to groups of individuals - If individuals constitute themselves into a group and such group owns capital assets, it was not easy to understand why value of such assets should not be included within legislative field covered by Entry 86 of Constitution - However, Constitution-makers were fully aware that Hindu citizens of country normally form HUF and if object was to levy taxes on capital value of assets - It was inconceivable that word 'individuals' was introduced in Entry with object of excluding from its scope such a large and extensive area which should be covered by HUF - Impugned section was valid, because Parliament was competent to legislate in respect of HUF under Entry 86 of Constitution - Word 'individual' was narrower than word 'Assessee', it did not mean only a human being, but was wide enough to include a group of persons forming a unit - Entry 86 of Constitution covers cases of HUF, it follows that impugned provision was valid under the said Entry itself - Appeal dismissed.
