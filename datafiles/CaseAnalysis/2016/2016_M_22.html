Yogesh Singh v Mahabeer Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

20 October 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 806; AIR 2016 SC 5160; 2017 CRLJ 291; 2017 (1) JCC 63; JT 2016 (10) SC 332; 2016 (4) RCR(Criminal) 753; 2016(10) SCALE 219; [2016] 7 S.C.R. 713

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Weapon, Child Witness, FIR, Marriage, Omission, Appreciation Of Evidence, Appreciation, Reasonable Doubt, Place Of Occurrence, Version, Prosecution Story, Implicate, Inquest Report, Medical Evidence, Site Plan, Ocular Evidence, Innocent, Interfere, Deposition, Contradiction, Enmity, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 157, 161, 162, 174, 313, 374 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 302, 379 - Murder - Acquittal - Sustainability - Respondents/accused caught hold of deceased and hit him with a Ballam and severed the head of deceased by hitting him with Phawara causing his death - Trial Court convicted accused nos. 1 to 6 for committing the offence u/s. 302 r/w s. 149 IPC and sentenced each of them to undergo RI for life and were also convicted severally u/ss. 147, 148 & 379IPC - Further, accused no. 7 was not found guilty and was acquitted - On appeal, HC allowed appeal and acquitted respondents of the charges u/s. 302 r/w s. 149 IPC - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the findings of fact recorded by HC call for interference in the facts and circumstances of the case.

Held, it was not the case of accused that they were prejudiced by alleged delay in dispatch of FIR to the nearest Magistrate competent to take cognizance of such offence. Further, non-recording of certain relevant entries in the inquest report do not constitute a material defect so grave to throw out prosecution story and the otherwise reliable testimonies of prosecution witnesses that have mostly remained uncontroverted. In instant case, SC do not find any major contradiction either in the evidence of witnesses or any conflict in medical or ocular evidence which would tilt the balance in favour of respondents. The minor improvements, embellishments etc., apart from being far yield of human faculties were insignificant and ought to be ignored since the evidence of witnesses otherwise overwhelmingly corroborate each other in material particulars. HC has also noted that deceased was a person with criminal antecedents and had fired at many persons and hence could have been targeted and killed by any of his enemies. Prosecution has not sought to prove its claim on the basis of circumstantial evidence which as a rule needs to be conclusive, excluding any possible hypothesis of innocence of accused. However, it was not incumbent on prosecution to discharge such burden to rule out every possible hypothesis inconsistent with the guilt of accused or consistent with the guilt of any other person. Thus, view expressed by HC was not a plausible one. Trial Court has correctly analyzed the material on record in factual as well as legal perspectives to arrive at its conclusion. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside and that of Trial Court was restored. Respondents were directed to surrender before Trial Court within a week, failing which Trial Court should take prompt steps to put respondents back in jail to undergo the sentence awarded to them. Appeal allowed.
