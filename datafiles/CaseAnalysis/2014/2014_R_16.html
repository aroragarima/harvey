Radhey Shyam v State of Rajasthan

Supreme Court of India

25 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 111; (2014) 5 SCC 389; JT 2014 (4) SC 271; 2014(3) SCALE 7

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Cries

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant/accused killed his children by cutting their throats - Charge sheet against appellant was filed u/s. 302 IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant u/s. 302 IPC - On revision filed by appellant HC upheld the conviction order of Trial Court - Hence instant appeal - Whether order of conviction was sustainable and whether prosecution was able to prove the case against appellant beyond reasonable doubt -

Held, there were too many drawbacks in prosecution case - Prosecution story to be inherently improbable - If appellant had committed such a heinous crime he would continue to sit in the room - Appellant's first reaction would have been to run away - It was also difficult to appreciate as to how those who had gathered at the scene of offence kept quiet after seeing such a gruesome crime - Reaction of the people would have been to take him to the police station - Prosecution heavily relied on the fact that appellant was alone in the room along with the children and no one else could have gone inside the room to kill the children - In instant case prosecution had not established even a single circumstance beyond doubt - SC was of the opinion that prosecution has suppressed the genesis of the case - Incident does not appear to have happened in the manner in which the prosecution wants the court to believe it had happened - Story of alleged dying declaration of one of the child R was not established - Discovery of blade from the almirah was not established and was rightly rejected by the Trial Court - Panch witness turned hostile - Recoveries were not established - PW13, wife of appellant, categorically stated that appellant loved his children and he was a normal person - Appellants conduct prior to the incident does not suggest guilty mind - Injuries suffered by children were so grave that the children would have raised cries - Nobody stated that they heard any cries - Story that the child witness saw the incident through the hole was difficult to digest - No independent witness was examined and the evidence of all the witnesses was replete with inconsistencies - All the circumstances made the prosecution story doubtful - Appellant, therefore, should be given benefit of doubt - Impugned order was set aside - Appeal disposed of.
