Â Messrs West Ramnad Electric Distribution Company Limited v State of Madras

Supreme Court of India

02 May 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K. Subba Rao, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 508; AIR 1962 SC 1753; 1964 (1) MLJ(SC) 126; 1964 (1) SCJ 484; [1963] 2 S.C.R. 747

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Competence Of The Legislature

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Madras Electricity Supply Undertakings (Acquisition) Act, 1954, s. 24 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) (As stood before 4th Constitutional Amendment) - Passing of Act, 1949 by Madras Legislature, providing for acquisition of electricity supply undertaking in province of Madras - Payment of compensation as per provision of said Act - Appellant's undertaking sought to be acquired - Validity of Act 1949 challenged - In Rajamundry Electric Supply Corporation Ltd. v. State of Andhra Pradesh, Court held Act 1949 to be ultra vires Govt. of India Act, 1935 - Constitution came into force - Position of legislative competence of Madras Legislature in respect of compulsory acquisition of commercial or industrial undertakings for public purposes has been materially altered, thereby - Another Act passed by Madras Legislature which incorporated main provisions of earlier Act of 1949 and purported to validate action taken under said earlier Act - Undertaking which had been taken over by respondent earlier continued to be in possession of respondent - Validating Act challenged on various grounds - (A) First ground,that there is no specific or express provision in Act which makes Act retrospective and order which recited that appellant concern shall vest in Govt. amounted to a notification which must be held to be a law u/art. 13 of Constitution and thus notification was invalid for two reasons, because it has been issued under provisions of an Act which was void as being beyond legislative competence of Madras Legislature, and it was void for additional reason that before it was issued, Constitution of India had come into force and it offended against provisions of art. 31 of Constitution, and so, art. 13(2) applied - Broad features of Act, its scheme and provisions especially s. 24, perused - Held, s. 24 is a saving and validating provision and it clearly intends to validate actions taken under relevant provisions of earlier Act which was invalid from start - (B) Whether s. 24 postulates existence of an antecedent law, before a citizen is deprived of his property in contravention of art. 31(1) of Constitution? - Held, contention not tenable - If Act is retrospective in operation and s. 24 has been enacted for purpose of retrospectively validating actions taken under provisions of earlier Act, it must follow by very retrospective operation of relevant provisions that at time when impugned notification was issued, these provisions were in existence - (C) Whether the power of legislature to make laws retrospective cannot validly be exercised so as to take care of contravention of fundamental rights retrospectively? - Held, for this contention reliance was placed on Deep Chand v. State of Uttar Pradesh, (1959 Indlaw SC 254) - Court observed that question for decision was whether or not it is competent to legislature to pass a law retrospectively to validate actions taken under a void Act and question was not decided in Deep Chand's case - Case relied upon distinguished - Further held, if a law is invalid for reason that it has been passed by a legislature without legislative competence, and action is taken under its provisions, said action can be validated by a subsequent law passed by same legislature after it is clothed with necessary legislative power - If legislature can by retrospective legislation cure invalidity in actions taken in pursuance of laws which were void for want of legislative competence and can validate such action by appropriate provisions, it is difficult to see why same power cannot be equally effectively exercised by legislature in validating actions taken under law which are void for reason that they contravened fundamental rights - (D) Whether s. 5 of Act which provides for payment of compensation to licensees whose undertakings are taken over, is invalid because it is inconsistent with art. 31(2) i.e., in name of compensation what had been offered was not a just equivalent? - Held, Scheme of s. 5 which provides for compensation, perused - Further held, in absence of material in support, contention of unjust equivalent compensation cannot be accepted, thus rejected - Appeals dismissed - (E) Constitution - Doctrine of Eclipse - Whether doctrine of eclipse applied to a law which was found to be invalid for reason that it contravened fundamental rights? - Relied on Deep Chand v. State of Uttar pradesh (1959 Indlaw SC 254) - Further held, it did not apply to such a law (Majority) - Applicability of doctrine - In dealing with a question as to applicability of doctrine of eclipse, a distinction has to be drawn between a law which was void either for want of legislative power at time when it was passed, or because it contravened fundamental rights on one hand, and law which was valid when it was passed put subsequently became invalid because of supervening circumstances on other - In latter case, law was valid when it was passed and became invalid because a cloud was cast on its validity by supervening circumstances - That being so, if constitutional amendment subsequently made removes cloud, validity of law is revived - That is effect of application of doctrine of eclipse but there can be no scope for application of said doctrine to a law which is void and non est either for want of legislative competence or because it contravenes fundamental rights - Appeals dismissed.
