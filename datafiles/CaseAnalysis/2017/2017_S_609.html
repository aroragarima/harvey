Balakram v State of Uttarakhand and others

Supreme Court of India

19 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohan M. Shantanagoudar, Dipak Misra, A.M. Khanwilkar

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 321; (2017) 7 SCC 668; AIR 2017 SC 2375; 2017 CRLJ 2933; 2017 (2) MLJ(Crl) 688; 2017 (2) RCR(Criminal) 1058; 2017(5) SCALE 220

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Entries, Diary, Contents, Memory, Case Diary, Police Diary, Right To Information Act, 2005, Refresh, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer, Inquiry

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 201 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 172, 313, 482 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 145, 155(3), 161 - Murder - Admission of Documents - Validity - Respondent no.3/accused along with co accused was charged for murder of deceased in complaint made by petitioner/complainant - Trial Court rejected application of respondent for submission of documents - Aggrieved, respondent no.3 filed application before HC u/s. 482 CrPC which was allowed - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, right of accused to cross examine the police officer with reference to the entries in police diary was very much limited in extent and even that limited scope arises only when Court uses such entries to contradict police officer or when police officer uses it for refreshing his memory and that again was subject to provisions of ss. 145 and 161 of the 1872 Act. Neither accused nor his agent was entitled to call for such case diary and were not entitled to see them during the course of inquiry or trial. The unfettered power conferred by the Statute u/s. 172(2) CrPC on Court to examine the entries of police diary would not allow accused to claim similar unfettered right to inspect the case diary. However, right of accused to cross examine the police officer with reference to the entries in police diary was very much limited in extent and even that limited scope arises only when Court uses such entries to contradict the police officer or when the police officer uses it for refreshing his memory and that again is subject to provisions of ss. 145 and 161 of the 1872 Act. Thus, the denial of right to accused to inspect the case diary cannot be characterized as unreasonable or arbitrary. The confidentiality was always kept in the matter of investigation and it was not desirable to make available the police diary to accused on his demand. HC was not justified in permitting accused to produce certain pages of police diary at the time of cross examination of PW15/IO. Impugned order passed by HC was set aside. Appeal allowed.
