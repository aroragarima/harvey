E. M. Sankaran Namboodiripad v T. Narayanan Nambiar

Supreme Court of India

31 July 1970

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, G.K. Mitter, A.N. Ray

Where Reported

1970 Indlaw SC 320; (1970) 2 SCC 325; (1970) SCC (Cr) 451; AIR 1970 SC 2015; 1970 CRLJ 1670; 1970 KLT 558; 1971 (1) SCJ 631; [1971] 1 S.C.R. 697; 1970 UJ 892

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 129, 134(1)(c) and 215 - Appellant convicted for Contempt of Court - Conviction is based on certain utterances of appellant, when he was Chief Minister, at a press conference - Report of press conference was published following day in some Indian newspapers - Proceedings were commenced in High Court on sworn information of an advocate of HC, based mainly on report in Indian Express - Whether HC was justified in convicting appellants of Contempt of Court? - Chief forms of contempt are insult to Judges, attacks upon them, comment on pending proceedings with a tendency to prejudice fair trial, obstruction to officers of courts, witnesses or the parties, abusing the process of court, breach of duty by officers connected with court and scandalising the Judges or the courts - While it is intended u/art. 19 that there should be freedom of speech and expression, it is also intended that in exercise of right, contempt of court shall not be committed - Each case must be examined on its own facts and the decision must be reached in the context of what was done or said - Law of contempt stems from right of courts to punish by imprisonment or fines persons guilty of words or acts which either obstruct or tend to obstruct administration of justice - Where therefore a person charged judiciary as 'an instrument of oppression' and the judges as 'guided and dominated by class hatred, class interests and class prejudices, instinctively favouring the rich against the poor', it is clearly an attack upon judges calculated to raise a sense of disrespect and distrust of all judicial decisions - Law punishes not only acts which do in fact interfere with courts and administration of justice but also those which have that tendency, that is to say, axe likely to produce a particular result - Held, appellant was guilty of contempt and HC was justified in convicting him - Appeal dismissed.
