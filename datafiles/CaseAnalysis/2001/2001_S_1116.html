State of Rajasthan v Teja Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

07 February 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Santosh Hegde, Shivaraj V. Patil

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20297; (2001) 3 SCC 147; (2001) SCC (Cr) 439; AIR 2001 SC 990; 2001 (1) Crimes 232; 2001 CRLJ 1176; 2001 (1) JCC 147; JT 2001 (2) SC 347; 2001 (1) RCR(Criminal) 698; 2001(1) SCALE 670; 2001 (2) SLT 18; 2001 (1) Supreme 621

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302/34 - Murder - Whether High Court erred in law by setting aside conviction of accused due to prosecution case not being proved beyond reasonable doubt? - Held, examined evidence of three eyewitnesses as also that of Investigating Officer - Perused evidence of Sarpanch (DW1) - Do not find any reason to differ with finding of HC which had every right to reappreciate evidence - HC, in that process, not committed any error - Explanation given by counsel with regard to delay in FIR reaching court not tenable - Requirement of law is that FIR should reach Magistrate concerned without any undue delay - With regard to next argument of appellant's counsel that HC was wrong in assuming that other villagers were sitting with witnesses 6, 7 and 9, assuming that it is an error even then there can be no doubt as could be seen from prosecution case that other villagers whether sitting with witnesses 6, 7 and 9 or not did rush to scene of occurrence, therefore, clear that apart from said eyewitnesses produced by prosecution many other villagers would have at least seen last part of occurrence including escape of accused and accused not being strangers to villagers could have been easily identified by them - By not examining those independent witnesses, prosecution has failed to produce available independent corroborative evidence to support evidence of interested witnesses because of which HC was justified in drawing adverse inference against prosecution - Lack of corroboration of evidence of witnesses 6, 7 and 9, coupled with fact that Witness 6 is admittedly a very old person having problems with his eyes, makes it difficult to believe that he was really in a position to identify accused persons - Evidence of eyewitness (PW9) also becomes suspect because of fact that though he was available in village, his evidence was recorded only after 5 days of crime for which explanation given by investigating officer is not at all satisfactory - Presence of third eyewitness also doubtful - Investigating officer failed to recover her blood stained clothes giving room to a genuine complaint that her presence is also doubtful - These factors coupled with evidence of Sarpanch who has stated that when he was told about incident these eyewitnesses did not mention name of accused persons to him, also makes prosecution case doubtful - Therefore, HC was justified in not placing any reliance on evidence of these 3 eyewitnesses without any independent corroboration and acquitting accused due to a weak prosecution case - Appeal dismissed.
