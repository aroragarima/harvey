Â Zaverbhai Amaidas v State of Bombay

Supreme Court of India

08 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 122; AIR 1954 SC 752; 1954 CRLJ 1822; 1954 SCJ 851; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 799

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1949, s. 7(As amended by Act of 1950) - Bombay Act, 1947, s. 2 - Bombay Food Grains (Regulation of Movement and Sale) Order, 1949, s. 5(1) - Criminal Procedure Code, sch II - Transportation of essential commodity i.e. Juwar without permit, contravening s. 5(1) of Order, 1949 - Trial - Conviction and sentence, by Resident First Class Magistrate(RFCM) - (A) Trial, whether within jurisdiction or whether contravention of s. 5(1) of Order, 1949, is punishable u/s. 2 of Bombay Act, 1947, in which case trial by RFCM would be without jurisdiction or whether it is punishable u/s. 7 of Essential Supplies Act as amended in 1950 in which case, trial by RFCM would be perfectly legal? - Contention raised that RFCM had no jurisdiction to try case, because u/s. 2 of Bombay Act, offence was punishable with imprisonment, which might extend to seven years, and under sch II to Code, it was only Sessions Courts that had jurisdiction to try such offence - Chronological sequence to statutes, referred and noted - Concept of 'repugnancy' u/art. 254(2) of Constitution, 1950 and s. 107(2) of Govt. of India Act, discussion - Act of 1950 is a legislation in respect of same matter as Act, 1947, and to establish repugnancy it was not necessary that one legislation should say 'do' what other legislation says 'don't', and repugnancy might result when both legislation covered same field - (1)Whether question of enhanced penalty under Bombay Act, 1947 was a matter different from that of mere punishment under Essential Supplies Act, 1949 as amended by 1950 Act? - (1) Yes - Question of punishment for contravention of orders under Essential Supplies Act, both under Act, 1947 and under Act, 1950, constitutes a single subject matter and cannot be split up and rule of construction for it is 'when punishment or penalty is altered in degree but not in kind, later provision would be considered as superseding earlier one' - (A) Held, s. 7 of Essential Supplies Act, 1946 as amended by Act of 1950, prevailed over s. 2 of Bombay Act, 1947, and thus trial conducted by RFCM, within jurisdiction - Appeal dismissed.
