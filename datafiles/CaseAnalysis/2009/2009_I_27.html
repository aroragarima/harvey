Indian Oil Corporation Limited and Others v Raja Transport Private Limited

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1066; (2009) 8 SCC 520; 2010 (2) AWC 1332; 2009 (6) CompLJ 376; 2009 (8) MLJ(SC) 885; 2009 (4) RCR(Civil) 705; 2009(11) SCALE 672; [2009] 13 S.C.R. 510

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Judge As Arbitrator

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration & Conciliation Act, 1996, s.11(6) - Appointment of arbitrator - Legality - Agreement executed between appellant and respondent - Respondent was appointed as appellant dealer for retail sale of petroleum products - Agreement provided for settlement of disputes by sole arbitration of the Director, Marketing of the appellant (Corporation) or of some officer of the Corporation who may be nominated by the Director Marketing - Appellant terminated dealership of respondent - Respondent filed suit before Civil Court - Appellant objected to that and sought reference to arbitration in terms of arbitration agreement - Respondent issued a notice by which respondent notified appellant that it was not willing for appointment of arbitrator in terms of agreement and that both should therefore hold discussions to decide upon an independent arbitrator - Thereafter, respondent filed an application u/s.11(6) of Act before Chief Justice of HC for appointment of independent arbitrator - Chief Justice appointed a retired Judge as Arbitrator, instead of person named in Arbitration Agreement - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether Chief Justice was justified in assuming that when an employee of one of parties to dispute is appointed as an arbitrator, he will not act independently or impartially? - No bar under Act, for an arbitration agreement providing for an employee of a government/ statutory corporation/public sector undertaking (which is a party to contract), acting as Arbitrator - Fact that named arbitrator is an employee of one of parties is not ipso facto a ground to raise a presumption of bias or partiality of lack of independence on his part - Held, a person being an employee of one of parties (which is state or its instrumentality) cannot per se be a bar to his acting as an Arbitrator - Chief Justice was not justified in his assumption of bias - (B) In what circumstances, Chief Justice or his designate can ignore appointment procedure or named arbitrator in arbitration agreement, to appoint an arbitrator of his choice? - Held, if circumstances exist, giving rise to justifiable doubts as to independence and impartiality of person nominated, or if other circumstances warrant appointment of an independent arbitrator by ignoring procedure prescribed, Chief Justice or his designate may, for reasons to be recorded ignore designated arbitrator and appoint someone else - (C) Whether respondent had taken necessary steps for appointment of arbitrator in terms of agreement, and appellant had failed to act in terms of agreed procedure, by not referring dispute to its Director (Marketing) for arbitration? - When dispute arose, respondent did not seek arbitration, but went to civil court - It was appellant who sought reference to arbitration in terms of arbitration agreement - It was respondent who failed to act in terms of agreed procedure and not appellant - Notice given by respondent could not be construed as a valid demand for arbitration - Held, finding of Chief Justice that non-compliance with such request would enable respondent to appoint an independent arbitrator, is clearly illegal - No justification for appointing an independent arbitrator - Appeal allowed.
