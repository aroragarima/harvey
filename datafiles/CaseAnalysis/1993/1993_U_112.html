Subhash Photographics v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 May 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

B.P. Jeevan Reddy, N. Venkatachala

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1373; (1993) Supp3 SCC 323; AIR 1993 SCW 2871; 1993 (2) CCR 234; 1993 (48) ECR 234; 1993 (66) E.L.T. 3; JT 1993 (4) SC 116; 1993(2) SCALE 909; [1993] 3 S.C.R. 654

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Tariff Act, 1934, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949

Summary: Customs - Classification - Customs Tariff Act, 1975, ss. 2 and 3, Chaps. 90 and 98 - Indian Tariff Act, 1934 - Indian Tariff (Amendment) Act, 1949 - Bombay Prohibition Act, 1949 - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 25, 156 and 157 - Project Imports Regulations, 1986, regn. 1, 2 and 3 - Central Boards of Revenue Act, 1963 - (A) Whether photographic machinery imported by appellants falls under Customs Tariff Heading No. 98 .01 and hence appellant is entitled to a concessional rate of duty? - Character of rules and regulations made u/ss. 156 and 157 respectively is same, both constitute delegated legislation - Regulations are subject to an additional limitation viz, they should not be contrary to rules made u/s. 156 - Purpose of sub-section (2) in both sections is inter alia to allocate certain matters to each of them exclusively subject to these subsections, both delegates can exercise power vested in them for carrying out purposes of Act - Chap. note (2) expressly states that expressions used in Heading 98.01 shall have meaning assigned to them in said regulations in accordance with said Chap. Note, Project Imports Regulations have been made excluding 'establishments designed to offer services of any description' from purview of 'industrial plant' and if said regulations are good and valid, there can be no escape from what they say - Held, photographic equipment does not fall within ambit of 'industrial plant' - Express power conferred by Chap. Note (2) of Chap. 98 cannot be curtailed or abridged with reference to alleged legislative practice relating to regulation-making power, assuming that such a practice is established and is relevant - (B) Whether Chap. Note (2) amounts to excessive delegation of legislative power? - Chap. 98 provides a concessional tariff inter alia to industrial plant - Expression 'industrial plant' is a term of wide connotation, all kind of industrial plants may not require to be encouraged; decisions of this nature have to be made from time to time - Parliament cannot obviously do this, it has, therefore, left function to board which is in immediate direct charge of administration of Act, along with and subject to guidance of Central Govt. - Held, applying principles of Avinder Singh case [1978 Indlaw SC 87], it cannot be said that Parliament has, by empowering board to define expression 'industrial plant' occurring in Chap. 98, delegated its essential legislative function - Power conferred by Chap. Note (2) is undoubtedly different from power of exemption conferred by s. 25, it makes little difference in principle that while an exemption notification is required to be laid on floor of Parliament, Regulations made u/s. 157 are not so required, absence of such requirement does not mean absence of control by Parliament over acts of delegate nor it is that by excluding industrial systems meant for establishments designed to offer services of any description, Board has travelled beyond - (C) Whether G O Ms No. 1312 D/- 02-12-1977 involved a major change of policy, which was a legislative function and therefore beyond competence of a subordinate legislating body? - Held, whenever there is a switch over from 'private sector' to 'public sector' it does not necessarily follow that a change of policy requiring express legislative sanction is involved, therefore, Chap. Note (2) cannot be faulted as an instance of excessive delegation of essential legislative function nor can Project Imports Regulations be faulted on ground of travelling beyond purview of statute - Appeals dismissed.
