Rameshbhai Chandubhai Rathod v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

27 April 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 569; (2009) 5 SCC 740; (2009) 2 SCC (Cr) 881; 2009 (3) G.L.R. 2319; 2009 (2) MLJ(Crl) 859; 2009 (3) RCR(Criminal) 618; 2009(6) SCALE 469; [2009] 6 S.C.R. 1055

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Bride Burning, Mitigating Circumstance, Mitigating Factor, Extra Judicial Confession, Blood Stains, Non-Explanation, Accused's Statement, On conviction appeal dismissed, on sentence matter referred to larger bench

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 363, 366, 376, 397 and 302 - Kidnap - Rape - Murder - Minor girl of 10 years - Conviction - Upheld by High Court - Circumstantial evidences - 'Last seen theory' - Accused/appellant, a watchman of apartment wherein deceased and family lived - Watchman was residing in an apartment opposite to that of deceased's - Deceased went missing - Missing report - Friend of complainant/father informed him that his old servant(PW24) had seen appellant taking the deceased with him on his cycle and when such servant shouted at him, appellant had not stopped - Appellant's search began - Appellant was than spotted by brother in law of complainant sitting at a place - On being asked he revealed as to that he raped and killed the deceased - Further he revealed the same thing to others including complainant - Place where dead body was lying led to by appellant - Recoveries made as to knife and on disclosure, cycle, school bag of deceased recovered - Circumstances highlighted by Trial Court and HC, noted and perused; contentions heard - Court noted that circumstances thus highlighted unerringly pointed to the accused to be the author of crime - Categorical statement of PWs 4, 5, 6, 17 and 24 as to that deceased was seen in the company of accused just before the time of death; Extra judicial confession was recorded in absence of police - Deliberation entered into on 'last seen theory' and 'circumstantial evidence' - Conviction recorded by TC and HC, upheld.

(B) Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 235(2) r/w 354(3), 366-370 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Offence of rape and murder - Death penalty - Whether proper or whether case is in the category of 'rarest of rare cases' to warrant death penalty? - Disagreement between Dr. Arijit Pasayat, J and Asok Kumar Ganguly, J - (A) Per Pasayat, J. - 'Principle of Proportionality', proportion between 'crime' and 'punishment' - Sentencing system; new challenges encountering society; cross cultural conflict - Undue sympathy to impose inadequate sentence would do more harm to the justice system to undermine the public confidence in the efficacy of law and society could not long endure under such serious threats, thus the duty of Court - Sentence; discretion of Judge; to permit sentences that reflect more subtle considerations of culpability that are raised by the special facts of each case; observation made, that yet in practice sentences are determined largely by other considerations like correctional needs of the perpetrator, desirability of keeping him out of circulation, and sometimes even the tragic results of his crime, thus inevitably these considerations cause a departure from just desert as the basis of punishment and create cases of apparent injustice that are serious and widespread - Plea that as conviction is based on circumstantial evidence therefore death sentence should not be awarded, held to be clearly unsustainable - Held, case falls in 'rarest of rare cases' category and warrants death sentence - Award of sentence upheld - (B) Per Ganguly J. - Case being dependent upon circumstantial evidence, each circumstance recorded by HC meticulously examined - Vital discrepancies found esp. in 7th, 8th and 9th circumstance, leaking vital aspects of prosecution as to that appellant was missing; further from deposition of mother of deceased it came out that appellant used to ferry deceased on his bicycle when school rickshaw was not coming, and deceased used to play with appellant's daughter and he never misbehaved with deceased - Extra judicial confession and statement u/s. 313 of CrPC, by appellant - Appellant retracted from his confession and such retraction had not been considered by the HC - HC is expected to consider such cases with greater caution and circumspection - Held, appreciation of evidence by HC in affirming death penalty not on proper perspective keeping in mind parameters of 'rarest of rare cases' formulated in Bachan Singh's case - HC compared present case with Dhananjoy Chatterjee's case - Judge noted vital differences in facts of the cases - '..in criminal law one additional or different fact may make a world of difference between the conclusions in two cases or between two accused in the same case' - Deliberation entered upon on 'death penalty' - Test laid down by Justice Krishna Iyer in Ediga Anamma's case referred to - 'Life sentence should be the rule and death sentence is an exception' - Explanation of principles of sentencing policy u/s. 354(3) of CrPC in Bachan Singh's case, noted - S. 354(3) must be read conjointly with s. 235(2); such provisions hold signal significance in death penalty cases - Prosecution has very heavy and onerous burden(beyond reasonable doubt) for making out a case of death sentence, and that burden has to be discharged by demonstrating the existence of aggravating circumstances and the consequential absence of mitigating circumstances - Further in such exercise accused must be given a real and effective chance of rebuttal and to disprove the existence of aggravating circumstance and for that accused must be separately heard on the sentence to be imposed where he can demonstrate all the mitigating circumstances; those must be weighed in the balance and they must receive a liberal and expansive interpretation by Court - On perusal of records of case no such chance appeared to have been given by TC, as it had returned finding of guilt on 18-05-2005 and on that day only allegedly heard the appellant on sentence and imposed death - Further no burden discharged by prosecution - Statutory obligation of Court in a case where both ss. 235(2) r/w 354(3) apply as per law laid in Bachan Singh's case emphasised; mandate of art. 141 of Constitution cannot be ignored either by the TC or HC - Regardless of whether accused asks for such chance of hearing, same must be offered to the accused and an adequate opportunity for bringing materials on record must be given to him esp. in case where s. 354(3) applies - Only thereafter can 'special reasons' as mandated by 354(3) be recorded by the Court - Provisions held to be violated by TC, death sentence awarded thus is illegal - Duties cast on HC while dealing with reference for confirmation of death penalty u/ss. 366-370 as pointed out in Bachan Singh's case, noted and deliberated upon - HC in instant case failed to correct flawed sentencing procedure followed by TC, thus error was committed in confirming death sentence which led to miscarriage of justice - Judge laid down that 'fairness, justice and reasonableness which constitute the essence of guarantee of life and liberty epitomized in art. 21 of Constitution also pervades the sentencing policy in ss. 235(2) and 354(3) of Code'; these two provisions virtually assimilate the concept of 'procedure established by law' within the meaning of art. 21 - Strict compliance is thus required of ss. 235(2) and 354(3) - Expression 'rarest of rare cases', a loaded expression, not to be trifled with; it is pregnant with respect for the inviolability of human life - Word 'rare' is used twice, and once in a superlative sense, thus its significance cannot be watered down on a perceived notion of a 'cry for justice' - In instant case appellant was a young man of 28 years, married with two daughters with no criminal antecedents, his behavior in general was not objectionable and certainly not with deceased; not a threat to society - Held, case not in the category of 'rarest of rare cases' - Death sentence to be altered to LI - Appeal dismissed in part - (C) In view of disagreement between the Judges on point of quantum of sentence matter referred to larger Bench only on question of sentence.
