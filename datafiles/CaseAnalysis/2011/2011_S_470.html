Surendera Mishra v State of Jharkhand

Supreme Court of India

06 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, H.S. Bedi, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 23; (2011) 11 SCC 495; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 623; 2011 CRLJ 1161; 2011 (1) JCC 427; 2011 (1) JhrCR(SC) 219; JT 2011 (1) SC 83; 2011 (1) MLJ(Crl) 776; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 416; 2011(1) SCALE 124; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 133

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 84 and 302 - Arms Act, 1950, s. 27 - Plea of unsound mind - Onus to prove - Appellant was tried for commission of the offence u/s. 302 of the IPC as also s. 27 of the Arms Act - Appellant pleaded that by virtue of unsoundness of mind, the act done by him comes within general exception u/s. 84 of the IPC and, therefore, he cannot be held guilty for the act done by him - Plea was rejected - Trial Court held him guilty on both the counts - On appeal, conviction and sentence was upheld by HC - Hence, present appeal - Whether appellant was a person of unsound mind at the time of the commission of the offence, his act comes within general exception as provided u/s. 84 of the IPC and hence the appellant deserves to be acquitted? - Held, an accused who seeks exoneration from liability of an act u/s. 84 of the IPC is to prove legal insanity and not medical insanity - To establish that acts done are not offence and come within general exception it is required to be proved that at the time of commission of the act, accused by reason of unsoundness of mind was incapable of knowing that his acts were wrong or contrary to law - In the present case, conduct of the appellant subsequent to the commission of the offence clearly suggested that he knew that whatever he had done was wrong and illegal - Hence, appellant though suffered from certain mental instability even before and after the incident but from that one cannot infer on a balance of preponderance of probabilities that the appellant at the time of the commission of the offence did not know the nature of his act; that it was either wrong or contrary to law - Plea of the appellant does not come within the exception contemplated u/s. 84 of the IPC - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 84 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 105 - Scope and ambit of the s. 84 of the IPC - (A) Meaning of expression 'unsoundness of mind' - Held, expression 'unsoundness of mind' has not been defined in the IPC and it has mainly been treated as equivalent to insanity - However, every person who is suffering from mental disease is not ipso facto exempted from criminal liability - (B) Onus to prove unsoundness of mind - Held, in law, the presumption is that every person is sane to the extent that he knows the natural consequences of his act - Burden of proof in the face of s. 105 of the Evidence Act is on the accused - Though the burden is on the accused but he is not required to prove the same beyond all reasonable doubt, but merely satisfy the preponderance of probabilities - Onus has to be discharged by producing evidence as to the conduct of the accused prior to the offence, his conduct at the time or immediately after the offence with reference to his medical condition by production of medical evidence and other relevant factors - However, even if the accused establishes unsoundness of mind, s. 84 of the IPC will not come to its rescue, in case it is found that the accused knew that what he was doing was wrong or that it was contrary to law - In order to ascertain that, it is imperative to take into consideration the circumstances and the behaviour preceding, attending and following the crime - Behaviour of an accused pertaining to a desire for concealment of the weapon of offence and conduct to avoid detection of crime go a long way to ascertain as to whether, he knew the consequences of the act done by him - Appeal dismissed.
