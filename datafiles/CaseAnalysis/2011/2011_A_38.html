C. Venkatachalam and another v Ajitkumar C. Shah and another

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, Mukundakam Sharma, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 554; (2011) 9 SCC 707; 2011 (6) AWC 6488; 2012 (4) Bom.C.R. 776; 2011 (3) CPJ(Sc) 33; 2011 (6) CTC 771; 2011 (5) ESC 777; JT 2011 (10) SC 207; 2012 (1) MahLJ 564; 2011 (6) MLJ(SC) 555; 2012 (1) MPLJ 306; 2011 (2) OLR 913; 2011 (4) RCR(Civil) 234; 2011(9) SCALE 479; [2011] 13 S.C.R. 814

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Consumer Protection; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Right To Practise, Consumer Protection Rules, 1987, Comprehensive, Legislations, Immigration and Asylum Act, 199, Consumer Product Safety Act, Merchandise Marks Act, 1887, Consumer Safety Act, 1978, Maharashtra Consumer Protection Rules, 2000

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Consumer Protection - Practice & Procedure - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Appearance of agents of parties before the Consumer Fora - Permissibility - Complaint was filed before the District Consumer Forum alleging deficiency of service against two tour operators - Opposite parties contended that the authorized agent should not be granted permission to appear on behalf of the complainants as he was not enrolled as an advocate - District Forum held that the authorized agent had no right to act and plead before the Consumer Forum as he was not enrolled as an advocate - State Consumer Commission, by an interim order in appeal proceedings, stayed the hearing of the matters in which authorized agents were appearing before the Consumer Forum - Interim order of State Commission was challenged before the HC - HC, by impugned judgment, held that a party before the District Consumer Forum/State Commission should not be compelled to engage services of an advocate and such right of the party was not inconsistent or in conflict with the provisions of the Advocates Act, 1961 - HC further held that the right of an advocate to practise was not an absolute right but was subject to other provisions of the Act - HC further held that permitting the authorized agents to represent parties to the proceedings before the District Forum/State Commission should not be said to practice law - Whether a person under the purported cover of being an 'agent' could represent large number of persons before the forums created under the Act and the Rules made thereunder - Held, in furtherance of the declared objective in the Act of protecting the consumer against exploitation as well as providing an inexpensive and speedy remedy, the competent authority had framed Rules which enabled the party to appear either personally or through an 'agent' - Provisions of Maharashtra Consumer Protection Rules, 2000 provided that while the advocates had not been debarred from pleading and appearing, the parties had been given an option to either appear personally or be represented by 'duly authorized' agents - Every advocate appointed by a party was an agent, however, the agent as contemplated under the rules need not necessarily be an advocate - Act and Rules had made specific provisions by which the agents had been permitted to plead and appear on behalf of the parties before the Consumer Forums, therefore, to interpret it differently would be contrary to legislative intention - Agent had been defined both in the Consumer Protection Rules, 1987 and under the Maharashtra Consumer Protection Rules, 2000 - Agents had been permitted to appear before the Consumer Forums - Appearance of authorized agents was not inconsistent with s. 33 of the Advocates Act, 1961 - Legislature in its wisdom had granted permission to the authorized agents because most of the cases before the Consumer Forums were small cases of relatively poor people where legal intricacies were not involved and great legal skills were not required, which should be handled by the authorized agents - Other reason was that a large number of litigants may not be able to afford heavy professional fees of trained advocates, therefore, authorized agents had been permitted - It was not really called upon to discern legislative intention because there was specific rule defining the agents and the provisions of permitting them to appear before the Consumer Forums - Agents had been permitted to appear to accomplish the main object of the act of disposal of consumers' complaints expeditiously with no costs or small costs - HC was fully justified in observing that the authorised agents did not practise law when they were permitted to appear before the District Forums and the State Commissions - In the impugned judgment the HC aptly observed that many statutes, such as, Sales Tax, Income Tax and Competition Act also permitted non-advocates to represent the parties before the authorities and those non-advocates should not be said to practise law - On the same analogy those non- advocates who appear before Consumer Fora also should not be said to practise law and hence the view taken by the HC in the impugned judgment was approved - Further, functioning, conduct and behaviour of authorized agents could always be regulated by the Consumer Forums - Advocates were entitled as of right to practise before Consumer Fora but that privilege should not be claimed as a matter of right by anyone else - When the legislature had permitted authorized agents to appear on behalf of the complainant, then the courts should not compel the consumer to engage the services of an advocate - However, direction issued to the National Consumer Commission to frame comprehensive Rules as expeditiously as possible in that regard - View taken by the HC in the impugned judgment could not be said to be erroneous and unsustainable in law - Appeal dismissed.
