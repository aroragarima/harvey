Naresh K. Aggarwala and Co. v Canbank Financial Services Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. SUDARSHAN REDDY

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 336; (2010) 6 SCC 178; AIR 2010 SC 2722; 2010 (3) AWC 2800; 2011 (1) BC 251; 2010 (3) Bom.C.R. 846; 2010 (3) CompLJ 390; JT 2010 (4) SC 511; 2010(4) SCALE 677; [2010] 6 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Capital Market; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Delhi Stock Exchange Rules

Summary: (A) Capital Market - Contract & Commercial - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Appellant, a stock broker, prayed for a money decree against respondent no. 1 in respect of net amount payable arising out of two sets of transactions in shares; (i) two transactions in the shares of Reliance Industries Limited (RIL); (ii) one transaction in respect of Steel Authority of India Limited (SAIL) - Appellant claimed that two contracts dt. 14-2-1992 and 23-3-1992 were entered into between the appellant and respondent no. 1 for purchase of two lakh shares of RIL but respondent no. 1 delivered only one lakh RIL shares - Respondent no. 1 contended that contract was cancelled by the appellant, hence he was not liable to deliver balance one lakh shares - Respondent no. 1 also contended that running account maintained by respondent no.1 shows a sum of Rs.2,53,75,000/- as due from the appellant and appellant filed suit solely with the intention of delaying or avoiding payment of a said sum - It was argued before the Special Court that the transaction was a cash delivery contract - Special Court negated the contention and it was held that there is no evidence to that effect and there is no provision to that effect either in the Act, rules framed by the Delhi Stock Exchange - Therefore cash delivery contract unless it is permitted by the Act, bye laws and regulations of the Stock Exchange is prohibited by the circulars - Special Court held that appellant is not entitled to make any claim in relation to RIL shares and passed a decree against the appellant - Hence, present appeal - (A) Applicability of Circular - In terms of the circular dated 27.6.1969, if the rules made under the act, bye laws and regulations of a recognized Stock Exchange permit contract for cash, hand delivery or special delivery, those types of transactions would also be permitted by the circulars - It is clear from the circular that after issuance of these Circular, transactions into securities by (i) Spot delivery contract; (ii) Contract for cash; (iii) Hand delivery and (iv) Special Delivery are only permitted - It was contended that the circular did not apply to unlisted securities - Held, circular dated 27.6.1969 issued u/s. 16 of the Securities Contract Regulation Act 1956 was in existence and in force - Provisions of the bye-laws of Delhi Stock exchange clearly permits spot delivery transaction, hand delivery transaction and special delivery transaction - Further, it does not make any distinction between listed securities and unlisted securities and therefore it is clear that the Circular will apply to the securities which are not listed on the Stock Exchange - In the present case, admittedly the contract note issued in relation to this transaction by the appellant does not show that it was a spot delivery contract, therefore the transaction was clearly contrary to the circular - Consequently in terms of the provisions of s. 16(2) the transaction was illegal and is not capable of being enforced - Therefore, it was correctly observed by the Special Court that the appellant was not entitled to make any claim either in relation to the Reliance Industries Shares nor in relation to contract for SAIL shares - Further as the appellant is not entitled to claim any amount from the respondent on account of the aforesaid transactions, there is no question of the appellant being entitled to any interest - (B) Liability towards respondent no. 1 - Held, contract with regard to SAIL shares being contrary to law was void ab initio - Therefore, the appellant could not possibly claim anything against the aforesaid SAIL shares on account of any difference in the contracted rate and the rate when the same were listed on the Delhi Stock Exchange - Therefore, the appellant was liable to pay to respondent no.1 for the RIL Shares, the contract dated 14.2.1992 having been cancelled - Thus the Special Court, correctly concluded that the appellant was liable to pay to the respondent no.1 - No cogent reason to interfere or to reduce the amount of interest awarded by the Special Court in the peculiar facts and circumstances of present case Appeal dismissed.

(B) Capital Market - Contract & Commercial - Securities Contract Regulation Act 1956, s. 16 - Burden of proving the continuance of the contract - Held, the Special Court correctly observed that in the absence of pleadings the statement made by the appellant had to be ignored - In view of the fact that respondent No.1 had taken a categorical plea that contract dated 14.2.1992 was cancelled by appellant on the same day, it was for the appellant to produce documentary evidence to show that in his books of accounts the contract had been shown as incomplete - But the appellant failed to produce the necessary evidence - View expressed by the Special Court acceptable view, and does not call for any interference - Appeal dismissed.
