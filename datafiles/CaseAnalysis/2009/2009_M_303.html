State Of Rajasthan v Mohan Lal

Supreme Court of India

16 April 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 470; (2009) 12 SCC 515; AIR 2009 SC 1872; 2009 (237) E.L.T. 435; 2009 (2) RCR(Criminal) 812; 2009(6) SCALE 240; [2009] 6 S.C.R. 406

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Demeanour Of Witness, Power Of The Court, Finding Of Facts, Hearing Of Appeal

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Chapter XXIX, s. 378(1)(2) - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 7, 13(1)(d) r/w 13(2) and 20 - Offence therein - Acquittal by Trial Court - Appeal by State - Dismissal by High Court - Dismissal of appeal whether proper? - Appeal under CrPC - Ambit and scope of the power of an appellate Court in an appeal against an order of acquittal; deliberation entered into by Court - Provisions under old CrPC of 1898, decisions of the Court thereunder, provisions of CrPC, decisions of Court, settled position all gone through by Court - HC has full power to re-appreciate, review and reconsider evidence at large, the material on which the order of acquittal is founded and to reach its own conclusions on such evidence; both questions of fact and of law are open to determination by the HC in an appeal against an order of acquittal: s. 378 CrPC - Double presumption of innocence in favour of accused has to be kept in mind, presumption of his innocence under fundamental principle of criminal jurisprudence, and reinforced presumption of such innocence in view of order of acquittal of Trial Court - Decisions from Sheo Swarup v. R. Emperor (1934) under old CrPC, to Chandrappa and Ors. v. State of Karnataka (2007 (4) SCC 415) referred and discussed - In Chandrappa's case general principle on the subject coming out of all previous decisions were conclusively enumerated - 'Proof beyond reasonable doubt' - Reasonability of Doubts; Doubts must be actual and substantial doubts as to the guilt of the accused persons arising from the evidence, or from the lack of it, as opposed to mere vague apprehensions; A reasonable doubt is not an imaginary, trivial or a merely possible doubt, but a fair doubt based upon reason and common sense. It must grow out of the evidence in the case - There is an unmistakable subjective element in the evaluation of the degrees of probability and the quantum of proof; Forensic probability must, in the last analysis, rest on a robust common sense and, ultimately, on the trained intuitions of the Judge - Held, views taken by Trial Court and HC are reasonable and possible views and there is no perversity therein - Impugned judgments upheld - Appeal dismissed.
