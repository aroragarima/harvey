Â Abhiram Singh and another v C.D. Commachen (Dead) by Lrs. and others

Supreme Court of India

02 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Adarsh Kumar Goel, T.S. Thakur, Uday Umesh Lalit, L. Nageswara Rao, Sharad Arvind Bobde, D.Y. Chandrachud

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 15; (2017) 2 SCC 629; AIR 2017 SC 401; 2017 (1) AWC 652; 2017 (1) Bom.C.R. 710; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 8; JT 2017 (1) SC 196; 2017 (1) KLT 229; 2017 (1) MLJ 522; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 927; 2017(1) SCALE 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election

Keywords: Fundamental Right, Constituency, Agent, Patents Act, 1970, Scheduled Castes, Elections, Vote, Disqualified, Religion, Candidate, Citizens Of India, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, Electorate, Any Other Person, Political Parties, Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, Purity, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Madan B. Lokur, J.

(A) Constitution - Election - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 102(1)(e), 19(1)(a), 191, 191(1), 25, 26, 29, 29(1), 30, 32, 342, 44, 51A - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 11A, 123(3), 123(3A)(2), 123(3A)(3), 123(5), 124(5), 16 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 153A, 295A - Electoral process - Corrupt practices - Vitiation - Appellants filed instant appeal challenging impugned order of HC wherein Constitution Bench noticed an anomalous situation arising out of an amendment to s. 123(3) of the Act in 1961 inasmuch as it appeared that a corrupt practice for the purposes of the Act prior to the amendment could cease to be a corrupt practice after the amendment - Whether s. 123(3) of the Act ought to be given a restricted application since the civil consequence that follows from a corrupt practice under such provision was quite severe.

Held, there was some uncertainty about the correct interpretation of sub-section (3) of s. 123 of the Act. It was not as if the interpretation was well-recognized and settled. That being the position, there was nothing that survives. The provisions of sub-section (3) of s. 123 of the Act were required to be read and appreciated in the context of simultaneous and contemporaneous amendments inserting sub-section (3A) in s. 123 of the Act and inserting s. 153A IPC. Further, read together and for maintaining the purity of electoral process and not vitiating it, sub-section (3) of s. 123 of the Act should be given a broad and purposive interpretation thereby bringing within the sweep of a corrupt practice any appeal made to an elector by a candidate or his agent or by any other person with the consent of a candidate or his election agent to vote or refrain from voting for the furtherance of the prospects of the election of that candidate or for prejudicially affecting the election of any candidate on the ground of the religion, race, caste, community or language of (i) any candidate or (ii) his agent or (iii) any other person making the appeal with the consent of candidate or (iv) the elector. It was a matter of evidence for determining whether an appeal had at all been made to an elector and whether the appeal if made was in violation of the provisions of sub-section (3) of s. 123 of the Act. Matter might be placed before Chief Justice for necessary orders. Reference answered.

S. A. Bobde, J.

(B) Constitution - Election - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 102(1)(e), 19(1)(a), 191, 191(1), 25, 26, 29, 29(1), 30, 32, 342, 44, 51A - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 11A, 123(3), 123(3A)(2), 123(3A)(3), 123(5), 124(5), 16 - Systematic appeal - Applicability - Whether that 'any other person' could be a person not authorized by the candidate to make a 'systematic appeal' for or on his or her behalf or make the 'systematic appeal' without the consent of candidate.

Held, SC agree that the bar u/s. 123(3) of the Act to making an appeal on the ground of religion should not be confined to the religion of candidate because of the word 'his' in that provision. Further, such a construction was not only warranted upon the application of purposive test of interpretation but also on textual interpretation. There seems no valid reason while construing a statute (be it a taxing or penal statute) why both rules of interpretation cannot be applied. Thus, the pronoun in the singular "his" refers to a candidate or his agent or any other person with the consent of a candidate or his election agent and to the voter. However, what was prohibited was an appeal by a candidate etc. to a voter for voting on the ground of his religion i.e. those categories preceding "his". Such construction was fortified by the purposive test. To consider it an unreasonable shrinkage to hold that only an appeal referring to the religion of candidate who made the appeal was prohibited and not an appeal which refers to religion of voter. It was quite conceivable that a candidate makes an appeal on ground of religion but leaves out any reference to his religion and only refers to religion of voter. There was no doubt that Parliament intended an appeal for votes on ground of religion was not permissible whether the appeal made on the ground of religion of the candidate etc. or of the voter. The words "his religion" should be construed as referring to all the categories of persons preceding those words. Reference answered.

T.S. Thakur, J.

(C) Constitution - Election - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 102(1)(e), 19(1)(a), 191, 191(1), 25, 26, 29, 29(1), 30, 32, 342, 44, 51A - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 11A, 123(3), 123(3A)(2), 123(3A)(3), 123(5), 124(5), 16 - Religious considerations - Permissibility - Whether based on grounds of religion, caste, language or community or any other ground an appeal could be permissible under the Act.

Held, Constitutional ethos forbids mixing of religions or religious considerations with the secular functions of State. That necessarily implies that interpretation of any statute should not offend the fundamental mandate under Constitution. SC has in several pronouncements ruled that while interpreting an enactment, Courts should remain cognizant of Constitutional goals and the purpose of the Act and interpret the provisions accordingly. State could and indeed has in terms of s.123(3) of the Act forbidden interference of religions and religious beliefs with secular activity of elections to legislative bodies. An appeal in the name of religion, race, caste, community or language was impermissible under the Act and would constitute a corrupt practice sufficient to annul the election in which such an appeal was made regardless whether the appeal was in the name of the candidate's religion or the religion of the election agent or that of the opponent or that of the voter's. The sum total of s. 123(3) of the Act even after amendment was that an appeal in the name of religion, race, caste, community or language was forbidden even when the appeal might not be in the name of the religion, race, caste, community or language of the candidate for whom it was made. So interpreted religion, race, caste, community or language would not be allowed to play any role in the electoral process and should an appeal be made on any of those considerations, the same would constitute a corrupt practice. Reference answered.

Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, J.

(D) Constitution - Election - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 102(1)(e), 19(1)(a), 191, 191(1), 25, 26, 29, 29(1), 30, 32, 342, 44, 51A - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 11A, 123(3), 123(3A)(2), 123(3A)(3), 123(5), 124(5), 16 - Interpretation of his - Validity - Whether interpretation of s. 123(3) of the Act was belied by the legislative history of statutory provision.

Held, legislature has carefully drafted s. 123(3) of the Act to reach out to a particular corrupt practice, which was even more evident when the ambit of s. 123(3A) of the Act was contrasted with s. 123(3) of the Act. One cannot be read into the other nor can the text of s.123(3) of the Act be widened on the basis of a purposive interpretation. The expression "his" in s. 123(3) of the Act should mean the religion, race, community or language of the candidate in whose favour an appeal to cast a vote was made or that of another candidate against whom there was an appeal to refrain from voting on the ground of religion, race, caste, community or language of that candidate. The Act has undergone several parliamentary amendments. Parliament would be aware of the interpretation which was placed by SC on the provisions of s. 123(3) of the Act. The interpretation which was earlier been placed on s. 123(3) of the Act was correct and certainly does not suffer from manifest error. Nor has it been productive of public mischief. No form of govt. was perfect. The Actual unfolding of democracy and the working of a democratic constitution might suffer from imperfections. But those imperfections cannot be attended to by an exercise of judicial redrafting of a legislative provision. Thus, SC hold that there was no necessity for SC to take a view at variance with what was laid down. The 'his' in s.123(3) of the Act does not refer to religion, race, caste, community or language of voter. 'His' is to be read as referring to religion, race, caste, community or language of the candidate in whose favour a vote was sought or that of another candidate against whom there was an appeal to refrain from voting. Reference answered.
