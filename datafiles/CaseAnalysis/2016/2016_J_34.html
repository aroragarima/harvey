State of Gujarat v Jayrajbhai Punjabhai Varu

Supreme Court of India

11 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R.K. Agrawal, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 491; AIR 2016 SC 3218; 2016 ALL MR (Cri) 3567; 2016 CRLJ 4185; 2016 (3) JCC 1888; JT 2016 (6) SC 423; 2016 (4) MLJ(Crl) 407; 2016(6) SCALE 708; [2016] 3 S.C.R. 265

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Dying Declaration, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Treatment, Kerosene, Unknown Person, Matrimonial, Deposition, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 114 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 201, 302, 307, 34 - Murder - Dying declaration - Sustainability - Victim/deceased was married to accused/respondent - Accused allegedly poured kerosene and set deceased on flame - Respondents(husband and in-laws) were charged for offences punishable u/ss. 302, 201 and 34 IPC - Trial Court convicted husband of deceased u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced him to suffer RI for life while acquitting co-accused - On appeal, DB allowed appeal and acquitted accused - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, burden of proof in criminal law is beyond all reasonable doubt. The prosecution has to prove the guilt of the accused beyond all reasonable doubt and it is also the rule of justice in criminal law that if two views are possible on the evidence adduced in the case, one pointing to the guilt of the accused and the other towards his innocence, the view which is favourable to the accused should be adopted. In the instant case, the evidence available on record and the sole evidence of the father of the deceased as compared to the dying declaration do not inspire confidence in the mind of SC to make it the basis for the conviction of the accused. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Dying declaration cannot form sole basis of conviction unless it is corroborated, as the rule requiring corroboration is merely a rule of prudence.
