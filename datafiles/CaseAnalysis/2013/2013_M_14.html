State Of Orissa & Ors. v M/S Mesco Steels Ltd. & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

06 March 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 126; (2013) 4 SCC 340; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 225; 2013 (98) ALR 207; JT 2013 (3) SC 552; 2013 (2) RCR(Civil) 318; 2013 (119) RD 681; [2013] 2 S.C.R. 245

Case Digest

Subject: Mines & Minerals; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Administrative - Quash of inter departmental proceedings - Sustainability - Appeal arose out of judgment and order passed by HC whereby Writ Petition filed by the respondent/company was allowed, inter- departmental communication in the form of letter addressed by the Director of Mines to Joint Secretary to Govt. quashed and by writ of mandamus State Govt. directed to execute mining lease for area in favour of the respondent - (A) Whether writ petition filed by the respondent was premature, the same having been filed against inter-departmental communication that did not finally determine any right or obligation of the parties - Held, there was no challenge to the communication before HC nor was any material placed before SC to suggest that any final decision was ever taken by Govt. on the question of deduction of the area granted in favour of the respondent so as to render the process of issue of show cause notice for hearing the respondent exercise in futility - On contrary, issue of the show cause notice setting out the reasons that impelled Govt. to claim resumption of part of the proposed lease area from respondent suggested that the entire process leading up to the issue of the show cause notice was tentative and no final decision on the subject had been taken at any level - It was only after Govt. provisionally decided to resume the area in part or full that show cause notice could have been issued - To put the matter beyond any pale of controversy, appellant made unequivocal statement at the bar on behalf of Govt. that no final decision regarding resumption of any part of the lease area has been taken by Govt. so far and all that had transpired till date should necessarily be taken as provisional - Such being the case HC was in error in proceeding on an assumption that final decision was taken and in quashing what was no more than inter-departmental communication constituting at best step in the process of taking final decision by Govt. - Writ petition in that view was pre-mature and ought to have been disposed of as such - (B) Whether the show cause notice could be ignored by HC simply because it was issued in violation of the interim order passed by it requiring the parties to maintain status quo - Held, if the validity of the show cause notice was itself in question on the ground that Govt. had no jurisdiction to issue the same, nothing prevented company from maintaining writ petition and challenging the notice on that ground - HC would in that event have had opportunity to examine the validity of the notice - In the absence of any such challenge HC could not simply ignore the notice even if it was issued in breach of the order passed by Court - HC could have taken the show cause notice as reason to relegate the parties to procedure which was just and fair and in which the respondent could urge all its contentions whether on facts or in law - (C) Whether the show cause notice was without jurisdiction and could, therefore, be quashed - Held, SC did not make any comment or express any opinion to the merits of the show cause notice - So long as the notice was not without jurisdiction as indeed it did not appear to be so, the question whether the grounds taken in the same provided good basis for proposed action could be left open for Govt. to decide - All that SC wanted to say was that parties made detailed submissions in regard to the grounds given in the notice and the validity thereof from their respective points of view and in support of their respective versions - Some of these grounds and submissions were quite attractive also - But so long as the matter was yet to be examined by Govt., SC considered it unnecessary to prejudice the issues or express any opinion about the merits of the said contentions on either side - Proper course, in SC's opinion, would be to leave the contentions available to the parties open for being determined by competent authority in Govt. who would, in SC's opinion, did well to consider the reply which the respondent might submit to the said show cause notice and pass reasoned order on the subject - Appeal allowed.
