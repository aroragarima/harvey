Shakuntala Sawhney v Kaushalya Sawhney

Supreme Court of India

04 April 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 620; (1980) 1 SCC 63; [1979] 3 S.C.R. 639

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, s. 15(1)(a) - Right of step-daughter in mother's property - Two sisters (with a common father but different mothers), became estranged when one (appellant) claimed a half share in the estate of her father, on whose death before 1956, the respondent's mother inherited her husband's estate but died after 1956, possessed of her husband's assets and her own - HC negatived the appellant's (step-daughter) right to a share as an heir in respondent's mother property - Hence, present appeal - Whether HC rightly negatived the appellant's (step-daughter) right in the respondent's property? - Held, parties came to compromise and as per said compromise some landed properties are to be made over to the appellant - Some cash is also to be paid to the appellant by the respondent - The discretion to fix the sum has been left by the parties to the Court - Court directed the respondent to pay a sum of Rs. 75,000/- to the appellant within two weeks of the attachment of the moneys by the trial court being withdrawn - Court also directed that the landed property worth Rs. 25,000/- will in addition be made over to the appellant from out of the suit property - Court further directed that all the rents due from the properties allotted to the appellant under the joint statement prior to this date and subsequent to this date shall be collectible by the appellant - There will be no more claims from the appellant on the respondent in regard to the estate of her step-mother, or in respect of its income or otherwise - Appeal partly allowed.

Obiter dictum

Family & Personal - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, s. 15(1)(a) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 141 - Right of step-daughter in mother's property - Do the expressions 'son' and 'daughter' mentioned u/s. 15(1)(a) of the Act, includes step-son and step-daughter or embrace only the son and daughter of the deceased female propositus? - Held, the text and the context and the application of traditional rules of statutory interpretation leave the position in an unsatisfactory dilemma of dual import - Even an equitable approach may not necessarily help reach a just solution, because equity shifts as the situation varies - Thus, the problem is a little tricky and may well arise frequently - Contradictory positions already taken by different HCs add to the difficulty and result in the deleterious uncertainty of the law - The SC may resolve the logomachic conflict by exercising its preference guided by the language and the milieu and following the customary canons of statutory interpretation - While its decision will be binding on account of art. 141 of the Constitution, it may still be fallible because the intendment of Parliament is best brought out by legislative clarification - Hence, Parliament should clarify its intention regarding s. 15(1) (a) - Appeals partly allowed.
