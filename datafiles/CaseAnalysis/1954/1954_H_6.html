Hem Raj v State of Ajmer

Supreme Court of India

17 March 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 27; AIR 1954 SC 462; 1954 CRLJ 1313; 1954 (1) MLJ(SC) 694; 1954 SCJ 449; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 1133

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Tort

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 342 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 386 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136(1) - (A) What are the powers of the Supreme Court u/art. 136 of the Constitution of India? - Held, Supreme Court's power u/art. 136 is discretionary - It should be used sparingly in cases where exceptional and special circumstances exist - A special leave petition does not entitle an appellant to contest all the findings of fact and raise every point which could be raised in the High Court - Only those points can be urged which are fit to be urged at the preliminary stage when the leave to appeal is asked for - (B) Whether a confession u/s. 164 of the CrPC, 1898 recorded in a jail valid? - Held, recording of a confession in jail instead of the court is an irregularity - In the circumstances of this particular case, the irregularity did not affect the voluntary character of the confessions - (C) Whether a confession can be said to have been made under undue influence by mere bald assertion by the prisoner that he was threatened, tutored or that inducement was offered to him? - Held, for the evidence of a confession by a prisoner to be admissible, it must be affirmatively proved that such confession was free and voluntary - Proof of undue influence should go beyond mere bald assertions of the same - (D) Whether a confession can be corroborated with evidence collected prior to its making? - Held, a confession can be made even during a trial and the evidence already recorded may well be used to corroborate it - It may be made in the court of the committing Magistrate and materials already in possession of the police may well be used for purposes of corroboration - Appeal dismissed.
