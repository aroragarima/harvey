Maharashtra State Electricity Board v Sterilite Industries (India) and another

Supreme Court of India

09 October 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Rajendra Babu, Doraiswamy Raju

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 72; (2001) 8 SCC 482; AIR 2001 SC 2933; 2002 AllCJ 751; 2001 (45) ALR 550; 2001 (3) ARBLR 532; 2002 (1) Bom.C.R. 415; 2002 (1) CRC 112; JT 2001 (8) SC 435; 2002 (1) MLJ(SC) 62; 2001 (3) RAJ 487; 2001 (4) RCR(Civil) 832; 2001(7) SCALE 82; 2001 (7) SLT 179; 2001 (7) Supreme 463; 2001 (2) UC 720

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Arbitration & ADR - Indian Contract Act, 1872, s. 73 - On certain disputes having arisen between the Maharashtra State Electricity Board and Sterilite Industries (India); matter was referred to Arbitral Tribunal consisting of three arbitrators - Arbitrators held that the petitioners were not entitled to invoke the provisions of s. 73 of the Act and they had failed to prove that any additional purchases were made to make up for the short supply resulting from the breach of contract by the respondents and even otherwise, the petitioners had failed to prove that they had suffered any damages and were, therefore, not entitled to any damages - Claim made by the petitioners was dismissed - On the award being sought to be made the decree of the court, objections were raised - Single Judge took the view that an award can only be set aside if there was an error of fact or an error of law and unless such error was apparent on the face of the record, the objections could not be sustained - On appeal, DB reiterated the view taken by the Single Judge - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, unless the error of law sought to be pointed out by petitioners in the instant case is patent on the face of the award neither the HC nor SC can interfere with the award. The exercise to be done by examining cl. 14(ii) of the contract entered into between the parties, construing the same properly and thereafter applying the law to it to come to a conclusion one way or the other, is too involved a process and it cannot be stated that such an error is apparent or patent on the face of the award. Whether under the context of the terms and conditions of a contract, a stipulation in the form and nature of clause 14(ii) operates as a special provision to the exclusion of s. 73 of the Act is a matter of appreciation of facts in a case, and when the decision thereon is not patently absurd or wholly unreasonable, there is no scope for interference by courts dealing with a challenge to the award. Therefore, view taken by the HC in this matter is correct and calls for no interference. Petition dismissed.
