Maharaj Singh v State of Uttar Pradesh and Others

Supreme Court of India

02 November 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, Hans Raj Khanna

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 653; (1977) 1 SCC 155; AIR 1976 SC 2602; [1977] 1 S.C.R. 1072

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Appurtenant Land, Corporate Body, Attorney-General

Summary: Land & Property - Uttar Pradesh Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1951 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 96 - State of UP extinguished all zamindari estates by Act and implemented a scheme of settlement of lands with intermediaries, tenants and others by first vesting all estates in State and empowering it to vest, divest and re-vest from time to time according to flexible needs and ad hoc requirements, same estates in Goan Sabhas or other local authorities - Settlement of trees, building and other specified items in intermediaries was also part of agrarian reform - Suit lands were part of an estate owned and possessed by appellant - By a notification u/s. 117 (1) of Act area of lands was vested by State in Gaon Sahba - Appellant who had been conducting a lucrative bi-weekly cattle fair persisted in this profitable adventure strengthened by s. 9 of Act which settles in intermediary all buildings and area appurtenant thereto - This resulted in possessory disputes between Gaon Sabha and defendant - Appellant claimed before High Court that trees and two plots with Shrine and Oushadhalaya should be deemed to have been settled with him - HC declined to go whole hog with appellant but granted plea to limited degree of giving all structures and a space of 5 yards running round each building - In view of Court hats, bazars, and melas could not be held by a private owner under scheme of Act - (A)Whether appeal to HC by State at all competent, entitlement as a 'party aggrieved' i.e. Gram Sabha, being absent, having regard to provisions of Act - (B) Is it sound to conceptualise 'area appurtenant to buildings' in s. 9 of Act so narrowly as has been done by HC? - Held, where a wrong against community interest is done, 'no locus standi' will not always be a plea to non-suit an interested public body chasing wrongdoers in courts - Right of resumption from Gaon Sabha, meant to be exercised in public interest, will be seriously jeopardised if estate slips into hands of a trespasser - Estate belonged to State, is vested in Gaon Sabha for community benefit, is controlled by State - Wholesome object of legislature of cautiously decentralised vesting of estates in local self-governing units will be frustrated, if State, watch-dog of whole project, is to be a helpless spectator of its purposeful boundary being wasted or lost - Therefore, State, in present case, is entitled to appeal - Second and from a practical point of view, equally potent ground of defense, is that 'appurtenant' space envelops whole area around buildings and suit for recovery of possession deserves to be dismissed in toto - Touchstone of 'appurtenance' is dependence of building on what appertains to it for its use as a building - Obviously, hat, bazar or mela is not an appurtenance to building - Law thus leads to clear conclusion that even if buildings were used and enjoyed in past with whole stretch of vacant space for a hat or mela, land is not appurtenant to principal subject granted by s. 9 - Appeal dismissed.
