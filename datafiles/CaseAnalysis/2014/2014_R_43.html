Dr. Subramanian Swamy and others v Raju, Through Member, Juvenile Justice Board and another

Supreme Court of India

28 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, P. Sathasivam, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 246; (2014) 8 SCC 390; AIR 2014 SC 1649; JT 2014 (4) SC 328; 2014 (2) MLJ(Crl) 137; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 361; 2014(4) SCALE 305; [2014] 9 S.C.R. 283

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Deem, United Nations Standard Minimum Rules, Powers of Criminal Courts (Sentencing) Act, 2000, Protection of Juveniles Deprived of their Liberty Rules, 1990, Administration of Juvenile Justice Rules, 1985, Youth Criminal Justice Act, 2002, Children Act, 1974

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art.14 - Juvenile Justice Act, 1986 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, art.376 - Rape - Conviction under Juvenile Justice Act - Legality - Respondent No.1 committed offence of rape - However, respondent No.1 was not convicted under s.376 of IPC but under the provisions of Act because the respondent No.1 was below the age of 18 years - Hence instant appeal - Whether respondent No.1 could be convicted under s.376 of IPC -

Held, in instant case there was no difficulty in understanding the clear and unambiguous meaning of the different provisions of the Act - There was no ambiguity, muchless any uncertainty, in the language used to convey what the legislature had intended - All persons below the age of 18 were put in one class/group by the Act to provide a separate scheme of investigation, trial and punishment for offences committed by them - A class of persons was sought to be created who would be treated differently - It was done to further/effectuate the views of the international community which India had shared by being a signatory to the several conventions and treaties - Classification or categorization need not be the outcome of a mathematical or arithmetical precision in the similarities of the persons included in a class and there might be differences amongst the members included within a particular class - So long as the broad features of the categorization were identifiable and distinguishable and the categorization made was reasonably connected with the object targeted, Art. 14 of Constitution would not forbid such a course of action - If the inclusion of all under 18 into a class called 'juveniles' was understood in the above manner, differences inter se and within the under 18 category might exist - Art. 14 of Constitution would, however, tolerate the said position - Precision and arithmetical accuracy would not exist in any categorization - But such precision and accuracy was not what Art. 14 of Constitution contemplates - Murthy Match Works and Others vs. The Asstt. Collector of Central Excise and Another, 1974 Indlaw SC 509, relied on - If the provisions of the Act clearly indicate the legislative intent in the light of the country's international commitments and the same was in conformity with the Constitutional requirements, it was not necessary for the Court to understand the legislation in any other manner - In fact, if the Act was plainly read and understood, which SC should do, the resultant effect thereof would be wholly consistent with art. 14 of Constitution - Act, therefore, need not be read down, as suggested, to save it from the vice of unConstitutionality for such unConstitutionality did not exist - That in certain foreign jurisdictions, to bring about clarity and completeness to the issues arising, the position otherwise would hardly be of any consequence so far as our country was concerned - Contrary international opinion, thinking or practice, even if assumed, did not dictate the legislation of a sovereign nation - If the legislature had adopted the age of 18 as the dividing line between juveniles and adults and such a decision was Constitutionally permissible the enquiry by the Courts should come to an end - Appeal dismissed.
