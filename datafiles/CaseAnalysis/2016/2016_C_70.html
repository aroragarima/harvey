Commissioner of Central Excise, Madras v Addison and Company Limited

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

L. Nageswara Rao, Anil R. Dave, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 607; (2016) 10 SCC 56; AIR 2016 SC 4007; 2016 (339) E.L.T. 177; 2016(8) SCALE 308; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 591

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Central Excise Act, 1944, Central Excises And Salt Act, 1944, Service Tax, Central Excises And Customs Laws (Amendment) Act, 1991

Summary: Civil Appeal No. 7906 of 2002, Special Leave Petition (C) Nos. 18426, 23722, 18423, 18425 of 2015 and 12282, 16141 and 16142 of 2016, Civil Appeal No. 14689 of 2015 & Special Leave Petition (C) No. 25055 of 2009

(A) Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11B, 11B(2), 11B(2)(e), 11B(2)(f), 12A, 12B, 12C, 4 - Central Excises and Customs Laws (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Central Excises and Salt Act, 1944, s. 4 - Refund claimed - Unjust enrichment - Denial - Respondent/assessee was manufacturer of cutting tools filed a refund claim for Rs. 40,22,133/- and a supplementary refund claim for Rs. 5,44,688/- towards excise duty paid on various taxes and discounts - Dept. held that refund towards turnover discount and additional discount was to be rejected as assessee was not eligible for deduction from wholesale price for determination of value u/s. 4 of the Act - Subsequently, an order was passed wherein as per amendment made to s. 11B of the 1944 Act an application filed for refund prior to the 1991 Act shall be deemed to have been made under the 1991 Act and considered - Assistant Collector issued a show cause notice assessee and passed an order holding that assessee is entitled for refund claimed - On appeal, Collector by an order rejected appeal and upheld order of Assistant Collector - On second appeal, Tribunal held that assessee would be entitled to grant of refund only if he had not passed on duty burden to his buyers - Assessee filed an application for reference before HC - HC answered reference in favour of assessee holding that Tribunal committed an error in holding that assessee was not entitled for refund despite assessee proving that duty was not passed on to its buyers - Hence, instant appeals - Whether assessee is entitled for a refund and whether there would be unjust enrichment if said refund is allowed.

Held, in instant case, assessee has admitted that incidence of duty was originally passed on to buyer. There is no material brought on record to show that buyer to whom incidence of duty was passed on by Assessee did not pass it on to any other person. There is a statutory presumption u/s. 12B of the 1944 Act that duty has been passed on to ultimate consumer. The refund claimed by assessee is for an amount which is part of the excise duty paid earlier and passed on. Assessee who did not bear the burden of duty, though entitled to claim deduction, is not entitled for a refund as he would be unjustly enriched. S. 11B (2) of the 1944 Act contemplates that amount of refund determined by Authorities shall be credited to the fund. The word 'buyer' in Cl.(e) to proviso to s. 11B (2) of the 1944 Act cannot be restricted to first buyer from manufacturer. It might be difficult to identify who had actually borne the burden but such verification would definitely assist Revenue in finding out whether manufacturer or buyer who makes an application for refund are being unjustly enriched. If it is not possible to identify the person/persons who have borne the duty, the amount of excise duty collected in excess will remain in the fund which will be utilized for the benefit of the consumers as provided in s. 12D of the 1944 Act. HC proceeded on an erroneous assumption of fact as well and HC's interpretation of s.11B of the 1944 Act was not correct. Thus, assessee is not entitled to refund as it would result in unjust enrichment. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside. Appeals disposed of.

Civil Appeal No. 8488 of 2009

(B) Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11B, 11B(2), 11B(2)(e), 11B(2)(f), 12A, 12B, 12C, 4 - Central Excises and Customs Laws (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Central Excises and Salt Act, 1944, s. 4 - Refund claimed - Chartered Accountant's certificate - Genuineness - Respondent/assessee is a 100 per cent Export Oriented Unit (EOU) manufacturing cotton yarn - Respondent filed an application for refund of an amount of Rs. 2,00,827/- on ground that it had paid excess excise duty at the rate of 18.11 per cent instead of 9.20 per cent - Deputy Commissioner rejected refund claim on ground that assessee did not submit either credit notes or Chartered Accountant's certificate at the time of filing refund application - On appeal, Commissioner allowed appeal and held that there was no reason to doubt genuineness of documents produced - On further appeal, Appellate Authority allowed and said order was confirmed by Tribunal - Aggrieved, appellant/Revenue filed an appeal before HC, HC confirmed the order of Tribunal and dismissed appeal - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, except for a factual dispute about genuineness of certificate issued by Chartered Accountant and credit notes raised by assessee regarding return of excess duty paid by assessee, there is no dispute in instant case of the duty being passed on to any other person by buyer. Therefore, it is clear that assessee has borne the burden of duty, it cannot be said that it is not entitled for refund of excess duty paid. Appeals disposed of.
