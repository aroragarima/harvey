Minerals and Metals Trading Corporation of India Limited v Sales Tax Officer and Others

Supreme Court of India

25 September 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

S.P. Bharucha, V.N. Khare

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 1236; (1998) 7 SCC 19; AIR 1999 SC 121; 1998 (78) ECR 753; JT 1998 (6) SC 595; 1998(5) SCALE 446; 1998 (8) SLT 156; 1998 (111) STC 434; [1998] Supp2 S.C.R. 112; 1998 (7) Supreme 547

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Customs Station, Customs Frontiers

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, ss. 2 and 5 - Levy of sales tax - Appellant functions as a canalising agent for the purpose of import and export of minerals and metals - On 31-3-1991, the Steel Authority of India Limited (SAIL) requested the appellant to register the import of 15, 000 mt of tin mill black plate (TMBP) coils - On 14-7-1991, SAIL opened a letter of credit directly in favour of the exporter, Samsung Co. Ltd., Seoul, South Korea - Consignee therein was shown as SAIL - On 2-8-1991, the appellant placed a purchase order with the exporter for and on behalf of SAIL - On 31-12-1994, the Sales Tax Officer levied sales tax on the aforesaid sale and ejected the appellant's case that no sales tax was payable, this being a sale in the course of import covered by S. 5(2) of the Act - It was held that there had been two sales, one between the exporter and the appellant and the other between the appellant and SAIL and that the sale to SAIL had not occasioned the import - Petitions were filed before HC challenging the levy of sales tax on the aforesaid sales - HC noted the argument that the aforesaid sales on high-seas basis had been effected prior to the imported goods 'crossing the customs frontier of India', which expression was defined in s. 2(ab) of the Act by an amendment which had taken place prior to the aforesaid sales - HC relying upon the judgment of the Karnataka HC in the case of Cashew Corpn. of India Ltd. v. State of Karnataka held that the appellant was liable to sales tax and dismissed the writ petitions - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, HC noticed the argument based on the latter part of s. 5 of the Act but did not address it. It relied upon the judgment of the Karnataka HC in the case of Cashew Corpn. of India . That was a case where notice was taken of the amendment introducing S. 2(ab) into the Act in 1976. It was held to be prospective in operation and, therefore, of no assistance in construing the meaning of the expression 'customs frontier of India' prior thereto. The HC failed to notice that in the case in hand, the aforesaid sales had taken place long after the introduction of s. 2(ab) of the Act and, therefore, the question whether they were sales in the course of import had to be judged on the basis of its provisions. The aforesaid sales being covered by the provisions of the latter part of s. 5(2) r/w s. 2(ab) of the Act, they are sales in the course of import and not liable to sales tax. Appeals allowed.
