Venkataraja and others v Vidyane Doureradjaperumal (Dead) Through Lrs. and others

Supreme Court of India

10 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 219; (2014) 14 SCC 502; 2013 (4) ALD(SC) 121; 2013(3) ALL MR 899; 2013 (98) ALR 673; 2013 (3) ALT 46; 2013 (3) AWC 2213; JT 2013 (4) SC 505; 2013 (4) MLJ 700; 2013 (3) RCR(Civil) 176; 2013 (120) RD 229; 2013(5) SCALE 511; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 814

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Keywords: Pondicherry Non-Agricutural Kudiyiruppudars (Stay of Eviction Proceedings) Act, 1980, French Civil Code, 1908

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Hindu Succession Act, 1956 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Suit for declaration - Maintainability - Appellant/plaintiff filed suit against defendant no. 1 for a direction that plaintiff was the heir of deceased 'R', who acquired suit property by way of donation deed dt.18-1-1897 and also for a direction to defendant no. 1 to not waste suit property - Trial Court held that since 'T' (sole inheritor of the property) was still alive, claim of appellant/plaintiff was premature - However, it observed that appellant/plaintiff was legal heir to deceased 'R' - On appeal, Appellate Court allowed appeal to the extent of holding that no repairs were necessary for suit property - Said 'T' died on 30-5-1978 - Defendant no. 1 and her husband, defendant no. 2, thereafter leased out suit property in favour of defendant nos. 3 to 9 on 30-5-1979, and were receiving rent for the same - Defendant No.10, who was husband and father of respondent nos. 4 and 5 respectively, purchased suit property from defendant no.1 vide registered sale deed dt.26-4-1980 - Deceased/plaintiff i.e. father of appellants filed suit for declaration that he was legal heir of deceased 'R', and thus had a proper title to suit property and for declaration that sale deed dt.16-7-1959 executed by 'T' in favour of defendant no. 1 was null and void as she had only a life estate and not an absolute title, to alienate the property - Trial Court found that appellant/plaintiff had filed suit only for declaration of his right to suit property, and since he had not asked for consequential relief of delivery of possession, suit was held to be not maintainable and was dismissed - On appeal, Appellate Court allowed appeal - Aggrieved by that, respondents filed appeal - It was during pendency of said appeals, that defendant no. 1 sold suit property to respondent nos. 1 to 3 on 31-3-1993 - HC held, that 'T' had acquired absolute title over property - As defendant no. 1 had purchased suit property from absolute owner 'T' vide sale deed dt.11-7-1959, she had become rightful owner and said sale deed was not null and void - In view of fact that said defendant no. 1 was in possession of suit property for over than 10 years, she had perfected the title to suit property by prescription, under provisions of French Civil Code and as a consequence thereof, suit for declaration was not maintainable without seeking relief of possession - Hence instant appeals - Appellants contended that HC had committed an error by holding that 'T' had acquired an absolute title over suit property, and that by selling suit property to defendant no. 1, who had purchased suit property from her, vide sale deed dt.16-7-1959, defendant no. 1 had become absolute owner of suit property and that sale deed was not null and void - Further, courts below recorded a finding that 'T' was only a life estate holder and thus, had not acquired an absolute title - HC had not given any reason whatsoever, for reversing said finding of fact and said finding was perverse being based on no evidence - Held, admittedly, defendant no. 1 had purchased suit property from absolute owner 'T', as per sale deed dt.16-7-1959 - Thus, she had become rightful owner, said sale deed being not null and void - Finding recorded by HC was not based on any evidence, and no reason was given by it to reverse the findings recorded by Trial Court as well as First Appellate Court that 'T' was only the life estate holder - Hence, HC had erred in recording such a finding - HC had no occasion to deal with issue of adverse possession, in view of earlier judgment of Trial Court, wherein in 1965, it was held that suit filed by appellant/plaintiff was pre-mature, as he could not file the same during life time of 'T' - Further, respondent nos. 3 to 10 were tenants, residing in the suit property - Said respondents were definitely in a position to deliver possession - Therefore, appellants would be entitled to file an independent proceedings for their eviction under a different statute, would amount to defeating the provisions of O. 2, r. 2 of CPC as well as proviso to s. 34 of Special Relief Act, 1963 - Thus, First Appellate Court as well as HC failed to consider question of paramount importance - It was evident that suit filed by appellants/plaintiffs was not maintainable, as they did not claim consequential relief - Appeals dismissed.
