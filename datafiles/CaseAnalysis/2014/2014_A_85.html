Rasheeda Khatoon (Dead) Through Lrs. v Ashiq Ali S/o Late Abu Mohammed (Dead) Through Lrs.

Supreme Court of India

10 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 690; (2014) 10 SCC 459; 2014 (6) ALD(SC) 89; 2014(6) ALL MR 900; 2014 (107) ALR 359; 2015 (1) ALT(SC) 17; 2015 (1) AWC 85; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 271; 2014 (5) CTC 778; JT 2014 (11) SC 438; 2014 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 4359; 2015 (2) MahLJ 96; 2015 (1) MPLJ 522; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 842; 2015 (126) RD 237; 2014(11) SCALE 694; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 31; 2015 (4) WLN(SC) 26

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Oral gift

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 129 - Registration Act, 1908, s. 17 - Oral gift - Compulsorily registrable - Legality - Predecessor-in-interest of appellants (plaintiff) filed a suit for recovery of possession of house in dispute from defendants on the basis of oral gift made by original owner in favour of plaintiff - Trial Court decreed the suit holding that plaintiff had proved the oral gift executed by original owner in her favour; that gift deed did not require registration; and that she was the owner of suit premises and entitled to get back possession - Aggrieved defendants filed an appeal, which was dismissed by First Appellate Court, against which a second appeal was filed before Single Judge - Single Judge treated gift deed to be an oral gift though it was a document under which transfer was made and was compulsorily registrable - Hence, instant appeals - Whether essential ingredients of the gift as understood in Muhammadan Law were satisfied.

Held, from the gift deed it is manifest that original owner had declared therein that he had always been the owner in possession and the entire house was in his exclusive ownership and possession and free from all encumbrances. Thus, the said recital belies the case of the plaintiff that there was an oral gift seven years prior to filing of the suit, that is, sometime in the year 1968. The learned trial Jude as well as the appellate court has brushed aside the said aspect by stating that it has not affected the stand of the plaintiff inasmuch as some witnesses have deposed about the gift having been made in 1968. As the deed would show the executant had stated that he had executed a Will earlier in favour of plaintiff. That apart, such a fact, had it been true would have definitely formed a part of the written instrument. Omission of such a fact, in our view, defies common sense. The conclusion that the gift deed dated 9.10.1970 evidences such a gift, is absolutely unacceptable. On a perusal of the deed of gift and the evidence brought on record it is demonstrable that original owner remained in the premises in question. He did not part with physical possession. The case of the plaintiff is that she resided with original owner and, therefore, the principle of donor getting fully divested or handing over of physical possession is not attracted. Though, such a finding has been recorded, Court find it wholly contrary to the evidence on record. The plaintiff was staying with her husband. The family register and voters list, indicate that plaintiff was residing in her house with her husband. Though the gift deed mentions that she was entitled to get her name mutated in respect of the premises, yet it was not done. It is extremely difficult to hold that she was residing with original owner in the premises in question. The first two courts have based their conclusions on conjecture and inferences. The HC, has not dwelled upon this aspect and has only negatived the finding of the courts below that the document did not evidence an oral gift. Thus scrutinized there remains no shadow of doubt that she was not in actual physical possession. Thus, the evidence on record, on a studied scrutiny, clearly reveal that plaintiff was not in constructive possession. Therefore, one of the elements of the valid gift has not been satisfied. That being the position there is no necessity to advert to the aspect whether the instrument in question required registration or not because there can be certain circumstances a deed in writing may require registration. As the plaintiff could not prove either actual or constructive possession, the gift was not complete and hence, the issue of registration does not arise. Impugned judgment of HC was affirmed. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Either there has to be actual delivery of possession from the donor or the donee must be in constructive possession to make a gift valid under the Muhammadan Law. Solely because the writing is contemporaneous of the making of the gift deed, under Muhammadan Law, it does not warrant registration u/s. 17 of 1908 Act.
