Â Distributors (Baroda) Private Limited v Union of India and Two Others

Supreme Court of India

01 July 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, Y.V. Chandrachud, Amarendra Nath Sen, D.P. Madon, M.P. Thakkar

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 405; (1986) 1 SCC 43; (1986) SCC (Tax) 159; AIR 1985 SC 1585; 1985 (2) CompLJ 389; 1985 (47) CTR 349; [1985] 155 ITR 120; 1985(1) SCALE 1216; [1985] Supp1 S.C.R. 778; [1985] 22 TAXMAN 49; 1986 (1) UJ 86

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Corporate; Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (Majority Order)

(P. N. Bhagwati, J for himself and for Y. V. Chandrachud, C.J., D. P. Madon, M. P. Thakkar, JJ.)

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 80M(1) and 80AA - Constitution of India, 1960, art. 19(1)(g) - Deduction u/s. 80AA - Constitutional validity - Petitioner no. 1, a limited company, received dividends on shares held by it in different domestic companies and paid interest on monies borrowed for the purpose of investment in such shares - While filing its return petitioner no. 1 claimed that the deduction permissible u/s. 80M of the Act must be calculated with reference to the full amount of dividends received by petitioner no. 1 from the domestic companies and not with reference to the dividends as computed in accordance with the provisions of the Income Tax Act, 1961 - The assessments of petitioner no. 1 were actually completed on the basis of his claim and the view taken by SC in Cloth Traders Private Limited, Etc vs. Additional Commissioner of Income Tax, Gujarat-I, Etc. 1979 INDLAW SC 229 in regard to construction of s. 80M of the Act - Subsequently, parliament enacted s. 80AA of the Act w.e.f. 1.04.1968 which provided that the deduction required to be allowed u/s. 80M of the Act shall not be computed with reference to the gross amount of dividend received by the assessee from a domestic company but with reference to the dividend income as computed in accordance with the provisions of the Act - Revenue filed appeal against assessment order passed in favour of petitioner no. 1 contending that the claim of petitioner no.1 for deduction on the basis of the full amount of dividend received by it from domestic companies was liable to be rejected and deduction could be allowed to petitioner no. 1 only with reference to the dividend income computed in accordance with the provision of the Act - Petitioner no. 1 filed writ petition challenging constitutional validity of s. 80AA of the Act on ground that it enhanced tax burden of petitioner no. 1 with retrospective effect going back for a period of almost 12 years and consequently imposed an unreasonable restriction on the right of petitioner no.1 to carry on its business in breach of art. 19 (1) (g) of the Constitution - Whether s. 80 AA of the Act was constitutional valid? - Held, the main object of the relief granted u/s. 80M of the Act was to avoid taxation once again in the hands of the receiving company of the amount which has already borne full tax in the hands of the paying company - Now when an amount by way of dividend was received by the assessee from the paying company the full amount of such dividend would have suffered tax, in the assessment of the paying company - Therefore, it was reasonable to assume that in enacting s. 80M of the Act, the Legislature intended to grant relief with reference to the amount of dividend computed in accordance with the provisions of the Act and not with reference to the full amount of dividend received from the paying company - Thus, s. 80AA of the Act which provided that the deduction required to be allowed u/s. 80M of the Act shall be computed with reference to the dividend income as computed in accordance with the provisions of the Act, was clearly declaratory in nature and merely declares what correct position had always been - Section 80AA of the Act had not effect of imposing any fresh tax with retrospective effect - Therefore, no question of imposition of any fresh tax with retrospective effect falls for consideration - Writ petition dismissed.

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 80M(1) and 80AA - Doctrine of stare decisis - Held, ordinarily SC would be reluctant to overturn a decision given by a Bench of SC, because it was essential that there should be continuity and consistency in judicial decisions, and law should be certain and definite - But there may be circumstances where public interest demands that the previous decision be reviewed and reconsidered - The doctrine of stare decisis should not deter the Court from overruling an earlier decision, if it was satisfied that such decision was manifestly wrong or proceeds upon a mistaken assumption in regard to the existence or continuance of a statutory provision or was contrary to another decision of the SC - In the instant case, there were over-riding considerations which compel reconsideration and review of the SC decision in Cloth Traders Case - The decision in Cloth Traders case was rendered by SC on 4.05.1979 and immediately thereafter, within a few months, Parliament introduced s. 80AA of the Act with retrospective effect from 1.04.1968 with a view to over-riding the interpretation placed on s. 80M of the Act in Cloth Traders case - The decision in Cloth Traders case did not therefore hold the field for a period of more than a few months and it could not be said that any assessee was misled into acting to its detriment on the basis of that decision - Moreover, there was no decision of SC in regard to the interpretation of sub-section (1) of s. 80M of the Act prior to the decision in Cloth Traders case and there was therefore no authoritative pronouncement of SC on this question of interpretation on which an assessee could claim to rely for making its fiscal arrangements - Hence, decision rendered by three bench of SC in Cloth Traders case was overruled - Writ petition dismissed.

(Minority Order)

A. N. Sen, J.

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 80M(1) and 80AA - Doctrine of stare decisis - Held, the authority and jurisdiction of a larger Bench of SC to override and over-rule any decision of a smaller Bench cannot be questioned - However, a decision of SC on any fiscal legislation involving the question of financial benefit and liability should not normally be interfered with and should be interfered with only in very rare cases - On the basis of the decision of SC on any fiscal legislation and any matter involving financial arrangements and adjustments, parties were entitled to arrange their financial affairs and in fact they so arrange and adjust the financial affairs on the basis of the law laid down by SC - Unsettling a position settled by the decision of SC may lead to the confusion and result in financial instability, causing serious prejudice not only to the parties concerned but also to the economic growth of the country as a whole - Moreover, if on interpretation of any provision of any fiscal legislation two views may be reasonably possible, a larger Bench of SC may not interfere with a view taken by a smaller Bench by SC mainly on the ground that the other view appears to the larger Bench to be the better view and may commend itself to the larger Bench - If, however, a decision of the smaller Bench had necessarily to interfere with the decision, as SC will not permit a wrong decision to operate as good law of the land - Writ petition dismissed.
