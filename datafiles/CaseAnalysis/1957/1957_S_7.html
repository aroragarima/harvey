Â Sardar Inder Singh v State of Rajasthan

Supreme Court of India

08 February 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 158; AIR 1957 SC 510; 1957 SCJ 376; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 605

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Constitution - Rajasthan (Protection of Tenants) Ordinance, 1949, Ordinance No. IX of 1949 - Rajasthan (Protection of Tenants) Amendment Act No. X of 1954 - (A) Whether Rajasthan (Protection of Tenants) Ordinance, 1949, Ordinance No. IX of 1949, notifications D/- 14-06-1951 and 20-06-1953, issued thereunder and of Rajasthan (Protection of Tenants) Amendment Act No. X of 1954 are violative of Constitution of India? - Whether it is vitiated as a delegated legislation or valid as conditional legislation - Held, (i) notifications D/- 14-06-1951, and 20-06-1953, are bad, as s.3 of Ordinance under which they were issued is ultra vires, as constituting delegation of legislative power;(ii) notification D/-20-06-1953, is further bad, because Legislature of Rajasthan had been constituted on 29-03-1952, and authority of Rajpramukh to legislate conferred by art. 385 of Constitution had, on that date, come to an end; (iii) Act No. X of 1954 is bad, as it purports to extend life of Ordinance No. IX of 1949 after said Ordinance had already become dead; (iv) impugned Ordinance is bad as being repugnant to art. 14 of Constitution; and (v) Ordinance also contravenes art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution in that it imposes unreasonable restrictions on right of petitioners to hold property - On ground that his authority under art. 385 ceased when legislature was constituted - It was not an independent piece of legislation such as could be enacted only by then competent legislative authority of State, but merely an exercise of power conferred by a statute which had been previously enacted by appropriate legislative authority - Exercise of such a power is referable not to legislative competence of Rajpramukh but to Ordinance No. IX of 1949, and provided s. 3 is valid, validity of notification is co-extensive with that of Ordinance - (B) On repugnancy of s. 15 to art. 14 - Further held, it is true that section does not itself indicate grounds on which exemption could be granted, but preamble to Ordinance sets out with sufficient clearness policy of Legislature; and as that governs s. 15 of Ordinance, decision of Govt. thereunder cannot be said to be unguided - Harishanker Bagla v State of Madhya Pradesh [1954 Indlaw SC 135] relied on - But even if s. 15 were to be held to be bad, that does not affect rest of legislation, as matter dealt with in that section is clearly severable - (C) On provisions being bad at altar of art. 19(1)(f) - Further held, object of Ordinance, as set out in preamble, is clearly not to put a restriction on right of an owner to himself cultivate lands, but to prevent him when he had inducted a tenant on land from getting rid of him without sufficient cause - A law which requires that an owner who is not himself a tiller of soil should assure to actual tiller some fixity of tenure, cannot on that ground alone be said to be unreasonable - Petition dismissed.
