Trishala Jain and others v State of Uttaranchal and others

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 341; (2011) 6 SCC 47; AIR 2011 SC 2458; 2011 (5) ALD(SC) 72; 2011 (88) ALR 270; 2011 (5) AWC 4877; 2011 (2) RCR(Civil) 947; 2011 (114) RD 339; 2011(5) SCALE 469; [2011] 8 S.C.R. 520; 2011 (3) UPLBEC 2102

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Central Excise Valuation Rules, 1975

Summary: Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Land acquisition proceedings - Compensation - Government of Uttar Pradesh issued a Notification u/s. 4(1) of the Act for acquiring some land for a public purpose - Declaration u/s. 6(1) of the Act was issued for acquisition for the said purpose - In furtherance to the Notification, possession of the acquired land was taken - Special Land Acquisition Officer (SLAO) pronounced his award - While determining compensation, the SLAO applied belting system to the acquired land and assessed the market value accordingly - However, the claimants, being dissatisfied with the award of the SLAO, filed applications u/s. 18 of the Act - Reference Court held application of belting system improper as entire land was acquired for one purpose, i.e. construction of Government Polytechnic Institute - It determined the market value of the land at the rate of ' 6,40,000 per bigha and after applying 20% deduction, enhanced compensation to flat rate of ' 5,12,000 per bigha along with other statutory benefits - State, aggrieved by the enhancement of compensation awarded to the claimants by the Reference Court, filed appeals - HC held that the Reference Court was fully justified in setting aside the order of the SLAO applying belting system for determination of compensation in relation to the acquired land - HC modified the order of the Reference Court only by raising the deduction from 20% to 33.33% on account of development charges and fixing of the final amount of compensation at the rate of '4,26,667 per bigha - Instant appeals - (A) What should be the just and fair market value of the acquired land on the date of issuance of notification u/s. 4 of the Act? - Claimants contended that the deduction applied by the HC was not justified and their claim for compensation in line with the two sale instances proved by them on record was to be upheld - Held, said sale deeds have been executed in favour of the family members or persons known to the claimants - There were circumstances and evidence which indicate that the sale instances relied upon by the claimants were result of collusion between these parties - These two sale instances which have been executed just about two months prior to the issuance of the notification u/s. 4(1) stand out as transactions which were sham, collusive, lack bona fide and have been executed with the intention to raise the price of the land in question with the pretence of it being actual market value - No infirmity in the view of the Reference Court which was rightly been upheld by the HC - (B) Whether in the facts and circumstances of the instant case there ought to be any deduction after determining the fair market value of the land? - Held, cases where the acquired land itself was fully developed and had all essential amenities, before acquisition, for the purpose for which it was acquired requiring no additional expenditure for its development, falls under the purview of cases of 'no deduction' - Furthermore, where the evidence led by the parties was of such instances where the compensation paid was comparable, was another category of cases where principle of 'no deduction' may be applied - In the instant case, there was evidence on record to show that plotting was done only on part of the acquired land and the land was surrounded by colonies but, there was no evidence to show that the acquired land itself was developed and was having all the required facilities and amenities - Deduction of 10% from the market value on account of development charges and other possible expenditures would be justifiable and called for in the facts and circumstances of the instant case - (C) What compensation and benefits were the claimants entitled to? - Held, in the instant case the 'principle of guesstimation' had to be applied inasmuch as it was essential for fixation of fair market value of the land which should be the basis for determining the compensation payable to the claimants - It was a case of acquisition of land which was situated on a reasonably good location surrounded by developed areas having civic amenities and facilities and further development activity was going on in nearby areas - It was evident that the land acquired had the potential of being developed for residential or institutional purposes and same was acquired for construction of a Government Polytechnic Institute - Therefore, it was a case where the Court should apply minimal deduction which would meet the ends of justice and would help in determining just and fair compensation for the land in question - 10% deduction from the market value of the acquired land would meet the ends of justice - Appeals disposed of.
