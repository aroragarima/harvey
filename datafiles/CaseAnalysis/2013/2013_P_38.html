Mritunjoy Biswas v Pranab @ Kuti Biswas and another

Supreme Court of India

08 August 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 490; (2013) 12 SCC 796; AIR 2013 SC 3334; 2013 CRLJ 4212; 2013 (4) JCC 2481; JT 2013 (11) SC 395; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 1; 2013(10) SCALE 129; [2013] 7 S.C.R. 1105

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 - Murder - Acquittal - Sustainability - Accused/respondent No. 1 allegedly fired at deceased wife of PW8 - Later deceased succumbed to the injuries - Charge sheet against accused was filed - Trial Court convicted appellant u/s. 302 IPC - Aggrieved on appeal HC set aside the order of Trial Court and acquitted accused - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in acquitting appellant against the order of conviction and sentence of Trial Court - Held, HC had taken serious exception to non-mentioning of the name of accused in FIR on the ground that informant had occasion to know the name of assailant from husband of deceased as he had told the name of accused to his nephew who had lodged the FIR and further deceased had not mentioned the name of the accused though she was conscious and was able to speak - Reasons ascribed by HC on this score were unacceptable, for they did not really stand to reason - Accused was named at the earliest opportunity and there was nothing brought on record to suggest that he was falsely implicated by way of an afterthought - In instant case, if the evidence of the witnesses read in a cumulative manner, the abscondence of accused gained significance - HC, had read the evidence not as a whole but in utter fragmentation and appreciated the same in total out of context - Aspect which was highlighted by HC pertained to non-examination of LB and the treating doctor at the Hospital - As far as non-examination of the treating doctor at the Hospital was concerned, SC was of the view that the same did not even remotely affect the case of prosecution - HC had taken exception to his non-examination solely on the base that his evidence in the court would have reflected the exact health condition of the deceased - Emphasis was laid on the same as the appellate court had felt that the same could have been a pointer to find out whether the deceased was in a conscious state and why she did not mention the name of accused - When the testimonies of other witnesses were accepted on their own creditworthiness, aspect had to melt into insignificance - As far as non-examination of LB was concerned, as per the prosecution version she had only accompanied the deceased - There was no denial of the fact that deceased had not mentioned the name of accused - SC failed to appreciate how the non-examination of the said witness created a concavity in the case of prosecution and, accordingly, SC was unable to concur with the reasoning of the HC - Absence of evidence regarding recovery of used pellets, bloodstained clothes, etc. could not be taken or construed as no such occurrence had taken place - Thus, when there was ample unimpeachable ocular evidence and the same was corroborated by the medical evidence, non- recovery of the weapon did not affect the prosecution case - Impugned judgment of acquittal passed by the HC being wholly unsustainable was set aside and the judgment of conviction of the Trial Court was restored - Appeal allowed.
