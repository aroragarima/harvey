Ram Prasad Rajak v Nand Kumar and Bros. and Another

Supreme Court of India

18 August 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Srinivasan, A.S. Anand

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 1144; (1998) 6 SCC 748; AIR 1998 SC 2730; 1998 (3) CLT 176; JT 1998 (5) SC 540; 1998 (2) RCJ 361; 1998 (2) RCR(Rent) 249; 1998(4) SCALE 577; 1998 (7) SLT 36; [1998] Supp1 S.C.R. 1; 1998 (6) Supreme 426

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Summary: Rent Control - Bihar Buildings (Leas, Rent & Eviction ) Control Act, 1982, s. 14 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2 and s. 100 - Maintainability of revision - Appellant (landlord) was landlord of a shop which was occupied by the respondents as tenants - Appellant filed eviction suit - Suit was dismissed and an appeal by the appellant also failed - During the pendency of second appeal, eviction suit was filed on the ground of bonafide requirement for personal occupation - Suit was dismissed by the Trial Court - On appeal, the appellant succeeded and got a decree for eviction against the respondent- HC allowed the revision petition filed by the respondents on two grounds (i) the second suit for eviction filed by the appellant was barred by the provisions of O. 2 r. 2 of CPC and (ii) the appellant's requirement for personal occupation was not bonafide - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the respondents filed a revision u/s. 14(8) of the Act against the judgment of the Appellant Court granting a decree for eviction in favour of the appellant, the revision was not maintainable as there is no provision in s. 14(8) of the Act for a revision against an Appellate Order. Hence the revision petition filed by the respondents before the HC was not maintainable. However, revision petition was treated as a second appeal u/s. 100 of CPC. Once the proceeding in the HC is treated as a second appeal u/s. 100 of CPC, the restrictions prescribed in the said Section would come into play. HC has acted beyond its jurisdiction in appreciating the evidence on record. The finding of fact rendered by the Second Additional District Judge in the first appeal is conclusive and the HC has exceeded its jurisdiction in interfering with the said findings. Appeal allowed.
