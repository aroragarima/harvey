Â Messrs Damodar Valley Corporation v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

21 November 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, A.K. Sarkar, Syed Jaffer Imam, K. Subba Rao, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 270; AIR 1961 SC 440; 1961 (2) SCJ 1; [1961] 2 S.C.R. 522; 1961 (12) STC 102

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Defence & Security Forces; Land & Property; Sales Tax

Keywords: Contract Of Sale

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Bihar Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Bihar Sales Tax Act, 1947, s. 25(1)- Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 132(1) - Govt. of India Act, 1935 - (A) Whether additional grounds of attack set out under reference of s. 25 of Bihar Sales Tax Act are open to Corporation? - Held, those additional grounds not open - They were never raised at any stage of proceedings before authorities below, or in High Court - Supreme Court sitting in appeal over decision of HC u/s. 25 of Act - HC in coming to its conclusion acting only in an advisory capacity - It is well settled that HC acting in its advisory capacity under taxing statute cannot go beyond questions referred to it, or on a reference called by it - Scope of appeal to court, even by special leave, cannot be extended beyond scope of controversy that could have been legally raised before HC - It is manifest that HC could not have expressed its opinion on any matter other than question actually before it as a result of reference made by Board of Revenue - (B) Whether transaction in question in this case amounted to a sale within meaning of Act? - Held, applying two tests - (i) whether there is any binding obligation on hirer to purchase goods; (ii) whether there is a right reserved to hirer to return goods at any time during subsistence of contract - It becomes clear that it was a case of sale of goods with a condition of repurchase on certain conditions depending upon satisfaction of corporation as to whether 'residual life' of machinery or equipment was not less than one-third of standard life in accordance with terms agreed between parties - It is clear on those terms that there is no right reserved to contractors to return goods at any time that they found it convenient or necessary - On other hand, they were bound to pay two-thirds of total approximate price fixed by parties in equal instalments - Contractors were not bound under terms to return any of machinery or equipments, nor was Corporation bound to take them back unconditionally - Term in agreement regarding 'taking over' of machinery or equipments by corporation on payment of 'residual value' is wholly inconsistent with a contract of mere hiring and is more consistent with property in goods having passed to contractors, subject to payment of all instalments of purchase pride - Further, stipulation that contractors themselves will have to supply spare parts, as and when needed, for replacements of worn out parts is also consistent with case of respondent that title had passed to contractors and that they were responsible for upkeep of machinery and equipments and for depreciation - If it were a mere contract of hiring, owner of goods would have continued to be liable for replacements of worn out parts and for depreciation - Transaction was a sale on deferred payments with an option to repurchase and not a mere contract of hiring, as contended on behalf of appellant - Appeal dismissed.
