Jharkhand State Electricity Board and others v Laxmi Business and Cement Company Private Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

28 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, K.S. Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 131; (2014) 5 SCC 236; AIR 2014 SC 1820; 2014 (4) AWC 3279; 2014 (2) CLT(SC) 145; JT 2014 (3) SC 507; 2014 (2) RCR(Civil) 236; 2014(3) SCALE 155; [2014] 3 S.C.R. 453

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Electricity & Energy

Keywords: Minimum Charge

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Administrative - Electricity Act, 2003, s. 86 - Electricity Regulatory Commission Act, 1998 - Consumers - Agreement - Tariff charged - Legality - Agreement was entered into between appellant/JSEB and consumers which, inter-alia, stipulated the tariff that was to be charged by appellant from consumers for supply of electricity to these consumers by appellant - In cl. 4(c) of the Agreement there was a provision of Minimum Guarantee Charges - Meanwhile 2003 Act was enacted and SERC passed order framing new tariff schedule u/s. 86 of the 2003 Act - Appellant however continued to send the bills as per the cl. 4(c) of agreement which were paid by the consumers under protest - Writ Petitions were filed by consumers for quashing of the energy bills on the ground that it had wrongly been raised as per cl. 4(c) of the Agreement which had ceased to have any effect on the framing of 2004 Tariff Schedule by the SERC - Petitions of the consumers were allowed and the order of Single Judge was upheld by DB - Hence instant appeals - (A) Whether after the enactment of the 2003 Act which came into force after passing of the new tariff order by JSERC, as per 2003 Act can the State Electricity Board still charge a tariff determined by itself -

Held, after the 2003 Act was enacted, power to frame tariff was given to SERC - Said power was statutorily conferred upon the SERC under the Act - Before the passing of 2003 Act, 1998 Act was enacted and u/s. 17 of the said Act, JSEBRC was constituted by State Govt. - On the passing of the 2003 Act, 1998 Act was repealed - At the same time, 2003 recognized the SERCs constituted under the 1998 Act - It was also not in dispute that 2004 Tariff Schedule framed by the SERC was in exercise of powers conferred upon it u/s. 86 (a) of 2003 Act - It was, thus, beyond the pale of doubt that the State Electricity Boards have no power whatsoever to frame tariff which was under the exclusive domain of the Commission - Legal position was judicially recognized - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Whether the issue of demand charge to HTS - 1 category of consumers was left non-considered by the State Commission in the tariff order so that the same may be continued in the manner existed in the State or whether the same was considered and given affect to in the tariff order which came into effect from 1-1-2004 -

Held, SERC fixed the tariff on the request of the JSEB itself when it approached the SERC for this purpose - Tariff Petition filed by JSEB before the SERC, the JSEB did not propose to continue the manner of 75% of contract demand and the SERC allowed the demand charge 140-KV-Month - Tariff Schedule which was the final outcome of the tariff process was binding on the State as well - JSEB itself in its application/reference to the SERC did not ask for fixing any minimum guarantee charges. It would be relevant to mention that the JSEB in its proposal for fixation of tariff for 2003-04, submitted before the Regulatory Commission, indicated both the existing tariff and the tariff proposed by it in respect of all consumers, including all categories of HTS (High Tension Service) consumers - SERC after undertaking the necessary exercise, fixed the tariff of all categories - Tariff order further revealed that the SERC had even compared the proposal of JSEB with the tariff prevailing in other States in India and after detailed analysis thereof, it approved the tariff for HTS consumers which was mentioned in table 5.31 of the 2004 Tariff Schedule - Therefore, it could not be said that the SERC was oblivious of the clause relating to minimum guarantee charges which JSEB was charging from its consumers as per the earlier agreements entered into with them - Position would become crystal clear from the following discussion in the 2004 Tariff Schedule wherein the SCRC gave specific reasons for revising and approving the tariff for HTS consumers - Appeals dismissed.

(C) What would be the effect of s. 185 (Repeal and Saving Clause) of the 2003 Act upon the HT supply Agreement entered upon the Board and the Consumer prior to2003 Act -

Held, even if proceed on the basis that the statutory agreements entered into earlier were saved, the agreement in question stands replaced by 2004 Tariff Schedule - BSES v. Tata Power Co.Ltd. 2003 Indlaw SC 871, referred to - SC was of the opinion that even the argument based on s. 185 of the 2003 Act would not bring any change to the results of instant case - SC thus, did not fault with the judgment of HC appealed against - Plea of unjust and enrichment would not be available to appellants - No such plea was raised before HC either before the Single Judge or DB - In the Special Leave Petition, submission was made for the first time at the time of hearing of instant appeals - Moreover, it was not a case of payment of tax which was a burden passed on the consumers - Mafatlal Industries Ltd. v. Union of India 1996 Indlaw SC 4128 that the question of unjust enrichment would arise for consideration - As far as issue like instant case was concerned, such a question was left open in the aforesaid judgment - Court had made it clear the concept of unjust enrichment had no application for refunds other than taxes - Appeals dismissed.
