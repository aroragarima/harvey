State of Uttar Pradesh v Nandu Vishwakarma and Others

Supreme Court of India

06 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 842; (2009) 14 SCC 501; AIR 2009 SC 3188; 2009 CRLJ 4157; 2009 (4) JCC 2525; 2010 (1) MLJ(Crl) 1001; 2010 (5) RCR(Criminal) 229; 2009(9) SCALE 81; [2009] 10 S.C.R. 185

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Power Of The Court

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 r/w 34 and 367 r/w 34 - Conviction and sentence by Trial Court - Reversed by High Court - Whether proper? - Held, Yes - Parties belonged to two different villages - Enmity due to land dispute and pending litigations - Instances of mutual assault - First informant was PW1, brother of deceased; PW2 was wife of first informant - It was stated by the two witnesses that they were assaulted by the respondents with 'lathi' and 'gandasa'; said fact find no mention in the FIR - Incident occurred in the village other than the villages where both parties resides - Admitted facts noted, materials on record perused and contentions heard - Per HC findings, in litigation between the parties decision was finally in favour of the deceased for which accused had obtained a stay - Informant had two plots in the village where incident had taken place, but what was mentioned in the FIR was that, that the incident had taken place at the disputed land; investigating officer also could not state and clarify in his deposition as to in which of the two plots the incident had actually taken place - Further from records it came out that there was some evidence to indicate that the accused persons were in possession of the land where the incident had taken place - Wife of informant was also injured in the incident but she was not taken to the hospital with the deceased and no explanation came from the IO as to why he did not take any step in getting PW2 admitted in the hospital - No 'gandasa' injury found by the doctor - Deposition of witnesses full of contradictions - Settled principle of law, that when on the basis of the evidence on record two views could be taken, one in favour of the accused and the other against the accused, the one favouring the accused should always be accepted - Impugned judgment of acquittal thus upheld on such grounds - Being without merit - Appeal dismissed.
