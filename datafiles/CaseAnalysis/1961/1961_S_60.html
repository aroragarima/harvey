Sri Sinha Ramanuja Jeer and others v Sri Ranga Ramanuja Jeer and Another

Supreme Court of India

27 April 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Raghubar Dayal, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 529; AIR 1961 SC 1720; 1962 (1) AnWR 1; 1962 (1) MLJ(SC) 1; [1962] 2 S.C.R. 509

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Practice & Procedure - CPC, 1908 - Maintainabilty of suit filed by Aradanaikar and trustee of temple - Whether facts and circumstances enable a court to hold that one temple is subordinate or part of other temple, so that office holders of one temple would become office holders of other? - Held, facts clearly establish that in fact and in law two institutions are different legal entities - In past, trustees of Athinathalwar temple might have disbursed tasdik allowances contributed by Govt. to various temples, including Emberumanar temple, but it is well known that for convenience of administration services of trustees of a large temple were very often utilized by Govt. in that regard; it might have been that sometime amounts payable to smaller temples were allowed to lapse, but there is nothing on record to show that it was not out of negligence of trustees of minor shrines in not making any pressing demands on trustees of Athinathalwar temple; it might also have been that trustee of bigger temple, in his supervisory capacity, dismissed once in a way trustee of a smaller shrine in locality, but that could be explained by paramount position of trustee of bigger temple in locality compared to that of minor temples - These and such acts may show that trustee of Athinathalwar temple had exercised similar supervisory control in past over minor temples; but that in itself does not make trustee of temple of Emberumanar an office-holder in bigger temple - It is well known that in past temples were under supervision of Revenue Board and later on under various temple committees - It cannot be suggested that on that account trustees of minor temples were officers in Revenue Board or temple committees, as case may be - It cannot also appreciate how mutual visits of idols to other's temple and honours shown to idols on such visits could have any bearing on question to be decided, though they reflect intimate relationship that exists between Lord and his ardent devotee Ramanuja in public consciousness - But such cordial relationship existing between two independent temples cannot in eye of law make one a part of other - Two independent institutions legally cannot, except in manner known to law, be amalgamated into one institution by developing merely sentimental attachment between them - This argument was rightly rejected by learned District Judge, and High Court went wrong in accepting it - Appeal allowed.
