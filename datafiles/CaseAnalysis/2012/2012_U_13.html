Delhi Race Club Ltd. v Union Of India & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

13 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 197; (2012) 8 SCC 680; AIR 2012 SC 3408; [2012] 347 ITR 593; 2012(6) SCALE 413; [2012] 8 S.C.R. 1; [2012] 53 VST 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Betting, Calcutta Municipal Act, 1951, Union Territories (Laws) Act, 1950, Andhra Pradesh Factories Rules, 1950, Mysore Race Courses Licensing Act, 1952, Delhi Race Course Licensing Rules, 1985

Summary: Constitution - Mysore Race Courses Licensing Act, 1952, s. 11(2) - Delhi Race Course Licensing (Amendment) Rules, 2001 - Delhi Race Course Licensing Rules, 1985 - Licence fee - Challenged - On 19-10-1984, the Central Government, extended the Act to the Delhi - In furtherance of the power conferred u/s. 11 of the Act, by a notification dt. 1-3-1985, the Administration of the Delhi, notified the 1985 Rules - On 7-3-2001, the Lt. Governor of Delhi enacted the 2001 Rules and enhanced the licence fee rates - Respondent No. 3 (Commissioner of Excise, Entertainment and Luxury Tax) issued a demand letter to the appellant informing them that the licence fee deposited by them was short by Rs.17,80,000/- for the year 2001-02 and by Rs.18,00,000/- for the year 2002- 03 - Validity of said demand notice was questioned by the appellant before HC, on the grounds that both the notifications, dt. 19-10-1984 and 7-3-2001 were illegal in as much as (i) delegation of powers u/s. 11 of the Act to the Lt. Governor, to fix the licence fee without any guidelines was excessive delegation of legislative power and was therefore, ultra vires, (ii) in the absence of an element of quid pro quo, the licence fee charged was not in the nature of a fee but a tax and (iii) the 10 fold increase in licence fee was highly excessive - HC dismissed the said petition and held that the licence fee in question was not a compensatory fee and consequently there was no requirement of a quid pro quo, the licence fee was in the nature of a regulatory fee and therefore, would not require any quid pro quo in the form of any social service - Hence, the instant appeal - Appellant contended that s. 11(2) of the Act confers unguided, unfettered and arbitrary power on the Government to fix the licence fee without a minute shred of guidance of any manner and hence was beyond the limits of permissible delegation and therefore, deserves to be struck down as unconstitutional - Whether impugned order of the HC could be upheld - Held, a licence fee imposed for regulatory purposes was not conditioned by the fact that there must be a quid pro quo for the services rendered, but that, such licence fee must be reasonable and not excessive - It would again not be possible to work out with arithmetical equivalence the amount of fee which could be said to be reasonable or otherwise - If there was a broad correlation between the expenditure which the State incurs and the fees charged, the fees could be sustained as reasonable - In the instant case, the object of the Act, was to regulate, monitor, control and encourage the sport of horse-racing - For said purpose, licences were issued subject to certain conditions - The compliance with the licence conditions was inevitable for renewal of the licences as well as significant to avoid any penalty under the Act - To ensure such compliance, District officers/entertainment tax officers were entrusted with the duty of inspection - The expenses incurred in carrying out such regular inspections have to be considerable - Hence, the licence fee imposed was a regulatory fee and need not necessarily entail rendition of specific services in return but at the same time should not be excessive - However, in the instant case, the appellant has not challenged the amount of the levy as unreasonable and expropriatory or excessive - Moreover, the challenge to the validity of s. 11(2) of the Act was raised after almost 15 years of its coming into force - Appellant, since the commencement of the Act, had been regularly paying the licence fee and the instant challenge was made only when quantum of the licence fee was increased by the Government - Evidently, the inflation during that period was taken as the criterion for increasing the quantum of the fee - It was a reasonable increase keeping in view the fact that the expenditure incurred by the Government in carrying out the regulatory activities for attaining the object of the Act would have proportionately increased - Impugned order of the HC that s. 11(2) of the Act as well as 2001 Rules do not suffer from any legal infirmity, to be upheld - Appeal dismissed.
