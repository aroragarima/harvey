Premium Global Securities Private Limited and others v Securities and Exchange Board of India and another

Supreme Court of India

09 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 814; [2016] 194 Comp Cas 92; 2016 (2) CompLJ 1; 2015(13) SCALE 401

Case Digest

Subject: Securities Exchange Board of India

Keywords: Brokerage, Stock, Trading, Restriction, Incorporated, Membership, Securities Contracts (Regulation) Rules, 1957, SEBI (Interest Liability Regularisation) Scheme, 2004

Summary: Securities Exchange Board of India - Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992, s. 11(2) - Securities Contracts (Regulation) Rules, 1957, rr. 8(1)(f), 8(3)(f) - Transfer of Business to Subsidiary - Demand of Payment of Fee - Challenged - Appellant no.1 was incorporated in compliance with directions of Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI),which took over membership of appellant no.3, and was approved by NSE without any transfer fee - SEBI issued scheme which envisage waiver of 80% outstanding interest if broker paid outstanding principal outstanding principal along with 20% outstanding interest during specific period - However, appellant no.1 was denied benefit and SEBI demanded payment of fees - Tribunal held that compulsion on appellant no.1 was not to extent to serve their brokerage business and transfer it to their subsidiary - Hence, instant appeals - Whether there was compulsion of law to transfer membership to appellant no.1.

Held, SEBI, as a trade regulator in the securities market, is entitled to charge registration fees for enabling it to carry out its functions as stipulated in s. 11(2) of the Act. However it appears at present that SEBI has pounced at the opportunity to charge fresh registration fees choosing to ignore the exemptions assured by it. The restriction imposed was to not have fund-based and trading activities together under one roof. Thus any action taken by the appellants to comply with restriction of not participating in both the activities simultaneously would be under compulsion of law. The compulsion of law under the Rules is directed towards the desired end and not concerned with the means, and it would be wrong to ascribe otherwise. Further, with regard to transfer of trading license by 'X' to 'Y', later transferred to appellant no.1, the trading membership was transferred twice but only the first transfer was under compulsion of law. Hence, impugned judgment of Tribunal in appeal by appellant no.1 is set aside and direct that the appellants be given the benefit of fee continuity. However, impugned order passed by Tribunal in respect of appeal by appellant no.2 does suffer from any infirmity and the same is dismissed. Appeals disposed of.
