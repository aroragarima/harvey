Sahadevan and another v State of Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 232; (2012) 6 SCC 403; AIR 2012 SC 2435; 2012 CRLJ 3014; 2012 (3) JCC 1756; JT 2012 (5) SC 151; 2012 (3) MLJ(Crl) 495; 2012 (2) OLR 316; 2012 (2) RCR(Criminal) 899; 2012(5) SCALE 415; [2012] 4 S.C.R. 366

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Extra-judicial confession - Conviction - Challenged - Appellants allegedly murdered the deceased and burnt his body - The trial Court convicted appellants u/s. 302 of IPC - HC dismissed appeal filed against said order - Hence, the instant appeal - Appellants contended that the case of the prosecution was solely based upon the extra-judicial confession, which was neither reliable nor has been recorded in accordance with law - Said extra-judicial confession cannot form the basis of conviction of the appellants since it has no corroboration and when examined in light of the settled principles of law, it was inconsequential, thus, the appellants were entitled to the benefit of doubt - Further contended that there was neither any eye-witness nor the prosecution has proved the complete chain of circumstances - The courts have erred in applying the theory of last seen together to return the finding of conviction against the appellants - Whether order of the trial court as affirmed by the HC could be upheld - Held, there was no doubt that there was no eye-witness - It was a case based upon circumstantial evidence - In case of circumstantial evidence, the onus lies upon the prosecution to prove the complete chain of events which shall undoubtedly point towards the guilt of the appellants - Furthermore, in case of circumstantial evidence, where the prosecution relies upon an extra-judicial confession, the court has to examine the same with a greater degree of care and caution - In the instant case, all the appellants were stated to have murdered the deceased and burnt his body - It was a case of circumstantial evidence where not only has the prosecution failed to prove all the facts and events to complete the chain of events pointing only towards the guilt of the appellants but there were also definite discrepancies in the case of the prosecution, contradictions between the statements of the material witnesses and the most important piece of prosecution evidence, the extra- judicial confession, was found entirely unreliable, not worthy of credence as well as the facts recorded in extra- judicial confession stand disproved by another prosecution witness herself, i.e., PW-2, who, in fact, has lost her husband - Hence, appellants were acquitted of the charge u/s. 302 of IPC - Appeal allowed.
