Siemons Public Communication Networks Private v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

06 November 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, C.K. Thakker, Lokeshwar Singh Panta

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2049; (2008) 16 SCC 215; AIR 2009 SC 1204; 2008 (155) DLT 621; JT 2008 (12) SC 353; 2009 (2) MLJ(SC) 293; 2008(14) SCALE 513; [2008] 15 S.C.R. 585

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Bidding - Transfer of Technology - Maintainability - Appellant no.1 along with seven others had submitted their bids in response to Request For Proposal('RFP') made by respondent no.2 for procurement of Digital Radio Trunking System('DRTS') - Proposed contract to be carried out in five phases spread over a period of time which included evaluation, trails which were to be conducted by Empowered Technical Committee - Terms of Tender also stipulated that licensed manufacture of DRTS should be undertaken by respondent no.2 through Transfer of Technology - Appellant no.1 , respondent nos.3 and 4 bids were read out in presence of representatives of all three bidders - Outcome of tender was not intimated - Writ petition was filed before DB of HC which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contented that appellant no.1 emerged as lowest bidder but not intimated about outcome of tender - Whether process adopted by authority was mala fide or intended to favour was maintainable - Held, none of criteria had been satisfied justifying Court's interference in grant of contract in favour of appellants - Further, when power of judicial review was invoked in matters relating to tenders or award of contracts, certain special features had to be considered - Furthermore, contract was commercial transaction and evaluating tenders and awarding contracts were essentially commercial functions - Therefore, principles of equity and natural justice stay at a distance - Furthermore, if decision relating to award of contracts was bonafide and was in public interest, Courts would not exercise power of judicial review and interfere even if it was accepted for sake of argument that there was a procedural lacuna - Thus, contract was in respect of sensitive Army equipments which were urgently needed and could not be held that process adopted or decision made was so arbitrary or irrational that no responsible authority acting reasonably or in accordance with relevant law could not have taken such a decision - Appeal dismissed.
