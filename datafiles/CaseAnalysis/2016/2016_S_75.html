Uttam v Saubhag Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

02 March 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

Case Digest

Subject: Fema; Land & Property

Keywords: Right To Sue, Operation Of Law, Joint Family, Hindu Succession Act, 1956, Hindu Law

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, ss. 4, 8, 19 - Claim of Share - Denial of Partition of Suit property Challenged - Appellant/plaintiff files suit seeking partition and claim of share in suit property - Trial Court decreed suit in favour of plaintiff on ground that suit property being ancestral property and was not earlier partitioned - First Appellate Court confirmed finding of Trial Court with regard to ancestral property, however disentitled plaintiff on ground that during life time of class I heir, i.e, his father/ respondent no.3, had no right to sue for partition and dismissed suit - Second Appellate Court upheld order of First Appellate Court - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, when a male Hindu dies after the commencement of the Act, having at the time of his death an interest in Mitakshara coparcenary property, his interest in the property will devolve by survivorship upon the surviving members of the coparcenary. In order to determine the share of the Hindu male coparcener who is governed by s.6 of the act, proviso, a partition is effected by operation of law immediately before his death. In this partition, all the coparceners and the male Hindu's widow get a share in the joint family property. Further, on the application of s. 8 of the Act, either by reason of the death of a male Hindu leaving self-acquired property or by the application of s.6 proviso, such property would devolve only by intestacy and not survivorship. In other words, on a conjoint reading of ss. 4, 8 and 19 of the Act, after joint family property has been distributed in accordance with s. 8 of the act, on principles of intestacy, the joint family property ceases to be joint family property in the hands of the various persons who have succeeded to it as they hold the property as tenants in common and not as joint tenants. In instant case, the ancestral property ceased to be joint family property on the date of death of 'X', and the other coparceners and his widow held the property as tenants in common and not as joint tenants. Hence, on the date of the birth of the appellant in 1977 the said ancestral property, not being joint family property, the suit for partition of such property would not be maintainable. Appeal dismissed.
