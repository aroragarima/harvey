Musheer Khan @ Badshah Khan And Another v State Of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

28 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 64; (2010) 2 SCC 748; AIR 2010 SC 762; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 933; 2010 (3) JCC 1648; JT 2010 (1) SC 535; 2010 (3) MLJ(Crl) 452; 2010 (1) RCR(Criminal) 817; 2010(1) SCALE 676; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 119

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 201 and 302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 100(4) and 100(5) - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 25(1)(b) and 27 - Murder - Conspiracy - Conviction - Circumstantial evidence - Charge sheet was filed against A-1 to A-7 - Prosecution case was that A-4 and A-5 were hired criminals and were engaged on payment by A-1, A-2, A-3 and A-6 for killing the deceased - Trial Court acquitted A-3 and convicted A-4 and A-5 u/s. 302 r/w s. 120B, IPC and ss. 25(1)(b) and 27 of the Arms Act and awarded death penalty - A-1, A-2 and A-6 were convicted u/ss. 302 r/w s. 120B and were awarded life sentence - On appeal, HC confirmed the death sentence against A-4 and A-5, but acquitted the other three accused - Hence, present appeals - (A) Circumstantial evidence - Present case was based on circumstantial evidence and it rests on the evidence of PW- 3 and PW-4 mainly - Held, circumstantial evidence against A-4 and A-5 do not constitute a complete chain consistent with the guilt of A-4 and A-5 and incompatible with their innocence - (B) Test identification parade - Assessing evidence of identification of the accused persons by PW-3 and PW-4 - Held, identification by PW-4 cannot be relied upon at all inasmuch there were grave doubts about the presence of PW-4 at the place of occurrence - There was vital contradiction between the versions of witnesses identifying and the person conducting the TI Parade - Further, no reliance ought to have been placed by the courts below or HC on delayed identification of A-5 for which there was no explanation by the prosecution - (C) Evidence of finger print expert - Held, evidence of finger print expert is not substantive evidence - Such evidence can only be used to corroborate some items of substantive evidence which are otherwise on record - In present case, finger print expert has not given any evidence of finger print on the alleged weapon of offence - Therefore, in the facts of present case and in view of the prosecution evidence the evidence of finger print expert does help the prosecution - (B) Discovery of the weapon of assault - Non-compliance with the provision of s.100(4) or s.100(5), CrPC - Effect thereof - Held, reliability of the materials discovered pursuant to the facts deposed by the accused in police custody depends on the facts of each case - If the discovery is otherwise reliable, its evidentiary value is not diluted just by reason of non-compliance with the provision of s.100(4) or s.100(5), CrPC - Reason is that s.100 falls under Chapter VII of the CrPC which deals with processes initiated to compel the production of things on a search - Safeguards in search proceedings based on compulsion cannot be read into discovery on the basis of facts voluntarily deposed - (C) Conviction - Held, acquittal of A-1, A-2, A-3 and A-6 which was upheld by this Court casts a serious doubt on the entire prosecution - Benefit of doubt to be given to A-4 and A-5 - Order of conviction of A-4 and A-5 set aside - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 201 and 302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Arms Act, 1959 - Appreciation of circumstantial evidence - Held, facts alleged as the basis of any legal inference from circumstantial evidence must be clearly proved beyond any reasonable doubt - If conviction rests solely on circumstantial evidence, it must create a network from which there is no escape for the accused - All the links in the chain of evidence must be proved beyond reasonable doubt and they must exclude the evidence of guilt of any other person than the accused - Further, in order to justify the inference of guilt, the inculpatory facts must be incompatible with the innocence of the accused and is incapable of explanation upon any other reasonable hypothesis except his guilt - Appeals disposed of.
