Justice P.D. Dinakaran v Hon’Ble Judges Inquiry Committee And Others

Supreme Court of India

05 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 402; (2011) 8 SCC 380; 2011(5) ALL MR 408; JT 2011 (7) SC 425; 2011 (8) MLJ(SC) 331; 2011 (2) OLR 445; 2011(6) SCALE 797; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 1064; 2011 (7) SLR 697

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968, Judges (Inquiry) Rules, 1969, U.P. (Temporary) Control of Rent and Eviction Act, 1947, Bengal Money-Lenders Act, 1940

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 217 r/w. 124(4) - Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968, s. 3(2) - Judges (Inquiry) Rules, 1969, r. 9(2)(c) - Quashing of impeachment proceedings - 50 members of the Rajya Sabha submitted a notice of motion for presenting an address to the President of India for removal of the petitioner, the Chief Justice of Karnataka HC u/art. 217 r/w. 124(4) of the Constitution - After the motion was admitted, the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha constituted a Committee comprising Mr. Justice V.S. Sirpurkar, Judge, SC, Mr. Justice A.R. Dave, the then Chief Justice of Andhra Pradesh HC and Shri P.P. Rao, Senior Advocate, SC (Respondent no.3) vide notification dt. 15.1.2010 - Said committee issued notice dt. 16.3.2011, which was served upon the petitioner on 23.3.2011, requiring him to appear on 9.4.2011 to answer the charges - Upon receiving the notice, the petitioner submitted representation to the Vice-President of India and the Chairman, Rajya Sabha with the prayer that the order admitting notice of motion may be withdrawn, the order constituting the Inquiry Committee be rescinded and notice issued by the Committee may be annulled on the ground respondent no. 3 was biased against him as he had actively participate in the seminar organised by the Bar Association of India on 28.11.2009 and already expressed views in the matter and declared him guilty of certain charges - Petitioner further claimed that respondent no. 3 had led a delegation of the advocates to meet the then Chief Justice of India and was a signatory to the representation made by the senior advocates against his elevation to the SC - After considering the objections of the petitioner, the said Committee held that the objection was raised for the first time only after a notice along with the charges and the list of witnesses and documents in support of the charges were served upon the petitioner - Committee also held that the stage and the time at which the objection was raised make it clear that the object was to somehow scuttle the enquiry by causing delay in the Committee's proceedings - Petitioner filed writ petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution for quashing order passed by the Committee and for grant of a declaration that the proceedings conducted by the Committee were null and void - (A) Whether participation of respondent no.3 in the seminar organised by the Bar Association of India where he made speech opposing the petitioner's elevation to SC and also drafted a resolution to that effect can lead to an inference that he was biased against the petitioner and he ought not to have been appointed as a member of the Committee in terms of s. 3(2)(c) of the Act? - Held, no man can be a Judge in his own cause and justice should not only be done, but manifestly be seen to be done - A person having interest in the subject matter of cause was precluded from acting as a Judge - One has to enquire as to whether there was real danger of bias on the part of the person against whom such apprehension was expressed in the sense that he might favour or disfavour a party - To disqualify a person from adjudicating on the ground of interest in the subject matter of lis, the test of real likelihood of the bias was to be applied - The test would be whether a reasonably intelligent man fully apprised of all the facts would have a serious apprehension of bias - In the instant case, respondent no.3 participated in the seminar organised by the Bar Association of India of which he was Vice-President - Respondent no. 3 demanded public inquiry into the charges levelled against the petitioner before his elevation as a Judge of SC - Thereafter, respondent no.3 drafted a resolution opposing elevation of the petitioner as a Judge of SC - He along with other eminent lawyers met the then Chief Justice of India - These facts could give rise to reasonable apprehension in the mind of an intelligent person that respondent no.3 was likely to be biased - Hence, petitioner's apprehension of likelihood of bias against respondent no.3 was reasonable and not fanciful, though, in fact, he may not be biased - (B) Whether order passed by the Committee should be quashed on the ground of reasonable likelihood of bias of respondent no. 3? - Held, the petitioner raised the plea of bias only after receiving notice which was accompanied by statement of charges and the lists of documents and witnesses - Petitioner's silence in this regard for a period of almost ten months militates against the bona fides of his objection to the appointment of respondent no. 3 as member of the Committee - A person on the petitioner's standing can be presumed to be aware of his right to raise an objection - If the petitioner had slightest apprehension that respondent no. 3 had pre-judged his guilt or he was otherwise biased, then, he would have on the first available opportunity objected to his appointment as member of the Committee - Petitioner could have done so immediately after publication of notification dt.15.1.2010 - He could have represented to the Chairman that investigation by a Committee of which respondent no. 3 was a member will not be fair and impartial because the former had already presumed him to be guilty - Such representation would have given an opportunity to the Chairman to consider the grievance made by the petitioner and take appropriate decision - However, petitioner never thought that respondent no. 3 was prejudiced or ill-disposed against him and this was the reason why he did not raise objection till April, 2011 against the inclusion of respondent no. 3 in the Committee - This leads to an irresistible inference that the petitioner had waived his right to object to the appointment of respondent no.3 as member of the Committee - Moreover, petitioner was an intelligent person and knows that in terms of r. 9(2)(c) of the Rules, the Presiding Officer of the Committee was required to forward the report to the Chairman within a period of 3 months from the date the charges framed u/s. 3(3) of the Act were served upon him - Therefore, he wants to adopt every possible tactic to delay the submission of report which may in all probability compel the Committee to make a request to the Chairman to extend the time in terms of proviso to r. 9(2)(c) of the Rules - For that reason, no Court can render assistance to the petitioner in a petition filed with the sole object of delaying finalisation of the inquiry - However, on the issue of bias, Chairman be requested to nominate another distinguished jurist in place of respondent no. 3 - The proceedings initiated against the petitioner have progressed only to the stage of framing of charges and the Committee was yet to record its findings on the charges and submit report - Therefore, nomination of another jurist will not hamper the proceedings of the Committee and the reconstituted Committee shall be entitled to proceed on the charges already framed against the petitioner - Writ petition dismissed.
