M. G. Eshwarappa and others v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

02 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Prafulla C. Pant, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 183; (2017) 4 SCC 558; AIR 2017 SC 1197; 2017 CRLJ 2263; 2017 (2) JCC 1289; JT 2017 (3) SC 67; 2017 (4) KarLJ 1; 2017 (1) OLR 1080; 2017 (2) RCR(Criminal) 259; 2017(3) SCALE 296; 2017 (2) WLN(SC) 58

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: First Information Report, Bone, Spot, Prosecution Story, Blood, Distance, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Complainant

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 323, 34, 354, 506 - Criminal assault - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants/accused armed with club, Kandli and iron rods allegedly assaulted deceased who succumbed to injuries - Charge-sheet was filed against accused for offences punishable u/ss. 506, 354 and 302 r/w s. 34 IPC - Trial Court acquitted accused holding that charge against them was not proved beyond reasonable doubt - On appeal, HC allowed appeal and convicted accused u/ss. 506, 354 and 302 r/w s. 34 IPC and sentenced them to imprisonment for life - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, Trial Court committed grave error by accepting the defence case that deceased might have died of the injuries suffered in an accident, as the possibility was not ruled out by PW2/doctor. SC have gone through the statement of PW2 that "such injuries can be caused to a person if he meets accident". There was no suggestion of the fact that at the place of incident any vehicle had passed through at the time of incident. Trial Court appears to have taken support of conjectures and surmises. HC has correctly held that the view taken by Trial Court was perverse and against the evidence on record. The statement of PW1/injured was corroborated not only from the statements of PW16, PW29 and PW30 but also from the medical evidence on record. Further, deceased was not in a conscious condition when he was admitted in hospital as such, there was no question of recording of dying declaration of patient in such a critical condition. FIR in instant case was prompt and copy of the same appears to have been sent on the very next day to Magistrate without delay. It was settled law that FIR was not an encyclopaedia, and if necessary details were there, on its basis detailed narration by witnesses cannot be doubted. Appeal dismissed.
