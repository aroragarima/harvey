State of Punjab v Mohar Singh

Supreme Court of India

20 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 57; AIR 1955 SC 84; 1955 CRLJ 254; 1955 SCJ 25; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 893

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, repealing Act, East Punjab Refugees (Registration of Land C) aims Ordinance 7 of 1948

Summary: Criminal - East Punjab Refugees (Registration of Land Claims) Ordinance 7 of 1948 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 438 - Punjab General Clauses Act, 1898, ss. 4 and 27 - Interpretation Act, 1889, s. 38(2) - Partnership Act,s. 69 - Repealing Act 12 of 1984, ss. 7, 11, 6 and 74(b) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 134(1)(c) - General Clauses Act, 1897, ss. 6 and 30 - Govt. Of India Act, 1935, s. 88 - Ordinance promulgated by Governor of East Punjab making provisions for registration of land claims of East Punjab refugees - Respondent, who purports to be a refugee from West Pakistan, filed a claim in accordance with provisions of this ordinance, stating that he had lands situated in West Punjab - Ordinance was repealed by Act - Claim filed by respondent investigated and found that statement was false - Prosecution was started against him - Magistrate convicted respondent - High Court set aside conviction of respondent - Appeal - (A) Whether a criminal liability incurred when ordinance was in force would continue after it came to an end? - Held, repealing Act reproduces provisions of ordinance in their entirety, but it nowhere provides that offences committed, when ordinance was in force, could be punished after its repeal - Offence was committed at a time when Act was not in force and no man could be prosecuted or punished under a law which came into existence subsequent to commission of offence - According to s. 30 of General clauses Act, which corresponds to s. 27 of Punjab Act, provisions of Act are applicable to ordinances as well - It has no application when a statute, which is of a temporary nature, automatically expires by efflux of time - Ordinance in present case was a temporary statute but period during which it was to continue had not expired when repealing Act was passed - Repeal, therefore, was an effective one which would attract operation of s. 6, General Clauses Act - (B) Whether fact of repeal of ordinance being followed by re-enactment would make provision of s. 6 of General Clauses Act inapplicable to present case? - Further held, whenever there is a repeal of an enactment, consequences laid down in s. 6 of General Clauses Act will follow unless a different intention appears - The ss. 4, 7 and 8 make it apparent that it was not intention of legislature that rights and liabilities in respect of claims filed under Ordinance shall be extinguished on passing of Act - Provisions of s. 6 of General Clauses Act will, apply to a case of repeal even if there is simultaneous enactment unless a contrary intention can be gathered from new enactment - Appeal allowed.
