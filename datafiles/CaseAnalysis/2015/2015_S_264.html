Vinod Kumar v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

08 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 8; (2015) 3 SCC 138; AIR 2015 SC 1032; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 803; 2015 CRLJ 1250; JT 2015 (1) SC 16; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 214; 2015 (1) RCR(Criminal) 532; 2015(1) SCALE 121; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 328

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Ransom

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 363 and 364A - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 27 - Kidnapping for ransom - Conviction - Challenged - Prosecution alleged that appellant kidnapped X for ransom - Appellant submitted that he was falsely implicated in the crime as he had expressed his unwillingness to work in the house of Y and demanded his salary - Trial Court, on the basis of the evidence brought on record, came to hold that the prosecution had not been able to establish any case against the appellant inasmuch as his name was not mentioned in the FIR and none of the witnesses had implicated him and from the disclosure statement of A, nothing was revealed which could be considered against appellant u/s. 27 of the Act, and accordingly acquitted him - On appeal, DB reversed the order of the Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, kidnapped boy was recovered at railway station. The appellant has not explained how the child could be brought to Delhi. Trial Court has noted certain discrepancies in the evidence of B, but without any justifiable reason. The Trial Court has really niggled on unimportant and unnecessary details. It is quite natural on the part of B to pose a question to A as he was slightly anxious to see a domestic help taking a child. This is inherent in human nature and, therefore, the version of B could not have been ignored. These aspects, weigh quite heavily against the appellant. Judgment of reversal by the HC is absolutely defensible and does not warrant any interference. Appeal dismissed.
