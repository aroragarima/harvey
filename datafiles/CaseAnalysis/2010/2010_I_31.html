IBA Health (India) Private Limited v Info-Drive Systems Sdn. Bhd.

Supreme Court of India

23 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, S.H. Kapadia

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 868; (2010) 10 SCC 553; [2010] 159 Comp Cas 369; 2010 (4) CompLJ 481; 2011 (3) KarLJ 1; 2011 (1) MLJ(SC) 745; 2010(10) SCALE 151; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 137

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate

Keywords: Frivolous

Summary: Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 433, 434(1)(a) and 439 - Winding up - Pursuant to a Cooperation Agreement entered into by the appellant-company and the respondent-company, the respondent-company introduced the appellant-company to 'M' for the sale and supply of the appellant-company Hospital Information Systems (HIS) Software applications and for that service, the appellant-company agreed to pay commission charges to the respondent-company - Dispute arose between the parties regarding the payment of the commission charges which led the parties entering into a deed of settlement, pursuant to which appellant-company agreed to pay the commission charges due to the respondent-company - Respondent-company issued a legal notice against appellant-company u/s. 434(1)(a) of the Companies Act, 1956, calling upon the appellant-company to pay the amount in terms of the Deed of Settlement entered into by the parties within twenty one days from the date of receipt of notice, failing which the appellant-company was informed that appropriate legal proceedings would be initiated - Appellant-company denied the liability to pay the amount demanded - Company petition was filed by the respondent-company praying for winding up of the appellant-company on the ground that it had failed to pay a sum in terms of the Deed of Settlement entered into by the parties - Company Court ordered to proceed further calling for an advertisement in the newspaper and also directed the parties to appear before the Mediation Centre for amicably settling the dispute - Order upheld by DB of HC - Hence, present appeal - (A) Commercially solvent - Whether factum of commercial solvency, as such, would be sufficient to reject the petition for winding up? - Held, examination of the company's solvency may be a useful aid in determining whether the refusal to pay debt is a result of a bona fide dispute as to the liability or whether it reflects an inability to pay but it cannot be characterized as a standalone ground - Hence, if there is no dispute as to the company's liability, the solvency of the company might not constitute a standalone ground for setting aside a notice u/s. 434(1)(a), meaning thereby, if a debt is undisputedly owing, then it has to be paid - If the company refuses to pay on no genuine and substantial grounds, it should not be able to avoid the statutory demand - Further, where the company has a bona fide dispute, the petitioner cannot be regarded as a creditor of the company for the purposes of winding up - Furthermore, Company Court is expected to ascertain that the company's refusal is supported by a reasonable cause or a bona fide dispute in which the dispute can only be adjudicated by a trial in a civil court - In the present case, there was a bona fide dispute with regard to the amount of claim made by the respondent-company in the company petition which was substantial in nature - However, Company Court was very casual in its approach and did not make any endeavor to ascertain as to whether the company sought to be wound up for non-payment of debt has a defence which is substantial in nature and if not adjudicated in a proper forum, would cause serious prejudice to the company - (B) Malicious proceedings for winding up - Held, party to the dispute should not be allowed to use the threat of winding up petition as a means of enforcing the company to pay a bona fide disputed debt - A Company Court cannot be reduced as a debt collecting agency or as a means of bringing improper pressure on the company to pay a bona fide disputed debt - (C) Public policy considerations - Held, publication in the Newspaper of the filing of winding up petition may damage the creditworthiness or financial standing of the company and which may also have other economic and social ramifications - Hence, Company Court has not only to look into the interest of the creditors, but also the interests of public at large - In the present case, order passed by the Company Court ordering publication of advertisement in the newspaper would tarnish the image and reputation of the appellant-company resulting in serious civil consequences and, hence, order passed by the Company Court set aside - Appeal allowed.
