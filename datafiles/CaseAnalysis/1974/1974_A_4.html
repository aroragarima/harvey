Â Acharaya Maharajshri Narandraprasadji Anandprasadji Maharaj v The State Of Gujarat & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

03 October 1974

Case Analysis

Bench

P.K. Goswami, A.N. Ray, M. Hameedullah Beg, A. Alagiriswami, R.S. Sarkaria

Where Reported

1974 Indlaw SC 514; (1975) 1 SCC 11; AIR 1974 SC 2098; [1975] 2 S.C.R. 317

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Trusts & Associations - Land & Property - Gujarat Devasthan Inams Abolition Act, 1969, ss. 5 and 31 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 25, 26(c), 31(2) and 31A - Devasthan Inam Lands - Abolition of Inams held by religious or charitable institution in Bombay area of State of Gujarat - Constitutional validity of Abolition Act - Act is passed in furtherance of agrarian reforms - On enforcement of Act, appellant's right over 729 bighas of Devasthan inam lands extinguished - Notices also issued under Bombay Land Revenue Code and under Act to hand over his record as inamdar - Challenged in writ before High Court - Writ dismissed - (A) Whether Abolition Act violates art. 26(c) of Constitution of India, 1950 on ground that it deprives religious denominations of their ownership of property? - Held, fields of two arts. 19 and 26 to some extent are different - State has not herein vide questioned acquisitions, tended to destroy or completely negate rights under art. 26 - Thus, when property is acquired by State in accordance with law and with provisions of art. 31(2) and acquisition cannot be assailed on any valid ground open to person concerned, be it a religious institution, right to own that property vanishes as that right is transferred to State - Thereafter there is no question of any right to own particular property subject to public order, morality and health and art. 26 will in circumstances be of no relevance - There is no conflict between arts. 26 and 31 - (B) Abolition Act is not saved by art. 31A as operation of art. 26(c) is not excluded under it; contention whether acceptable? - Held, Act is squarely protected under saving provision of art. 31A - No rights in an organised society can be absolute - Enjoyment of one's rights must be consistent with enjoyment of rights also by others - Act and its provisions do no violence to rights guaranteed u/art. 26(c) - Right guaranteed u/art. 26(c) not being absolute and unqualified is consistent with reasonable regulations made by State provided substance of freedom is not affected - Act does not make any inroad in such a way as to affect directly substance of that freedom - A particular fundamental right cannot exist in isolation in a water tight compartment - One fundamental right of a person may have to co-exist in harmony with exercise of another fundamental right by others and also with reasonable and valid exercise of power by State in light of Directive Principles in interests of social welfare as a whole - Court's duty is to strike a balance between competing claims of different interests - Further held, Abolition Act is saved by art. 31A - (D) Whether s. 31 is violative of art. 26(c) on ground that compensation available to religious denominations for lands covered by Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act, 1948 and Gujarat Agricultural Land Ceiling Act, 1960, is grossly inadequate? - Held, inadequacy of compensation cannot be agitated against a legislation which relates to agrarian reform protected u/art. 31A - Appeals dismissed.
