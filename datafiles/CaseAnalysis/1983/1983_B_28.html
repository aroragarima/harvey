Begulla Bapi Raju v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

23 August 1983

Case Analysis

Bench

R.B. Misra, A.P. Sen, E.S. Venkataramiah

Where Reported

1983 Indlaw SC 286; (1984) 1 SCC 66; AIR 1983 SC 1073; 1983(2) SCALE 141; [1983] 3 S.C.R. 701; 1983 UJ 1038

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Insurance; Land & Property; Service

Summary: Constitution - Land & Property - Andhra Pradesh Land Reforms (Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings) Act, 1973, s. 3(f) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 136, 32, 14, 31A and 31B - Maharashtra Agricultural Land (Celling on holdings) Act, 1961 - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976 - Issues arise - (A) Whether a separated minor son is not a member of 'family unit' and, therefore, his property cannot be tagged with that of his father? - Held, in view of explanation added to s. 4 of Andhra Pradesh Act, land held by all members of family unit shall be aggregated for purpose of computing holding of family unit - Obviously, therefore, where a family unit consisted of father, mother, and minor sons or daughters land held by all these persons would have to be clubbed together and then ceiling area limit applied to aggregate holding - No distinction has been made in definition of a family unit between a divided minor son an undivided minor son - Both stand on same footing and a divided minor son is as much a member of family unit as an undivided minor son - Definition of family unit along with explanation does not leave slightest doubt that a separated minor son is as much a member of family unit as a joint son with his father - (B) Whether definition of family unit u/s. 3(f) as interpreted by High Court is also violative of art. 14 of Constitution? - Held, it is not possible to strike down an enactment particularly enactment dealing with agrarian reform which has been put on Statutes Book with avowed purpose of bringing about equality or rather reducing inequality between haves and have-nots, as being violative of art. 14 of Constitution simply because it has failed to make a provision for what was regarded as an exceptional case or a rare contingency - Material furnished on behalf of State Govt. by way of justification for adopting an artificial definition of family and a double standard for fixing ceiling is sufficient to rebel attack on these provisions u/art. 14 - (C) Whether land transferred by petitioners under various transfer deeds to outsiders and who came in possession also could not be included in holding of petitioners? - Held, this point covered by a decision of SC in State of Andhra Pradesh v Mohd Ashrafuddin - (D) Whether s. 3 (f) of Andhra Pradesh Act coupled with explanation thereto being destructive of art. 21 of Constitution is violative of basic structure of Constitution? - Held, this point covered by two decisions of Supreme Court - In re Sant Ram(2) dealing with art. 21 of Constitution, a Bench of Five Judges of this Court held: 'The argument that word' life' in art. 21 of Constitution includes' livelihood' has only to be stated to be rejected' - Same view was reiterated by a Bench of three Judges in A V Nachane v Union of India - Petitioners have been deprived of their holding in form of surplus land but it was only for purpose of giving relief to downtrodden and poor agricultural labourers - Surplus land would vest in State and State in its turn would give it to poor and downtrodden - No infirmity in any of provisions of Andhra Pradesh Act - Petitions dismissed.
