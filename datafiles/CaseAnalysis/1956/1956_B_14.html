Bipin Chander Jaisinghbhai Shah v Prabhawati

Supreme Court of India

19 October 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 34; AIR 1957 SC 176; 1957 SCJ 144; [1956] 1 S.C.R. 838

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Family & Personal - Practice & Procedure - Bombay Hindu Divorce Act (12 of 1947), s. 3(d) - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 101, 102, 103 and 104 - Divorce on ground of desertion - Burden of proof - Constructive desertion - (A)Whether for offence of desertion condition for deserting spouse must exist? - So far as deserting spouse is concerned, two essential conditions must be there, namely, (i) factum of separation, and (ii) intention to bring cohabitation permanently to an end (animus deserendi) - Similarly two elements are essential so far as deserted spouse is concerned i.e., (i) absence of consent, and (ii) absence of conduct giving reasonable cause to spouse leaving matrimonial home to form necessary intention aforesaid - Desertion is a matter of inference to be drawn from facts and circumstances of each case - Held, offence of desertion commences when fact of separation and animus deserendi co-exist but its not necessary that they should commence at same time as it is not necessary that at the time when wife left her husband's home she should have at same time animus deserendi - (A1) Whether deserting spouse can end desertion in locus poeni tentiae provided by law under Bombay Hindu Divorce Act? - Further held, that if a deserting spouse takes advantage of locus poeni tentiae thus provided by law and decides to come back to deserted spouse by a bona fide offer of resuming matrimonial home with all implications of marital life, before statutory period is out or even after lapse of that period, unless proceedings for divorce have been commenced, desertion comes to an end - (A2) Whether to prove offence of desertion in proceeding of divorce, burden of proof lies on plaintiff? - Further held, that it is well settled that in proceedings for divorce plaintiff must prove offence of desertion, like any other matrimonial offence - Courts insists upon corroborative evidence under Evidence Act, unless its absence is accounted for to satisfaction of courts - (B) Whether husband in present case is guilty of constructive desertion under s. 3 (d) of Bombay Hindu Divorce Act? - If one spouse by his words and conduct compel other spouse to leave marital home, former would be guilty of desertion though it is latter who has physically separated from other and has been made to leave marital home - Further held, that when wife is forcibly turned out of her marital home by husband, husband is guilty of 'constructive desertion', because test is not who left matrimonial home first - Plaintiff must satisfy court that defendant had been in desertion for continuous period of four years as required by Act - In present case wife was unable to satisfy court that husband was guilty for constructive desertion - (C) Whether wife leaving husband's place in shame renders her to be deserter under s. 3 (d) of Bombay Hindu Divorce Act?- Further held, fact that wife leaves her husband's place in shame not having courage to face husband after discovery of her reprehensible conduct does not render her in eye of law a deserter - (D) Whether presumption of continous desertion can be made when it is found that one of the spouse has been in desertion u/s. 3 (d) of Bombay Hindu Divorce Act ? Held, that once it is found that one of the spouses had been in desertion, presumption is that desertion had continued and it is not necessary for deserted spouse actually to take steps to bring deserting spouse back to matrimonial home - Appeal dismissed.
