Anil Bajaj and another v Vinod Ahuja

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 340; AIR 2014 SC 2294; 2014(4) ALL MR 430; 2014 (210) DLT 58; JT 2016 (11) SC 230; 2014 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 2841; 2014 (2) RCR(Civil) 974; 2014 (1) RCR(Rent) 484; 2014(6) SCALE 572

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Summary: Rent Control - Delhi Rent Control Act, 1958, s.14(1)(e) - Eviction - Denied - Sustainability - Appellants, who were the landlords, sought to challenge the order passed by HC granting leave to the respondent-tenant to contest the proceedings for his eviction u/s. 14(1)(e) of Act - Order of HC was in reversal of the order passed by the Additional Rent Controller who had refused to grant leave to defend to the respondent - Whether Hc committed error in grating leave to respondent to contest the proceedings of eviction -

Held, in the instant case it was clear that while appellant No. 1 was carrying on his business from a shop premise located in a narrow lane, the respondent was in occupation of the premises located on the main road which the landlord considers to be more suitable for his own business - Materials on record, in fact, disclosed that the landlord had offered to the respondent the premises located in the narrow lane in exchange for the tenanted premises which offer was declined by the tenant - It was not the tenant's case that the appellant No. 1 did not propose to utilize the tenanted premises from which eviction was sought for the purposes of his business - It was also not the tenant's case that the landlord proposes to rent out/keep vacant the tenanted premises after obtaining possession thereof or to use the same is any way inconsistent with the need of the landlord - What the tenant contended was that the appellant had several other shop houses from which he was carrying on different business and further that appellant had other premises from where the business proposed from the tenanted premises could be effectively carried out - It would hardly require any reiteration of the settled principle of law that it was not for the respondent to dictate to the landlord as to how the property belonging to the appellant should be utilized by him for the purpose of his business - Also, the fact that the appellant was doing business from various other premises cannot foreclose his right to seek eviction from the tenanted premises so long as he intends to use the said tenanted premises for his own business - Grounds on which leave to defend was sought by the respondent and was granted by HC ran counter to the fundamental principles governing the right of a respondent to contest the claim of bonafide requirement of the suit premises by appellant under Act - Even assuming the assertions made by the respondent to be correct, the same did not disclose any triable issue so as to entitle the respondent to grant of leave to defend - Appeal allowed.
