M. Durai v Madhu and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 659; (2007) 3 SCC 114; 2008 (1) ALD(SC) 124; 2007 (67) ALR 151; 2007 (2) AWC 1377; 2007 (3) CTC 69; 2007 (2) JhrCR(SC) 419; 2007 (1) RCR(Civil) 889; 2007 (102) RD 615; 2007(2) SCALE 309; 2007 (6) SCJ 205; [2007] 1 S.C.R. 816

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Limitation Act, 1963, arts. 64 and 65 - Adverse possession - Burden of proof - Adverse possession really means hostile possession which is expressly or impliedly in denial of title of true owner - In order to constitute adverse possession possession proved must be adequate in continuity, in publicity and in extent so as to show that it is adverse to true owner - 'Animus Possidendi' is one of ingredients of adverse possession - Unless person possessing land has a requisite animus period for prescription does not commence - Held, in instant case, appellant categorically states that his possession is not adverse as that of true owner, logical corollary is that he did not have requisite animus - Further held, to establish perfection of title by remaining in possession would be on defendants - Appeal allowed.
