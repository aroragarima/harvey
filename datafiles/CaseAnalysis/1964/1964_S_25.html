Laxmi Devi v Sethani Mukand Kanwar and others

Supreme Court of India

09 October 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 467; AIR 1965 SC 834; 1965 (2) MLJ(SC) 133; [1965] 1 S.C.R. 726

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 21 r. 90 and O. 21 r. 66 - Respondent No. 3 executed a document in favour of his mother, respondent No. 1, whereby her maintenance was guaranteed - This document created charge over certain properties belonging to respondent No. 3 - Meanwhile, property was mortgaged to appellant - Later, a money decree was obtained against respondent No. 3 for a large amount, and in execution of this money decree he brought the property in question to sale - Auction sale was held and the appellant purchased the property subject to the pre-existing mortgage in her favour - Respondent No. 1 filed applications under O. 21 r. 90 of CPC challenging auction sale - Executing Court set aside said sale - Appeal was filed thereagainst wherein appellant contended that application made by respondent No. 1 did not satisfy the requirements of O. 21 r. 90 of CPC inasmuch as appropriate allegations had not been made in the application showing that substantial injury was suffered by respondent No. 1 by reason of the irregularities which, according to her, had vitiated the said sale - Judicial Commissioner remanded the case back with a direction that the issue as to substantial injury should be tried afresh - After remand, the Executing Court held that respondent No. 1 had failed to show any substantial injury and ordered that her application under O. 21 r. 90 should be dismissed, and the sale should be confirmed - Appeal filed thereagainst was allowed - Hence, present appeal - Whether respondent No. 1 can be said to have alleged that she suffered substantial injury by reason of the fact that she alleged a material irregularity which, in law, necessarily leads to substantial injury? - Held, before an application made under O. 21 r.90 can succeed, the applicant has to show that the impugned sale was, vitiated by a material irregularity or fraud in publishing or conducting it; and as required by the proviso, it is also necessary that he should show that in consequence of the said irregularity or fraud he had sustained substantial injury - Further, whether or not the injury suffered by the charge-holder is substantial, must depend upon several relevant facts like how many properties have been sold at the auction sale; how many out of them were the subject-matter of the charge; what is the extent of the claim which the charge-holder can legitimately expect to enforce against the properties charged - In the present case, application made by respondent No. 1 was defective, because it does not, in terms, allege that as a result of the irregularity alleged in the application, respondent No. 1 suffered substantial injury - Furthermore, though respondent No. 1 was able, to show that her charge could not be enforced against the appellant, it was not shown that this circumstance caused substantial injury to her - Hence, requirement of the proviso to O. 21 r. 90 of the CPC was not satisfied in the present case - Order passed by the HC set aside - Appeal allowed.
