State Bank of India v Santosh Gupta and others

Supreme Court of India

16 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 909; (2017) 2 SCC 538; AIR 2017 SC 25; 2017 (121) ALR 505; 2017 (1) BC 289; [2017] 202 Comp Cas 249; 2017 (1) DRTC 471; JT 2016 (12) SC 508; 2017 (1) MLJ 604; 2017 (135) RD 545; 2016(12) SCALE 1044; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 985

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Constitution

Keywords: Immovable Property, Tribunal, Jurisdiction, Borrower, Indian Contract Act, 1872, State Legislature, General Clauses Act, 1897, Legislative Power, Mortgaged Property, Government of India Act, 1935, Privileges, Securitisation And Reconstruction Of Financial Assets And Enforcement Of Security Interest Act, 2002, All India Services (Discipline and Appeal) Rules, 1955, Constitution of India, 1950, Constitution Application to Jammu & Kashmir Order, 1950, Bombay Courts of Small Causes (Enhancement of Pecuniary Jurisdiction and Amendment) Act, 1986, Jammu & Kashmir Government Servants Prevention of Corruption (Commission) Act, 1962, Jammu & Kashmir Big Landed Estates (Abolition) Act, 1950, Transfer of Property Act of Jammu & Kashmir, 1920, Constitution of Jammu & Kashmir, 1957, All India Services Rules

Summary: Constitution - Banking & Finance - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 1, 246, 309, 35(c ), 35A, 370, 370(1)(b) - Transfer of Property Act of Jammu & Kashmir, 1920, s. 140 - Securitistion and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act, 2002, 13, 13(4), 17(A), 18(B), 34, 45, 36 - Grant of Financial Help - Non-Enforceability of Provisions Challenged - Appellant/bank granted financial help to respondent/borrowers with in state of Jammu & Kashmir - On default of repayment of money, bank availed provisions of SARFAESI Act - HC held that parliament does not had legislative competence to make law in ss. 13, 17(A), 18(B), 34, 45 and 36 so far as related to state of J&K and collide with s. 140 of 1920 Act - Provisions of the Act were not enforceable/inapplicable to banks, as appellant bank which were all India banks - Hence, instant appeals - Whether SARFAESI Act in its application to State of Jammu & Kashmir would be held to be within legislative competence of Parliament.

Held, the State of Jammu & Kashmir, though a state within the meaning of art. 1 of Constitution has been accorded a special status from the very beginning because of certain events that took place at the time that the erstwhile Ruler of Jammu & Kashmir acceded to the Indian Union. The recovery of debts by banks falls within Entry 45 List I and the SARFAESI Act relatable to entry 4 list I. However, any enactment made under the State List would have to give way to SARFAESI by virtue of the application of art. 246 of Constitution. S. 140 of 1920 Act, deals with exemptions of certain instruments from restriction imposed on transfer of immovable property. However, Rule 8(5) of the Rules, 'Provided that in case of sale of immovable property in the State of Jammu and Kashmir, the provisions of Jammu and Kashmir Transfer of Property Act, shall apply to the person who acquires such property in the State. This rule makes it amply clear that s. 140 of the Transfer of Property Act of Jammu & Kashmir will be respected in auction sales that take place within the State. This being the case, it is clear that there is no collision or repugnancy with any of the provisions of SARFAESI. The HC is absolutely wrong in finding that as s. 140 of 1920 Act will be infracted, SARFAESI cannot be held to apply to the State of Jammu & Kashmir. Hence, impugned order passed by HC is set aside and notices issued by banks in terms of s. 13 and other coercive methods taken under the said sections are valid and can be proceeded with further. Appeals allowed.
