Gurcharan Singh v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

02 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 914; (2017) 1 SCC 433; AIR 2017 SC 74; JT 2016 (11) SC 432; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 118; 2016(12) SCALE 414; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 741

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Presumption, Conspiracy, Suicide, Cruelty, Harassment, Omission, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Abetment, Version, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 306, 34, 498A, 107 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 113A - Abatement of Suicide - Conviction Challenged - Appellant/accused along with co-accused was charged for commission of offence of abatement of suicide of 'X' - Trial Court convicted accused u/s. 306 of IPC and sentenced to suffer RI of six years - HC in appeal by accused affirmed judgment of Trial Court and reduced sentence to five years of same description - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, to constitute abetment, the intention and involvement of the accused to aid or instigate the commission of suicide is imperative. Any severance or absence of any of this constituents would militate against this indictment. Remoteness of the culpable acts or omissions rooted in the intention of the accused to actualize the suicide would fall short as well of the offence of abetment essential to attract the punitive mandate of s. 306 of IPC. Contiguity, continuity, culpability and complicity of the indictable acts or omission are the concomitant indices of abetment. S. 306 of IPC thus criminalises the sustained incitement for suicide. Further, s. 113A of 1872 Act permits a presumption as to the abetment of suicide by a married woman by her husband or any relative of his, if it is proved that she had committed the act within a period of seven years from the date of her marriage and that her husband or such relative of his had subjected her to cruelty. In instant case, the materials on record, do not suggest even remotely any act of cruelty, oppression, harassment or inducement so as to persistently provoke or compel the deceased to resort to self-extinction being left with no other alternative. Hence, the ingredients of the offence of s. 306 of IPC have remained unproved and thus the appellant deserves to be acquitted. Appeal allowed.
