C. N. Arunachala Mudaliar v C. A. Muruganatha Mudaliar and Another

Supreme Court of India

14 October 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Mehr Chand Mahajan, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 60; AIR 1953 SC 495; 1954 ALJ 25; 1953 (2) MLJ(SC) 796; 1953 SCJ 707; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 243

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Partition - Ancestral property - Maintainability - Suit was commenced by respondent No. I in this appeal for specific allotment, on partition, of his one-third share in properties on allegation that they were joint properties of family and that he was entitled in law to one-third share in same - Trial Court held that properties bequeathed to defendant No. I by his father should be held to be ancestral properties in his hands and as other properties were acquired by defendant No. 1 out of income of ancestral estate, they also became impressed with character of joint property - Decree was made in favour of respondent no. 1 and claim was allowed - Aggrieved with decision defedant No. I filed appeal to HC - HC dismissed appeal - HC rejected defendant No. 1's application for leave to appeal to SC but succeeded u/art. 136 of Constitution - Hence, instant appeal - Whether order passed by HC was maintainable - Held, property gifted by father to his son could not become ancestral property in hands of donee that donee got it from his father or ancestor - As law was accepted and well settled that Mitakshara father had complete powers of disposition over his self acquired property, it might follow as necessary consequence that father was quite competent to provide expressly, when he made gift, either that donee would take it exclusively for himself or that gift would be for benefit of his branch of family - On reading will as whole it was clear that testator intended legatees to take properties in absolute right as their own self-acquisition without being fettered in any way by rights of their sons and grandsons and did not intend that property should be taken by sons as ancestral property - Appeal allowed.
