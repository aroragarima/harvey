Justice Ripusudan Dayal (Retd.) and others v State of M. P. and others

Supreme Court of India

25 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Ranjan Gogoi, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 114; (2014) 4 SCC 473; AIR 2014 SC 1335; 2014 (4) ALD(SC) 90; 2014 (6) AWC 5807; JT 2014 (3) SC 336; 2014 (1) KLT 863; 2014 (4) MPLJ 56; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 1; 2014(2) SCALE 716; [2014] 3 S.C.R. 242

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Parliament & Legislature; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Sea Customs Act, 1878, Comments, Process Server, Conduct Of Business, Privileges, M.P. Special Police Establishment Act, 1947, Central Provinces and Berar Special Police Establishment Act, 1947, Madhya Pradesh Lokayukt Evam Uplokayukt Act, 1981, Madhya Pradesh Vidhan Sabha Procedure and Conduct of Business Rules, 1964

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Practice & Procedure - Madhya Pradesh Lokayukt Evam Uplokayukt Act, 1981, s. 2(a) - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 21, 32, 105, 142, 194(3) - Constitution Forty-Fourth Amendment Act, 1978, s. 26 - Breach of legislative privileges - Writ petition - Maintainability - Petitioner Lokayukta and officers filed instant petition u/art. 32 of Constitution challenging the validity letters issued by respondent no. 4 Secretary of Vidhan Sabha with regard to a case registered by the SPE of the Lokayukta against the officials of Vidhan Sabha Secretariat as well as against the concerned officials of Capital Project Administration (Contractor Company) alleging irregularity in the construction work- Petitioners contended that said letters violated their fundamental rights u/art. 14, 19 and 21of Constitution and were contrary to art. 194(3) of the Constitution and sought direction to quash the said letters and complaints filed by respondent nos. 5, 6 (since expired), 7, 8 and 9 - Further contended that impugned proceedings which were mere letters calling for response as they relate to breach of privilege, amount to violation of rights u/art. 21 of Constitution, thus instant petition was maintainable -

Held, there was no dispute that all the impugned proceedings or notices/letters/complaints made by various members of State Assembly claimed that petitioners violated the privilege of the House - Ultimately, if their replies were not acceptable, petitioners have no other remedy except to face the consequence, action 1964 Rules - If any decision was taken by House, petitioners might not be in a position to challenge the same effectively before the Court of law - If it was established that the proposed actions were not permissible involving infringement of arts. 14 and 21 of Constitution, SC was well within its power to pass appropriate order in exercise of its jurisdiction u/arts. 32 and 142 of Constitution - Further, if petitioners were compelled to face the privilege proceedings before the Vidhan Sabha, it would cause prejudice to them - Further, if the petitioners were compelled to face the privilege motion in spite of the fact that no proceeding was initiated against Speaker or Members of the House but only relating to the officers in respect of contractual matters, if urgent intervention was not sought for by exercising extraordinary jurisdiction, undoubtedly, it would cause prejudice to petitioners - Thus, instant petition u/art. 32 of Constitution was maintainable - Petition allowed.

Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Practice & Procedure - Madhya Pradesh Lokayukt Evam Uplokayukt Act, 1981, s. 2(a) - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 21, 32, 105, 142, 194(3) - Constitution Forty-Fourth Amendment Act, 1978, s. 26 - Corruption charge against Legislative Secretary - Investigation by Lokayukta - Breach of legislative privileges -

Held, privileges were not defined but art. 194 provided that until the same were so defined (i.e. by the legislature by law), they should be those which the House or its members and committees enjoyed immediately before the coming into force of s. 26 of 1978 Act - In the matter of application of laws, particularly, the provisions of 1981 Act (Lokayukt Act) and 1988 Act, insofar as the jurisdiction of the Lokayukt or the State Special Establishment was concerned, all public servants except the Speaker and Dy. Speaker of the State Vidhan Sabha for the purposes of Lokayukt Act fall in the same category and could not claim any privilege more than an ordinary citizen to whom the provisions of the said Acts apply - Privileges were available only insofar as they were necessary in order that the House might freely perform its functions but did not extend to the activities undertaken outside the House on which the legislative provisions would apply without any differentiations - Privileges did not exempt the Members from their obligations under any statute which continue to apply to them like any other law applicable to ordinary citizens - Thus, enquiry or investigation into an allegation of corruption against some officers of the Legislative Assembly could not be said to interfere with the legislative functions of the Assembly - No one enjoys any privilege against criminal prosecution - In instant case, petitioners had not made any inquiry even against the members of Legislative Assembly or Speaker or about their conduct and, thus, the complaints made against the petitioners by some of the members of Legislative Assembly were completely uncalled for, illegal and unconstitutional - Speaker had no jurisdiction to entertain any such complaint, which was not even maintainable - Assembly did not enjoy any privilege of a nature that might have the effect of restraining any inquiry or investigation against the Secretary or Dy. Secretary of the Legislative Assembly - Petitioners could not, while acting under the Lokayukt Act and 1988 Act, be said to have lowered the dignity of the very Assembly which had conferred the power upon the petitioners - Authority to act was conferred upon the petitioners under the Lokayukt Act by the Legislative Assembly itself and, thus, action taken by petitioners under the said Act could not constitute a breach of privilege of that Legislative Assembly - Action being investigated by the petitioners had nothing to do with the proceedings of the House and as such said action could not constitute any breach of privilege of the House or its members -Impugned letters/notices were quashed - Petition allowed.
