Ranjit Singh and Co. v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1404; (2006) 13 SCC 130; 2006(11) SCALE 603; [2006] Supp8 S.C.R. 802

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Murder - Conviction - Reliability of dying declaration - Appellants were tried for commission of offence of murder by order passed by Additional Sessions Judge - Appeal was made before HC where judgment of conviction and sentence passed by Additional Sessions Judge was affirmed - However, husband of deceased was acquitted by HC upon grant of benefit of doubt - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether dying declaration alone was wholly reliable and could made basis for conviction - Held, in both dying declaration of deceased, genesis of occurrence was stated to be same, however, mode and manner in which same was done as also role played by appellants and her father-in-law were somewhat inconsistent - Deceased in her first dying declaration ascribed role of her father-in- law as pouring kerosene on her and role of ablaze by match stick to her mother-in-law, but in her statement before Judicial Magistrate, she stated that mother-in-law sprinkled kerosene on her and then she set ablazing her with match stick and it was thus, evident that role of mother-in-law/appellant no. 3 in lighting match stick was consistent - Rule of caution should be applied and benefit of doubt should be given to appellant nos. 2 to 5 - It was not taken into consideration purported oral dying declaration made by deceased before her brother and it was not necessary to discuss his evidence and it was to much to contend that he gave his statement before IO but same was not recorded - It was well settled that conviction could be recorded on basis of dying declaration alone, if same was wholly reliable, but in event there exists any suspicion as regards correctness or otherwise of said dying declaration, Courts in arriving at judgment of conviction should look for some corroborating evidence - In case where inconsistencies in dying declarations, in relation to active role played by one or other accused persons, exist, Court should lean more towards first dying declaration than second one - Therefore, it was not correct to suggest that Judicial Magistrate recorded statement of deceased although she was not in fit condition to make statement - Hence, appeal of appellant no. 3 was dismissed and appeal filed by appellant no.2 to 5 was allowed - Appeal dismissed.
