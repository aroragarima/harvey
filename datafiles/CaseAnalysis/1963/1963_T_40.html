T. Prem Sagar v Standard Vacuum Oil Companymadras and Others

Supreme Court of India

16 December 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K.C. Das Gupta

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 225; AIR 1965 SC 111; 1964 (8) FLR 247; 1964 (1) LLJ 47; [1964] 5 S.C.R. 1030

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Madras Shops and Establishments Act, 1947, ss. 2(5), 2(12)(iii), 2(18), 4(1)(a) and 51 - Validity of Writ of Certiorari issued - Error in jurisdiction of tribunal - (A) Whether Writ of Certiorari can be issued only in cases where an error of jurisdiction is established, or fraud proved ? - It is not true that since there is no error of jurisdiction in the present case and no allegation of fraud, High Court was not justified in issuing a writ - If the order of Commr. is shown to suffer from infirmity of an error of law apparent on face of record, HC would be justified in issuing a writ notwithstanding fact that s. 51 of Act purports to make Commr.'s order final - Held, it is not correct to say that unless an error of jurisdiction is established, or fraud proved, no writ of certiorari can be issued - In such a case, validity of order could be questioned in a regular suit brought before a Civil Court (B) Whether HC was right in holding that Commr.'s order was vitiated by an error apparent on face of record? - It is difficult and indeed inexpedient to lay down any general test to determine which errors of law can be described as errors apparent on face of record - Further held, where Labour Commr. after enumerating proper tests, applied them after considering entire material on record and came to his conclusion about the status of appellant, it cannot be said that his order is vitiated by an error apparent on face of record - (C) Whether HC is justified in exercising its jurisdiction to consider evidence for itself and reach its own conclusions in matters which have been left by Legislature to decisions of specially constituted tribunals ? - In writ proceedings if an error of law, apparent on face of record is disclosed and a writ is issued, usual course to adopt is to correct error and send the case back to tribunal for its decision in accordance with law - Further held, it would be inappropriate for HC exercising its writ jurisdiction to consider evidence for itself and reach its own conclusions in matters which have been left by Legislature to decisions of specially constituted tribunals - Appeal allowed.
