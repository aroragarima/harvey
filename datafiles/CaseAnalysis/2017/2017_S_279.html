Ajay Singh and another etc. v State of Chhattisgarh and another

Supreme Court of India

06 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 28; (2017) 3 SCC 330; AIR 2017 SC 310; JT 2017 (1) SC 167; 2017 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 22; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 559; 2017(1) SCALE 400

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Fair Trial, Jurisdiction, Sufficient Ground, Inherent Power, Additional Sessions Judge, Presiding Officer, Open Court, Dealing, Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1952, Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, Superintendence, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 194, 309, 353, 465, 482 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304B, 328, 34, 498A - Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, ss. 3, 4 -Dowry death - Acquittal without judgment - Challenged - Appellant no. 1/accused and deceased/wife were married - Deceased committed suicide at her matrimonial home - Charge sheet was filed against accused (husband and inlaws) u/ss. 304B, 498A/34, 328 IPC r/w s. 3/4 of the Act - Trial Court passed an order and recorded that accused persons were acquitted - Member of State Bar Council sent a complaint to Registry of HC alleging that Trial Court had acquitted accused persons but no judgment was rendered - Registrar issued a memorandum to District Judge to inquire into the matter and submit a report - District Judge submitted a report to HC stating that no judgments were found in the records of such cases - HC passed a resolution imposed with the punishment of compulsory retirement of Trial Judge - Accused filed writ petitions before HC which was transferred to Trial Court for reconsideration - Hence, instant appeals - Whether Trial Judge had really pronounced the judgment of acquittal on 31-10-2007.

Held, though CrPC does not define the term "judgment", yet it has clearly laid down how the judgment is to be pronounced. The provisions clearly spell out that it is imperative on the part of Trial Judge to pronounce the judgment in open Court by delivering the whole of the judgment or by reading out the whole of the judgment or by reading out the operative part of the judgment and explaining the substance of the judgment in a language which is understood by accused or his counsel. SC had already noted that the judgment was not dictated in open Court. Non availability of judgment, needless to say, can never be a judgment because there was no declaration by way of pronouncement in the open Court that accused was convicted or acquitted. In instant case, the judgment was not available on record and there could be no shadow of doubt that the declaration of the result cannot tantamount to a judgment as prescribed in CrPC. That leads to the inevitable conclusion that Trial in both the cases has to be treated to be pending. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 194, 309, 353, 465, 482 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304B, 328, 34, 498A - Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, ss. 3, 4 - - Transfer for rehearing - Validity - Whether HC could have in exercise of its administrative power treated the trial as pending and transferred the same from Trial Court to District Judge for rehearing and disposal.

Held, HC on the administrative side had transferred the case to Trial Judge by which it has conferred jurisdiction on Trial Court which has jurisdiction to try the sessions case under CrPC. Thus, it has done so as it has, as a matter of fact, found that there was no judgment on record. There was no illegality. DB on appeal preferred at the instance of appellants thought it appropriate to quash the order as there was no judgment on record but a mere order-sheet. In a piquant situation like the instant one, SC disposed to think that HC was under legal obligation to set aside the order as it had no effect in law. HC has correctly done so as it has the duty to see that sanctity of justice was not undermined. HC has done so as it has felt that an order which was a mere declaration of result without the judgment should be nullified and become extinct. The instant case constrains SC to say that a Trial Judge should remember that he has immense responsibility as he has a lawful duty to record the evidence in the prescribed manner keeping in mind the command postulated in s. 309 CrPC and pronounce the judgment as provided under the Code. However, when a situation like the instant one crops up, it causes agony, an unbearable one, to the cause of justice and hits like a lightning in a cloudless sky. It hurts the justice dispensation system and no one has any right to do so. HC by rectifying the grave error has acted in furtherance of the cause of justice. Accused persons might have felt delighted in acquittal and affected by the order of rehearing, however they should bear in mind that they were not the lone receivers of justice. There were victims of crime. Trial Court to whom the cases were transferred was directed to proceed in accordance with law. Appeals dismissed.
