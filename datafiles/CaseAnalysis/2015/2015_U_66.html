Sanjiv Rajendra Bhatt v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

13 October 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Arun Mishra, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 882; (2016) 1 SCC 1; 2016 CRLJ 185; 2016 (3) G.L.R. 2172; 2015(10) SCALE 651; [2015] 12 S.C.R. 498

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Contempt Of Court, Conspiracy, Criminal Contempt, CBI, Administration Of Justice, Summons, Litigation, Secretary, Lawyer, Copies, Reply, Further Investigation, Credibility, Deem, Judicial Proceedings, Interfere, Access, Terrorist And Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, Consultation, Due Course Of Justice, Antecedents, Economic Offences, Judiciary, Encounter, Inquiry

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 114, 120B, 189, 193, 195, 341, 342 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 160, 161, 164, 173, 173(2), 173(8) - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 2( C)(iii), 20 - Information Technology Act, 2000, s. 66 - Criminal Intimidation - Claim for Independent Agency - Petitioner rendered service with police department and was present in meeting convened on night of Godhra incident - However, petitioner accessed email of AAG and came across very unusual e-mail between SIT and AAG and alleged that of leaking sensitive and confidential contents - Further, petitioner mother was ransacked - FIR was filed by 'X' for charges u/ss. 189, 193, 341, 342 of IPC and another FIR was lodged by AAG against petitioner for commission of offence u/s. 6 of 2000, Act - Hence, instant transfer petitions.

Held, an aggrieved person can only claim that the offence he alleges be investigated properly, but he has no right to claim that it be investigated by any particular agency of his choice. Further, once a charge-sheet is filed in the competent court after completion of the investigation, the process of monitoring by this Court for the purpose of making CBI and other investigative agencies concerned perform their function of investigating into the offences concerned comes to an end; and thereafter it is only the court in which the charge-sheet is filed which is to deal with all matters relating to the trial of the accused, including matters falling within the scope of s. 173(8) of Cr.P.C. Howerver, the Court can transfer investigation after chargesheet is filed and which can be done only in extraordinary cases. In instant case, SIT constituted by this Court has already investigated into the main cases and the scope of cases in hand is not so wide in magnitude so as to direct the SIT or CBI to investigate into the matters. Hence, no case is made out to constitute SIT. Petitions dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure -Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 160, 161, 164, 173, 173(2), 173(8) - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 2( C)(iii), 20 - Information Technology Act, 2000, s. 66 - Hacking and Tampering of Emails - Claim for Independent Agency - Petitioner rendered service with police department and was present in meeting convened on night of Godhra incident - However, petitioner accessed email of AAG and came across very unusual e-mail between Sit and AAG and alleged that of leaking sensitive and confidential contents - Further, consequently, petitioner mother was ransacked - FIR was lodged against petitioner for commission of offence u/s. 6 of the Act - Hence, instant criminal petitions.

Held, as per s. 2(c )(iii) of 1971, Act, tendency to scandalise the court or tendency to lower the authority of the court or tendency to interfere with or tendency to obstruct the administration of justice in any manner or tendency to challenge the authority or majesty of justice, would be a criminal contempt. In other words, judicial contempt is not to be invoked unless there is real prejudice which can be regarded as a substantial interference with due course of justice and the Court will not exercise its jurisdiction upon a mere question of propriety Therefore, merely sending some representation which was to be submitted to the President and Prime Minister of India, and other documents to an advocate who was a politician also would not tantamount to criminal contempt unless and until it is shown that the information was intended to help the accused in any manner whatsoever, it cannot be said that sharing of information tantamount to criminal contempt. In instant case, it does not appear that the e-mail exchange between the then AAG and other functionaries tantamounts to causing prejudice or amounts to substantial interference in any other manner in due course of justice. Hence, no case is made out of criminal contempt. Petitions dismissed.
