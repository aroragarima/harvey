Pallav Sheth v Custodian and Others

Supreme Court of India

10 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Kirpal, Brijesh Kumar, Ruma Pal

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 19968; (2001) 7 SCC 549; AIR 2001 SC 2763; AIR 2001 SCW 3025; 2001 (5) ALD(SC) 51; [2001] 107 Comp Cas 76; 2001 (4) CompLJ 161; 2001 CRLJ 4175; 2002 (2) G.L.R. 1788; JT 2001 (6) SC 330; 2001 (3) RCR(Criminal) 781B; 2001(5) SCALE 127; 2001 (3) SCJ 546; 2001 (5) SLT 702; 2003 (Supp) Bom.C.R. 51; [2001] Supp1 S.C.R. 387; 2001 (5) Supreme 763

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Corporate; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Court Of Record, Law Officer, Criminal Procedure Code, 1963, Contempt of Courts Act, 1926, Special Courts (Repeal) Act, 1982

Summary: Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, s. 23 - Special Courts (Repeal) Act, 1982 - Calcutta High Court Contempt of Courts Rules, 1975, r.2(1) - Constitution, 1950, arts. 215, 129 and 226 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1926, ss. 2(b), 20, 4, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, 17, 20 and 22 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Limitation Act, 1963, ss. 5, 20 and 29(2) - Special Court (Trial of Offences Relating to Transactions in Securities) Act, 1992, s. 11 A - Custodian appointed under Special Court Act filed Application No. 193 of 1993 and sought a decree for Rs. 50 crores on behalf of notified party against Appellant - Rs. 2 crores were paid but thereafter appellant defaulted in payment of further instalments - Custodian then moved an Execution Application - Special Court thereupon required appellant to disclose all his assets and restrained alienating same - Appellant declared his assets - Income-Tax Department detected that appellant was de facto owner of five companies - Special Court passed an order holding appellant to be guilty of contempt of court - Whether contempt proceedings extinguished by limitation? - If there is any provision of law which stultifies or abrogates power to punish for its contempt under art. 129 and/or art. 215 such law would not be regarded as having been validly enacted - In case of, apparent or likelihood of, conflict provisions should be construed harmoniously - If s. 20 Limitation Act is so interpreted that it does not stultify powers under art. 129 or art. 215 then, like other provisions of Contempt of Courts Act relating to extent of punishment which can be imposed, a reasonable period of limitation can also be provided - Under s. 23 of Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 power has been given to SC and HC to make rules not inconsistent with provisions of Act providing for any matter relating to its procedure - Rules framed by all courts in India show that proceedings are initiated inter alia with filing of an application or a petition in that behalf - If proceedings are properly initiated by filing of an application in case of civil contempt like present case before court within period of limitation then provisions of s. 20 will not stand in way of court exercising its jurisdiction - S. 20 has to be construed in a manner which would avoid hardship both as regards litigant as also by placing a pointless fetter on part of court to punish for its contempt - A rigid interpretation must therefore be avoided - Proper construction to be placed on s. 20 must be that action must be initiated, either by filing of an application or by court issuing notice suo motu, within a period of one year from date on which contempt is alleged to have been committed - A party should not be penalised for failing to adopt legal proceedings when facts or material necessary for him to do so have been wilfully concealed from him and also that a party who has acted fraudulently should not gain benefit of limitation running in his favour by virtue of such fraud - Provisions of s. 17, Limitation Act are applicable in present case - Fraud perpetuated by appellant was unearthed only on custodian receiving information from Income - Tax Department - Held, action taken by Special Court to punish appellant for contempt was valid - Appeals dismissed.
