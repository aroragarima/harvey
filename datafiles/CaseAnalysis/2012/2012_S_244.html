Sheoraj Singh Ahlawat & Ors. v State Of Uttar Pradesh & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 469; (2013) 11 SCC 476; AIR 2013 SC 52; 2013 CRLJ 331; JT 2012 (11) SC 296; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 153; 2013 (1) RLW 810; 2012(11) SCALE 107; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 1034

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Women & Children

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 498-A - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 239 - Discharge - Refusal - Challenged - Appellant No. 3 and respondent No. 2 tied the matrimonial knot and the couple were blessed with a daughter three years after marriage - Appellants No.1 and 2 who happen to be her parents in-law kept harassing her for dowry ever since the marriage was solemnized - Physical and mental torture of respondent No. 2 also did not stop even after the said payment, for the sake of a luxury car as an additional item of dowry - Respondent No. 2 was forced into a car by appellants who then abandoned her at a deserted place on a lonely road and threatened to kill her if she returned to her matrimonial home - Complaint about the incident was lodged - Respondent No. 2 came to know about appellant No. 3 having obtained an ex parte decree for divorce against her - Steps was taken and the decree was eventually set aside by the Court concerned - Police filed a closure report to which respondent No. 2 protested and Trial Court took cognizance of an offence punishable u/s. 498-A IPC against appellants - Appellants then filed an application for discharge u/s. 239 of CrPC, said application was dismissed - Aggrieved, on revision petition HC dismissed the revision - Hence instant appeal - Whether accused or any one of them deserved to be discharged - Held, allegations made against appellants were specific not only against husband but also against parents-in-law of respondent No. 2 - Whether or not those allegations were true was a matter which could not be determined at the stage of framing of charges - Any such determination could take place only at the conclusion of the trial - It may at times put an innocent party, falsely accused of commission of an offence to avoidable harassment but so long as the legal requirement and the settled principles did not permit a discharge the Court would find it difficult to do much, conceding that legal process at times is abused by unscrupulous litigants especially in matrimonial cases where the tendency has been to involve as many members of the family of opposite party as possible - While such tendency need to be curbed, the Court would not be able to speculate whether the allegations made against accused were true or false at the preliminary stage to be able to direct a discharge - Appellant No. 1 was a Major General, by all means, a respectable position in the Army - Nature of the allegations made against couple and those against the husband, appear to be much too specific to be ignored at least at the stage of framing of charges - Courts below, therefore, did not commit any mistake in refusing a discharge - Appellant Nos. 1 and 2 should stand exempted from personal appearance before the Trial Court except when the Trial Court consider it necessary to direct their presence - Appeal dismissed.
