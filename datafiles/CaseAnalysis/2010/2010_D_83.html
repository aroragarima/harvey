Minor Marghesh K. Parikh v Dr. Mayur H. Mehta

Supreme Court of India

26 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 913; (2011) 1 SCC 31; AIR 2011 SC 249; 2011 (3) ALD(SC) 57; 2011 (84) ALR 769; 2011 (1) AWC 714; 2011 (2) Bom.C.R. 646; 2011 (1) JhrCR(SC) 87; JT 2010 (11) SC 453; 2010 (4) RCR(Civil) 938; 2010 (4) RCR(Criminal) 881; 2010(11) SCALE 313; 2010 (4) WLN(SC) 13

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection

Summary: Consumer Protection - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, s. 19 - Medical Negligence - Compensation - Appellant filed a complaint against respondent alleging that due to the respondent's failure to pay requisite attention, the appellant's left leg had to be amputated below the knee - Respondent submitted that the appellant was hospitalized for gastro-enteritis, dehydration acidosis and septicemia shock and mal-nutrition and anemia and seriousness of the case was communicated to his father - Respondent further submitted that treatment was given to the appellant keeping in view the laboratory reports and pleaded that he had taken every possible care in treating the appellant and even got him examined by Doctor 'X' - State Commission concluded that the respondent had not exercised reasonable care while treating the appellant and awarded compensation of Rs.5,00,000/- with interest at the rate of 9% from the date of complaint -The National Commission allowed the appeal preferred by the respondent and set aside the order of the State Commission only on the ground that in his cross-examination, Doctor 'Y' admitted that there could be ten other reasons for gangrene - Hence, present appeal - Whether the finding recorded by the State Commission that the respondent did not exercise due diligence and skill in treating the appellant was correct and the National Commission committed an error by upsetting the order of the State Commission? - Held, yes - A critical analysis of the order of the State Commission shows that it did not accept the respondent's version that the appellant had been brought to his hospital in a serious condition and he was suffering from gastro-enteritis, dehydration acidosis and septicemia shock and mal-nutrition and anemia by observing that if that was so, there was no valid reason for the respondent to stop medication and withdraw glucose on 2.11.1994 - The State Commission also took serious view of the respondent's conduct in producing the case papers after a gap of 6 years from the date of filing the complaint and that too, after the appellant's father and Doctor 'Y' had been cross-examined - On the other hand, the National Commission did not advert to these important aspects and allowed the appeal on the solitary ground that on his cross-examination, Doctor 'Y' had admitted that there could be ten to twelve other reasons for development of gangrene - Omissions on the part of the National Commission are extremely serious and have resulted in failure of justice - Impugned order set aside and matter remanded to the National Commission for fresh disposal of the appeal filed by the respondent - Appeal allowed.
