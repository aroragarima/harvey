Bashira v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

19 April 1968

Case Analysis

Bench

Vishishtha Bhargava, S.M. Sikri, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1968 Indlaw SC 487; AIR 1968 SC 1313; 1968 CRLJ 1495; [1969] 1 S.C.R. 32

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 554 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21 and 227 - General Rules (Criminal), 1957, chap. V, r. 37 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Culpable homicide amounting to murder - Trial - Procedural lapse/error - Committal court on prima facie evidence committed case to Sessions Court - Sessions Court fixed a date for actual trial - On that very date Court appointed amicus curiae for accused - On that very date evidence of two principal prosecution witnesses (PW) was recorded - Application to recall of PW 2 for further cross-examination rejected - Award of death sentence by Trial Court and confirmation by High Court - (A) Whether Rules, 1957 and particularly provision of r. 37, mandatory or just an enabling provision? - Word used in r. 37 is 'may' - Intention of rule is perfectly clear that no accused person should remain totally unrepresented by a lawyer, if he is being tried on a charge for which a capital sentence can be awarded - Held, word 'may' in this rule must be interpreted as laying down a mandatory direction to court to engage a counsel, if conditions laid down in rule are otherwise satisfied - (B) Whether sufficient time allowed to amicus curiae to prepare for his case? - Respective clause of r. 37 requires that counsel appointed under Rules shall be furnished with necessary papers free of cost and allowed sufficient time to prepare for defence - Held, from facts of case, no sufficient time allowed - Further held, rule casts a duty on court itself to grant sufficient time to counsel for this purpose and record should show that rule was complied with by granting him time which court considered sufficient in particular circumstances of case - (C) Contention raised that no fundamental right u/art. 21, can be said to be violated on ground of non-compliance of Rules, 1957 as such Rules was not enacted by any legislature and, consequently, it should not be held to be a part of procedure established by law - Contention whether acceptable? - Position settled vide decisions in cases starting from A K Gopalan's case till 'Makhan Singh v. State of Punjab, (1964)' - That, Rules made by a subordinate legislative authority in exercise of its delegated power of legislation granted by Constitution or a statute enacted by legislature are 'law' for purposes of art. 21, though, it is always open to person affected to challenge validity of those Rules - In instant case, r. 37 of Rules has been framed in exercise of powers of HC u/art. 227 of Constitution and s. 554 of CrPC, 1973 and is a valid rule - Further held, conviction of appellant in a trial held in violation of that rule and award of sentence of death will result in deprivation of his life in breach of procedure established by law - (D) Contention raised that no prejudice has been caused to accused due to such non-compliance of Rules - In such a case, question of prejudice does not arise when a citizen is deprived of his life without complying with procedure prescribed by law - Moreover court noted that in facts of case prejudice had in fact caused to accused - Further held, there was error in procedure adopted at trial, which led to violation of art. 21 - Conviction and sentence set aside - Direction issued for trial afresh - Appeal allowed.
