State Bank of India Through General Manager v National Housing Bank and others

Supreme Court of India

31 July 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, R.M. Lodha, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 478; (2013) 16 SCC 538; AIR 2013 SC 3478; 2013 (4) BC 146; [2013] 180 Comp Cas 15; 2013 (4) CompLJ 193; 2014 (2) DRTC 314; JT 2013 (10) SC 545; 2013(9) SCALE 710

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Bonds, Commissions Of Inquiry Act, 1952, Custodian, National Housing Bank, Nhb

Summary: (A) Banking & Finance - Civil Procedure - Suit for recovery of money - Decree by Special Court - Propriety - Plaintiff Bank drew a cheque on Reserve Bank of India in favour of defendant No. 1 Bank - Plaintiff sought delivery of Bank Reciepts (BRs)/Securities or return of amount - Defendant No. 1 denied existence of any outstanding transaction with plaintiff - Plaintiff filed suit before Special Court - Special Court decreed partly in favour of plaintiff, however, directed plaintiff to pay certain amounts to defendant No. 5, who was custodian defendant No. 2, a broker of stock exchange - Whether impugned decree of Special Court was legal - Held, there was absolutely no evidence on record for alleged payment of Rs.55 crore (approx.) by plaintiff to another bank at the instance of defendant No. 2 except Janakiraman Committee Report and correspondence which was neither proved nor content of correspondence was explained - Suit was required to be dismissed on the ground that there was no evidence led by plaintiff to establish its case - Further, plaintiff approached Special Court with unclean hands by suppressing relevant material facts - Whole attempt of plaintiff and defendant banks was to shield officers on either side taking refuge under attractive legal pleas - Suit transaction was an innocuous transaction and was a sheer abuse of legal process - Hence, suit and decree thereto were set aside - Order accordingly.

(B) Banking & Finance - Civil Procedure - Suit for recovery of money - Suppression of facts - Whether plaintiff Bank had suppressed relevant facts - Held, plaint, as originally filed, stated that cheque in question was drawn in favour of defendant No. 1 in respect of sale by defendant No. 1 to plaintiff of 9% IRFC Bonds of face value Rs.100 crore - However, subsequently plaint was amended omitting reference of purchase of said IRFC Bonds - Further, defendant No. 1 paid various amounts in accordance with instructions of defendant No. 2 after encashing cheque in question as per instructions of some body high up in administration of plaintiff Bank - Neither of banks explained genesis of such practice - Entire effort of plaintiff in suit, was to suppress all relevant information in order to shield delinquent officers of bank who were responsible for such dealings - Order accordingly.

(C) Banking & Finance - Civil Procedure - Special Court (Trial of Offences Relating to Transactions in Securities) Act, 1992, ss. 9-A(4), 9-A(1) - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 8 - Special Courts - Procedure to be followed - Whether Special Court was justified in disregarding procedure set out under O. 8 of CPC while considering counter claim of defendant No. 2 - Held, procedure that was required to be followed in cases of set off or counter claim was detailed under O. 8 of CPC - Procedure contemplated under O. 8 of CPC was not followed in the instant case - However, u/s. 9-A(4) of the Act, Special Court was not bound by procedure laid down by CPC but should be guided by principles of natural justice and had power to regulate its own procedure - U/s. 9-A(1) of the Act, Special Court had all jurisdiction to adjudicate any matter or claim arising out of a transaction in securities entered into during the period specified in the said section in which a notified person was involved in whatever capacity - Thus, Special Court was authorised by law to adjudicate claim of defendant No. 2 without being shackled by procedural fetters imposed under CPC - Order accordingly.

(D) Banking & Finance - Practice & Procedure - Special Court (Trial of Offences Relating to Transactions in Securities) Act, 1992 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Report of Administrative Committee - Evidentiary value - Whether Special Court was justified in relying upon the findings of Janakiraman Committee Report - Held, Special Court based its conclusions on Janakiraman Committee Report and correspondence between various parties - Course adopted by Special Court of looking into correspondence between parties, which had not been proved, was not permissible in law - 1992 Act though declared that Court was not bound by CPC, it did not relieve Special Court from obligation to follow the 1872 Act - Further, Special Court extensively relied upon second interim report of Jankiraman Committee on the ground that the same was tendered by 1st defendant - Courts were not bound by conclusions and findings rendered by such Commissions - Statements made before such Commission could not be used as evidence before any civil or criminal court - Even conclusions based on such statements could also not be used as evidence in any Court - Janakiraman Committee was not even a statutory body authorised to collect evidence in legal sense - It was a body set up by Governor of Reserve Bank of India in exercise of its administrative functions - Hence, report of Janakiraman Committee was not evidence within the meaning of 1872 Act, which Special Court was bound to follow - Order accordingly.
