Bondu Ramaswamy v Bangalore Development Authority and others

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, K.G. Balakrishnan, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 348; (2010) 7 SCC 129; JT 2010 (6) SC 57; 2010 (5) KarLJ 177; 2010 (5) MLJ(SC) 541; 2010(5) SCALE 70; [2010] 6 S.C.R. 29

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Competence Of The Legislature, Existing Laws, Karnataka Government (Transaction of Business) Rules, 1977, City of Bangalore Municipal Corporation Act, 1949, City of Bangalore Improvement Act, 1945, Karnataka Municipal Corporation Act, 1976, Bangalore Development Authority (Allotment of Site) Rules, 1984, Government of Karnataka (Execution of Business) Rules, 1977

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Constitution - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(3) - Land acquisition - Assent of President - Requirement of - Respondent/Authority issued notices to appellant/landowners u/s. 17(5) of the 1976 Act as a part of land acquisition - Appellants filed objections and respondent rejected objections - Respondent issued final declaration u/s. 19(1) of the Act after getting the approval from the State Government - Appellants challenged acquisition proceedings before HC - Single Judge quashed the proceedings - Appellants filed appeal before the DB - DB set aside the Single Judge order - Whether 1976 Act, in so far as it provides for compulsory acquisition of property, is still-born and ineffective as it did not receive the assent of the President, as required by art. 31(3) of the Constitution - Held, art. 31 was omitted from the Constitution by Constitution (Forty Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978 and hence the need for President's assent disappeared and the impediment for enforcement of the provisions in the 1976 Act relating to acquisition also disappeared - Art. 31 did not render the enactment a nullity if there was no assent of the President, it only directed that a law relating to compulsory acquisition would not have effect unless the law received the assent of the President - Principles on which the compensation is to be determined and the manner in which the compensation is to be determined set out in the 1894 Act, become applicable to acquisitions under 1976 Act, thus, there is no violation of art. 31(2) - Art. 31(3) does not specify any fundamental right, but relates to the procedure for making a law providing for acquisition and it does not nullify any laws, but postpones the enforcement of a law relating to acquisition, until it receives the assent of the President - Once the requirement of assent disappeared, the provisions relating to acquisition became enforceable - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Land & Property - Constitution - Municipalities & Local Governments - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976, ss. 2(c), 15 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Constitution of India, 1950, Part IX - Land acquisition - Applicability of Part IX of the Constitution - Extent - Whether the provisions of the 1976 Act, in particular s.15 r/w s. 2(c) dealing with the power of the Authority to draw up schemes for development for Bangalore Metropolitan Area (BDA) became inoperative, void or was impliedly repealed, by virtue of Parts IX and IX(A) of the Constitution inserted by the 73rd and 74th Amendments to the Constitution - Held, members of the BDA represent different interests and groups, technical persons and elected representatives and no development scheme can be finalised or put into effect without the sanction of the State Government which in turn has to take note of any representation by the Bangalore Municipal Corporation in regard to the development scheme - Therefore, the mere fact that BDA is not wholly elected body as in the case of a municipal corporation would make no difference - Membership pattern of BDA is more suited to fulfil the requirements of a specialist agency executing development schemes, therefore, no merit in the contention that provisions of BDA Act become inoperative, on Parts IX and IX-A of the Constitution coming into force - Further, area in which the 1976 Act operates is totally different from the areas in which Part IX A of the Constitution which relate to local self-governments operate - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Land & Property - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976, ss. 2(c), 15 - Land acquisition - Territorial jurisdiction of acquisition body - Whether the sixteen villages where the lands have been acquired, fall outside the Bangalore Metropolitan Area as defined in s. 2(c) of the BDA Act and therefore, the BDA has no territorial jurisdiction to make development schemes or acquire lands in those villages - Held, the core area or the inner circle area, that is Bangalore City, is a part of Bangalore Metropolitan Area in view of the definition u/s. 2(c) - 218 villages specified in the notification are the villages immediately surrounding and adjoining Bangalore city and it forms the first concentric circle area around core area of Bangalore city - 325 villages listed in I Schedule to the second notification are situated beyond the 218 villages and form a wider second concentric circle around the central core area and the first concentric circle area of 218 villages - Therefore, the three areas, namely, the central core area, the adjoining 218 villages constituting the first concentric circle area and the next adjoining 325 villages forming the second concentric circle are all included within the Bangalore Metropolitan Area - Boundaries indicated in the final declaration would clearly include the 16 villages which are the subject matter of the acquisition and hence BDA has territorial jurisdiction to form any development scheme in regard to the 16 villages which are the subject matter of the final declaration - Appeals disposed of.

(D) Land & Property - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976, ss. 15, 17, 19 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4,5,6 - Land acquisition - Final declaration - Limitation - Whether the amendment to s. 6 of the 1894 Act requiring the final declaration to be issued within one year from the date of publication of the preliminary notification is applicable to the acquisitions under the 1976 Act; and whether the declaration u/s. 19(1) of 1976 Act, having been issued after the expiry of one year from the date of the preliminary notification u/s.17(1) and (3) of 1976 Act, is invalid - Held, where there are already provisions in the 1976 Act regulating certain aspects or stages of acquisition or the proceedings relating thereto, the corresponding provisions of 1894 Act would not apply to the acquisitions under the 1976 Act - Only those provisions of 1894 Act, relating to the stages of acquisition, for which there is no provision in the 1976 Act, are applied to the acquisitions under 1976 Act - 1976 Act contains specific provisions relating to preliminary notification and final declaration - In fact the procedure up to final declaration under 1976 Act is different from the procedure under the 1894 Act relating to acquisition proceedings up to the stage of final notification - Therefore, having regard to the Scheme for acquisition u/ss. 15 to 19 of 1976 Act and the limited application of 1894 Act in terms of s. 36 of 1976 Act, the provisions of ss. 4 to 6 of 1894 Act would not apply to the acquisitions under 1976 Act - If s. 6 of 1894 Act is not made applicable, the question of amendment to s. 6 of 1894 Act providing a time limit for issue of final declaration, would also not apply - Hence, final declaration does not suffer from any infirmity on account of the same having been published a few days beyond one year from the date of publication of the preliminary notification u/s. 17 (1) and (3) of the 1976 Act - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Land & Property - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4, 5, 6 - Land acquisition - Enquiry u/s. 5A of 1894 Act - Requirement of - Whether the provisions of ss. 4, 5A, 6 of 1894 Act, would be applicable in regard to acquisitions under the 1976 Act and whether non- compliance with those provisions, vitiate the acquisition proceedings - Held, s. 5A of 1894 Act is inapplicable to acquisitions under the 1976 Act and the scheme of 1976 Act also contemplates consideration of objections but does not require any personal hearing or inquiry - While the scheme for acquisition under the 1894 Act and the 1976 Act contemplates notice to the landholders/persons interested, the procedure thereafter is markedly different - While 1894 Act requires an 'enquiry' where the Dy. Commissioner is required to give the objectors opportunity of being heard in person and conducting such further inquiry as he thinks necessary, 1976 Act requires issuing notices to the persons interested to show-cause why acquisition should not be made and consider the representations received and where there is no personal hearing or 'enquiry' is as contemplated - Therefore, it is impermissible to import the requirement of ss. 4, 5 and 6 of 1894 Act in regard to acquisitions under the 1976 Act - Appeals disposed of.

(F) Land & Property - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976, ss. 15 to 19 - Land acquisition - Non-compliance of procedure - (a) Whether the development scheme and the acquisitions are invalid for non-compliance with the procedure prescribed u/ss. 15 to 19 of the 1976 Act in regard to the absence of specificity and discrepancy in extent of land to be acquired - Held, mere fact that there were some modifications from time to time when the initial proposal was mooted till the issue of the notification u/ss. 17(1) and (3) or that some lands were omitted/deleted in the declaration u/s. 19(1) would not affect the validity of the scheme - In fact deletion of some items of land or reducing the extent proposed to be acquired in some items of land, when issuing final declaration is made is quite common and is indeed a result of the process prescribed under any Act providing for acquisitions - Further, changes and modifications are contemplated in the process of making the scheme u/ss. 15 to 19 of 1976 Act - (b) Whether respondent failed to furnish material particulars to the Government as required u/s. 18(1) r/w s. 16 of the 1976 Act - Held, project map was not one of the items that had to be furnished, however, scheme report had been made available to the Government - Government in its reply stated that whatever particulars that were required to be furnished, had been furnished and they were satisfied that the scheme required to be sanctioned - Therefore, there is no failure on the part of respondents to furnish material particulars to the Government before getting sanction - (c) Whether there was any absence of valid sanction by the Government u/s. 18(3) of the 1976 Act - Held, approval granted by the Chief Minister, subject to the ratification of the Cabinet was treated by the Urban Development Department as approval for the sanction and a Government order was made thereafter in the name of the Governor granting sanction u/s. 18(3) of the 1976 Act - State Government also issued a final declaration u/s. 19(1) of 1976 Act - State Government proceeded on the basis that the order of approval of the Chief Minister for the sanction, was sufficient for grant of sanction u/s. 18(3) - Even if it is to be assumed that such approval was irregular as it was made subject to ratification, as the ratification was subsequently made, the challenge for want of proper approval of the Cabinet for the sanction cannot be accepted - Appeals disposed of.

(G) Land & Property - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976 - Land acquisition - Discrimination - Whether the deletion of some items from the proposed acquisition, while proceeding with the acquisition of similar contiguous lands of appellants amounted to hostile discrimination and therefore the lands of appellants also required to be withdrawn from acquisition - Held, BDA does not seriously dispute the fact that there were some amount of arbitrariness and discrimination in the matter of inclusions and exclusions - Further, BDA has not come up with true and correct position even in the present matter - Break up of deletions and the reasons for such deletions were not disclosed and BDA did not choose to explain the exact extent of the government land involved therein - Where arbitrary and unexplained deletions and exclusions from acquisition, of large extents of notified lands, render the acquisitions meaningless, or totally unworkable, the court will have no alternative but to quash the entire acquisition, however, to salvage the acquisition and to avoid hardships to BDA and its allottees and to avoid prolonged further round litigations, a more equitable way would be to uphold the decision of the DB - Direction issued to the respondents to provide an option to the land-losers to seek allotment of developed plots in lieu of compensation or to provide for preferential allotment of some plots at the prevailing market price in addition to compensation - Appeals disposed of.
