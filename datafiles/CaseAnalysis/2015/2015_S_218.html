Ghusabhai Raisangbhai Chorasiya and others v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

18 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 114; AIR 2015 SC 2670; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 1188; 2015 CRLJ 3613; JT 2015 (2) SC 345; 2015 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1225; 2015 (1) OLR 761; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 70; 2015(2) SCALE 500; [2015] 2 S.C.R. 594

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conviction, Sustainability, Dowry harassment

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498A, 306, 201 r/w s. 114 - Dowry harassment - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) were charged for alleged offences u/ss. 498A, 306, 201 r/w s. 114 of IPC - Trial Court found appellants guilty u/ss. 498A, 306, 201 and 114 of IPC and sentenced them to undergo RI for 7 years - Aggrieved appellants filed an appeal before DB, which was dismissed by impugned judgment - Hence, instant appeal - Whether there was cruelty by husband and his relations that could have driven the deceased to commit suicide.

Held, both the Courts have found that cruelty, as alleged by the prosecution u/s. 498A of IPC was established as a result of which the deceased committed suicide. It is quite clear from the findings and evidence on record that there was no demand of dowry. The Trial Court as well as the HC has proceeded on the base that there was cruelty as per the first limb of s. 498A of IPC - In instant case, in fact, there is no demand of dowry. If the evidence is appropriately appreciated, the deceased was pained and disturbed as the husband was having an illicit affair with the appellant no. 4. Whether such a situation would amount to cruelty under the first limb of s. 498A of IPC is to be seen. The factum of divorce has not been believed by the Trial Court and the HC. But the fact remains is that the husband and the wife had started living separately in the same house and the deceased had told her sister (PW21) that there was severance of status and she would be going to her parental home after the 'Holi' festival. True it is, there is some evidence about the illicit relationship and even if the same is proven, cruelty, as envisaged under the first limb of s. 498A of IPC would not get attracted. It would be difficult to hold that the mental cruelty was of such a degree that it would drive the wife to commit suicide. Mere extra-marital relationship, even if proved, would be illegal and immoral, but it would take a different character if the prosecution brings some evidence on record to show that the accused had conducted in such a manner to drive the wife to commit suicide. In instant case, the accused may have been involved in an illicit relationship with the appellant no. 4, but in the absence of some other acceptable evidence on record that can establish such high degree of mental cruelty, the Explanation to s. 498A of IPC which includes cruelty to drive a woman to commit suicide, would not be attracted. There is no allegation of any kind of physical torture. The evidence brought on record against appellants with regard to cruelty is absolutely sketchy and not convincing. On the basis of the evidence, it is difficult to sustain the conviction u/ss. 306 and 498A of IPC. Conviction and sentence of appellants are set aside. Appeal allowed.
