Anand Mohan v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

10 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Patnaik, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 404; (2012) 7 SCC 225; 2013 CRLJ 2644; 2012 (4) MLJ(Crl) 286; 2012 (3) RCR(Criminal) 715; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, 307, 147, 149, 109, 114 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 154(1) - Murder - FIR - Cryptic information - Conviction - Sustainability - District Magistrate/deceased was shot dead during a procession - Charge sheet against appellants was filed u/ss. 307, 147, 149, 109 and 114 IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant and 6 accused persons and acquitted remaining all the other accused - On appeal HC held that prosecution had not been able to establish a case of unlawful assembly with common object of causing death of the deceased, thus there could be no conviction u/ss. 147 and 302/149 IPC - However HC further held on the basis of the evidence that appellant had exhorted the lone shooter to kill deceased and hence he alone was guilty of the offence of abetment of murder u/ss. 302/109 IPC - Hence instant appeal - Appellant contended that occurrence took place at 4.15 pm. on 5-12-1994 and soon thereafter information was sent through wireless to the District Headquarter, about the incident and hence that information was the real FIR and would disclose the first account of the occurrence - Further contended that instead of wireless message, a typed report of the informant PW14 had been treated as FIR - Whether the wireless message sent soon after the incident was the real FIR as contended on behalf of the defence or whether the typed report subsequently lodged by PW14 in the Police Station was the FIR as contended on behalf of the prosecution - Held, it would be clear from the language of s. 154(1) CrPC that every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence whether given in writing or reduced to writing shall be signed by the person giving it - Hence, the person who gives the information and who has to sign the information has to choose which particular information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence was to be treated as an FIR - In instant case, PW14, the informant has chosen not to treat the wireless message but the subsequent typed information as FIR and the police has also not treated the wireless message but the subsequent typed information as the FIR - Wireless message was cryptic and did not sufficiently disclosed the nature of offence committed much less the identity of the persons who committed the offence - Unless and until more information was collected on how exactly the deceased was killed, it was not mandatory for either PW14 to lodge the same as FIR or for the Officer Incharge of a police station to treat the same as an FIR - SC in Sheikh Ishaque and Others v. State of Bihar 1995 Indlaw SC 1761 held that, cryptic information did not even disclose the commission of a cognizable offence nor did it disclose who were the assailants and such a cryptic statement could not be treated to be an FIR within the meaning of s. 154 CrPC - (B) Whether the typed report of PW14 which has been treated as the FIR was lodged at 10.10 pm. on 5-12-1994 as claimed by prosecution or was actually lodged at the Police Station in the morning of 16-12-1994 as contended by the defence - It appeared that PW17 and PW21 were not aware of any shooting incident at all and they were under the impression that the deceased had been injured by the assault of the mob after he was pulled out from the car - PW17 and PW21, did not seem to know what exactly happened after they were pulled out from the car and beaten up by the mob - On the basis of their evidence, the Court could not discard the evidence of ten other witnesses that deceased was shot by X with the revolver on the exhortation of appellant when the medical evidence established that the cause of death of the deceased was on account of the bullet injuries on the deceased and not the assault by the mob - Moreover, PW17 and PW21 might not have supported the prosecution case but their evidence also did not belie the prosecution case that deceased was shot by X on the exhortation by appellant - Prosecution established that appellant was at the place of occurrence and had exhorted X to shoot at the deceased - If appellant wanted the Court to reject this prosecution version as not probable, burden was on him to lead evidence that he was not at the spot and did not exhort X to shoot at the deceased - Since he had not discharged burden, the HC was right in holding that appellant was guilty of the offence u/ss. 302/109 IPC - HC held that though the deceased was a District Magistrate, he was killed in another district as an occupant of a car by chance on account of mob fury and exhortation by appellant and firing by X and as appellant was not the assailant himself, rigorous imprisonment for life and death sentence would not be the appropriate sentence - HC and SC were of the view that instant case was not one of those rarest of rare cases where the HC should have confirmed the death sentence on appellant - Appellant was liable for rigorous imprisonment for life - Appeals dismissed.
