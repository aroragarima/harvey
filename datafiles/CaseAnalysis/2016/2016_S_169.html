Sudhir Chaudhary etc. v State (NCT of Delhi)

Supreme Court of India

29 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

D.Y. Chandrachud, T.S. Thakur, A.M. Khanwilkar

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 555; (2016) 8 SCC 307; AIR 2016 SC 3772; JT 2016 (7) SC 405; 2016 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 2998; 2016 (2) OLR 539; 2016 (3) RCR(Civil) 939; 2016 (3) RCR(Criminal) 952; 2016 (4) RLW 3173; 2016(7) SCALE 467; [2016] 7 S.C.R. 927

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Expert, Conversation, Voice, Facilitate, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 482 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 384, 420, 511 - Voice sample - Inculpatory statement - Sustainability -Appellants/accused demanded a sum of money to refrain from telecasting programmes on a television channel pertaining to alleged involvement of a corporate entity in a wrongful activity pertaining to allocation of coal blocks - FIR was registered against appellants for offences u/ss. 384, 511, 420 and 120B IPC - Appellants were directed by IO to read out from a paper instead of voice sample - Appellants filed an application under CrPC for monitoring investigation and for a direction to IO to provide material for purpose of a voice sample "which does not contain any inculpatory statement" in presence of a judicial magistrate - Additional Chief Metropolitan Magistrate (ACMM) dismissed application holding that once consent was granted accused would have to abide by instructions of IO and cannot dictate terms on which voice sample has to be given - On revision, HC held that once appellants had furnished their consent to furnishing their voice samples, it was not open to them to dictate course of investigation - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, SC do not find substance in contention that text which is to be read by appellants in course of drawing their voice samples should contain no part of inculpatory words which are a part of the disputed conversation. A commonality of words is necessary to facilitate a spectrographic examination. SC allowed an adjournment to respondent to seek instructions from expert concerned whether or not a sample of words in such number as expert may suggest would suffice for experts to give their opinion by scientific voice sampling methods. IO was directed to file a transcript of disputed conversation in a sealed cover. Director/CBI was called upon to file in a sealed cover a proposed passage of a written text which appellants shall be required to read out for purpose of giving their voice samples using words, but not sentences appearing in disputed conversation necessary for purpose of comparison. Therefore, SC directions ensure that text which appellants would be called upon to read out for purpose of drawing their voice samples will not have sentences from inculpatory text. Similarly, permitting the text to contain words drawn from disputed conversation would meet legitimate concern of investigating authorities for making a fair comparison. Appeals disposed of.
