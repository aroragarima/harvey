Indra Sarma v V. K. V. Sarma

Supreme Court of India

26 November 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Radhakrishnan, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 773; (2013) 15 SCC 755; AIR 2014 SC 309; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 319; 2014 (102) ALR 711; 2013 (4) KLT 763; 2014 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 698; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 59; 2014 (1) RCR(Civil) 263; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 179B; 2013(14) SCALE 448

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Family & Personal; Practice & Procedure; Women & Children

Keywords: Woman, Concubine, Civil Partnership Act, 2004, Domestic Violence, Crime and Victims Act, 2004, Domestic Violence Intervention Act, 2001, Property (Relationships) Act, 1984

Summary: Family & Personal - Criminal - Women & Children - Practice & Procedure - Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005, ss. 2(f), 3 and 12 - Maintenance claimed by female from living relationship - Entitlement to - Appellant and respondent started living together against the wishes of appellant's family and that of respondent's wife and two children - Respondent's family constantly opposed their live-in relationship and ultimately forced him to leave the company of the appellant and it was alleged that he left the company of the appellant without maintaining her - Appellant then preferred criminal case u/s. 12 of Act and prayed for maintenance from respondent - Trial Court allowed the prayer of appellant - Lower appellate court also agreed with Trial Court - However, HC denied relief prayed by appellant - Hence instant appeal - Whether a 'live-in relationship' would amount to a 'relationship in the nature of marriage' falling within the definition of 'domestic relationship' u/s. 2(f) of Act and the disruption of such a relationship by failure to maintain a women involved in such a relationship would amount to 'domestic violence' within the meaning of s. 3 of Act - Held, appellant had entered into the relationship knowing well that the respondent was a married person and encouraged bigamous relationship - By entering into such a relationship, the appellant had committed an intentional tort, i.e. interference in the marital relationship with intentionally alienating respondent from his family, i.e. his wife and children - If the case set up by the appellant was accepted, SC would have to conclude that there was an attempt on the part of the appellant to alienate respondent from his family, resulting in loss of marital relationship, companionship, assistance, loss of consortium etc., so far as the legally wedded wife and children of the respondent were concerned, who resisted the relationship from the very inception - Marriage and family were social institutions of vital importance - Alienation of affection was an intentional tort which gave a cause of action to the wife and children of the respondent to sue the appellant for alienating the husband/father from the company of his wife/children, knowing fully well they were legally wedded wife/children of the respondent - Pinakin Mahipatray Rawal case 2013 Indlaw SC 569, relied on - SC was, therefore, of the view that the appellant, having been fully aware of the fact that the respondent was a married person, could not have entered into a live-in relationship in the nature of marriage - All live-in- relationships were not relationships in the nature of marriage - Appellant's and the respondent's relationship was, therefore, not a 'relationship in the nature of marriage' because it had no inherent or essential characteristic of a marriage, but a relationship other than 'in the nature of marriage' and the appellant's status was lower than the status of a wife and that relationship would not fall within the definition of 'domestic relationship' u/s. 2(f) of Act. If SC were to hold that the relationship between the appellant and the respondent was a relationship in the nature of a marriage, it would be doing an injustice to the legally wedded wife and children who opposed that relationship - Consequently, any act, omission or commission or conduct of the respondent in connection with that type of relationship, would not amount to 'domestic violence' u/s. 3 of Act - SC found that the appellant's status was that of a mistress, who was in distress, a survivor of a live-in relationship which was of serious concern, especially when such persons were poor and illiterate, in the event of which vulnerability was more pronounced, which was a societal reality - Children born out of such relationship also sufferred most which called for bringing in remedial measures by the Parliament, through proper legislation - SC was conscious of the fact that if any direction was given to the respondent to pay maintenance or monetary consideration to the appellant, that would be at the cost of the legally wedded wife and children of the respondent, especially when they had opposed that relationship and have a cause of action against the appellant for alienating the companionship and affection of the husband/parent which was an intentional tort - Appeal dismissed.
