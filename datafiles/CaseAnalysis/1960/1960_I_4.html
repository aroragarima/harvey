Â Calcutta Discount Company Limited v Income Tax Officer, Companies District, and Another

Supreme Court of India

01 November 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta, S.K. Das, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 132; AIR 1961 SC 372; [1961] 41 ITR 191; [1961] 2 S.C.R. 241

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: (Majority View)

K.C. Das Gupta, J. for himself, S. K. Das and N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, JJ

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 22, 22 (2), 23 (3), 34, 34 (1), 43, 66 and 66 (2) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - A/ys 1942-43, 1943-44 and 1944-45 - Income escaping assessment - Non-disclosure of material facts - Appellant, a private limited company, was assessed to income tax for the a/ys 1942-43, 1943-44 and 1944-45 by three separate orders u/s. 23(2) of 1922 Act - Said assessments were made upon returns filed by the appellant accompanied by the statements of account - The taxes assessed were duly paid up by the appellant - Subsequently, on 28.03.1951, three notices purporting to be u/s. 34 of the 1922 Act were issued by the Income Tax Officer (ITO) calling upon the appellant to submit fresh returns of its total income - Appellant furnished returns in compliance with the notices but thereafter applied to the HC u/art. 226 of the Constitution for issue appropriate writ restraining the ITO from initiating assessment proceedings on the basis of the said notices on the ground that that he had no jurisdiction to issue the said notices - Appellant also contended that the conditions precedent for the assumption of jurisdiction u/s. 34 of the 1922 Act were also not satisfied - The HC held that the said notices issued were without jurisdiction - Accordingly he made an order prohibiting the ITO from continuing the assessment proceedings on the basis of the impugned notices - Hence, the present appeal - Whether ITO was right in issuing notices on the assessee u/s. 34(1)(a) of the 1922 Act? - Held, before ITO could issue a notice u/s. 34(1)(a) of 1922 Act, two conditions precedent must co-exist, namely, that he must have to believe that income, profits or gains had been under-assessed and that such under-assessment was due to non-disclosure of material facts by the assessee - Burden of disclosing all the primary facts must invariably be on the assessee - In the instant case, there was no non-disclosure of a primary material fact which the assessee was bound to disclose u/s. 34(1)(a) of the 1922 Act, the ITO had no jurisdiction to issue the notices in question - Appeal allowed.

(Minority View)

J. M. Hidayatullah, J.

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 22, 22 (2), 23 (3), 34, 34 (1), 43, 66 and 66 (2) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - A/ys 1942-43, 1943-44 and 1944-45 - Income escaping assessment - Non-disclosure of material facts - Held, the explanation to s. 34(1) of the 1922 Act clearly indicates that the duty of the assessee does not end by merely producing evidence or disclosing the primary facts, but also extends to the disclosure of such other facts relating to status, agency, benami nature of the transaction, the nature of the trading and the like, which he knows but do not appear from the evidence, and which may be necessary for interpreting the evidence - If the evidence produced hides nothing and discloses everything, the assessee cannot be subjected to s. 34 of the 1922 Act merely because the ITO misinterprets such evidence - But it is otherwise if the assessee raises a contention that is contrary to fact and requires the ITO to discover the truth for himself for that would be to suppress a material fact that would attract the section - Since, in the instant case, the appellant not only knowingly suppressed that fact but contended otherwise, there was non-disclosure of a material fact necessary for its assessment, and sufficient to attract s. 34(1) (a) of the 1922 Act - Appeal dismissed.

J. C. Shah, J.

Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 22, 22 (2), 23 (3), 34, 34 (1), 43, 66 and 66 (2) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - A/ys 1942-43, 1943-44 and 1944-45 - Income escaping assessment - Non-disclosure of material facts - Held, the expression 'has reason to believe' in s. 34(1)(a) of the 1922 Act does not mean a purely subjective satisfaction of the ITO but predicates the existence of reasons on which such belief has to be founded - That belief, therefore, cannot be founded on mere suspicion and must be based on evidence and any question as to the adequacy of such evidence is wholly immaterial at that stage - Where the existence of reasonable belief that there had been under-assessment due to non-disclosure by the assessee, which is a condition precedent to exercise of the power u/s. 34(1)(a) of 1922 Act is asserted by the assessing authority and the record prima facie supports its existence, any enquiry as to whether the authority could reasonably hold the belief that the under assessment was due to non-disclosure by the assessee of material facts necessary for the assessment must, be barred - Appeal dismissed.
