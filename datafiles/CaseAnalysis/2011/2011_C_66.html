Commissioner Of Central Excise, Mumbai v M/S. Kalvert Foods India Pvt. Ltd. & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

09 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 526; (2011) 12 SCC 243; 2012(2) ALL MR 31; 2011 (270) E.L.T. 643; JT 2011 (9) SC 126; 2011(8) SCALE 320; [2011] 9 S.C.R. 902

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Indirect Tax

Summary: Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944 - Demand - Clandestine removal - Respondent no. 1 was engaged in the manufacture of P & P food products - On receiving information, search was conducted at the premises of the respondent no. 1 whereby several incriminating documents, articles and records were found - On the basis of said documents, a show cause notice was issued to the respondent no.1 and respondent no. 2 (Director of the Company) and duty demand was raised from the respondents and penalty was also imposed on the respondents - On appeal, Commissioner confirmed the duty demand and ordered confiscation of the seized goods and also imposed penalty equivalent to the amount of duty on the respondents - Tribunal set aside the findings of the Commissioner and held that the respondents were not guilty of clandestine removal of excisable goods and also that the goods of respondents were not excisable inasmuch as they were allegedly not packed in containers under a brand name and therefore not required to pay any excise duty - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether impugned order of the tribunal could be upheld? - Held, statements of respondent no. 2 and other persons containing all the details about the functioning of the respondent no. 1, were recorded by the Central Excise Officers and they were not police officers - Therefore, such statements could not have been obtained through coercion or duress or through dictation - No reason why the aforesaid statements made in the circumstances of the case should not be considered, looked into and relied upon - Moreover, tribunal also failed to consider and discuss the specific allegation that respondent no. 1 maintained two sets of computerized commercial invoices, one for excisable products and the other for non-excisable goods - Hence, it was clear that the respondent no. 1 was guilty of clandestine removal of excisable goods as a non-excisable goods in order to evade excise duty - Tribunal committed manifest error in coming to its conclusion and therefore the order passed by the Tribunal was set aside and the order passed by the Commissioner was restored - Appeals allowed.
