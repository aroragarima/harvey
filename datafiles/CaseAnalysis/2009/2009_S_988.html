Sukanti Moharana v State of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

29 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 2309; 2009 (4) JCC 2585

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Women & Children

Keywords: Voluntary, Dying Declaration, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Signature, Treatment, Kerosene, CONSTITUTION, Version, Doctor, Mental Condition, Informant, Oral Dying Declaration, Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, Rigorous Imprisonment, Indian Penal Code, 1860

Summary: Criminal - Women & Children - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss.302,304B, 498A - Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, s.4 - Evidentiary value of Dying Declaration - Deceased succumbed to burn injuries in hospital and PW1 lodged FIR before PS and later, case for commission of offences u/ss. 498A/307/34 of IPC and s.4 of the Act was registered against appellant and Benudar-husband of deceased - Upon conclusion of trial, Trial Court convicted appellant u/ss.302,304B and 498A of IPC and u/s.4 of the Act and Benudhar was convicted and sentenced u/ss.304B and 498A of IPC and u/s.4 of the Act - Aggrieved, appellant and Benudhar filed appeal before HC - Upon hearing parties, HC had set aside conviction and sentence of Benudhar and he was acquitted of charges levelled - Further, HC allowed appeal of appellant partly by setting aside conviction and sentence u/ss.304B and 498 of IPC and s.4 of the Act but maintained conviction and sentence u/s.302 of IPC - Hence, instant appeal by appellant - Whether conviction and sentence passed against appellant is sustainable.

Held, there is no infirmity in the dying declaration of the deceased as the same vividly mentions the manner in which the deceased suffered the burn injuries on pouring kerosene oil on her by appellant who also lighted the matchstick which caused the fire and burnt the deceased and also closed the door after going out of the said room where the deceased was left to burn by fire. The said description given by deceased in dying declaration and recorded by doctor is clear and unambiguous. Further, dying declaration recorded by doctor is also corroborated by oral dying declaration made before PWs 1 and 3 and the said fact also finds corroboration from the statement of PWs1 and 3 and also from the FIR which were proved through the evidence of PW1. Further, doctor who recorded the dying declaration was examined as a witness and he had in his deposition categorically stated that the deceased while making the aforesaid statement was conscious and was in a fit mental condition to make such a statement. Under such circumstances, dying declaration could be relied upon as the same was truthfully recorded and the said statement gave a vivid account of the manner in which the incident had taken place. Hence, considering all the facts and circumstances of the case and the evidence on record, there are no grounds to interfere with the order of conviction and sentence recorded by Trial Court as confirmed by HC holding appellant guilty of offence u/s.302 of IPC. Appeal dismissed.
