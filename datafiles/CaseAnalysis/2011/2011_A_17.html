FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Abhay Singh Chautala and another v C.B.I.

Supreme Court of India

04 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 391; (2011) 7 SCC 141; JT 2011 (7) SC 313; 2011 (4) MLJ(Crl) 876; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 443; 2011(6) SCALE 658; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 949

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Air Force

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 6, 13(1)(e), 13(2 ) and 19 - Previous sanction necessary for prosecution - Appellants tried for the offences u/ss. 13(1)(e) and 13(2) of the Prevention of Corruption Act r/w s. 109 of IPC in separate trials - Separate charge sheets were filed against both the appellants - It was alleged that appellants while working as the Members of Legislative Assembly had accumulated wealth disproportionate to their known sources of income - Charges were filed on the basis of the investigations conducted by the CBI - However, there was no sanction to prosecute u/s. 19 of the Act against both the appellants - Objection regarding the absence of sanction was raised before the Special Judge, who held that the allegations in the charge sheet did not contain the allegation that the appellants had abused their current office as member of Legislative Assembly and, therefore, no sanction was necessary - Order was upheld by HC - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the sanction u/s. 19 of Prevention of Corruption Act was necessary against both the appellants and, therefore, whether the trial which was in progress against both of them, a valid trial? - Held, HC was absolutely right in relying on the decision in Prakash Singh Badal v. State of Punjab 2006 INDLAW SC 941 to hold that the appellants in both the appeals had abused entirely different office or offices than the one which they were holding on the date on which cognizance was taken and, therefore, there was no necessity of sanction u/s. 19 of the Act - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Constitution - Precedent - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, s. 6 and 19 - (A) Whether the judgment in RS Nayak v. A R. Antulay 1984 INDLAW SC 338 and the law decided therein, was obiter? - Held, in said case, complainant had specifically and basically raised the point that since the accused had ceased to hold the office of Chief Minister on the date of cognizance, there was no question of any sanction and that was the main issue which was decided in Antulay's case as the basic issue - Hence, finding given in Antulay's case was not obiter and did not required reconsideration - Principles regarding the abuse of a particular office, decided in Antulay's case, could not be termed as Obiter dicta - (B) Whether decision in Antulay's case was hit by the doctrine of per incuriam? - Appellant contended that in Antulay's case, s. 6(2) of the Act, as it therein existed, was ignored and that s. 6(2) which was parimateria with s. 19(2) of the Act provides that in case of doubt as to which authority should give the sanction, the time when the offence was alleged to have been committed was relevant - Held, in Antulay's case, it was not as if s. 6(2) of the Act as it then existed, was ignored or was not referred to, but the Constitution Bench had very specifically made a reference to and had interpreted s. 6 as a whole - Further, once in Antulay's case, once the Court had held that if the public servant had abused a particular office and was not holding that office on the date of taking cognizance, there would be no necessity to obtain sanction - In Antulay's case, the Court hold that where a public servant holds a different capacity altogether from the one which he is alleged to have abused, there would be no necessity of sanction at all - This view was taken on the specific interpretation of s. 6 generally and more particularly, s. 6(1)(c), which was parimateria to s. 19(1)(c) of the Act - Once it was held that there was no necessity of sanction at all, there would be no question of there being any doubt arising about the sanctioning authority - Doubt expressed in s. 19(2), was not a pointer to suggest that a public servant may have abused any particular office, but when he occupies any other office subsequently, then the sanction was a must - Words in s. 19(2) were to be read in conjunction with ss. 19(1)(a), 19(1)(b) and 19(1)(c) - Appeals dismissed.
