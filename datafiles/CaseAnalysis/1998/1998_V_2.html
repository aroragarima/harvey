Central Bureau of Investigation v V. C. Shukla and Others

Supreme Court of India

02 March 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M.K. Mukherjee, K.T. Thomas, S.P. Kurdukar

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 1448; (1998) 3 SCC 410; (1998) SCC (Cr) 761; AIR 1998 SC 1406; 1998 (2) CalLT(SC) 16; 1998 (1) CCR 333; 1998 (1) Crimes 219; 1998 CRLJ 1905; 1998 (75) ECR 484; JT 1998 (2) SC 172; 1998 (2) RCR(Criminal) 17; 1998(2) SCALE 84; [1998] 1 S.C.R. 1153; 1998 (3) SLT 189; 1998 (2) Supreme 329

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Fema

Summary: Criminal - FEMA - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 7, 12, 13(1)(d), 13(2) - Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1973 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 10, 17, 21, 34 - Searches - Recovery - Charge-Sheet - Framing of charges - Sustainability - CBI following searches on premises of JK Jain recovered two diaries and other files purportedly containing details of receipts of various amounts from different sources, through hawala means, recorded in abbreviated form and details of payments made to various public servants to get favourable decisions - CBI registered case on 4-3-1995 under various sections of 1988 Act and the 1973 Act and filed 34 charge sheets - In two of the charge-sheets, respondents (Members of Parliament) figured as accused - Trial Court passed orders framing charges against respondents - On appeal, HC set aside Trial Court's order on ground that documents were not admissible in evidence u/s. 34 of the 1872 Act - Hence instant appeals - Appellant contended that co-accused persons were in habit of making payments to influential public servants and political leaders of high status expecting official favours from them and payments were made to respondents with the same motive - Whether Order of HC could be sustained - Held, various SC decisions would make it clear that even correct and authentic entries in books of account should not without independent evidence of their trustworthiness, fix liability upon a person - In view of the same, even if it was assumed that entries made were correct still those entries would not be sufficient to charge respondents, with accusations levelled against them for there was not an iota of independent evidence in support thereof - In instant case, appellant sought to prove that there were two separate conspiracies, in both of which co-accused together figured as common party respondents, as the other - Since it was already found that prosecution had not been able to made out prima facie case to prove that respondents were parties to such conspiracies, charges of conspiracy, as framed should not stand against the co-accused, for the simple reason that in a conspiracy there should be two parties - Further, s. 107 IPC defined abetment as per which a person would abet doing of a thing when he did any of the acts mentioned in three clauses, instigating any person to do that thing or engaging with one or more other person or persons in any conspiracy for doing of that thing or intentionally aiding by act or illegal omission, doing of that things - For understanding scope of the word 'aid' in the third clause it would be advantageous to see Explanation 2 in s. 107 IPC which would read, 'Whoever, either prior to or at the time of commission of an act, does anything in order to facilitate the commission of that act, and thereby facilitates the commission thereof, was said to aid the doing of that act' - It was thus clear that under third clause when a person abetted by aiding, the act so aided should have been committed in order to make such aiding an offence - In other words, unlike the first tow clauses the third clause applies to a case where the offence is committed - In instant case, appellant intended to prove abetment of Jains by aiding and since it was found that no prima facie case was made out against respondents of their having committed offence u/s. 7 of the 1988 Act, the question of co-accused committing offence u/s. 12 would not arise - Appeals dismissed.
