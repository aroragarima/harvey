M/S. Sms Tea Estates Pvt. Ltd. v M/S. Chandmari Tea Co. Pvt. Ltd.

Supreme Court of India

20 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 458; (2011) 14 SCC 66; 2011 (5) ALD(SC) 149; 2011 (5) AWC 5013; 2011 (3) CLT(SC) 217; 2011 (3) CompLJ 666; 2011 (4) CTC 574; 2011 (8) MLJ(SC) 560; 2012 (1) RCR(Civil) 305; 2011(7) SCALE 747; [2011] 9 S.C.R. 382

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration & Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 11 - Unregistered contract - Arbitration clause - Enforceability - Appellant entered into a sale agreement with the respondent for the acquisition of two tea estates - Appellant, subsequently, entered into a lease agreement with the respondent whereby respondent granted a lease to the appellant with respect to the same subject property for 30 years - Appellant invested huge sums for improving the tea estates, however, respondent abruptly evicted the appellant from the subject property and took over the management - Appellant invoked arbitration clause and calling upon the respondent to refer the matter to arbitration u/cl. 35 of the agreement - Since respondent failed to comply with the appellant's request, appellant filed an application u/s. 11 of the Act before the HC for appointment of an arbitrator - Chief Justice of HC, by impugned order, dismissed appellant's application holding that the lease deed was compulsorily registrable u/s. 17 of the Registration Act, 1908 and s. 106 of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882; and as the lease deed was not registered, no term in the said lease deed could be relied upon for any purpose and therefore cl. 35 could not be relied upon for seeking reference to arbitration - HC also held that the arbitration agreement contained in cl. 35 could not be termed as a collateral transaction, and therefore, the proviso to s. 49 of the Registration Act would not assist the appellant - Whether an arbitration agreement contained in an unregistered (but compulsorily registrable) instrument was valid and enforceable - Held, when a contract contained an arbitration agreement, it was a collateral term relating to the resolution of disputes, unrelated to the performance of the contract - It was as if two contracts; one in regard to the substantive terms of the main contract and the other relating to resolution of disputes; had been rolled into one, for purposes of convenience - An arbitration clause was therefore an agreement independent of the other terms of the contract or the instrument - Resultantly, even if the contract or its performance was terminated or came to an end on account of repudiation, frustration or breach of contract, the arbitration agreement would survive for the purpose of resolution of disputes arising under or in connection with the contract - Hence, even if a deed of transfer of immovable property was challenged as not valid or enforceable, the arbitration agreement would remain unaffected for the purpose of resolution of disputes arising with reference to the deed of transfer - Further, an arbitration agreement did not require registration under the Registration Act - Therefore, having regard to the proviso to s. 49 of Registration Act r/w s. 16(1)(a) of the Act, an arbitration agreement in an unregistered but compulsorily registrable document could be acted upon and enforced for the purpose of dispute resolution by arbitration - Appeal allowed.

(B) Arbitration & ADR - Practice & Procedure - Arbitration & Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 11 - Indian Stamp Act, 1899, ss. 35 - Unregistered arbitration agreement - Validity - Whether an arbitration agreement in an unregistered instrument which was not duly stamped, was valid and enforceable - Held, court should, before admitting any document into evidence or acting upon such unregistered document, examine whether the instrument/document was duly stamped and whether it was an instrument which was compulsorily registrable - If the document was found to be not duly stamped, s. 35 of the 1899 Act barred the said document being acted upon including the arbitration clause therein - Court should then proceed to impound the document u/s. 33 of the 1899 Act and follow the procedure u/ss. 35 and 38 of the 1899 Act - If the document was found to be duly stamped, or if the deficit stamp duty and penalty was paid, either before the Court or before the Collector (as contemplated in s. 35 or 40 of the 1899 Act), and the defect with reference to deficit stamp was cured, the court should treat the document as duly stamped - Once the document was found to be duly stamped, the court should proceed to consider whether the document was compulsorily registrable - If the document was found to be not compulsorily registrable, the court should act upon the arbitration agreement, without any impediment - If the document was not registered, but was compulsorily registrable, having regard to s. 16(1)(a) of the 1996 Act, the court should de-link the arbitration agreement from the main document, as an agreement independent of the other terms of the document, even if the document itself should in any way affect the property or should not be received as evidence of any transaction affecting such property - Where the document was compulsorily registrable, but was not registered, but the arbitration agreement was valid and separable, what was required to be borne in mind was that the arbitrator appointed in such a matter should not rely upon the unregistered instrument except for two purposes, that is: (a) as evidence of contract in a claim for specific performance; and (b) as evidence of any collateral transaction which did not require registration - Appeal allowed.

(C) Arbitration & ADR - Practice & Procedure - Arbitration & Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 11 - Unregistered contract - Arbitration clause - Appointment of arbitrator - Whether there was an arbitration agreement between the appellant and respondent and whether an arbitrator should be appointed - Held, appellant sought arbitration in regard to the following three distinct disputes: (a) for enforcing an alleged agreement of sale of two tea estates, (b) for enforcing the lease for thirty years; and (c) for recovery of amounts spent by it in regard to the estates on the assumption that it was entitled to purchase the property or at least have a lease of 30 years - It was clear from the appellant's petition that the alleged agreement of sale was entered prior to the lease deed and there was no arbitration agreement in regard to such agreement of sale - When admittedly there was no arbitration agreement in regard to the alleged agreement of sale, the appellant could not seek arbitration with reference to any dispute regarding such agreement of sale, whether it was for performance or for damages for breach or any other relief arising out of or with reference to the agreement of sale - Since the lease deed was not registered, the arbitrator could not rely upon the lease deed or any term thereof and the lease deed should not affect the immovable property which was the subject matter of the lease nor be received as evidence of any transaction affecting such property - Hence, the arbitrator would not be able to entertain any claim for enforcement of the lease - Impugned HC order was set aside and the matter was remitted to the Chief Justice of HC to first decide the issue of stamp duty, and if the document was duly stamped, then appoint an arbitrator in accordance with law - Appeal allowed.
