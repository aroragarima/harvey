Â Collector of Monghyr and Others v Keshav Prasad Goenka and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 March 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, K. Subba Rao, J.R. Madholkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 371; AIR 1962 SC 1694; 1962 BLJR 863; 1963 (42) ILR(Pat) 112; [1963] 1 S.C.R. 98

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Bihar Private Irrigation Works Act, 1922, ss. 3, 5 A - (A) Whether it is mandatory for a Collector to record reasons u/s. 5A and failure to do would affect legality of order? - Question whether circumstances recited in s. 5A(1) exist or not is entirely for Collector to decide in his discretion; if reasons recorded by him were totally irrelevant as a justification for considering that an emergency had arisen or for dispensing with notice and enquiry u/ss. 3 to 5, exercise of power u/s. 5A would be void as not justified by statute - Held, requirement that Collector should record his reasons for order made is mandatory and this requirement having not been complied with in present cases, Collector's order was therefore, null and void - (B) Whether respondents were persons who had sustained a loss by reason of a thing done by Collector and entitled to compensation u/s. 5B? - Further held, 'loss' for which section provides compensation is that directly arising from doing of work, ie, loss sustained by third parties and not liability to make apportioned cost u/ss. 11 and 12 for very basis of liability under these provisions is that person from whom payments are demanded has benefited by work being done in that he being under an obligation to effect repairs, that obligation was discharged by work done at instance of Collector - (C) Whether respondents must be deemed to have acquiesced in orders passed u/s. 5A by not objecting to them immediately and were now estopped from contending them to be void having obtained a benefit by repair of irrigation? - Further held, there is, before completion of work, no provision in statute for landlord to make his representations, even assuming that he is shown to have knowledge of passing of order, thus argument on basis of failure to object cannot be sustained - (D) Whether High Court had no jurisdiction to afford respondents relief u/art. 226 of Constitution? - Further held, respondents were not seeking to set aside several orders passed by Collector u/s. 5A but only demands based on them on ground of illegality and HC had certainly jurisdiction to direct that these demands be quashed - Appeals dismissed.
