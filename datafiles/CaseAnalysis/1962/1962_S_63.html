Â Raj Rajendra Sardar Maloji Marsingh Rao Shitole v Sri Shankar Saran and Others

Supreme Court of India

30 April 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

J.L. Kapur, A.K. Sarkar, K.C. Das Gupta, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 183; AIR 1962 SC 1737; 1962 (2) ILR(All) 991; 1963 MahLJ 921; 1963 MPLJ 809; [1963] 2 S.C.R. 577

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Indian Independence Act, 1947, Decree For Execution

Summary: Constitution - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Government of India Act, 1935 - Indian Independence Act, 1947 - Pre independence rules - Foreign decree - Execution - Challenged - Maintainability - Appellant filed a suit for recovery of money against respondents at Gwalior in 1947 - Respondents were residents of Uttar Pradesh and did not appear before court in Nov 1948 - Gwalior court passed ex- parte decree - In Sept 1951 Gwalior court transferred decree for execution to Allahabad Civil Court - Appellant filed an application for execution of decree before Allahabad Civil Court - Respondents contented that appeal was not executable at Allahabad court as it was a foreign court before independence and therefore decree passed and execution application were not maintainable - Decree holder/Appellant and respondents/judgment debtors appealed before SC - Hence, instant appeal - Whether decree passed was a decree of a foreign court and if Gwalior Court's order to transfer decree was valid for execution at Allahabad Court u/ss. 43, 44 of CPC and if respondents could take an objection to execution of decree on the ground that it was an absolute nullity, being decree of a foreign Court? - Held, constitutional changes did not affect any change in status or nationality of Gwalior Court till after passing of decree of 1948 and there being no specific provision to the contrary of changes in CPC left decree unaffected - The United State (Madhya Bharat) had not become a part of Dominion of India despite various constitutional documents executed by rulers of Indian States - On the day when decree was passed Gwalior court was considered foreign court within the meaning of s.2(5) of CPC -It was stated that respondents were not subjects of Gwalior and they were not residents in Gwalior at the time when suit was filed - Respondents were not temporary when process was served upon them and did not select forum which passed decree against them - Respondents did not voluntarily appear before the court and they had not contracted to submit to jurisdiction of CPC which was a different court from that which passed decree under local code - It was not a court which passed decree within meaning of ss. 37 to 42 of CPC which dealt with execution of decree passed by courts governed by Indian Code - Decree could not be executed under provisions of s. 43 of CPC at any time - After its adaptation in 1950, s. 43 applied to a 'decree passed by a civil court in a Part B state' - There were no Part B state at the time when decree was passed and those words could not be read as 'a decree passed by a civil court in what became a Part B State' nor could decree be executed u/s. 44 as that section was also inapplicable to decree passed - Art. 261 (3) of constitution which provides that final judgments or orders of Civil Courts in any part of territory of India shall be capable of execution anywhere within that territory was inapplicable to decree of Gwalior court as provision was prospective and not retrospective - Decree passed by Gwalior Court was not a foreign decree and Allahabad court had no power to execute it either u/s. 38 nor under ss. 43 , 44 of CPC - s. 38 provided that a decree may be executed either by court which passed it or by court to which it was sent for execution - Allahabad Court was not the court which passed the decree - S. 39 empowers court which passed decree to transfer it for execution to another court - The word 'court' in the phrase 'court which passed the decree' in s. 39 contemplated only courts governed by CPC - Gwalior court which was governed by Gwalior Code when it passed decree had a distinct identity from the court at Gwalior after it came to be governed by Indian Code - Court which transferred decree was not the court which passed decree and order of transfer and therefore order was not valid - s.43 of CPC provided for execution of decrees passed by civil courts in places where CPC did not extend - Decree of Gwalior Court did not fall within s.43 of CPC as it stood before the Constitution - After adaptation in 1950 section was applied to a decree passed 'by a civil court in a Part B State' - Those words could not be read as 'by a civil court in an Indian state which had later been included in a Part B State' - Gwalior court which passed decree was not a civil court in a Part B state - s.44 was equally inapplicable to the decree - section after adaptation in 1950 applied only to decrees of revenue courts - Before adaptation it could have been applied only if there was a notification issued by Govt. of Uttar Pradesh but no such notification was issued - Appeal Dismissed.
