Krishna Bus Service Limited v Smt. Mangli and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 January 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 563; (1976) 1 SCC 793; 1976 ACJ 183; AIR 1976 SC 700; [1976] 3 S.C.R. 178

Case Digest

Subject: Tort

Keywords: Negligent

Summary: Tort - Negligence - Res ipsa loquitur - Deceased while traveling in bus belonging to defendant no. 1 and driven by defendant no. 3 died when bus overturned - Plaintiffs, who were legal heirs of deceased, instituted suit against defendants for damages alleging negligence of defendant no.1 and 3 - Trial Court dismissed suit on ground that plaintiffs failed to prove accident was due to rash or negligent driving by defendant no. 3 - However, on appeal, HC held that accident was due to negligence attributable to defendant no. 3 and awarded decree for Rs.21,600 as damages against defendant nos. 1 and 3 - Hence, instant appeal by defendant no. 1 - Whether accident was caused due to the negligence of defendant no. 1 or both defendant nos. 1 and 3.

Held, evidence rendered by passenger, local man, motor mechanic, i.e., PWs 5, 6 and 8 respectively, was reliable and cogent to establish facts which, in their totality, unerringly point to the conclusion that the accident was due to the negligence of the driver, defendant no. 3. Further, buses in sound road worthy condition, driven with ordinary care, do not normally overturn. It would be for the driver who had special knowledge of relevant facts to explain why the vehicle overturned. Further, maxim res ipsa loquitur would be attracted and defendant nos. 1 and 3 failed to rebut the presumption of negligence that arose from the manifest circumstances of the case. Hence, defendant no. 1 is fully liable for the negligent act of its employee, defendant no. 3 and the injury resulting therefrom. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Where in an action for negligence the thing causing fatal injury to the deceased and consequent pecuniary loss to the plaintiff, is shown to be under the management of the defendant or his servants and the accident is such that in the ordinary course of events does not happen, if those who have the management use proper care, that affords reasonable evidence, in the absence of explanation by the defendants, that the accident arose from want of care.
