Â Kuldip Nayar v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

22 August 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.K. Sabharwal, K.G. Balakrishnan, S.H. Kapadia, C.K. Thakker, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 432; (2006) 7 SCC 1; AIR 2006 SC 3127; JT 2006 (8) SC 1; 2006(8) SCALE 257; [2006] Supp5 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election

Keywords: Council Of States, Qualification For Membership, Legislative Councils, House Of The People, Government of India Act, 1919, British North America Act, 1867, Constitution (Fifty-Second Amendment) Act, 1985, Electorate, Ordinarily Resident, Registration of Electors Rules, 1960, Purity, Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1951, Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2003, Sanctity, Election Laws(Amendment)Act, 2003

Summary: [A.] Constitution - Election - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 136, 143, 19(1)(a), 212, 302, 32, 327, 379, 80, 80(4), 84 - Representation Of People Act, 1951, ss. 128, 2(e), 3, 5(c), 59, 94 - Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956 - Representation of People Act, 1950, ss. 14, 16, 20, 20(7) - Government of India Act, 1935 - Election Laws (Amendment) Act, 2003 - Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, s. 15, s. 26 - Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2003 - Registration of Electors Rules, 1960 - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, rr. 23(3), 23(5)(a), 23(5)(b), 31(2), 38(4), 39(1), 39(5), 39(6), 39(8) - Challenge to amendments made in Representation of People Act, 1951 - Representation of People (Amendment) Act 40 of 2003 by which requirement of 'domicile' in State Concerned for getting elected to Council of States is deleted - Plea that it violates principle of Federalism, a basic structure of Constitution - Issue relates to content and significance of word 'domicile' and importance of concept of 'secrecy' in voting under constitutional scheme - Held, residence is not constitutional requirement and, therefore, question of violation of basic structure does not arise - Representative of State is person chosen by electors who can be any person who, in opinion of electors, is fit to represent them - Constitution, has no requirement that a person chosen to represent a State in Council of States must necessarily be a voter in that State itself - Ultimate choice and decision is always that of State Legislatures - If they decide to elect a person who is not ordinarily a resident of State they would do so with full knowledge of all circumstances and it would be their decision as to who should be representative of their State - Open voting eradicates evil of cross-voting by electors who have been elected to Assembly of particular State on basis of party nomination cannot be lightly brushed aside - Impugned amendment does not infringe any Constitutional provision - It cannot be found to be violative of any Fundamental Rights - Impugned legislation cannot be struck down as unconstitutional - Petitions dismissed.
