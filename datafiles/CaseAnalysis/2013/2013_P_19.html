Balmer Lawrie & Co. Ltd. & Ors. v Partha Sarathi Sen Roy & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

20 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 98; (2013) 8 SCC 345; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 926; 2013 (3) AWC 2557; 2013 (1) CLT(SC) 363; [2013] 178 Comp Cas 297; 2013 (4) CompLJ 51; JT 2013 (3) SC 111; 2013(2) SCALE 696; [2013] 4 S.C.R. 118

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution

Keywords: Conduct, Discipline and Review Rules, Service Condition Rules

Summary: Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14 - Manual of Officers in 1976 - Service Condition Rules - Termination - Unconscionable clause - Justifiability - Respondent joined appellant/company as a Management Trainee, and was later on confirmed vide order dt. 1-6-1976 as an officer in Grade-III - Later, his services were terminated vide order dt. 27-2-1981, in view of cl. 11(a) of letter of appointment - Respondent filed writ petition in HC challenging said termination order - Single Judge dismissed petition holding that appellant was neither a State, nor any other authority within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution - Aggrieved, respondent filed an appeal against said judgment of Single Judge - Meanwhile, another writ petition was decided by another Single Judge of same HC, holding that appellant was infact a State within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution - Appellant preferred an appeal against said judgment - Thus, matter was referred to a third Judge, who held appellant to be a State within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution, and directed that matter be placed before an appropriate bench for decision of writ petitions on merits - Hence, instant appeal - Whether appellant was in fact, a State within meaning of art. 12 of Constitution - Held, HC had not dealt with issue on merits with respect to termination of services of respondents - However, considering fact that such termination took place several decades ago, and litigation in respect of same remained pending not only before HC, but also before SC, it was desirable that dispute come to quietus - Therefore, SC could not approve 'hire and fire' policy adopted by appellant, and terms and conditions incorporated in the 1976 manual, could not be held to be justifiable, and same being arbitrary, could not be enforced - Thus, cl. 11 of appointment letter was unconscionable clause, and thus the Rules were held to be violative of art. 14 of Constitution to such extent - Hence, contract of employment was held to be void to such extent - Instances of such type of legislation were usury laws, debt relief laws and laws regulating hours of work and conditions of service of workmen and their unfair discharge from service, as also control orders directing a party to sell a particular essential commodity to another - Thus, respondent was entitled to receive 60% of arrears of wages due to him, from date of his termination to date of his superannuation - Appeal disposed of.
