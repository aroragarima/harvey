M/S Northern Plastics Ltd. v Hindustan Photo Films Mfg.Co. Ltd

Supreme Court of India

20 February 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Majmudar, S.P. Bharucha

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 1018; (1997) 4 SCC 452; 1997 (66) DLT 485; 1997 (91) E.L.T. 502; JT 1997 (3) SC 101; 1997 (19) RLT 5; 1997(2) SCALE 263; [1997] 2 S.C.R. 252; 1997 (2) Supreme 365

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Importation, Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporation Act, 1949, Finance Act, 1984, Customs and Excise Revenues Appellate Tribunal Act, 1986, Bombay Cinemas Regulation Act, 1953

Summary: Customs - Practice & Procedure - Customs Act, 1962, s.129-A - Locus standi - Scope - Appellant filed instant appeals against impugned order dt.9-3-1990 passed by HC in 2 writ petitions filed by respondent no. 1 that respondents had locus standi - Appellant contended that DB had patently erred in taking view in the impugned common judgment that respondent no. 1 as well as Industries Ministry of Union of India were 'aggrieved person' within meaning of s. 129-A of the Act - Whether first respondents in these Civil writ appeals could be said to be 'persons aggrieved' within the meaning of s. 129-A of the Act - Held, wider concept of locus standi in PIL moved before SC u/art. 32 of the Constitution which itself was a fundamental right or u/art. 226 before HC which also offered a constitutional remedy could not be imported for deciding the right of appeal under statutory provisions contained in the Act - Whether any right of appeal was conferred on anyone against orders passed under the Act in the hierarchy of proceedings before authorities had to be judged from statutory settings of the Act and not before them - Therefore, HC in the impugned judgment had erred in drawing the analogy from the more elastic concept of locus standi u/art. 32 of constitution evolved by SC by its decisions on the subject - For judging the competence and locus standi of Union of India or respondents for moving appeals before Tribunal against order of Additional Collector of Customs passed u/s. 122 of the Act the answer should be found from within the four corners of the Act itself - Appellate Tribunal was a creature of statute and derived its jurisdiction and powers only from statute creating it and not outside the same - Then, s. 129-A dealing with 'Appeals to the Appellate Tribunal' - Further, in absence of any special statutory provision for protecting the interest of Government concerns or public sector undertakings no statutory locus standi could be called out in their favour on the express language of the relevant provisions of the Act - Therefore, respondent was a mere business rival operating in the same commercial field and carrying on the same commercial activities as appellant - Its locus standi to challenge the order of Additional Collector of Customs in favour of appellant, therefore got squarely ruled out by decision of SC in Jasbhai Motibhai Desai v. Roshan Kumar Haji Bashir Ahmed and others, 1975 Indlaw SC 372 - Appeal filed by respondent before Tribunal also should be treated to be incompetent and could not be covered by the sweep of s. 129-A(1) of the Act - HC had committed a patent error of law in taking the view that concerned writ petitioners before it had sufficient locus standi to prefer appeals before Tribunal - Decision of Tribunal holding that Respondents had no such locus standi was perfectly justified on the scheme of the Act and it was wrongly set aside by HC - Consequently appeals would be required to be allowed - Common impugned order of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
