State Of Rajasthan v Islam

Supreme Court of India

24 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, Deepak Verma

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 373; (2011) 6 SCC 343; AIR 2011 SC 2317; 2011 CRLJ 3110; 2011 (3) JCC 1927; JT 2011 (6) SC 452; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 953; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 73; 2011 (3) RLW 1971; 2011(6) SCALE 389; [2011] 6 S.C.R. 988

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 304 - Murder - Acquittal - Sustainability - Respondent accused allegedly murdered the deceased - Trial Court convicted and sentenced the respondent u/s. 302 IPC - HC, however, in appeal, set aside the respondent's conviction u/s. 302 and converted it u/s. 304 Part II IPC considering that he had already undergone detention for more than six years - Hence the instant appeal by State - Whether the order of acquittal recorded by the HC was sustainable - Held, HC while converting the conviction of the respondent from s. 302 IPC to s. 304 Part-II held that the relations between respondent and the deceased were cordial and only one blow was caused by respondent on the head of the deceased and that proved fatal - HC further said that the injury inflicted by respondent was not pre-meditated and the respondent did not take any undue advantage nor acted in a cruel manner and as such, the case of respondent was covered by Explanation IV appended to s. 300 IPC and could only be held guilty u/s. 304 Part II IPC - However, It should not be said that respondent had no intention to kill the deceased - It may be true that initially there was no pre-mediation or intention of the respondent but it was well settled that intention could develop on the spot and in the instant case, there was some amount of pre-meditation on the part of respondent when he had gone to his house and came back to the place of occurrence armed with a deadly weapon and in furtherance of that intention struck the deceased with that weapon repeatedly and at a vital part of his body - In the background of that consistent evidence against respondent, conversion of the conviction of respondent from s. 302 IPC to s. 304 Part II IPC should not be sustained and the entire approach of the HC was misconceived - Finding of the HC that the act of the respondent was coming under the fourth Exception could not be sustained at all - It was clear that respondent did not strike the deceased at the first instance, but he struck him after an interval of time since he left the place of occurrence, went to his home and then came back armed with deadly weapon - In order to bring a case under Exception (4) to s. 300 IPC, the evidence should show that the accused acted without any pre-mediation and in a heat of passion and without having taken undue advantage and he had not acted in a cruel or unusual manner - Every one of those circumstances was required to be proved to attract Exception (4) to s. 300 IPC and it was not sufficient to prove only some of them - In the facts of the instant case, none of the said ingredients had been proved from the evidence to bring the case under Exception (4) to s. 300 IPC - HC's finding to the contrary was totally against the evidence on record - Hence impugned HC judgment of acquittal was set aside and Trial Court order of conviction was restored - Appeal allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Murder - Order of acquittal by HC - Interference by SC - Scope - Whether SC should interfere in exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 136 of the Constitution when an order of acquittal was granted by the HC and the accused had suffered imprisonment for 6 years - Held, no such absolute proposition in law available with regard to the order of acquittal by HC and its interference by SC - When SC exercised its jurisdiction u/art. 136, it definitely exercised a discretionary jurisdiction but such discretionary jurisdiction was required to be exercised in order to ensure that there was no miscarriage of justice - If the consideration by the HC was misconceived and perverse, there was nothing in law which prevented SC from exercising its jurisdiction u/art. 136 against an order of acquittal when such acquittal could not be sustained at all, in view of the evidence of record - Further, in reversing an acquittal, SC kept in mind that presumption of innocence in favour of the accused was fortified by an order of acquittal and if the view of the HC was reasonable and founded on materials on record, SC should not interfere with - However, if SC was of the opinion that the acquittal was not based on a reasonable view, then it may review the entire material and there would be no limitation on SC's jurisdiction u/art. 136 to come to a just decision quashing the acquittal - Appeal allowed.
