Â Dunlop Rubber Company v Workmen

Supreme Court of India

10 November 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, S.M. Sikri, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, R.S. Bachawat, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 199; AIR 1965 SC 1392; 1966 (12) FLR 372; 1965 (1) LLJ 426; [1965] 2 S.C.R. 139

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Service - Misconduct - Dismissal from service - Revoked by Tribunal - - Sustainability - Appellant filed appeal against judgement of Tribunal whereby punishment of dismissal from service awarded to 12 workmen/respondents by appellant was set aside and awarded 25 percent back wages - Punishment was awarded because respondents were found to be deliberately going slow with work allotted - Hence instant appeal - Whether decision of Tribunal was sustainable - Held, two cls. of Standing Order 10, dealt with insubordination and inter alia with going slow - It was contended that the words "go slow" did not figure in this charge as they did in the charges against respondents in one of the mill i.e., X Mill - It was to be remembered that these respondents were expressly warned that from 15-1-1960 they were going slow and that 'go slow' action was misconduct u/cl. 10(XVI) of the appellant's Standing Orders for Operators - No doubt the person, who drew up the charge, had not mentioned go slow in these charges as he had done in the charges framed against the respondents of the Banbury Mill, but it was nevertheless clear that these charges referred to go slow and indeed the respondents in their replies to the charge denied that they were going slow - It might be pointed out that the person who drew the charge had stated before the Enquiry Officer that the charge was 'go slow' - Log books also showed that from 12-1-1960 remark was 'slow work' was found against another mill i.e., Dual Auto Mills - It was clearly established by the records produced that instead of 17 batches 15 batches or less were turned out at each shift - It proved that there was deliberate 'go slow' no sooner one of the dismissed respondents left on leave and the Dual Auto Mills came into the exclusive hands of one Union - Union thought that the opportunity was too good to be wasted to force their demand for increase of wages by the tactics of 'go slow' - Explanation of the respondents that the mixture received from the Banbury Mill was too cold and had to be reheated before it could be processed in the Dual Auto Mills was false - They attributed the cooling of the mixture to the working of new machine called the festooner from 12-1-1960 - It was clear that the machine was tried for three months before it was put into operation and had worked for three months prior to 12-1-1960 and so such complaint had been made by the respondents - It was possible that the Banbury Mill operators, who were also suspended and dismissed, were cooling the mixture unduly by means of their blower to delay operation - But whether the Banbury Mill cooled it and the Dual Auto Mills were required to reheat it or the Dual Auto Mills delayed the operations, it was clear that the motivating force behind it was the action of Union to force the hands of appellant in support of their demands - It was sufficient to say that after the new respondents had got trained in the working of the Dual Auto Mills the production again reached the same number of batches and after the figure was even better though the festooner continued in operation - SC was satisfied that the respondents were going slow from 12-1-1960, that the charge of 'go slow' was incorporated in the charge-sheet read with the warning letter and that it was fully substantiated - That amounted to misconduct under Standing Order No. 10 and was not minor offence as contended before SC by respondents - Minor offences deal with conduct of very different kind - Appeal allowed.
