Case Analysis

Bench

Summary: T.S. Thakur, J.

(A) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Entry of goods - Taxability restrictions - Justifiability - States in exercise of their legislative powers under Entry 52 of List II of Constitution enacted laws that provide for levy of a tax on the entry of goods into local areas comprising States - Aggreived, assesses/dealers questioned filed writ petitions before HCs on ground that the same were violative of Constitutionally recognised right to free trade commerce and intercourse guaranteed u/art. 301 of Constitution - DB dismissed the said petitions on ground that the levy was compensatory in character and outside the purview of art. 301 of Constitution - Hence, instant appeals - Whether the levy of a non-discriminatory tax per se constitute infraction of art. 301 of Constitution.

Held, taxes whether high or low do not constitute restrictions on the freedom of trade and commerce. SC held so based on textual understanding of the provisions of Part XIII of Constitution which was matched by contextual interpretation. Mere fact that a tax casts a heavy burden was no reason for holding that it was a restriction on the freedom of trade and commerce. Any such excessive tax burden might be open to challenge under Part III of Constitution but the extent of burden would not by itself justify the levy being struck down as a restriction contrary to art. 301 of Constitution. Further, levy of taxes was both an attribute of sovereignty and an unavoidable necessity. No responsible govt. could do without levying and collecting taxes for it was only through taxes that govts. were run and objectives of general public good achieved. The conceptual or juristic basis underlying the need for taxation has not been disputed by counsel for dealers and in SC opinion, rightly so. That taxation was essential for fulfilling the needs of govt. was even otherwise well-settled. Thus, SC declare that a non-discriminatory tax does not per se constitute a restriction on the right to free trade, commerce and intercourse guaranteed u/art. 301 of Constitution. Order accordingly.

(B) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Taxing statute - Non fiscal - Permissability - Whether the Constitutional validity of impugned legislations has to be tested by reference to both arts. 304(a) and 304(b) of Constitution.

Held, art. 304(b) of Constitution does not deal with taxes as restrictions. The restrictions referred to in art. 304(b) of Constitution were non-fiscal in nature. Constitutional validity of any taxing statute had to be tested only on the anvil of art. 304(a) of Constitution and if the law was found to be non-discriminatory, it could be declared to be constitutionally valid without the legislation having to go through the test or the process envisaged by art. 304(b) of Constitution. However, should the statute fail the test of non-discrimination u/art. 304(a) of Constitution it should be struck down for the same cannot be sustained even if it had gone through the process stipulated by art. 304(b) of Constitution. That was because what is constitutionally impermissible in terms of art. 304(a) of Constitution cannot be validated and sanctioned through the medium of art. 304(b) of Constitution. Suffice it to say that a fiscal statute should be open to challenge only u/art. 304(a) of Constitution without being subjected to the test of art. 304(b) of Constitution either in terms of the existence of public interest or reasonableness of the levy. Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - State enactments - Exemption - Whether impugned State enactments violate art. 304(a) of Constitution.

Held, SC noted that the word discrimination was not used in art. 14 of Constitution as it was used in arts. 16, 303 and 304(a) of Constitution. The use of the word in 304(a) of Cosntitution involved an element of "intentional and unfavorable bias". So long as there was no such bias evident from the measure adopted by State, mere grant of exemption or incentives aimed at supporting local industries in their growth, development and progress did not constitute discrimination. SC accept the contention of State that so long as the intention behind the grant of exemption/adjustment/credit was to equalize the fall of fiscal burden on the goods from within State and those from outside State such exemption or set off would not amount to hostile discrimination offensive to art. 304(a) of Constitution. SC leave open for examination by regular benches hearing the matters whether impugned enactment achieve the object of such equalization or lead to a situation that exposes goods from outside tate to suffer any disadvantage vis-a-vis those produced or manufactured in taxing State. Order accordingly.

Shiva Kirti Singh, J.

(D) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Tax imposed - Constitutionality - Whether Entry Tax levied by States violative of art. 301 of Constitution and in particular have the impugned State enactments relating to entry tax to be tested with reference to both arts. 304(a) and 304(b) of Constitution for determining their validity.

Held, States felt the need to exercise their legislative power to impose taxes even for general welfare measures and police duties. It became more and more difficult to justify such tax as compensatory tax and such attempts brought excessive strain on the very concept of regulatory and compensatory tax. Limitation on such power should be explicit in Constitution. For safeguarding freedom of trade and commerce, such limitation was to be found only in art. 304(a) of Part XIII of Constitution. Since, in the matter of levy of taxes the compensatory theory was no more relevant, State Legislatures were free to exercise their taxing powers without the need of declaring and showing that taxes imposed by them on outside goods were for the benefit of concerned traders or manufacturers. But such tax should be in essence, non-discriminatory, both, in the ultimate tax burden and in machinery provisions. To muster compliance with Part XIII of Constitution, the tax shouldpass the twin tests embodied in art. 304(a) of Constitution. Similar goods produced locally should also be subjected to similar tax and such State action should not attract the vice of discrimination between the two varieties of goods. Thus, it deserve to be treated as obiter or interpreted to mean such taxes which impose an impediment to the free flow of trade, commerce and intercourse by creating discriminatory tariff wall/trade. For Part XIII of Constitution there could be no real impediment through tax unless the so called wall or barrier was one of hostile discrimination between local goods and outside goods. Order accordingly.

N. V. Ramana, J.

(E) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Movement of goods - Tax levied - Barred - Whether the Entry Tax could be termed a tax on the movement of goods when there was no bar to the entry of goods at State border or when it passes through a local area within which they were not sold, used or consumed.

Held, power of State to levy any tax on goods imported was specifically saved and declared in the final clause and would be impermissible to test a law imposing entry tax with reference to art. 304(b) of Constitution. Art. 304(a) of Constitution does not bar or limit State power to levy non-discriminatory taxes on the goods imported from other States. What was restricted is levy of discriminatory tax only, so to say, similar goods manufactured or produced in that State were subjected to tax, so as not to discriminate between the goods imported and goods manufactured or produced in State. Part XIII of Constitution does not contemplate tax laws within its ambit except to the extent of art. 304(a) of Constitution. Arts. 304 (a) and (b) of Constitution were disjunctive. Further, restrictions mentioned u/art. 304(b) of Constitution do not include tax. It was not correct to say that since goods being taxed were not produced in State the power to levy a tax gets obliterated, that art. 304(a) of Constitution does not bar levy of tax if the goods were not manufactured or produced within State. Art. 304(a) of Constitution protects from discrimination (for protectionism) and not mere differentiation. Order accordingly.

R. Banumathi J.

(F) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Entry tax levied - Compensation - Validity - What were the tests for determining whether the tax or levy was compensatory in nature.

Held, proposition that taxes do not constitute a restriction on the freedom of trade and commerce (save and except for a discriminatory tax which violates art. 304(a) of Constitution) does not reflect a valid constitutional principle. Art. 304(a) of Constitution does not constitute the entire universe of taxation for the purpose of Part XIII of Constitution. Neither of the two positions which lie at the extreme ends of spectrum was valid at one end is the position that all taxes are restrictions and at the other end, is the position that no tax (except a discriminatory tax on goods) is a restriction. The burden to establish that was on the person who seeks to assail the validity of a particular tax on the ground that it amounts to a restriction on the freedom guaranteed by art. 301 of Constitution. Unless such threshold was crossed, the proviso to art. 304(b) of Constitution would have no application for it was only when there was a restriction that the question of its reasonableness could arise. Thus, art. 304(a) of Constitution does not fetter State legislatures from ensuring an equality of tax burden between goods that were imported from other States and goods manufactured or produced within. While enacting entry tax legislation referable to Entry 52 of List II of Constitution, it was permissible for State legislature to have regard to the equalisation of tax burdens between goods imported from other States and goods manufactured or produced within. However, whether the scheme of exemptions and set offs has achieved an equalisation of tax burdens as between goods domestic to a State and those imported from other States was an issue to be considered in each case having due regard to the provisions of State legislation. Therefore, there could be no a priori assumption that an entry tax constitutes a restriction or impediment to trade and commerce. Order accordingly.

Ashok Bhushan, J.

(G) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Non-discrimiantory tax - Approval - Whether non-discriminatory indirect State tax which was capable of being passed on and was passed on by traders to the consumers infringes art. 301 of Constitution.

Held, levy of a non-discriminatory tax might constitute infraction of art. 301 of Constitution if it impedes the freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse. All taxes which contain restrictions to trade, commerce and intercourse, discriminatory or non-discriminatory infringe art. 301 of Constitution unless they were saved u/arts. 302 and 304 of Constitution. The compensatory tax theory as judicially evolved in Automobile Transport was not compatible to Constitutional scheme as delineated by Part XIII of Constitution. The compensatory taxes were out of the reach of art. 301 of Constitution cannot be approved. The nature and content of taxation at best might throw light on the aspect as to whether it contains restriction on freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse. Framers of Constitution have provided for all exceptions under which freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse guaranteed u/art. 301 of Constitution could be overridden, the compensatory tax not being included as one of the exceptions, the same cannot be added as an exception by any judicial interpretation. The compensatory tax theory brings dichotomy which was inconsistent with the language employed in art. 301 of Constitution. Order accordingly.

(H) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 304(a), 304(b) - Trade tax - Violation - Whether a tax on goods within the State which directly impedes the trade and violates art. 301 of Constitution could be saved by reference to art. 304 of Constitution alone or could be saved by any other article.

Held, law made by State Legislature complying cl. (a) of art. 304 of Constitution and not containing any restriction on the freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse need not comply art. 304(b) of Constitution. However, a law even though complies with art. 304(a) of Constitution but contains restrictions on freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse has to be routed through proviso to cl.(b) of art. 304 of Constitution. The compliance of art. 304(b) of Constitution proviso whether required or not should depend on the nature and content of State legislation. Part XIII (including art. 301) of Constitution to which legislative power of State was subject, does not have effect of denuding any sovereign power of State or effecting the federal structure of Constitution. There was no question of affecting the separation of power between the Legislature and judiciary on the ground that levy of taxes u/art. 304(b) of Constitution which contains restriction to the freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse have to be routed through President of country as per Constitutional scheme. Constitution contains large number of provisions including art. 304(b) of Constitution where a State legislation was subject to Presidential sanction which provisions were in accordance with the Constitutional scheme and does not affect the separation of power between Legislature and judiciary. Art. 304(b) of Constitution enables State Legislature to frame legislations containing restriction on freedom of trade, commerce and intercourse after routing the legislation through proviso to art. 304(b) of Constitution. Order accordingly.
