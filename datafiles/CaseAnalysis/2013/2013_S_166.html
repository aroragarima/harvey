Som Raj @ Soma v State Of H.P.

Supreme Court of India

22 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

J. S. KHEHAR, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 112; (2013) 14 SCC 246; AIR 2013 SC 1649; 2013 CRLJ 2237; JT 2013 (3) SC 387; 2013 (2) MLJ(Crl) 92; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 211; 2013(2) SCALE 774; [2013] 4 S.C.R. 433

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Agricultural Implement

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant/accused allegedly assaulted deceased with 'darat' (Traditional agricultural implement) on back side of the head of deceased - Trial Court convicted appellant for offence punishable u/s. 302 of IPC - On appeal, HC affirmed the same - Hence instant appeal - Appellant contended that even if the singular fatal blow was taken to have been inflicted by him, he could only be punished for the offence u/s. 304 Part-II of IPC, and not for the offence of murder u/s. 302 of IPC - Further, there was no premeditation to commit the offence on the date of occurrence and evidence produced by prosecution did not reveal any prior enmity between appellant and deceased - Therefore, the action should be treated as 'culpable homicide not amounting to murder' - Whether appellant could be stated to have intentionally caused such bodily injury to the deceased, as he knew was so imminently dangerous, that it would in all probability cause his death - Held, it was apparent from the factual narration of witnesses produced by prosecution that appellant was not carrying 'darat' but had picked up the same from house of PW2 - Having picked up 'darat', committed an assault on deceased, it was apparent that appellant was aware of the nature of injury he was likely to cause with the weapon of incident - On perusal of evidence, would leave no room for doubt, that appellant had chosen sharp side of 'darat' and not blunt side - When a blow with a deadly weapon was struck with ferocity, it was apparent that assailant intended to cause bodily injury of a nature which he knew was so imminently dangerous, that it should in all probability cause death - Further, it was not the case of appellant that occurrence arose out of a sudden quarrel or that blow was struck in heat of the moment or that he had retaliated as a consequence of provocation at the hands of deceased - Therefore appellant had no excuse for such an extreme act - Hence, appellant should be deemed to have committed the offence of 'culpable homicide amounting to murder' u/s. 302 of IPC - Impugned order of conviction was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
