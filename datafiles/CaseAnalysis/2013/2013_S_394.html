State of Maharashtra and another v Indian Hotel and Restaurants Association and others

Supreme Court of India

16 July 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, Altamas Kabir

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 439; (2013) 8 SCC 519; AIR 2013 SC 2582; 2013(5) ALL MR 392; 2013 (5) Bom.C.R. 821; 2013 (3) RCR(Civil) 859; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 455; 2013(9) SCALE 47; [2013] 7 S.C.R. 654

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Women & Children

Keywords: Morality, Woman, Constitutionality, Prostitution, Degree Of Harm

Summary: Administrative - Constitution - Women & Children - Bombay Police Act, 1951, s.33A - Bombay Police (Amendment) Act, 2005 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 19(1)(g) - Obscene dances - Prohibition of dance bars - Legality - Appeals sought to challenge judgment passed by the HC whereby s.33A of 1951 Act as inserted by 2005 Act was declared to be ultra vires arts. 14 and 19(1)(g) of Constitution - Whether HC rightly decreed that s.33A of 1951 Act as inserted by 2005 Act was ultra vires arts. 14 and 19(1)(g) of Constitution - Held, Held, end result of the prohibition of any form of dancing in the establishments covered u/s. 33A of 1951 Act would lead to the only conclusion that these establishments had to shut down - It was evident from the fact that since 2005, most if not all the dance bar establishments had closed down - It had led to the unemployment of over 75,000 women workers - It was brought on the record that many of them were compelled to take up prostitution out of necessity for maintenance of their families - Impugned legislation was proved to be totally counter productive and could not be sustained being ultra vires the art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution - Impugned legislation could still be protected by reading down the provision - It was not possible to read down the expression 'any kind or type' of dance by any person to mean dances which were obscene and derogatory to the dignity of women - Such reading down could not be permitted so long as any kind of dance was permitted in establishments covered u/s. 33B of 1951 Act - Submission of appellant that the provisions contained in s. 33A of 1951 Act could be declared constitutional by applying the doctrine of severability could not be accepted - Even if s. 33B of 1951 Act was declared unconstitutional, it would still retain the provision contained in s. 33A 1951 Act which prohibited any kind of dance by any person in the establishments covered u/s. 33A of 1951 Act - It would be more appropriate that the appellants re-examined the recommendations made by the Committee which was constituted by appellants - Committee had prepared a report and submitted the same to appellants - Appellants had in fact sent a communication to all District Judicial Magistrates and Police Commissioner to amend the rules for exercising control on Hotel Establishments presenting dance programmes - Despite the directions made by appellants, the authorities did not take steps to implement the recommendations - On the contrary, impugned legislation was enacted in 2005 - It would be more appropriate to bring about measures which would ensure the safety and improve the working conditions of the persons working as bar girls - In instant case, restrictions in the nature of prohibition could not be said to be reasonable, inasmuch as there could be several lesser alternatives available which would have been adequate to ensure safety of women than to completely prohibit dance - In fact, a large number of imaginative alternative steps could be taken instead of completely prohibiting dancing, if the real concern of appellants was the safety of women - Appeals dismissed.
