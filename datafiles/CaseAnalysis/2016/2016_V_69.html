Voluntary Health Association v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

08 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 805; AIR 2016 SC 5122; 2016 (6) Bom.C.R. 481; JT 2016 (10) SC 570; 2016 (8) MLJ 171; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 833; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 995; 2016(10) SCALE 531B; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 192

Case Digest

Subject: Health & Drug

Keywords: Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques (Regulation and Prevention of Misuse) Act, 1994, Pre-conception and Pre-natal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Rules, 1996, Pre-conception and Prenatal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Act, 1994, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Writ Petition (Civil) No. 349 of 2006

(A) Health & Drug - Women & Children - Constitution of India, 1950 - Pre-conception and Prenatal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Act, 1994, ss. 23, 27,6 - Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques (Regulation and Prevention of Misuse) Act, 1994 - Pre-conception and Pre-natal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Rules, 1996, rr. 18A, 18A(6), 9 - Female foeticide - Stringent law implementation - Validity - Petitioners/association filed instant writ petitions stating that increase of female foeticide, resultant imbalance of sex ratio and indifference in implementation of stringent law and there was fulcrum of anguished grievance lays stress on non-implementation of the provisions of the Rules framed under the 1994 Act by competent authorities who were obliged to do so.

Held, constitutional identity of a female child cannot be mortgaged to any kind of social or other concept that has developed or was thought of. It only permits affirmative steps that were constitutionally postulated. Further, when rights were conferred by Constitution, it has to be understood that such rights were recognised regard being had to their naturalness and universalism. When a female foetus is destroyed through artificial means which is legally impermissible, the dignity of life of a woman to be born is extinguished. It corrodes the human values. The Legislature has brought a complete code and it subserves the constitutional purpose. Having stated about the scheme of the 1994 Act and the purpose of the various provisions and also the Rules framed under the 1994 Act, dropping of sex ratio still remains a social affliction and a disease. Therefore, with certitude and without allowing any room for any kind of equivocation or ambiguity, perception of any individual or group or organization or system treating a woman with inequity, indignity, inequality or any kind of discrimination was constitutionally impermissible. Female foeticide wasconceived by society that definitely includes parents because of unethical perception of life and nonchalant attitude towards law. When freedom of free choice was allowed within constitutional and statutory parameters, others cannot determine the norms as that would amount to acting in derogation of law. Decrease in the sex ratio is a sign of colossal calamity and it cannot be allowed to happen. Concrete steps have to be taken to increase the same so that invited social disasters do not befall on society. The present generation was expected to be responsible to posterity and not to take such steps to sterilize the birth rate in violation of law. Thus, neither the validity of the 1994 Act nor the Rules has been specifically assailed in writ petition what was prayed is to read out certain provisions and to add certain exceptions. SC was of convinced view that averments in instant petitions with such prayers cannot be entertained and decline to interfere. Petitions disposed of.

Writ Petition (Civil) No. 575 of 2014

(B) Health & Drug - Women & Children - Constitution of India, 1950 - Pre-conception and Prenatal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Act, 1994, ss. 23, 27,6 - Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques (Regulation and Prevention of Misuse) Act, 1994 - Pre-conception and Pre-natal Diagnostic Techniques (Prohibition of Sex Selection) Rules, 1996, rr. 18A, 18A(6), 9 - So called implementation - Misuse - Undue harassment - Petitioners/Indian Medical Association (IMA) filed instant writ petitions contending that under the 1994 Act and r. 9(4) of the Rules were being misused and wrongly interpreted by concerned authorities thereby causing undue harassment to medical professionals all over Country under the guise of 'so-called implementation'.

Held, whenever there was an abuse of process of law, individual could always avail legal remedy. SC found neither the validity of the Act nor the Rules was specifically assailed in writ petition. What was prayed is to read out certain provisions and to add certain exceptions. SC was of view that contentions of present nature with such prayers cannot be entertained and decline to interfere. Petitions disposed of.
