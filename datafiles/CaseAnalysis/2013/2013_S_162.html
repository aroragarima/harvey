Sunil Mehta & Anr. v State Of Gujarat & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

20 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 105; (2013) 9 SCC 209; 2013 (2) JCC 1155; JT 2013 (3) SC 328; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 238; 2013(2) SCALE 686; [2013] 2 S.C.R. 56

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, 406, 420, 114 r/w s. 34 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, chap. 15, 19, ss. 156(3), 202, 244, 245(2), 246 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Framing of charges - Before cognizance - Challenged - Respondent no. 2 (complainant) filed a complaint before Magistrate for alleged offences u/ss. 406, 420, 114 r/w s. 34 of IPC - After examination of complainant, Magistrate u/s. 156(3) of CrPC directed enquiry - However, enquiry u/s. 202 of CrPC and issued process against appellants (accused) - Thereafter, appellants before Magistrate contended that no charge could be framed against appellants as complainant did not lead any evidence in terms of s. 244 of CrPC and depositions recorded u/s. 202 of CrPC could be considered as evidence for framing charges - But, charges were framed against appellants - Aggrieved appellants filed a revision before Lower Appellate Court, which allowed for non-compliance of s. 245(2) of CrPC and directed to proceed u/s. 244 to 247 of CrPC - Aggrieved complainant filed an appeal before HC, which was allowed by impugned order - Hence, instant appeal - Whether depositions of complainant and his witnesses recorded under chap. 15 of CrPC before cognizance was took by Magistrate would constitute evidence to frame charges against appellant under Part B of chap. 19 of CPC - Held, evidence referred to in ss. 244, 245 and 246 of IPC should, on a plain reading of said sections and provisions of 1872 Act, be admissible only if same was produced and, in the case of documents, proved in accordance with procedure established under 1872 Act which included rights of parties against whom that evidence was produced to cross-examine the witnesses concerned - Whole object underlying recording of evidence u/s. 244 of CrPC after the accused has appeared was to ensure that not only did accused have opportunity to hear the evidence adduced against him, but to defend himself by cross-examining the witnesses with a view to showing that witness was either unreliable or that a statement made by him did not have any evidentiary value or that it did not incriminate him - Whether or not a case was made out against accused, could be decided only when accused was allowed to cross-examine the witnesses for otherwise accused might not be in a position to demonstrate that no case was made out against him and thereby claim a discharge u/s. 245 of CrPC - It was elementary that ultimate quest in any judicial determination was to arrive at the truth, which was not possible unless deposition of witnesses goes through the fire of cross-examination - Right of cross-examination granted to an accused u/s. 244 to 246 of CrPC even before framing of charges did not, in the least, cause any prejudice to complainant or result in any failure of justice, while denial of such a right was likely and indeed bound to prejudice the accused in his defence - Fact that after the Court has found a case justifying framing of charges against accused, accused has a right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses u/s. 246(4) of CrPC did not necessarily mean that such a right could not be conceded to accused before the charges were framed - HC erred in considering deposition of complainant and witnesses recorded before the appearance of appellants u/s. 202 of CrPC to be 'evidence' for purposes of framing of charges against appellants - Order of Lower Appellate Court was restored and impugned order of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
