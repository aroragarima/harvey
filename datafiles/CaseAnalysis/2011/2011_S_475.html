Surinder Kumar v State Of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

21 October 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 697; (2011) 10 SCC 173; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 696; 2012 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 685; 2012 (1) MLJ(Crl) 356; 2011 (4) RCR(Criminal) 658; 2012 (2) RLW 1500; 2011(12) SCALE 172; [2011] 12 S.C.R. 1205

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction by HC - Sustainability - Appellant accused allegedly murdered the deceased by pouring kerosene and set her fire - Trial Court, after giving benefit of doubt, acquitted the appellant - Respondent State filed appeal - HC, by impugned judgment, reversed the findings of Trial Court and sentenced the appellant for life imprisonment with fine - Whether the dying declaration of the deceased was reliable, acceptable and based on which conviction was sustainable - Held, at the time of recording the dying declaration of the deceased by PW2 Executive Magistrate, no endorsement of the PW9 doctor was made about her position to make such statement - Further, when PW2 recorded the statement of the deceased PW9 doctor was not present and subsequently on the request of the police officer, he offered his opinion to the effect that the patient was fit to make a statement - Procedure adopted by PW2 while recording the statement of dying declaration was not acceptable - Dying declaration did not carry a certificate by the Executive Magistrate to the effect that it was a voluntary statement made by the deceased and that he had read over the statement to her - Dying declaration was not even attested by the doctor - Though the Magistrate had stated that the statement had been made in mixed dialect of Hindi and Punjabi and the statement was recorded only in Hindi - Further, there was evidence that the deceased was under the influence of seductive injections and was not supposed to be having normal alertness - Hence, the Trial Court rightly rejected the dying declaration altogether shrouded by suspicious circumstances and contrary to the story of prosecution and acquitted the appellant - On the basis of materials available on record, there was no case to presume that the death of the deceased occurred at the hands of the appellant especially, when deceased's statement was shrouded by suspicious circumstances and contrary to the claim of the prosecution - When the deceased was alleged to have 97% burns and being under constant sedatives, in such a situation she could not be expected to make a statement at a stretch without asking any questions - Further, the Executive Magistrate, PW2 did not put any question and recorded her answers - On the date of the incident, the deceased had two children aged about six and four years respectively and both of them were present in the place of occurrence, however, Investigating Officer had not enquired them about the genuineness of the incident - Though, there were number of immediate neighbours/co-tenants in the same premises, their statements were not recorded which means that nobody supported the version of the prosecution - Thus, dying declaration was totally in conflict with the version of the prosecution as to the time of her burning, relation of the appellant with the deceased, except for the implication part, which was clarified in favour of the appellant by PW10 in his cross- examination - In such circumstances, the dying declaration was totally unacceptable, could not be believed as trustworthy, which was rightly not believed so by the Trial Court - Inasmuch as the dying declaration was the only piece of evidence put forward against the appellant accused, he was entitled to the benefit of doubt - Conviction and sentence ordered by the HC was set aside and the order of acquittal passed by the Trial Court was restored - Appeal allowed.
