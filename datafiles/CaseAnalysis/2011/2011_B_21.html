Balchandra L. Jarkiholi & Ors v B.S. Yeddyurappa & Ors

Supreme Court of India

13 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Cyriac Joseph

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 357; (2011) 7 SCC 1; 2011 (6) AWC 5810; JT 2011 (6) SC 132; 2011 (7) MLJ(SC) 283; 2011 (3) RCR(Civil) 223; 2011(6) SCALE 172; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 877

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Constitution (Fifty-Second Amendment) Act, 1985, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (Disqualification of Members on Ground of Defection) Rules, 1986

Summary: (A) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Karnataka Legislative Assembly (Disqualification of Members on Ground of Defection) Rules, 1986 , r. 7 - Appellants, Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs) wrote letters to the Governor stating that they had been elected as MLAs on ruling party tickets, but had become disillusioned with the functioning of the Government headed by 1st respondent Chief Minister and hence withdrew their support to the Government - Governor consequently asked 1st respondent to prove his majority in the House - 1st respondent, on the same day, filed an application before the Speaker of the House for disqualifying appellants in view of the disqualification incurred them under Tenth Schedule of Constitution - Speaker sent show cause notice to appellants asking them to file replies and resultantly, appellants filed replies - Speaker, by considering the materials produced before it, passed an order disqualifying the appellants - Appellants challenged Speaker's order before HC - HC dismissed appellants' writ petition - (a) Did the appellants voluntarily give up their membership of the ruling political party - Held, appellants had in writing informed the Governor that having become disillusioned with the functioning of the Government headed by 1st respondent, they had chosen to withdraw support to the Government headed by 1st respondent - However, the said stand was re-emphasized in their replies to the show cause notices submitted by the appellants wherein they had, inter alia, denied that their conduct had attracted the vice of 'defection' within the scope of Paragraph 2(1)(a) of the Tenth Schedule - In their said replies, appellants had categorically indicated that nowhere in the letter they had indicated that they would not continue as Members of the Legislature Party of the ruling party - Appellants had reiterated that they would continue to support the ruling party and any Government formed by the ruling party headed by any leader, other than 1st respondent, as Chief Minister of the State - Appellant also reiterated that they would continue to support any Government headed by a clean and efficient person who could provide good governance to the people of the State according to the Constitution of India and that it was only to save the party and Government and to ensure that the State was rid of a corrupt Chief Minister, that the letter had been submitted to the Governor - Hence appellants did not voluntarily give up membership of ruling political party - (b) Since only three days' time was given to the appellants to reply to the show cause notices, as against the period of 7 days or more, prescribed in r. 7(3) of the Rules, were the said notices vitiated - Held, explanation given as to why notices to show cause had been issued to the appellants u/r. 7 of the Disqualification Rules, giving the appellants only three days' time to respond to the same, despite the stipulated time of seven days or more indicated in r. 7(3) itself, was not very convincing - No compulsion was there on the Speaker to decide the Disqualification Application filed by 1st respondent in such a great hurry within the time specified by the Governor to the Speaker to conduct a Vote of Confidence in the Government headed by 1st respondent - Such a course of action was adopted by the Speaker on 10-10-2010, since the Vote of Confidence on the Floor of the House was slated for 12-10- 2010 - Speaker ignored the claim of the appellants to be given reasonable time to respond to the show cause notices and also to the documents which were handed over to the appellants at the time of hearing of the Disqualification Application - Further incidence of partisan behaviour on the part of the Speaker would be evident from the fact that not only were the appellants not given an adequate opportunity to deal with the contents of the affidavits affirmed by State President of the ruling party and other two member of the House (who originally withdrew the support but later retracted the same), but the time given to submit the show-cause on 10-10-2010, was preponed from 5.00 p.m. to 3.00 p.m., making it even more difficult for the appellants to respond to the show cause notices in a meaningful manner - Explanation given by the Speaker that the appellants had filed detailed replies to the show cause notices did not stand up to the test of fairness when one took into consideration the fact that various allegations had been made in the three affidavits filed by Party President and other two members, which could only be answered by the appellants themselves and not by their advocates - Speaker had proceeded on the basis that the allegations should be deemed to have been proved, even in the absence of any corroborative evidence, simply because the same had not been denied by the appellants - Speaker apparently did not take into consideration the rule of evidence that a person making an allegation had to prove the same with supporting evidence and the mere fact that the allegation was not denied, did not amount to the same having been proved on account of the silence of the person against whom such allegations were made - Hence show cause notice was vitiated due to non-provisions of 7 days or more time in responding to such notices - Appeals allowed.

(B) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Karnataka Legislative Assembly (Disqualification of Members on Ground of Defection) Rules, 1986 - Did the Speaker acted in hot haste in disposing of the Disqualification Application filed by 1st respondent introducing a whiff of bias as to the procedure adopted - Held, appellants were not given copies of the affidavit filed by the State President of ruling party - Since State President was not a party to the proceedings, the Speaker should have caused service of copies of the same on the appellants to enable them to meet the allegations made therein - Not only did the Speaker's action amount to denial of the principles of natural justice to the appellants, but it also revealed a partisan trait in the Speaker's approach in disposing of the Disqualification Application filed by 1st respondent - If the Speaker wished to rely on the statements of a third party which were adverse to the appellants' interests, it was obligatory on his part to have given the appellants an opportunity of questioning the deponent as to the veracity of the statements made in the affidavit - Said conduct on the part of the Speaker was also indicative of the 'hot haste' with which the Speaker disposed of the Disqualification Petition as complained of by the appellants - Procedure adopted by the Speaker seemed to indicate that he was trying to meet the time schedule set by the Governor for the trial of strength in the Assembly and to ensure that the appellants and the other independent MLAs stood disqualified prior to the date on which the Floor Test was to be held - Disqualification Application filed by 1st respondent contained only bald allegations, which were not corroborated by any direct evidence - Application did not even mention the provision under which the same had been made - By allowing State Party President, who was not even a party to the proceedings, and two other member who retracted their statement later, to file their respective affidavits, the short-comings in the Disqualification Application were allowed to be made up - Speaker, however, relied on the said affidavits to ultimately declare that the appellants stood disqualified from the membership of the House, without even serving copies of the same on the appellants, but on their advocates, just before the hearing was to be conducted - Hence conduct of the proceedings by the Speaker and the decision given by the Speaker on the basis thereof did not meet even the parameters laid down in Jagjit Singh v. State of Haryana, 2006 INDLAW SC 933 - Appeal allowed.

(C) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 212, 136 - What is the scope of judicial review of an order passed by the Speaker under Paragraph 2(1)(a) of the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution, having regard to the provisions of art. 212 thereof - Held, on the question of justiceability of the Speaker's order on account of the expression of finality in paragraph 6 of the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution, such finality did not include the powers of the superior Courts u/arts. 32, 226 and 136 of the Constitution to judicially review the order of the Speaker - Under paragraph 2(1)(a) of the Tenth Schedule, the Speaker functions in a quasi-judicial capacity, which made an order passed by him in such capacity, subject to judicial review - Scope of paragraph 2(1)(a) of the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution, therefore, enabled the Speaker in a quasi-judicial capacity to declare that a Member of the House stood disqualified for the reasons mentioned in paragraph 2(1)(a) of the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution - Appeal allowed.
