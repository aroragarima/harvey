Om Prakash v State Of Rajasthan & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

13 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Gyan Sudha Misra, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 126; (2012) 5 SCC 201; AIR 2012 SC 1608; 2012 CRLJ 2266; 2012 (3) JCC 1747; JT 2012 (4) SC 94; 2012 (3) MLJ(Crl) 135; 2012 (2) RCR(Criminal) 770; 2012 (2) RLW 1743; 2012(4) SCALE 348; [2012] 5 S.C.R. 237

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act 2000 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 376 - Rape - Juvenile protection - Benevolent Legislation - Eligibility - Sustainability - Petitioner's minor daughter was raped by Respondent No. 2 and another accused and Trial Court, considering oral and documentary evidence declared respondent to be juvenile and had allowed to avail benefit of protection to a juvenile and ordered to refer him for trial to a juvenile court under 2000, Act - Petitioner preferred appeal before HC, HC due to lot of contradictory evidence held that respondent No. 2 was rightly referred to Juvenile Justice Board, dismissed appeal - Aggrieved instant petition - Petitioner contended that although courts below could not record a finding that he, in fact, was a juvenile since he had not attained age of 18 years on date of incident -Whether respondent/accused herein who is alleged to have committed an offence u/s. 376 of IPC be allowed to avail benefit of protection to a juvenile in order to refer him for trial to a juvenile court and avail benefit of 'benevolent legislation' relating to Juvenile Justice Act - Held, respondent No.2 and his father had failed to prove that respondent No.2 was a minor at time of commission of offence and hence could not have been granted benefit of Juvenile Justice Act which undoubtedly was a benevolent legislation but cannot be allowed to be availed of by an accused who had taken plea of juvenility merely as an effort to hide his real age so as to create a doubt in mind of courts below who thought it appropriate to grant him benefit of a juvenile merely by adopting principle of benevolent legislation but missing its vital implication that although Juvenile Justice Act by itself was a piece of benevolent legislation, protection under same cannot be made available to an accused who in fact was not a juvenile but sought shelter merely by using it as a protective umbrella or statutory shield - SC, was under constraint to observe that this would have to be discouraged if evidence and other materials on record failed to prove that accused was a juvenile at time of commission of offence - Juvenile Justice Act which was certainly meant to treat a child accused with care and sensitivity, offering him a chance to reform and settle into mainstream of society, same could not be allowed to be used as a ploy to dupe course of justice while conducting trial and treatment of heinous offences - This would clearly be treated as an effort to weaken justice dispensation system and hence cannot be encouraged - SC, set aside judgment and order of HC, and court below and ordered respondent No. 2 be sent for trial before court of competent jurisdiction - Appeal allowed.
