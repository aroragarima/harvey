Sterlite Industries (India) Ltd. and Others v Union Of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

02 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Patnaik, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 186; (2013) 4 SCC 575; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 135; JT 2013 (4) SC 388; 2013 (2) KLT 235; 2013 (3) MLJ 741; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 545; 2013(5) SCALE 202; [2013] 6 S.C.R. 573

Case Digest

Subject: Environment, Wildlife & Animal

Keywords: Emission, Manufacture, Storage and Import of Hazardous Chemical Rules, 1989

Summary: Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981, s. 21 - Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974, s. 25 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 137 - Pollution of air and water - Order of closure of company - Sustainability - Appellant/company applied and obtained 'No Objection Certificate' from State Pollution Control Board for setting up copper smelter plant - However, one Trust challenged consent orders received by appellant u/s. 21 of 1981 Act and u/s. 25 of 1974 Act through writ petition - Further one political party also raised objection against such sanction by State Pollution board through another writ petition - HC heard writ petitions and by the common judgment allowed and disposed of the writ petitions with the direction to the appellant to close down its plant - Hence instant appeal - Whether misrepresentation and suppression of material facts made in the special leave petition for allowing filing of appeal was enough for order of closure of appellant's plant - Held, it would be clear from the extracts from the Executive Summary of (National Environmental Engineering and Research Institute)NEERI in its report of 2005, that while some of the emissions from the plant of the appellants were within the limits stipulated by the State Pollution Board, some of the emissions did not conform to the standards stipulated by State Pollution Board - It would also be clear from the extracts from the Executive Summary relating to water environment that the surface water samples were found to be within the prescribed limits of drinking water whereas ground water samples showed high mineral contents in terms of dissolved solids as compared to the drinking water standards, but concentrations of nutrient demand parameters revealed that the phosphate and nitrate contents were within the limits stipulated under drinking water standards and levels of chromium, copper and lead were found to be higher in comparison to the parameters stipulated under drinking water standards, whereas the heavy metal concentrations, namely, iron, manganese, zinc and arsenic were within the drinking water standards - Soil samples also revealed heavy metals - Regarding the solid waste out of slag in the plant site, Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) had taken view in its communication to State Pollution Board that the slag was non- hazardous - Thus, the NEERI report of 2005 did show that the emission and effluent discharge affected the environment but the report read as whole did not warrant conclusion that the plant of the appellants could not possibly take remedial steps to improve the environment and that the only remedy to protect the environment was to direct closure of the plant of the appellants - In fact, SC passed orders on 25-2-2011 directing joint inspection by NEERI with the officials of CPCB as well as the State Pollution Board - Accordingly, an inspection was carried out and report was submitted by NEERI to SC - SC directed Govt. and the State Pollution Board to submit their comments with reference to the NEERI report - SC directed State Pollution Board to file synopsis specifying the deficiencies with reference to the NEERI report and suggest control measures that should be taken by the appellants so that SC could consider the direction to be issued for remedial measures which could be monitored by the State Pollution Board - Accordingly, State Pollution Board filed affidavit along with the chart of deficiencies and measures to be implemented by the appellants and SC directed State Pollution Board to issue directions, in exercise of its powers under 1981 Act and 1974 Act to the appellants to carry out the measures and remove the deficiencies indicated in the chart - Pursuant to the order, State Pollution Board issued directions to the appellants and appellants claimed before SC that they had removed the deficiencies pointed out by State Pollution Board and SC directed that joint inspection should be carried out by State Pollution Board and CPCB and joint report was to be submitted to SC - Considering the magnitude, capacity and prosperity of the appellant, SC of the view that the appellant should be held liable for a compensation of Rs. 100 crores for having polluted the environment in the vicinity of its plant and for having operated the plant without renewal of the consents by State Pollution Board for fairly long period and according to SC, any less amount, would not have the desired deterrent effect on the appellant - Further, amount would be deposited with the Collector of X District, who would invest it in Fixed Deposit with Nationalized Bank for period of five years - Interest therefrom would be spent for improving the environment, including water and soil, of vicinity of the plant after consultation with State Pollution Board and approval of the Secretary, Environment, Govt. - There was no doubt that there was misrepresentation and suppression of material facts made in the special leave petition but to decline relief to the appellants in instant case would mean closure of the plant of the appellants - Plant of the appellants contributed substantially to the copper production in India and copper was used in defence, electricity, automobile, construction and infrastructure etc - Plant of the appellants had about 1300 employees and it also provided employment to large number of people through contractors - Number of ancillary industries were also dependent on the plant - Through its various transactions, plant generated huge revenue to Central and State Govt. in terms of excise, custom duties, income tax and VAT - It also contributed to 10 percent of the total cargo volume of Y port - For these considerations of public interest, SC did not think it would be proper exercise of our discretion u/art. 136 of the Constitution to refuse relief on the grounds of misrepresentation and suppression of material facts in the special leave petition - Appeals allowed.
