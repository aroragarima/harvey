Commissioner of Central Excise, Goa v Cosme Farma Laboratories Limited

Supreme Court of India

07 April 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Anil R. Dave, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 237; 2015 (318) E.L.T. 545; JT 2015 (4) SC 65; 2015 (3) MLJ 489; 2015(4) SCALE 563; [2015] 4 S.C.R. 360

Case Digest

Subject: Excise

Keywords: Assessable Value, Central Excises And Salt Act, 1944

Summary: Excise - Health & Drug - Central Excise Act, 1944 - Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 - Manufacturer - Job workers - Respondent manufactures certain medicaments and gets certain other medicaments manufactured through job workers - Commissioner sent notices to respondent to show cause why respondent be not treated as manufacturer under 1944 Act in respect of medicaments manufactured by job workers and called upon respondent to make payment of duty and job workers were sent notices to show cause why they be not directed to pay penalty - After hearing parties, Commissioner held that respondent was manufacturer of medicaments manufactured at the premises of its job workers within the meaning of provisions of 1944 Act and the rules made there under - Aggrieved, respondent filed appeals before Tribunal and the same were allowed - Hence, instant appeals by Revenue - Whether respondent, who was getting its medicaments manufactured through the job workers, could be considered to be an independent manufacturer.

Held, manufacturing activity was done by job workers only in their premises and with the help of their labour force and machinery and just because the job workers had to adhere to the quality control or the specifications with regard to the quality prescribed by the respondent, it would not mean that respondent is the manufacturer. Further, from agreements entered into between respondent and job workers it was clear that the job workers were not manufacturing drugs as agents of respondent or on behalf of respondent, but they were carrying out the manufacturing activity independently, and hence, they were manufacturers of drugs as per provisions of 1944 Act. Furthermore, when Tribunal after appreciating relevant evidence, has come to conclusion that job workers were the manufacturers and not respondent, said finding of fact by Tribunal could not be interfered when the same is correct and not perverse. Furthermore, assessable value of goods manufactured by job workers would be sum total of raw material, labour charges and profit of the job workers, as per Circular and decision of SC in Pawan Biscuits, 2000 INDLAW SC 366 and other cases. Hence, price at which respondent brand owner sells its goods would not be assessable value because the duty is to be paid at the stage at which the goods are manufactured and not at the stage when the goods are sold. Hence, all appeals are dismissed. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - Term 'manufacturer' or loan licensee used under provisions of 1940 Act is different from term 'manufacturer' under provisions of 1944 Act.
