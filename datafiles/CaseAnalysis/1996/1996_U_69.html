Â Mafatalal Industries Limited Etc. Etc. v Union Of India Etc. Etc.

Supreme Court of India

19 December 1996

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, B.P. Jeevan Reddy, K.S. Paripoornan, B.L. Hansaria, S.C. Sen, J.S. Verma, S.C. Agrawal, A.S. Anand, B.N. Kirpal

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Customs; Excise; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Recovery Of Duties, Finance (No.2) Act, 1980, Short Levy, Sale Price, Refund Of Duty, Price List, Central Duties Of Excise (Retrospective Exemption) Act, 1986, Warehouse, Refund Claims, Paid Under Mistake Of Law, Lost, Central Excises And Customs Laws (Amendment) Act, 1991, Stores, Enactment, Retention, Administrative Action, Madras General Sales Tax Act, 1939, Time And Manner Of Payment Of Duty, Levy Of Tax, Blended Yarn, Constitutionality, Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, Directive Principles, Unconstitutionality, Excise Duty Act, Kerala Sales Tax Act, Madhya Bharat Sales Tax Act, 1950, Revenue Act, 1928, Mysore Elementary Education Act, 1941, Customs, Central Excises And Salt And Central Boards Of Revenue (Amendment) Act, 1978, Agricultural Adjustment Act, Central Provinces and Berar Municipal Act, Finance (No. 2) Act, 1940, Finance Statutes Amendment Act, 1981, Gasoline Tax Act, 1948, Indian Sales of Goods (Amendment) Act, 1940, Iron and Steel Duties Act, 1934, Madhya Bharat Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act,, Matches (Excise Duty) Act, 1934, Mechanical Lighters (Excise Duty) Order, 1934, Motor Spirit (Duties) Act, 1970, Revenue Act, 1918, Revenue Act, 1921, Revenue Act, 1924, Silver (Excise Duty) Act, 1930, Stamps Act, State Transport Co-ordination (Barring of Claims and Remedies) Act, 1954, Sugar (Excise Duty) Act, 1934, Tobacco (Excise Duty) Act, 1943, Tyres (Excise Duty) Act, 1941, Vegetable Product (Excise Duty) Act, 1943, Sugar (Excise Duty) Order, 1934

Summary: Majority View

B. P. Jeevan Reddy, for himself and J.S. Verma, S.C. Agrawal, Dr. A.S. Anand and B.N. Kirpal, JJ

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Validity and meaning of the provisions introduced by the 1991 (Amendment) Act - Validity of s. 11B - Refund - Held, amendments made and the provisions inserted by the Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 in the Central Excises and Salt Act and Customs Act are constitutionally valid and are unexceptionable - By virtue of s. 11B(3) of 1944 Act as amended by the 1991 Amendment Act, and by virtue of the provisions contained in s. 27(3) 1962 Act as amended by the 1991 Amendment Act, all claims for refund (excepting those which arise as a result of declaration of unconstitutionality of a provision whereunder the levy was created) have to be preferred and adjudicated only under the provisions of the respective enactment - No suit for refund of duty is maintainable in that behalf - So far as the jurisdiction of the HCs u/art. 226 of the Constitution or of SC u/art.32 is concerned, it remains unaffected by the provisions of the Act - Even so, the Court would, while exercising the jurisdiction under the said articles, have due regard to the legislative intent manifested by the provisions of the Act - The writ petition would naturally be considered and disposed of in the light of and in accordance with the provisions of s. 11B of 1944 Act - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Whether s. 11B of 1944 Act is retrospective? - Held, S. 11B(1) of 1944 Act clearly states that s. 11B of 1944 Act do not apply to pending proceedings - They apply to all proceedings where the refund has not been made finally and unconditionally - Where the refund proceedings have finally terminated , in the sense that the appeal period has also expired before the commencement of the 1991 (Amendment) Act, they cannot be re-opened and/or governed by s. 11-B(3) (as amended by the 1991 (Amendment) Act) - This, however, does not mean that the power of the appellate authorities to condone delay in appropriate cases is affected in any manner by this clarification - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Is s. 11B a mere device to retain illegally collected taxes? - Held, s. 11B of 1944 Act does provide for the purchase making the claim for refund provided he is able to establish that he has not passed on the burden to another person - It, therefore, cannot be said that s. 11B is a device to retain the illegally collected taxes by the State - This is equally true of s. 27 of the 1962 Act - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Meaning and scope of s. 11B(3) - Held, s. 11B of 1944 Act applies to all pending proceedings notwithstanding the fact that the duty may have been refunded to the petitioner pending the proceedings or under the orders of the Court/Tribunal/Authority or otherwise - Section 11B of 1944 Act exhaustive on the question of refund and excluded the jurisdiction of the civil court in respect of all refund claims - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Whether Sales Tax Officer, Banaras and Others vs. Kanhaiya Lal Mukundlal Saraf 1958 INDLAW SC 178, has been correctly decided and if not, in what respects? - Held, decision of SC in Kanhaiyalal case, held to have been wrongly decided as it held that where the taxes are paid under a mistake of law, the person paying it is entitled to recover the same from the State on establishing a mistake and that this consequence flows from s. 72 of 1872 Act - However, it is not permissible to claim refund by invoking s. 72 of 1872 Act as a separate and independent remedy when such a course is expressly barred by the provisions in the Act, viz., r. 11 of 1944 Rules and s. 11B of 1944 Act - For this reason, a suit for refund would also not lie - SC in Kanhaiyalal case, also held that it is open to an assessee to claim refund of tax paid by him under orders which have become final or to re-open the orders which have become final in his own case on the basis of discovery of a mistake of law based upon the decision of a court in the case of another assessee, regardless of the time-lapse involved and regardless of the fact that the relevant enactment does not provide for such refund or re-opening - However, theory of mistake of law and the consequent period of limitation of three years from the date of discovery of such mistake of law cannot be invoked by an assessee taking advantage of the decision in another assessee's case - All claims for refund ought to be, and ought to have been, filed only under and in accordance with r. 11/s. 11B and under no other provision and in no other forum - An assessee must succeed or fail in his own proceedings and the finality of the proceedings in his own case cannot be ignored and refund ordered in his favour just because in another assessee's case a similar point is decided in favour of the manufacturer/assessee - SC in Kanhaiyalal case, also held that the equitable considerations have no place in situations where s. 72 of the 1872 Act is applicable - However, since the manufacturer is claiming the refund and also because the fact of passing on the burden of duty is within his special and exclusive knowledge, it is for him to allege and establish that he has not passed on the duty to a third party - This is the requirement which flows from the fact that s. 72 of 1872 is an equitable provision and that it incorporates a rule of equity - This requirement flows not only because s. 72 of 1872 Act incorporates a rules of equity but also because both the Central Excises duties and the Customs duties are indirect taxes which are supposed to be and are permitted to be passed on to the buyer - SC in Kanhaiyalal case, also held that the spending away of the taxes collected by the State is not a good defence to a claim for refund of taxes collected contrary to law - However, where the petitioner alleges and establishes that he has not passed on the burden of the duty to others, his claim for refund may not be refused - Where the petitioner has not himself suffered any loss or prejudice (having passed on the burden of the duty to others), there is no justice or equity in refunding the tax (collected without the authority of law) to him merely because he paid it to the State as it would be a windfall to him - As against it, by refusing refund, the monies would continue to be with the State and available for public purposes - Order accordingly.

A. M. Ahmadi, Chief Justice

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Jurisdiction of ordinary courts in respect of refund claims - Held, actions by way of suits or petitions u/art. 226 of the Constitution cannot be completely eliminated in respect of refund claims - Ordinarily, all such claims must be preferred under the provisions of the Excise Act and the Rules framed thereunder by strictly adhering to the stipulated procedure - However, in cases where the authorities under the Excise Act arrogate to themselves jurisdiction even in cases where there is clear want of jurisdiction, the situation poses some difficulty - In such a situation, there is nothing to prevent the authorities from exercising jurisdiction in cases which are ultra vires the Excise Act but intra vires the Constitution - Hence, where the authorities under the Excise Act initiate action though lacking in inherent jurisdiction, the remedy by way of a suit u/s. 72 of the 1872 Act or a writ u/art. 226 of the Constitution will lie - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Whether s. 11B of 1944 Act is retrospective? - Held, in respect of decrees and orders that have become final but have not been executed, the procedure and conditions prescribed in s. 11B of 1944 Act will have to be complied with - However, under the scheme of the amended Excise Act, the application for refund which is a pre- requisite for invoking s. 11B(2) of 1944 ACT, is required to be made within six months from the payment of duty - It is obvious that this requirement cannot be complied with in respect pending decrees and orders - But it must at the same time be realised that in such a case, the assessee was protesting the recovery of the excise duty from him for which he had even initiated legal proceedings - It would therefore be in order to assume that he had paid the duty even though he was protesting its recovery - To ensure that such orders and decrees are not frustrated, it must be deemed that the duties of excise in such cases were paid "under protest" within the meaning of the second proviso to Clause (1) of s. 11B of 1944 Act - Order accordingly.

Paripoornan, Justice

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Jurisdiction of ordinary courts in respect of refund claims - Held, the jurisdiction of civil courts is not barred in entirety regarding the attack against the levy and/or claim for refund - It is not possible to conclude that any and every claim for refund of illegal/unauthorised levy of tax, can be made only in accordance with the provisions of the Act (Rule 11, s. 11B etc., as the case may be), and an action by way of suit or writ petition u/art. 226 of Constitution will not be maintainable under any circumstances - An action by way of suit or a petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution is maintainable to assail the levy or order which is illegal, void or unauthorised or without jurisdiction and/or claim refund, in cases covered by propositions - Order accordingly.

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Whether in an action claiming refund of excise duty (tax) paid under mistake of law, is it essential for the person claiming such refund, to establish "loss or injury" to him? - Held, if the person claiming the refund has passed on the burden of duty to another and has not really suffered any loss or prejudice, there is no question of reimbursing him and he cannot successfully sustain an action for restitution, based on s. 72 of the 1872 Act - The presumption is that the taxpayer has passed on the liability to the consumer (or third party) - It is open to assessee to rebut the presumption - The matter is exclusively within the knowledge of the assessee, whether the price of the goods included the 'duty" element also and/or also as to whether he has passed on the liability since he is in possession of all relevant details - Revenue will not be in a position to have an indepth analysis in the innumerable cases to ascertain and find out whether the taxpayer has passed on the liability - The matter being within the exclusive knowledge to the taxpayer, the burden of proving that the liability has not been passed on should lie on him - The very basis requirement for a claim of restitution u/s. 72 of the 1872 Act is that the person claiming restitution should plead and prove a loss or injury to him - Order accordingly.

Minority View

Suhas C. Sen, Justice

Indirect Tax - Excise - Customs - Constitution - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A and 11B - Central Excise Rules, 1944, r. 11 - Customs Act, 1962, s. 27 - Central Excises and Customs Law (Amendment) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Contract Act, 1872, s. 72 - Validity and meaning of the provisions introduced by the 1991 (Amendment) Act - Validity of s. 11B - Refund - Held, s.11B is a device for denying the claim for refund of duty to a tax-payer and must be struck down as violative of art. 265 of the Constitution - It in effect tries to perpetuate an illegal levy without altering the basis of the law under which the levy was made in any way - It is also a colourable piece of legislation and must be struck down and government is permitted to levy and retain only that much of excise duty which can be lawfully levied and collected under the Central Excise Act read with the Central Excise Tariff Act, 1985 and the Central Excise Rules and various notifications issued from time to time. - Anything collected beyond this is unlawful and cannot be retained by the Government under any pretext- The illegal levy and collection of duty violate not only the Central Excise Act and the Rules but also offends art. 265 of the Constitution of India - Order accordingly.
