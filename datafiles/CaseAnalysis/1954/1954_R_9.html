Â Central Bank of India v Ram Narain

Supreme Court of India

12 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 69; AIR 1955 SC 36; 1955 CRLJ 152; 1955 (57) PLR 153; 1955 SCJ 4; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 697

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Migration, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 188 and 412 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 134(1)(c) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 4, 380 and 454 - Money and stocks of appellant stolen by respondent - Appellant applied to East Punjab Govt. for sanction for prosecution of respondent for offences committed in Pakistan - East Punjab Govt. accorded sanction - Respondent at this time residing in Gurgaon - Appellant filed a complaint against Respondent before DM, Gurgaon - High Court held that trial of respondent, by a Magistrate in India was without jurisdiction - Whether a person accused of an offence u/s. IPC, 1860 and committed in a district which after partition of India became Pakistan, could be tried for that offence by a criminal court in India after his migration to that country, and thereafter acquiring status of a citizen? - Held, persons domiciled in India at time of coming into force of Constitution were given status of citizens and they thus acquired Indian nationality - If Respondent had Indian domicile at time of commission of offence, he would certainly come within ambit of s. 4, IPC, 1860 and s. 188, CrPC, 1898 - Partition of India and events that followed in its wake were unprecedented and it is difficult to cite any historical precedent for situation that arose - Hardly any occasion to form intentions requisite for acquiring domicile in one place or another - Respondent and his ancestors lived in Multan District, and he had considerable business there - Respondent's domicile of origin was in district of Multan and when district of Multan fell by partition of India in Pakistan, he had to be assigned Pakistan domicile till time he expressed his unequivocal intention of giving up that domicile and acquiring Indian domicile and also took up his residence in India - An Indian domicile cannot be ascribed to respondent - Subsequent acquisition by respondent of Indian domicile cannot affect question of jurisdiction of courts for trying him for crimes committed by him while he did not possess an Indian domicile - No evidence that at moment he committed offence he had finally made up his mind to take up his permanent residence in India, and a matter of this kind cannot be decided on conjectural grounds - Respondent could not be tried in any Court in India for offences committed in Mailsi in November, 1947, and Provincial Govt. had no power u/s. 188, CrPC, 1898 to accord sanction to his prosecution - Appeal dismissed.
