Ranjit Singh & Ors. v State Of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

27 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 945; (2011) 4 SCC 336; AIR 2011 SC 255; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 315; 2011 CRLJ 283; JT 2010 (12) SC 167; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 44; 2011 (1) RLW 936; 2010(11) SCALE 391

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 148, 149, 323, 324, 342 and 365 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - A charge-sheet was filed against 34 persons u/ss. 148, 365, 342, 323, 324 and 324/149 of IPC - Trial court acquitted 22 accused and convicted appellants - On appeal, HC acquitted two appellants and confirmed the conviction and sentences of other appellants - Hence, present appeals - Whether appellants rightly convicted? - Held, evidence on record and the manner in which the offence was committed makes it clear that the appellants intended to kill both deceased - Injuries caused to both the deceased had been grievous in nature and inflicted on vital parts of their bodies and were sufficient to cause the deaths of the deceased persons, as revealed by the medical evidence - No merit in appeal - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - (A) Falsus in Uno, Falsus in Omnibus - Applicability of - Held, legal maxim is not applicable in India and the court has to assess to what extent the deposition of a witness can be relied upon - Court has to separate the falsehood from the truth and it is only in exceptional circumstances when it is not possible to separate the grain from the chaff because they are inextricably mixed up, that the whole evidence of such a witness can be discarded - Number of witnesses required to prove the offence by members of a large unlawful assembly - (B) Accused-not named in the FIR - Effect thereof - Held, in case the informant fails to name a particular accused in the FIR, and the said accused is named at the earliest opportunity, when the statements of witnesses are recorded, it cannot tilt the balance in favour of the accused - (C) Whether accused can be convicted on the basis of testimony of a sole eye-witness? - Held, in a case involving an unlawful assembly with a very large number of persons, there is no rule of law that states that there cannot be any conviction on the testimony of a sole eye-witness, unless that the court is of the view that the testimony of such sole eye-witness is not reliable - Though, generally it is a rule of prudence followed by the courts that a conviction may not be sustained if it is not supported by two or more witnesses who give a consistent account of the incident in a fit case the court may believe a reliable sole eye-witness if in his testimony he makes specific reference to the identity of the individual and his specific overt acts in the incident - Rule of requirement of more than one witness applies only in a case where a witness deposes in a general and vague manner, or in the case of a riot - Appeal dismissed.
