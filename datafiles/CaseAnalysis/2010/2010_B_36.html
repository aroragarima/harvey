Budh Ram & Ors. v Bansi & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

05 August 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 635; (2010) 11 SCC 476; 2010 (5) AWC 4353; JT 2010 (8) SC 115; 2010 (3) RCR(Civil) 944; 2011 (1) RLW 499; 2010(7) SCALE 750; [2010] 9 S.C.R. 674

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: (A) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 22 rr. 1 and 2, O. 22 rr. 4(4) - Abatement of appeal - Respondents filed a civil suit against the appellants and some of their predecessors-in-interest alongwith a proforma defendant (respondent No. 4), for seeking declaration to the effect that respondents were co-owners and co-sharers in joint possession of land - Trial Court decreed the suit in favour of the respondents and respondent No. 4 - Appeal filed - During pendency of appeal, respondent no. 4 died and therefore an application under O. 22 r. 4 of CPC for substitution of the LRs of respondent No. 4 was filed - After an inordinate delay an application for condonation of delay was also filed - Appellate Court rejected said application and also held that in view of the fact that there was a joint possession and co-ownership of the respondents and respondent No. 4 and as the Trial Court had passed a joint decree in their favour, the appeal stood abated in toto - Appeal filed thereagainst dismissed - Hence, present appeal - Held, question as to whether non-substitution of LRs of the defendants/respondents would abate the appeal in toto or only qua the deceased defendants/respondents, depend upon the facts and circumstances of an individual case - Where each one of the parties has an independent and distinct right of his own, not inter-dependent upon one or the other, nor the parties have conflicting interests inter se, the appeal may abate only qua the deceased respondent - However, in case, there is a possibility that the Court may pass a decree contradictory to the decree in favour of the deceased party, the appeal would abate in toto for the simple reason that the appeal is a continuity of suit and the law does not permit two contradictory decrees on the same subject matter in the same suit - Thus, whether the judgment/decree passed in the proceedings vis-a-vis remaining parties would suffer the vice of being a contradictory or inconsistent decree is the relevant test - In the present case, a declaratory decree was passed in favour of respondents and respondent No. 4 to the effect that they were co-owners, though, they had specific shares but were held entitled to be in "joint possession" - Once a decree had been passed in her favour, a right had vested in favour of respondent No. 4 - On her death, the said vested right devolved upon her heirs - Thus, appeal against respondent No. 4 stood abated - Further, application for condonation of delay as well as the substitution of LRs of respondent No. 4 was rejected - Hence, HC rightly reached the conclusion that there was a possibility for the Appellate Court to reverse the judgment of the Trial Court - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 22 rr. 1 and 2, O. 22 rr. 4(4) - Abatement of the appeal - Applicability of O. 22 rr. 4(4) - Held, Abatement takes place automatically by application of law without any order of the court - Abatement in fact results in denial to hearing of the case on merits - Provisions of O. 22 rr. 4(4) of CPC, provide that in case, the deceased defendant did not contest the suit and did not file a counter affidavit, the substitution may not be warranted - Said provision requires the presentation of an application before the Court, before it pronounces its judgment for seeking such a relief and once such an application is allowed, in that case, it can only be taken against the said defendant notwithstanding the death of such defendant and such a decree shall have the same force and effect as if it was pronounced before the death had taken place - Appeal dismissed.
