Biecco Lawrie Ltd. & Anr. v State Of West Bengal & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

28 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Tarun Chatterjee, H.S. Bedi

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 928; (2009) 10 SCC 32; AIR 2010 SC 142; 2010 (124) FLR 108; JT 2009 (10) SC 340; 2009 (4) LLN 91; 2009 (8) MLJ(SC) 451; 2009(10) SCALE 334; [2009] 11 S.C.R. 972; 2010 (1) SLR 649; 2009 (4) UPLBEC 3171

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Discharge Simpliciter

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 7A - Misconduct - Instigation and insubordination - Dismissal - Dispute raised - Tribunal upheld dismissal - On appeal, Single Judge of HC remanded matter back to Tribunal - On remand, Tribunal held that appellant failed to establish charges against respondent and directed reinstatement with full back wages - Appellants challenged aforesaid order before HC - Single Judge and DB of HC upheld order of Tribunal - Present special leave petitions - P /&gt;(A) Principles of natural justice - Violation of - Principle of natural justice attracted whenever a person suffers a civil consequence or prejudice being caused to him by administrative action - Both sides should be heard and person should be served with proper notice - In present case, charges laid down in charge sheet were precise and specific in nature along with relevant provision of standing order and neatly lays down consequences thereof - Held, respondent furnished with proper notices - Respondent was fully aware of charges and even their specifications and also probable witnesses for his misconduct - Respondent had been afforded every opportunity to defend his case in most desirable manner - No violation of principles of natural justice - P /&gt;(B) Whether Tribunal justified in reversing its own decision subsequently when there had been no further evidence adduced? - Held, no - Tribunal erred in reversing its own decision on same evidence - Difficult to understand how same forum could appreciate same evidence differently - P /&gt;(C) Whether HC right in their appreciation of evidence and exercising power in matter of interfering with order of dismissal? - General trend of judicial decisions is to minimize interference when punishment is not harsh and definitely for charges that are leveled against respondent and in instant matter, dismissal is absolutely not shocking to conscience of court - Punishment was not harsh in comparison to the charges leveled against the respondent - Held, - Single Judge misused power vested in him by remanding back matter to industrial tribunal for reconsideration when charges were found to be proved - Impugned judgment and order of Division Bench of HC as well as of Single Judge liable to be set aside - Order of dismissal passed against respondent restored - Appeal allowed.
