Badshah v Urmila Badshah Godse and another

Supreme Court of India

18 October 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 691; (2014) 1 SCC 188; AIR 2014 SC 869; 2013 (101) ALR 704; 2014 CRLJ 1076; 2013 (6) CTC 86; 2013 (4) JCC 2765; 2013 (4) KLT 367; 2014 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 489; 2014(2) M.P.H.T. 499; 2013 (4) RCR(Civil) 830; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 764; 2013 (4) RLW 3670; 2014 (2) RLW 1841; 2013(12) SCALE 681; [2013] 10 S.C.R. 259

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Women & Children

Summary: Family & Personal - Women & Children - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 125 - Claim of maintenance - Respondent No. 1 filed application u/s. 125 of CrPC claiming maintenance from petitioner - It was stated that respondent No. 1 married petitioner as per Hindu Rites and customs - After her marriage, she resided and cohabited with the petitioner - Later, it came to the knowledge of respondent No. 1 that petitioner was already married and was having two children out of the wedlock - It was alleged that, respondent No. 1 came back to the house of her parents where respondent No.2, was born - On the averments, the respondents claimed maintenance for themselves - Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC) proceeded on the basis that the petitioner was married to 'S' and was having two children out of the wedlock - However, at the time of solemnizing the marriage with respondent No.1, the petitioner intentionally suppressed this fact from her and co-habited with respondent No.1 as his wife - JMFC allowed the application filed by respondent No. 1 - HC upheld the award of maintenance - Hence, instant petition - Whether respondents were entitled to claim compensation from petitioner - Held, at the time when the petitioner married the respondent No.1, he had living wife and the said marriage was still subsisting - Therefore, under the provisions of Act, the petitioner could not have married second time - However, petitioner duped respondent No.1 by not revealing the fact of his first marriage and pretending that he was single - After this marriage both lived together and respondent No.2 was also born from this wedlock - Hence, petitioner could not be permitted to deny the benefit of maintenance to the respondent, taking advantage of his own wrong - View taken by the courts below was justified - Petition dismissed.
