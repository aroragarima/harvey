Nagabhushanammal (Dead) By Lrs. v C. Chandikeswaralingam

Supreme Court of India

26 February 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Kurian Joseph, R.F. Nariman

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 182; (2016) 4 SCC 434; AIR 2016 SC 1134; 2016 (3) ALD(SC) 92; 2016 (116) ALR 508; 2016 (3) AWC 2721; 2016 (1) CLT(SC) 276; 2016 (2) CTC 345; JT 2016 (2) SC 465; 2016 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1857; 2016 (2) MLJ 328; 2016 (1) OLR 842; 2016 (2) RCR(Civil) 469; 2016 (132) RD 160; 2016(3) SCALE 5; [2016] 2 S.C.R. 19

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Res Judicata, Adverse Possession, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 11 - Suit for Partition - Res-judicata Challenged - Appellant/plaintiff's father settled suit property in favour of plaintiff husband, who subsequently settled property in plaintiff's name - Trial Court dismissed suit for possession of suit property on basis of settlement - However, plaintiff filed another suit seeking partition of suit property, which was dismissed on ground of res judicata and adverse possession of defendant was upheld- First Appellate Court set aside judgment of Trial Court and decreed suit - HC in second appeal by respondent, set aside judgment of First Appellate Court and decree that of Trial Court dismissing suit was restored - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, 'Res judicata' literally means a 'thing adjudicated' or 'an issue that has been definitively settled by judicial decision'. As per s.11 of CPC, if the matter was in issue directly and substantially in a prior litigation and decided against a party then the decision would be res judicata in a subsequent proceeding. In any event the filing of subsequent Suit is nothing but re-litigation. The principle operates as a bar to try the same issue once over. In instant case, the 1962 suit for the entire property was based on a settlement deed and it was a suit for possession. Whereas, the 1988 suit for partition was for plaintiff's one-half share in the property based on her birth right. Cause of action is entirely different. Hence, suit property is partitioned and appellants shall be entitled to 35% and respondents 65%. Appeals disposed of.
