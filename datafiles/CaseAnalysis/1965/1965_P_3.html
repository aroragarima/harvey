Commissioner of Income Tax, Madras v P. K. N. Company Limited

Supreme Court of India

10 December 1965

Case Analysis

Bench

J.C. Shah, S.M. Sikri, K. Subba Rao

Where Reported

1965 Indlaw SC 320; AIR 1966 SC 1256; [1966] 60 ITR 65; 1966 (1) MLJ(SC) 149

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Adventure In The Nature Of Trade

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, s. 66(2) - Pudukottai Company Regulation V of 1929 - Partnership - Purchase - Assessment - Acquisition - Incorporation - Partnership was carrying on money-lending business - In course of its business respondent/firm acquired rubber estates and other immovables - Share capital of company was of face value of 6,60,000 Malayan dollars - Between 23-03-1939 and 8-07-1939, an area of more than 3,000 acres of rubber plantations, several houses and open plots of land, which were assets of firm, were transferred to company for an aggregate consideration of 16, 50, 000 Malayan dollars - Company purchased a rubber estate for 2, 62, 655 Malayan dollars - Company purchased for 5,000 Malayan dollars a house in co-ownership with another firm - On 1-08-1949, company was assessed by Income-tax Officer as a dealer in real estate and profits amounting to 34,272 Malayan dollars and 40, 613 Malayan dollars were brought to tax in assessment years 1949-50 and 1950-51 - Income-tax Appellate Tribunal set aside orders - Hence instant appeal - Whether surplus of $1, 41,326 realised by assesse/-company by sale of some of its estates and properties was income chargeable to tax under 1922 Act - Held, assessee/company did acquire two large blocks of properties between years 1939 and 1941, but thereafter no substantial acquisitions were made - Limitation upon admission of members to company and other attendant features suggest an intention of conserving properties of members of firm - Some of houses purchased from firm were destroyed by fire and vacant sites and outlying properties were sold on account of difficulty of management of outlying portions of estate - Occasional sales of small and unimportant portions out of estate acquired from firm was motivated by necessity and not to realize profits - circumstance that a single plot of land was acquired and was thereafter sold as a whole or in plots decisive - Profit motive in entering into a transaction was also not decisive - Incidental sale of uneconomic or inconvenient plots of land or houses could not convert what was essentially an investment into a business transaction in real estate - Appeal dismissed.
