Samir Chandra Das v Bibhas Chandra Das and others

Supreme Court of India

07 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 346; (2010) 6 SCC 432; AIR 2010 SC 1962; 2010 (6) ALD(SC) 109; 2010(3) ALL MR 906; 2010 (81) ALR 251; 2010 (4) AWC 3682; 2011 (3) Bom.C.R. 248; 2010 (3) CalLT 93; 2010 (3) G.L.R. 2183; JT 2010 (4) SC 579; 2010 (6) MLJ(SC) 426; 2010 (4) MPLJ 156; 2010 (3) RCR(Civil) 238; 2010 (110) RD 725; 2010(5) SCALE 359; [2010] 6 S.C.R. 528; 2010 (3) UC 1581

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Family & Personal

Summary: Civil Procedure - Family & Personal - Indian Succession Act, 1925, ss. 223, 229 and 230 - Grant of probate - Concept of deemed renunciation - Testator expired leaving his widow and four sons and three daughters - In his Will, he named his widow and appellant (one of the sons) as the executors - A property was given to the widow for life and thereafter to the three sons of the testator on the condition that the three sons would bear the maintenance and marriage expenses of the two unmarried daughters for which the wife had specific authority to sell the land even during her life time to meet the expenses of maintenance and marriage of the two daughters, if the three sons did not bear the same - During the lifetime of testator, two sale deeds were executed by widow and a part of land was sold - These sale deeds were countersigned by appellant and testator - Remaining land was also sold during the pendency of probate proceedings - Trial Court came to the conclusion that the Will was genuine and was validly executed and attested - Court ordered grant of probate - On appeal, Appellate Court, concluded that both the executors having espoused an interest over the subject matter of the Will which was adverse to the interest of the testator, no probate could be granted in their favour since by their conduct they had renounced the executorship - Appeal was allowed and the suit was dismissed - Hence, present appeal - Held, at the time of first two sale deeds, even the testator had put his signatures as a witness and as he was alive on that day, the Will was irrelevant - Therefore, if the earlier two sale deeds were executed during the lifetime of the testator and he himself had acted as a witness, there was no question of any rival or hostile title being set up by widow and further by the appellant who put his signatures as a witness alongwith his father, the testator on the sale deeds - It cannot be presumed that there was any idea of setting up a hostile title - Remaining property which was sold during the pendency of the probate application was admittedly a part of the aforementioned property - When the sale deed was executed, widow and her two daughters had inherited the property under the Will, which they sold and they were undoubtedly the owners of the properties - There was no renunciation or deemed renunciation - HC erred in taking the stand that the executor had taken a hostile stance against the testator - Order of the Appellate Court set aside - Matter remanded back to the appellate Court for decision on merits regarding the valid execution or attestation of the Will - Appeal allowed.
