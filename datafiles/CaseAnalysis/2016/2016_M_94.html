Commissioner, M. P. Housing Board and others v Mohanlal and Company

Supreme Court of India

19 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Rohinton Fali Nariman

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 992; AIR 2016 SC 3592; 2016 (6) ALD(SC) 53; 2016 (119) ALR 499; 2016 (4) CLT(SC) 113; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 140; 2016(7) SCALE 321; [2016] 3 S.C.R. 357

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitral Award, Jurisdiction, Limitation Act, 1963, Limitation, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 11, 34, 34(2), 34(3) - Limitation Act, 1963, ss. 14, 14(1), 34(2) - Arbitral proceedings - Jurisdiction - Validity - Respondent entered into a contract with appellant - Disputes arose between parties and Arbitrator did not find any justification to allow any of the claims of respondent - Respondent filed an application u/s. 14 of the 1963 Act seeking that he was bonafidely prosecuting the case in Court having no jurisdiction - District Judge allowed application holding that respondent was entitled to exclusion of time u/s. 14 of the 1963 Act - Appellant filed revision before HC which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether in the obtaining factual scenario, s. 14 of the Act would be applicable.

Held, in instant case respondent appeared before arbitrator and after the award was passed, chose not to file any objection to the award immediately. S. 14(1) of the 1963 Act lays down that the proceedings should relate to the same matter in issue. It emphasises on due diligence and good faith. Filing of an application u/s. 11 of the 1996 Act for an appointment of arbitrator was totally different than an objection to award filed u/s. 34 of the 1996 Act. One was at the stage of initiation, and the other at the stage of culmination. Therefore, proceedings relate to "same matter in issue". Additionally, respondent had participated in the arbitral proceeding and was aware of passing of award. Respondent might be by design, invoked the jurisdiction of HC for appointment of an arbitrator. Further, liberal interpretation should be placed on s. 14 of the 1963 Act, but if the fact situation exposits absence of good faith of great magnitude, law should not come to the rescue of such a litigant. Respondent instead of participating in arbitration proceedings, could have immediately taken steps for appointment of arbitrator as he thought appropriate or he could have filed his objections u/s. 34(2) of the 1996 Act within permissible parameters but he chose a way, which was an innovative path, possibly harbouring the thought that he could contrive the way where he could alone rule that was neither diligence nor good faith and it was absence of both. HC has fallen into grave error by concurring with the opinion expressed by District Judge and both the orders deserve to be lancinated. Appeal allowed.
