Hussainara Khatoon and Others (I) v Home Secretary, State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

12 February 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, A.D. Koshal, R.S. Pathak

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 113; (1980) SCC (Cr) 35; (1980) 1 SCC 81; AIR 1979 SC 1360; 1979 CRLJ 1036; 1980 MLJ 76; [1979] 3 S.C.R. 169

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Constitution; Criminal; International

Keywords: European Convention On Human Rights, Debt Laws

Summary: Human Rights - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Pre-trial release - Prolonged detention in prison of undertrials - Judicial power to release a prisoner awaiting trial on bail or on execution of a personal bond without sureties - Held, if the Court is satisfied, after taking into account, on the basis of information placed before it, that the accused has his roots in the community and is not likely to abscond, it can safely release the accused on his personal bond - Decision as regards the amount of the bond should be an individualised decision depending on the individual financial circumstances of the accused and the probability of his absconding - Amount of the bond should be determined having regard to these relevant factors and should not be fixed mechanically according to a schedule keyed to the nature of the charge - Order accordingly.

Criminal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Delay in disposal of case - Violation of fundamental rights - Held, though speedy trial is not specifically enumerated as a fundamental right, it is implicit in the broad sweep and content of art. 21 of the Constitution as interpreted by SC in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India 1978 Indlaw SC 212 wherein it was held that art. 21 confers a fundamental right on every person not to be deprived of his life or liberty except in accordance with the procedure prescribed by law and it is not enough to constitute compliance with the requirement of that article that some semblance of a procedure should be prescribed by law, but that the procedure should be 'reasonable, fair and just' - Hence, procedure prescribed by law for depriving a person of his liberty cannot be 'reasonable, and fair or just' unless that procedure ensures a speedy trial for determination of the guilt of such person - No procedure which does not ensures a reasonably quick trial can be regarded as 'reasonable, fair or just' and it would fall foul of art. 21 - There can, therefore, be no doubt that speedy trial, is an integral and essential part of the fundamental right to life and liberty enshrined in art. 21 - Order accordingly.
