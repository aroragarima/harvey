Â Lord Krishna Sugar Mills Limited, and Another v Union of India and Another

Supreme Court of India

06 May 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Syed Jaffer Imam, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 235; AIR 1959 SC 1124; 1960 SCJ 1119; [1960] 1 S.C.R. 39

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Trade

Summary: (A) Trade - Constitution - Sugar Export Promotion Act, 1958 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Sundry manufactured commodities - Discrimination - Legality - Petitioners filed instant writ petitions challenging Constitutionality of the Act and also legality of certain orders passed by respondent no. 2 purporting to be under the Act - Petitioners contended that manufacturers of commodities other than sugar were not compelled to export in a like manner, and thus there was discrimination - Held, it was quite obvious that Central Govt. could not order export of all and sundry manufactured commodities from country, without being assured of a market in foreign countries - Necessarily, Govt. could only embark on export policy in relation to these products, for which there was an easy and readily available market abroad - For such reason, sugar produced by vacuum pan process might have been selected, because such sugar was perhaps in demand abroad and not sugar produced by any other process - It should be realised that goods manufactured in our country had to stand heavy competition from goods produced abroad, and even such export could only be made at great sacrifice, and was made only to earn foreign exchange, which would not, otherwise, be available - Thus, it could not be said that there was discrimination in so far as sugar manufacturers by vacuum pan process were concerned - Govt. was best judge as to which commodities were most likely to earn foreign exchange, and selection thus made was justifiable as a reasonable classification which was related to object of the Act, earning of foreign exchange - Petitions dismissed.

(B) Trade - Constitution - Sugar Export Promotion Act, 1958 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(f), 19(1)(g) - Infringement of fundamental rights - Legality - Whether whole export programme in respect of sugar amounts to an infringement of petitioners fundamental rights u/arts. 19(1)(f) and 19(1)(g) of Constitution and amounts to a compulsory acquisition of their property without payment of compensation - Held, object of the Act did not infringe fundamental rights of petitioners - To prevent any loss to petitioners, countervailing additional prices were allowed on sales of sugar for internal consumption - Petitioners did not stand to lose ultimately, as quota was fixed at 2-1/2 per cent of their total production, and it was inconceivable that they were unable to sell sugar in open home market - Hence, there could be no ground for holding that there was an infringement of fundamental rights of petitioners - Further, restriction was not unreasonable, because arrangement was made to save owners of factories from loss, and loss entailed by export of sugar was to be borne by consumers in India and not by producers - Owners received payment after sale prices were received from abroad - Necessary deductions of expenses have to be made, and proceeds were then distributed - No doubt, such payment was likely to be somewhat delayed but looking to small quantity involved (i.e. not more than 20 per cent under the Act and in actuality, only 2-1/2 per cent) it was not likely to make it very hard for owners, who were in meantime breaking such loss at the rate of 50 nP. for every maund of sugar sold in India - Thus, none of sections considered were even viewed separately, were ultra vires - Petitions dismissed.
