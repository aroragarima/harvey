Director General of Foreign Trade and another v Kanak Exports and another

Supreme Court of India

27 October 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.F. Nariman

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 871; (2016) 2 SCC 226; JT 2015 (12) SC 135; 2015(12) SCALE 123

Case Digest

Subject: Customs

Keywords: Promissory Estoppel, EXCHANGE CONTROL & FDI, Capital Goods, Imported Goods, Part, Omission, Importer, Exim Policy, Licensing Authority, Foreign Trade (Development And Regulation) Act, 1992, General Clauses Act, 1897, India, Give, Imports and Exports (Control) Act, 1947, Retention, Repatriation, Export Oriented Units

Summary: Customs - Failure to grant benefit - Retrospective effect of notification - Justification - Export Import (EXIM) Policy 2002-2007 was framed by Central Govt. - Further, vide notification dt. 28-1-2004, Govt. sought to amend certain provisions of EXIM Policy by which exporters were denied certain benefits - Further, vide other notification, Govt. announced exclusion of export performance in relation to four classes of goods from computation of entitlement under Scheme - Bombay HC gave partial relief to exporters, however, Gujarat HC has substantially affirmed validity of notifications while giving relief on one particular aspect - Gujarat HC held that main purpose of notification was to prevent transfer of export orders from one group company to another company belonging to same group in order to show enhanced export performance of such another company and, therefore, it was clarificatory in nature - Hence, present appeal.

Held, purport behind Notifications was bona fide which was actuated with conditions of public interest in mind. Further, with respect to notification being clarificatory in nature, SC held that amendments do not impinge upon right of any party to export its goods in accordance with EXIM policy. Clarification only excludes exports which were never intended in first place to be covered by Special Scheme under consideration. Further, Sub-note (ii) of Note 1 now provides that export turnover of units pertaining to SEZ/EOU/EHTP/STP or products manufactured by them and exported through DTA units are not to be included and taken into account for purpose of calculating value of exports. Further, both HCs in impugned judgments have held it to be clarificatory on ground that such export turnover was excluded as these units, namely, those pertaining to SEZ/EOU/EHTP/STP schemes are getting all facilities for import without payment of duty on various types of goods including capital goods required by them for their activities and there was no intention in original scheme also to confer double benefit. SC held that petitioners could not refute or deny that such SEZ/EOU//EHTP/STP are getting benefit of exports made by them in form of facilities for import without payment of duty on various types of goods including capital goods required by them for their activities. Hence, exactly same benefit which is sought to be given to status holders for achieving incremental growth as provided in scheme was already conferred upon. Further, purpose of scheme was not to give double benefit for same exports. Further, if that is allowed, it would be clear case of misuse of scheme inasmuch as for same export turnover units operating under SEZ/EOU/EHTP/STP would get certain incentives and status holders also manage to extract same benefits exploiting scheme by exporting goods manufactured by these STZ/EOU etc. Hence, SC agreed with opinion of HC that sub-note (ii) was merely clarificatory in nature. Further, Sub-note (v) to Note 1 stipulates that if supply were made by one status holder to another status holder, these shall also be excluded while calculating value of exports. Likewise, sub-note (vi) of Note 1 excludes export performance made by one status holder on behalf of other status holder, SC held that having regard to nature of sub-note (v) and that two status-holders if they carry out exports and made target as per Scheme were entitled to benefit of Scheme, SC agree with HC that even insertion of these clauses is clarificatory in nature inasmuch as it only states that supply made by one status-holder to another status-holder will not be counted. This clarification was issued, as rightly pointed out by HC's, to ensure that two status-holders belonging to same group may not start pooling and try to take undue advantage. Further, with respect to sub-note (vii) of Note 1 is concerned, it stipulates that supplies made or export performance affected by non status holder to status holder will not be taken into account for purpose of calculating value of exports, if applicant as well as non status holder have less than 25% incremental growth over their respective previous years. SC held that it is clearly clarificatory in nature inasmuch as purpose of Scheme was to give benefit to those who are able to achieve incremental growth of 25%. Thus, each such status holder has to independently attain growth target stipulated in scheme to avail benefit. Further, if it has not been able to achieve 25% incremental growth, such export house cannot take advantage by including exports of non status holders to show that it has achieved 25% incremental growth. Hence, notification in question is clarificatory in nature and its validity stands upheld. Further, with respect to Public Notice issued by DGFT, which sought to exclude export performance related to class of goods, is within jurisdiction, SC held that Public Notice alters provisions of EXIM Policy which power is with Central Govt. It would, therefore, amount to amending EXIM Policy, whether clarificatory or otherwise. SC held that Govt. itself realised that DGFT had no such power. It is for this reason that what was sought to be achieved by said Public Notice, was formalised by Central Govt. by issuing notifications dt. 21/ 23-4-2004 - 96. Hence, public notice dt. 28-1-2004 issued by DGFT, so far it excludes four items, is ultra vires. Further, with respect to notifications bad in law on ground that they seek to apply retrospectively, SC held that s. 5 of Act does not give any such power specifically to Central Govt. to make rules retrospective. Hence, exporters had actually achieved targets set down in original Scheme and thereby acquired any 'vested right'. Hence, impugned Notification issued u/s. 5of Act could not be retrospective in nature, such retrospectivity have not deprived writ petitioners of their right inasmuch as no right had accrued in favour of such persons under Scheme. Further, with respect to Constitutional validity of EXIM Policy 2004-2009 and Notification dt. 20-2- 2006 and 12-6-2006, by which certain amendments in aforesaid EXIM Policy were made and were given retrospective effect, SC held that s. 5 of Act does not empower Govt. to make amendments with retrospective effect, thereby taking away rights which have already accrued in favour of exporters under Scheme. Hence, notification dt. 20-2-2006 and 12-6-2002 cannot be applied retrospectively and they would be effective only from dates they were issued. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Policies framed by Govt. cannot be quashed when same are in accordance with law and are framed to protect interest of party.
