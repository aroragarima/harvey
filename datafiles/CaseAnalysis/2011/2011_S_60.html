State of Rajasthan and others v Shankar Lal Parmar

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Verma, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 610; (2011) 14 SCC 235; AIR 2012 SC 1913; 2013 (1) AWC 135; 2011 (5) ESC 749; 2011 (131) FLR 944; 2012 (2) MLJ(SC) 314; 2011(11) SCALE 360; [2011] 11 S.C.R. 762; 2011 (5) SLR 722

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Summary: Service - Selection Grade - Entitlement - Appellant State issued Office Order dated 25-1-1992 prescribing Selection Grades to lowest posts in Class IV Ministerial Subordinate Services for resolving the problem of stagnation wherein cl. 7 of the Order stipulated that only those employees would be entitled for grant of Selection Grades, whose service record had been satisfactory - Appellant State, in order to clarify the position, issued another Circular dated 23-7-1992 stating that the censure should not be taken into account as unsatisfactory service for the purpose of grant of Selection Grade - Appellant State started granting Selection Grades to all those employees, who had completed requisite number of years in service, even if they had earned censure in previous years but had not been promoted - Appellant State issued another Office Order dated 24-7-1995 stating that if an employee had earned censure during his service, then his grant of Selection Grade would be deferred by one year - However, such clarification was issued by the appellant State after expiry of almost more than 3 years from the date of issuance of the first Office Order on 25-1-1992 - During the interregnum period between 25-1-1992 to 24-7-1995, certain employees were granted the benefit of the Selection Grades, despite having earned censure - After issuance of the subsequent Office Order dated 24-7-1995, appellant State started the recovery of the amounts from those employees who were granted Selection Grades even though they had earned censure - On the strength of an order pronounced by DB of the HC in the matter of Devi Singh v. State of Rajasthan & Ors. (reported in 2004 (2) CDR-925 (Raj)), several matters came to be filed in the HC - HC allowed the writ petitions without appreciating or adverting to the ratio decidendi of Devi Singh case properly - Whether an employee would be entitled for the grant of Selection Grade, automatically, at the first instance, after the completion of 9 years, at the second instance, after the completion of 18 years and at the third and last instance, after the completion of 27 years of service, even when he had earned censure in the past years of service - Held, all the circulars collectively stipulated that for the purposes of grant of Selection Grade, in cases where an employee had earned a censure, the censure should not be treated either as an impediment or obstruction for consideration of his promotion but his case for such a grant would be deferred by one year - In Devi Singh case, it had been decided that an employee who had already been granted the benefit of Selection Grade, such benefits could not be taken back by the Appellant State, without issuance of a Show Cause Notice to him in this regard - Thus, primarily and basically, it was decided in favour of Devi Singh on the ground of violation of Principles of Natural Justice, however, the cases filed subsequently before the HC were not same - It cannot be said that an employee who had earned censure would automatically be entitled for promotion or respective Selection Grade after the completion of 9, 18 or 27 years of service - However, during the interregnum period between the first Office Order, issued on 25-1-1992 and the subsequent clarificatory Office Order dated 24-7-1995, some of the employees were granted the benefit of Selection Grade - Appellant State would not be entitled to claim refund from such employees who had already been granted benefit in that period - Subsequent Office Order further made it clear that all those employees who had earned censure in service should also be entitled for the Selection Grade but the grant of Selection Grade to them would be deferred by one year - It was an absolute reasonable and perfect classification as otherwise every employee who had a clean image and another employee, who had earned censure would be treated at par, which was not permissible in the service jurisprudence and was also in violation of art. 14 of the Constitution - Appellant State was fully justified in issuing the subsequent Office Order dated 24-7-1995, putting all controversies at rest - No discrimination had been made out against the respondents/employees - Office Order dated 24-7-1995 cannot be said to be illegal, arbitrary, unconstitutional or without authority of law - Impugned HC orders were set aside - Appeal allowed.
