Competition Commission of India v Co-ordination Committee of Artists and Technicians of W. B. Film and Television and others

Supreme Court of India

07 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 165; (2017) 5 SCC 17; AIR 2017 SC 1449; 2017(4) ALL MR 432; 2017 (2) AWC 1856; 2017 (2) CompLJ 372; 2017 (2) CPJ(Sc) 5; 2017 (3) MLJ 866; 2017(3) SCALE 511

Case Digest

Subject: Competition; Media & Entertainment

Keywords: Trade and Merchandise Marks Act, 1958, Copyright Act, 1957, Patents Act, 1970, Designs Act, 2000, Trade Marks Act, 1999, Competition Act, 2002, Geographical Indications of Goods (Registration and Protection) Act, 1999, Trade Union Act, Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: (A) Competition - Media & Entertainment - Competition Act, 2002, ss. 19, 2(l), 2(r), 3, 3(1), 3(3), 3(3)(b) - Dubbed serial telecast - Objections raised - Permissibility - An advertisement was issued in Daily Newspapers informing public that serial would be telecast in Bangla - Eastern India Motion Picture Association (EIMPA) and artists and Committee of Artists and Technicians of West Bengal Film and Television Investors (Coordination Committee) raised objections - Respondent no. 2/informant sought direction from appellants not to yield to threats of EIMPA and Coordination Committee and restart the telecast of serial - Director General (DG) concluded that actions on part of EIMPA and Coordination Committee were in violation of the provisions of s. 3(3)(b) of the Act, since they restricted and controlled the market and supply of dubbed versions of serials - Coordination Committee as well as EIMPA raised objections with regard to jurisdiction of DG before appellants which was dismissed affirming DG's report - On appeal, Tribunal allowed appeal holding that there was no contravention of s. 3 of the Act by Coordination Committee - Hence, instant appeal - What was the 'relevant market' for purposes of inquiry into impugned activity of Coordination Committee.

Held, while identifying the relevant market in a given case, appellant was required to look at evidence that was available and relevant to the case at hand. Appellant has to define the boundaries of relevant market as precisely as required by the circumstances of the case. Where appropriate, it may conduct its competition assessment on the basis of alternative market definitions. In instant case, the geographic market was State and to that extent there was no quarrel inasmuch as activities of Coordination Committee were limited to said State. Tribunal has upheld the minority view of appellant in saying that nature of the information does not show anything which could even be distinctly connected with the whole 'film and television industry in State'. The information was only against showing the dubbed serial on television and it has no relation whatsoever with production, distribution, etc. of any film or any other material on the TV channels. SC felt that such was a myopic view taken by Tribunal which ignores many other vital aspects of case, most important being the width of the effect of the aforesaid cause on which the agitation was led by Coordination Committee. The effect was not limited to the telecast or broadcast of the television serial. No doubt, Coordination Committee was against the 'broadcast of the television serial however, even as per the agitators, the said broadcast was going to adversely affect TV and Film Industry of State and alleged purport behind the threats was to save the entire TV and Film Industry. Appeal allowed.

(B) Competition - Media & Entertainment - Competition Act, 2002, ss. 19, 2(l), 2(r), 3, 3(1), 3(3), 3(3)(b) - Action of committee - Violation - Validity - Whether the action and conduct of Coordination Committee was covered by the provisions of s. 3 of the Act.

Held, in instant case admittedly Coordination Committee was not undertaking any economic activity by itself. Coordination Committee, which was a trade union acting by itself, and without conjunction with any other, would not be treated as an 'enterprise' or the kind of 'association of persons' described in s. 3 of the Act. However, what was lost in translation by Tribunal i.e. in applying the aforesaid principle of the activity of the trade union, is a very pertinent and significant fact, which was taken note of by the DG as well as appellant in its majority opinion. When the lenses of reasoning process were duly adjusted with their focus on picture, the picture gets sharpened and haziness disappears. One can clearly view that prohibition on the exhibition of dubbed serial on the television prevented the competing parties in pursuing their commercial activities. Thus, appellant rightly observed that the protection in the name of language goes against the interest of competition, depriving consumers of exercising their choice. Acts of Coordination Committee definitely caused harm to consumers by depriving them from watching the dubbed serial on TV channel albeit for a brief period. It also hindered competition in the market by barring dubbed TV serials from exhibition on TV channels in State. It amounted to creating barriers to the entry of new content in the said dubbed TV serial. Such act and conduct also limited the supply of serial dubbed in Bangla, which amounts to violation of the provision of s. 3(3)(b) of the Act. Appeal allowed.
