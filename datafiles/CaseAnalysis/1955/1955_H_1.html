Â Matajog Dobey v H. C. Bhari

Supreme Court of India

31 October 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Chandrasekhara Aiyar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose, Syed Jaffer Imam, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 44; AIR 1956 SC 44; 1956 CRLJ 140; [1955] 28 ITR 941; 1956 (1) MLJ(SC) 79; 1956 SCJ 110; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 925

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Criminal - Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 197 - Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 136 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 323, 341, 342, 109 and 504 - Taxation on Income (Investigation Commission) Act, 1947, ss. 6(7), 6(9), 5(1) and r.8 - Warrant issued to search premises by Income Tax Investigation Commission to four officials - Forcibly entered and when challenged assaulted two persons - Two complaints were sent over for judicial inquiry to two different Magistrates - Magistrate held in first complaint that a prima facie case had been made out u/s. 323 against all four accused and u/s. 342 against two policemen - Respondent filed petition taking objection of want of sanction u/s. 197, CrPC - Objection was upheld and all accused discharged - Same objection raised in second complaint as well - Criminal revision petition - Held, officers could reasonably claim that what they did was done by them in exercise of their official duty - Appeal - (A) Whether s. 197 is discriminatory in nature? - Art. 14 does not render s. 197, CrPC, ultra vires as discrimination is based upon a rational classification - Further held, public servants have to be protected from harassment in discharge of official duties while ordinary citizens not so engaged do not require this safeguard - (B) Whether s. 197, CrPC vests an absolute arbitary power in Govt. to grant or withhold sanction? - Held, there is no question of any discrimination between one person and another in matter of taking proceedings against a public servant - It has to be borne in mind that a discretionary power is not necessarily a discriminatory power and that abuse of power is not to be easily assumed where discretion is vested in Govt. and not in a minor official - Further held, Court could take cognizance of case without previous sanction and for this purpose Court has to find out if act complained against was committed by accused while acting or purporting to act in discharge of official duty - Once this is settled, case proceeds or is thrown out - (C) Whether use of force in execution of their duty by authorised officials was outside their scope of employment? - Held, there must be a reasonable connection between act and official duty - It does not matter even if act exceeds what is strictly necessary for discharge of duty, as this question will arise only at a later stage - Further held, where a power is conferred or a duty imposed by statute or otherwise, and there is nothing said expressly inhibiting exercise of power or performance of duty by any limitations or restrictions, it is reasonable to hold that it carries with it power of doing all such acts or employing such means as are reasonably necessary for such execution - Appeals dismissed.
