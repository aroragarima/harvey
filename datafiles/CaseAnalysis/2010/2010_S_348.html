Brahm Swaroop & Anr v State Of U.P.

Supreme Court of India

26 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 944; (2010) 6 SCC 288; AIR 2011 SC 280; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 3975; 2011 CRLJ 306; JT 2010 (11) SC 437; 2010 (4) RCR(Criminal) 898; 2010(11) SCALE 443; 2010 (3) UC 1858

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 148, 149, 302, 307 and 396 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 174 - Chargesheet u/ss. 396, 148, 302 r/w ss. 149, 307/149 of IPC was submitted against accused persons - Trial Court acquitted A.5 and A.6 of all the charges but convicted A.3 and A.2 u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC and were awarded death sentence - A.1 and A.4 were convicted u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 of IPC and were awarded imprisonment for life - On appeals, HC dismissed the appeal with the modification that the sentence of death imposed by Trial Court was altered to life imprisonment and also convicted A.5 and A.6 u/ss. 302 and 307 r/w s. 34 of IPC - Present appeals - (A) Whether omission in the inquest is fatal to the prosecution's case and such omissions would necessarily lead to the inference that FIR is ante-timed? - Held, object of the proceedings u/s. 174 of CrPC is merely to ascertain whether a person died under suspicious circumstances or met with an unnatural death and, if so, what was its apparent cause - Omissions in the inquest report are not sufficient to put the prosecution out of court - Inquest report cannot be treated as substantive evidence but may be utilised for contradicting the witnesses of inquest - Hence, it cannot be held that any omission or discrepancy in the inquest is fatal to the prosecution's case and such omissions would necessarily lead to the inference that FIR is ante-timed - (B) Delay in sending report to the Magistrate - Effect thereof - Held, defence did not put any question in this regard to the investigating officer, thus, no explanation was required to be furnished by him on this issue - Thus, the prosecution had not been asked to explain the delay in sending the special report - More so, evidence on record showed that the FIR was lodged promptly - FIR contained all the essential features of the prosecution's case including names of eye witnesses, time and place of incident, names of the victim, motive, name of the accused persons, weapons in their hands and manner of assault - (C) Whether testimonies of witnesses can be discarded on ground that they were closely related to the deceased persons? - Held, their relationship to one of the parties is not a factor that effects the credibility of a witness, more so, a relation would not conceal the actual culprit and make allegations against an innocent person - A party has to lay down a factual foundation and prove by leading impeccable evidence in respect of its false implication - Hence, merely because the witnesses were closely related to the deceased persons, their testimonies cannot be discarded - (D) Appeal against acquittal - Interference by Appellate Court - Held, in exceptional cases where there are compelling circumstances, and the judgment under appeal is found to be perverse, the appellate court can interfere with the order of acquittal - In the present case, acquittal of A.5 and A.6 by the Trial Court cannot be held to be based on cogent reasons - HC rightly reversed their acquittals for offences u/ss. 304/34 and 307/149 of IPC, but has rightly upheld their acquittal u/s. 25 of the Arms Act - Appeal dismissed.
