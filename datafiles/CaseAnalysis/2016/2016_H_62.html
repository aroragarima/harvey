Centrotrade Minerals and Metal Inc v Hindustan Copper Limited

Supreme Court of India

15 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, R.K. Agrawal, D.Y. Chandrachud

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 1035; (2017) 2 SCC 228; AIR 2017 SC 185; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 144; 2017 (3) MahLJ 771; 2017 (2) RCR(Civil) 706B; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 83

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitration Agreement, Arbitral Award, Arbitral Tribunal, Jurisdiction, Registration Act, 1908, Arbitration Act, 1940, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Settlement, Arbitration Clause, Conciliation, Uncitral, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Delhi Hindustan Mercantile Association Rules

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 34, 34(1), 35, 48, 36 - Breach of Terms of Contract - Two-tier Arbitration - Challenged - Appellant/company entered into agreement with respondent /company and appellant invoked arbitration clause to settled dispute - Arbitrator passed nil award and on invocation of second part of clause, Arbitrator in London passed award in accordance with rules - Appellant filed application u/s. 48 of the Act for enforcement of award - DB of HC dismissed appeal of appellant and allowed appeal of respondent - However, in view of difference of opinion, instant reference was made before large bench - Whether 1996 Act prohibits two-tier arbitration system by necessary implication.

Held, an arbitral award can be defined as a final decision by the arbitrators on all or part of the dispute submitted to them, whether it concerns the merits of the dispute, jurisdiction, or a procedural issue leading them to end the proceedings. The arbitration result in instant case has all the elements and ingredients of an arbitration award. However, combined reading of s. 34(1) and 35 of the Act ,an arbitral award would be final and binding on the parties unless it is set aside by a competent court on an application made by a party to the arbitral award. This does not exclude the autonomy of the parties to an arbitral award to mutually agree to a procedure whereby the arbitral award might be reconsidered by another arbitrator or panel of arbitrators by way of an appeal and the result of that appeal is accepted by the parties to be final and binding subject to a challenge provided for by the Act. In instant case, the 'arbitration result' in the first part of cl.14 of the contract must mean an arbitration award given by the arbitral panel of the Indian Council of Arbitration. However, the plain language of cl. 14 specifically provides for a second arbitration, in the form of an 'appeal' against the award of the arbitration panel of the Indian Council of Arbitration. In other words, party autonomy is the guiding principle in determining the procedure to be followed in an international arbitration. It is a principle that is endorsed not only in national laws, but also by international arbitral institutions worldwide, as well as by international instruments such as the New York Convention and the Model Law. This is precisely what the parties have in fact agreed upon and could not see difficulty in honouring their mutual decision and accepting the validity of their agreement. There is nothing in the Act that prohibits the contracting parties from agreeing upon a second instance or appellate arbitration - either explicitly or implicitly. Hence, answer the question in affirmative that the arbitration clause in the agreement between the parties does not violate the fundamental or public policy of India by the parties agreeing to a second instance arbitration. Order accordingly.
