Union of India v A.L. Rallia Ram

Supreme Court of India

19 April 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

J.C. Shah, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 277; AIR 1963 SC 1685; [1964] 3 S.C.R. 164

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Constitution; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration Act, 1940 - Government of India Act, 1935 - Arbitral award - Government of India, invited tenders for purchasing the stock of American cigarettes - Tender submitted by respondent was accepted by Government of India - Acceptance letter included arbitration clause - Respondent took delivery but on inspection it was found that some cigarettes were unfit for use - Respondent requested the Government of India to agree to a uniform reduction of 50 per cent in price on the cigarettes delivered to him as well as those still with the Government - Government of India thereafter cancelled the contract in respect of the undelivered cigarettes - Respondent accepted the offer made by the Government, reserving his right 'to claim incidental expenses' - Thereafter, respondent claimed compensation in respect of goods delivered to him under the contract, interest on the amounts raised by him for carrying out the contract and for incidental expenses incurred by him after the delivery of the goods - Dispute was referred to arbitration - Arbitrators were appointed but they could not agree upon a decision, and dispute was referred to umpire - Umpire awarded to the respondent compensation in respect of goods delivered to him under the contract, interest on the amounts raised by him for carrying out the contract - Thereafter, respondent applied to the Subordinate Judge for filing the award u/s. 14 of the Arbitration Act, and the Union of India (UOI) applied for an order setting aside award - It was contended by the UOI that there was no legally binding contract between the UOI and the respondent, for the acceptance note was not signed on behalf of the Governor-General of India, and the entire proceeding including the appointment of the arbitrators and the umpire was vitiated for want of compliance with s. 175(3) of the Government of India Act, and that in any event the award contained errors of law apparent on its face - Subordinate Judge, refused the motion for setting aside the award and ordered that a decree be issued in terms of the award - On appeal, HC confirmed the order - HC held that the Government of India having agreed to refer differences to arbitration and having taken part in the proceeding before the arbitrators and the umpire, had waived the objection as to the illegality of the contract and could not therefore raise any such objection in an application for setting aside the award - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether award liable to be set aside on the ground that there was in existence no valid arbitration agreement in conformity with s. 175(3) of the Government of India Act which authorised the umpire to make his award? - Held, every contract to bind the Government must comply with the requirements of s. 175(3) of the Government of India Act and waiver will not preclude the Government from pleading absence of a contract in consonance with the law - Further, it is from the terms of the arbitration agreement that the arbitrator derives his authority to arbitrate : if in law there is no valid arbitration agreement, the proceedings of the arbitrator could be unauthorised - Also, a tender for purchase of goods in pursuance of an invitation issued by or on behalf of the Governor-General of India and acceptance in writing which is expressed to be made in the name of the Governor-General and is executed on his behalf by a person authorised in that behalf would conform to the requirements of s. 175(3) of the Government of India Act - In the present case, goods offered to be sold belonged to the Government of India - Tender notice was also issued by the Government of India - Correspondence between the parties resulting in the acceptance note amounts to a contract expressed to be made by the Government and therefore by the Governor-General, because it was the Governor-General who had invited the tender through the Director of Purchases, and it was the Governor-General who through the Chief Director of Purchases accepted the tender of the respondent subject to the conditions prescribed therein - Hence, in view of the contents of the letter, in the light of the obligations undertaken thereunder, it would be reasonable to hold that the contract was executed on behalf of the Governor-General - (B) Whether the award of the umpire was in law erroneous on the face of it? - Held, award is the decision of a domestic tribunal chosen by the parties, and the civil courts which are entrusted with the power to facilitate arbitration and to effectuate the awards, cannot exercise appellate powers over the decision - Wrong or right the decision is binding, if it be reached fairly after giving adequate opportunity to the parties to place their grievances in the manner provided by the arbitration agreement - Hence, award being a decision of an arbitrator, ordinarily not liable to be challenged on the ground that it is erroneous - In the present case, umpire awarded interest on the amounts raised by him for carrying out the contract - However, there was no principle on which the Government of India could be rendered liable for payment of interest in the circumstances relied upon - In respect of that part of the contract which was abandoned, if any liability to pay interest had arisen it was for the respondent to claim it in settling the terms on which cancellation of the contract was to be made - In respect of the goods which were returned by him, he could claim compensation for breach of warranty, but such compensation could not include interest as damages for detention of money - Interest was therefore allowed on a view of the law which appeared on the face of the award to be erroneous - Award of the umpire set aside in so far as it awards interest and incidental expenses - Appeal partly allowed.
