Sri Gangai Vinayagar Temple and another v Meenakshi Ammal and others

Supreme Court of India

09 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, A.R. Dave, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 705; (2015) 3 SCC 624; 2014 (6) AWC 6082; 2014 (4) CalLT(SC) 48; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 256; JT 2014 (12) SC 29; 2014 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 3802; 2015 (6) MahLJ 96; 2015 (4) MPLJ 374; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 920; 2014(11) SCALE 654; 2015 (2) WLN(SC) 124

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Subsequent Suit, Former Suit, Matter Directly And Substantially In Issue, Puducherry Hindu Religious Institutions Act, 1972

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2 - Suits relating to tenancy - Res judicata - Applicability - Defendant trust leased suit land to plaintiff tenant - Defendant trust sold suit land to defendant Nos. 7 to 9 - Defendant trust filed two suits for arrears of rent - Plaintiffs also filed a suit challenging alienation of suit property - Trial Court partly decreed suit of trust and dismissed tenant's suit on the ground of res judicata - On plaintiffs' appeal, HC held that res judicata would not apply to plaintiffs' suit - Whether plaintiffs' suit is barred by res judicata.

Held, pleadings on behalf of the tenant were common in all three suits, and that Issues on this aspect of the dispute had been claimed by the tenants in all the three suits. On a holistic and comprehensive reading of the pleadings of the tenant in all the three suits, it is inescapable that the tenant had intendedly, directly and unequivocally raised in its pleadings the question of the title to the demised premises and the legal capacity of the trustees to convey the lands to the transferees. This is the common thread that runs through the pleadings of tenant in all three suits. An appeal ought to have been filed by the tenant even in respect of trust's suit, for fear of inviting the rigours of res judicata as also for correcting the 'dismissal' order. Tenant had been completely non-suited once it was held that no cause of action had arisen in its favour and the suit was 'dismissed'. Ignoring that finding and allowing it to become final makes that conclusion impervious to change. Having failed or neglected or concertedly avoided filing appeals against the decrees in defendants' suits the cause of plaintiff tenants was permanently sealed and foreclosed since res judicata applied against them. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - No party must be vexed twice for the same cause; it is in the interest of the State that there should be an end to litigation; a judicial decision must be accepted as correct in the absence of a challenge.
