Â Kone Elevator India Private Limited v State of Tamil Nadu and others

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, R.M. Lodha, A.K. Patnaik, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 344; (2014) 7 SCC 1; 2014 (304) E.L.T. 161; JT 2014 (6) SC 436; 2014(7) SCALE 226; [2014] 5 S.C.R. 912; 2014 (34) S.T.R. 641; [2014] 71 VST 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Sales Tax

Keywords: Madras General Sales Tax Act, 1939, Indivisible Contract, Solicitor, Work And Labour, Haryana Value Added Tax Rules, 2003, Levy of sales tax, Madras General Sales Tax (Amendment) Act 25 of 1947, Bombay Lifts Rules, 1958, Bombay Lifts Act,1939, Bombay Lifts (Amendment) Rules, 2010, Manufacturing and installation of Lifts

Summary: Dipak Misra, J., for R. M. Lodha (C.J), A. K. Patnaik, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya, JJ.

Sales Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 366(29A), 366(29A)(b) - Manufacturing and installation of Lifts - Works contract - Levy of sales tax - Taxability - Petitioner (company) was engaged in manufacture, supply and installation of lifts involving civil construction - For A/y 1995-96, Appellate Tribunal, considering the case of petitioner, opined that nature of work was a 'works contract', for the erection and commissioning of lift could not be treated as 'sale' - On a revision filed, HC affirmed the view of Tribunal and dismissed tax revision filed by Revenue - Grieved by decision of HC, State filed appeal before SC and by judgment dt.17.2.2005 in 2005 Indlaw SC 13, view of HC was overturned - After pronouncement in said case, State Govt. called upon petitioner to submit returns treating the transaction as sale - Similarly, in some other States, proceedings were initiated proposing to reopen the assessments that had already been closed treating the transaction as sale - Said situation compelled petitioner to prefer the petition u/art. 32 of Constitution - Petitioner contended that manufacture, supply and installation were controlled by statutory provisions under an enactment of the legislature and also the Rules made in consonance with the Act which would reflect that immense skill was required for such installation and separate parts of the lift were not sold like goods, but it only becomes operational after it was installed, adjusted, tested and commissioned in a building - Whether a contract for manufacture, supply and installation of lifts in a building was a 'contract for sale of goods' or a 'works contract' - Hence, instant reference -

Held, a works contract could not have been liable to be taxed under the State sales tax laws and whether the contract was a works contract or a contract for sale of goods was dependent on the dominant intention as reflected from the terms and conditions of contract and many other aspects - In certain cases, Court had not treated the contract to be a works contract by repelling the plea of assessee after taking into consideration certain special circumstances - No straitjacket formula could have been stated to be made applicable for the determination of the nature of contract, for it depended on facts and circumstances of each case - As works contract could not be made amenable to sales tax as the State Legislatures did not have the legislative competence to charge sales tax under Entry 48 List II of sch. 7 of Constitution on an indivisible contract of sale of goods which had component of labour and service and it was not within the domain of the assessing officer to dissect an indivisible contract to distinguish the sale of goods constituent and the labour and service component - Installation of a lift in a building could not be regarded as a transfer of a chattel or goods but a composite contract - If the contract was a composite one which falls under the definition of works contracts as engrafted u/art. 366(29A)(b) of Constitution, the incidental part as regards labour and service pales into total insignificance for the purpose of determining the nature of contract - If there were two contracts, purchase of components of the lift from a dealer, it would be a contract for sale and similarly, if separate contract was entered into for installation, that would be a contract for labour and service - But, a pregnant one, once there was a composite contract for supply and installation, it had to be treated as a works contract, for it was not a sale of goods/chattel simpliciter - It was not chattel sold as chattel or, for that matter, a chattel being attached to another chattel - Thus, it would not be appropriate to term it as a contract for sale on the bedrock that the components were brought to the site, i.e., building, and prepared for delivery - In instant case, Show-cause notices, which were issued by taking recourse to reopening of assessment, should stand quashed - Assessment orders which were framed and are under assail before Court were set aside - Where assessments were framed and have attained finality and were not pending in appeal, they should be treated to have been closed, and where the assessments were challenged in appeal or revision, same should be decided in accordance with the decision rendered - Reference answered.

Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla, J. (Dissenting)

Held, whether contract between petitioner and its purchaser would fall within the definition of 'works contract' in order to attract art. 366(29-A)(b) of Constitution - In fact, if an answer to the said question could be held in the affirmative, then that would axiomatically lead to an answer in favour of petitioner - When a detailed reference to terms agreed upon between Petitioner and Purchaser was made, it would not be proper to merely go by such expression used sporadically to hold that contract was a 'Works Contract' - Court found that what the Petitioner had agreed under the Contract, was only to supply its branded LIFT in the premises of Purchaser - Court could firmly and validly state that a careful analysis of terms contained in the contract would lead only to that conclusion and not any other conclusion - Major part of work was directed to be carried out by Purchaser, in its premises, in order to enable the Petitioner to erect its LIFT/ELEVATOR in the said premises - In a very insignificant manner, Petitioner undertakes to attend to certain aspects while erecting the LIFTS in the premises of its Purchaser, such as connecting the power supply to LIFT after fixing it in the identified place where the Purchaser had prepared the Hoist/Well in its premises and such other aspects as mentioned in the contract - Petitioner could not be heard to say that it brings different parts of LIFT and that its activity of assembling the same in the premises of the Purchaser should be construed as one of service - In view of the nature of product that Petitioner agreed to supply to its Purchaser, it has to necessarily assemble different parts in the premises of the Purchaser and thereby, fulfill its contract of supply of LIFT/ELEVATOR in a working condition - Contract should disclose in no uncertain terms that it was one for carrying out 'the work' and the supply of materials were part of such agreement to carry out any such specified work - In instant case, contract was only for supply of LIFTS/ELEVATOR and whatever element of works which the Petitioner claims to carry out in effecting the supply was virtually very insignificant as compared to the element of sale, which was paramount as found in terms of contract - Whole of preparatory work for the erection of LIFT was that of Purchaser and Petitioner merely goes to the Purchaser's premises and fixes the various parts of the LIFT in the slots created for it - Contract was only one for manufacture and supply of LIFT/ELEVATOR and installation though mentioned in the contract, had very insignificant relation to consideration agreed upon between parties - In any event, as Court found that contract of supply and installation were divisible in very many aspects, it was difficult to hold that it was a 'Works Contract' - Thus, it would have to be held that manufacture, supply and erection of LIFT/ELEVATOR agreed upon by Petitioner to any of its customers, would only fall within the expression 'Sale' and can never be called as 'Works Contract' - Once that was the conclusion that could be made based on the contractual terms as agreed between the Petitioner and its customers, the application of art. 366(29A)(b) of Constitution could not be made and did not in any way support contentions raised by petitioner - Activity of petitioner in the manufacture, supply and installation of LIFT/ELEVATOR was a 'Sale' and not a 'Works Contract', having regard to specific terms of the contract placed before Court - Unless a contract was proved to be a 'Works Contract' by virtue of the terms agreed as between the parties, invocation of art. 366 (29A)(b) of Constitution, could not be made - Alternatively, if terms of contract disclose or lead to a definite conclusion that it was not a 'Works Contract', but one of outright sale, the same would have to be declared as a 'Sale' attracting the provisions of the relevant sales tax enactments - Having regard to nature of the terms of contract displayed, it would have to be held that manufacture, supply and installation of LIFTS/ELEVATORS comes under the definition of 'Sale' and not 'Works Contract' - Reference answered.
