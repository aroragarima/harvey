Union of India v United India Insurance Company Limited and Others

Supreme Court of India

22 October 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Jagannadha Rao, S.B. Majmudar

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 3125; (1997) 8 SCC 683; AIR 1998 SC 640; AIR 1998 SCW 245; JT 1997 (8) SC 653; 1997(6) SCALE 497; [1997] Supp4 S.C.R. 643; 1997 (9) Supreme 69

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation

Keywords: Joint Tort Feasor

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, ss. 110-1 and 110-B - Motor Vehicles Rules, 1939, r. 100 - Issues arise - (A) What are common law duties of a motor vehicle driver at a railway level-crossing and whether, on facts, bus driver was negligent? - Held, rule postulates existence of a signboard as mentioned requiring conductor to 'get down' - Writing on signboard at level-crossing was moth-eaten and no writing was visible - Hence, no special obligations created by rule, which were in addition to common law requirements, can be said to apply - In absence of a board statutorily requiring vehicle to 'stop' and conductor to 'get down', there was only an ordinary common law duty as applicable to prudent persons, which was a duty to 'stop', ' see and hear' and find out if any train was coming - (B) Whether driver's negligence in any manner vicariously attaches to passengers of motor vehicle of which he was driver? - Held, no - That principle is not applicable to a passenger in a vehicle in sense that negligence of driver of vehicle in which passenger is travelling, cannot be imputed to passenger - (C) Whether under law of torts claimants in rail-motor collisions can claim that obligations of Railway under statute as well as under common law will run concurrently? - Held, law in this behalf is again well settled that claimants can at their choice sue Railways to enforce either or both types of these duties, i.e., under common law as well as under statute - It must, therefore, be accepted that claimants can sue Railways concurrently for breach of common law or statutory duties or for breach of either of duties - (D) What are common law duties of Railways at level-crossings from time to time? - Held, in principle, liability is not based on matters of title but on perilous nature of operation and de facto relationship which would be called 'proximity' or 'neighbourly' relation between railway operator and a substantial number of persons lawfully using level-crossing - Duty to care at common law is therefore based upon dangerous or perilous nature of operations of Railways - (E) Whether there can be any breach of a common law duty on part of Railway if it does not take notice of increase in volume of rail and motor traffic at unmanned level-crossing and if it does not take adequate steps such as putting up gates with a watchman so as to prevent accidents at such a point? - Held, at a reasonable distance on either side, prominently written boards can be affixed, asking road-users to be beware of trains - If track on either side is visible from near caution board or within a short distance from crossing, this would be sufficient because a diligent road-user could look around and see train - It is stated that a plaintiff is entitled to rely on reasonable care and proper precautions being taken and, in places to which public has access, he is entitled to assume existence of such protection as public has, through custom, become justified in expecting - Appeals dismissed.
