Â Macherla Hanumantha Raoand Others v State of Andhra Pradesh (with connected petition)

Supreme Court of India

17 September 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Sudhi Ranjan Das, A.K. Sarkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 50; AIR 1957 SC 927; 1957 CRLJ 1463; 1957 (1) MLJ 56; 1958 (1) MLJ(SC) 56; 1958 SCJ 129; [1958] 1 S.C.R. 396

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Constitutionality, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898,ss. 161(3), 162, 164, 169, 173, 190, 197, 215, 208, 208(3), 209(1), (2), 213(2), 215, 435 and 439 and chap. XVIII and XIV - Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1955, ss. 207 A, (2), (4) and (6) - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 27, 101 to 106 and 114(1)(g) - Indian Penal Code,1860, ss.34, 147, 148, 149, 302, 323 and 324 - Constitution of India, 1950 arts. 14 and 134 - (A) Constitutionality of provisions of ss. 207 and 207 A, Code introduced into Code by Act XXVI of 1955 - Amending Act introduced changes into old Code with a view to simplify and expedite the procedure relating to trial of offences and to inquiries preceding such trials - Principle of classification of offences and of different categories of cases relating to trial of offences is a well-establisbed rule of criminal procedure - Impugned sections have prescribed two different procedures in respect of commitment proceedings - Held, it is by no means clear that changes introduced by amending Act XXVI of 1955 are always to disadvantage or prejudice of an accused person - No difference in procedure at trial in contradistinction to procedure relating to enquiry leading up to commitment of an accused person to a Court of Session or a High Court - Procedure laid down in s. 208 and sections following that section, naturally gives greater facilities to persons accused of an offence, to vindicate their character - Legislature has been well-advised to amend procedure relating to commitment proceedings in cases which have been investigated by a competent police officer - (B) Whether provisions of classification of offences and of different categories of cases relating to trial of offences violates art. 14 of Constitution? - Art. 14 forbids class legislation, it does not forbid reasonable classification for purposes of legislation - Held, basis of classification is clearly connected with underlying principle of administration of justice that an alleged criminal should be placed on his trial as soon after commission of crime as circumstances of case would permit - This classification cannot be said to be unreasonable and not to have any relation to object of legislation, namely, a more speedy trial of offences without any avoidable delay - There is no discrimination and that provisions of art. 14 of Constitution have not been contravened - Provisions of Code, impugned in this case, must, therefore, be held to be constitutional - Appeal dismissed.
