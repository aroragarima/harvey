Ramakant Mishra @ Lalu Etc. v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

27 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 149; (2015) 8 SCC 299; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 1651; 2015 (2) CGLJ 207; 2015 CRLJ 1947; JT 2015 (3) SC 466; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 747; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 857; 2015(3) SCALE 186; [2015] 2 S.C.R. 961

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Hearsay

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304B, 498A - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 32, 113B - Dowry death - Burden of proof - Deceased died due to burn injuries and accused-appellants were put on trial for the same - Trial Court convicted accused-appellants and passed sentence u/ss. 498A and 304B of IPC and on appeal, HC affirmed the same - Hence, instant appeal - Whether alleged dying declaration is genuine.

Held, unnatural death of deceased occurred within seven years of solemnization of marriage, and around fifteen days prior to unnatural death of deceased, PW1 who was grandfather of deceased, refused to send deceased to matrimonial house due to harassment and cruelty meted out by accused-appellants to deceased for not meeting their demand of dowry. Hence, requirement of s. 304B of IPC that dowry demand should be made soon before the death stands satisfied and so, prosecution succeeded in showing or proving prima facie, that dowry demands had been made by the accused-appellants even shortly before the death of deceased. Further, burden and necessity of proving sequence of events stood transferred to shoulders of accused-appellants since s. 304B of IPC had been attracted. The Dying declaration appears to have mysteriously popped up and referred to at the time of praying for bail and chain or sequence of events which lead to its recording remained undisclosed. Further, Tehsildar has not clarified the manner in which he happened to record the Dying declaration and the timing of its to the Court. Since the onus of proof had shifted to accused-appellants, alleged sequence of events should have been proved beyond reasonable doubt by them. The alleged exculpating Dying Declaration is hence shrouded in suspicion and could not be accepted as genuine document and defence failed to comply with s. 113B of the Act. Further, presence of accused at scene at time of occurrence added to their responsibility to give credible version of their innocence in the dowry death. Hence, appeals are dismissed. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - In order to attract s. 304B of IPC, dowry demand should be made soon before the death of deceased and death should occur within seven years of solemnization of marriage. Further, burden of proving innocence beyond reasonable doubt lies upon accused.
