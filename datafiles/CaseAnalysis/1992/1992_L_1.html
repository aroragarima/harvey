Hawabi Sayed Arif Sayed Hanif (Smt.) v L. Hmingliana and Others

Supreme Court of India

14 October 1992

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Ratnavel Pandian, R.M. Sahai

Where Reported

1992 Indlaw SC 671; (1993) 1 SCC 163; (1993) SCC (Cr) 304; AIR 1993 SC 810; 1993 (1) BLJR 248; 1992 (3) CCR 451; 1992 (3) Crimes 635; 1993 CRLJ 172; 1992 CRLR 751; JT 1992 (6) SC 162; 1993 (1) MWN 58; 1992(2) SCALE 796

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and Other Maritime Zones Act, 1976

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Detention - Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, ss.1(i), 2(d), 3(1), 3(3), 8 and 9(1) - Prevention of Illicit Traffic In Narcotic Drugs And Psychotropic Substances Act, 1988, s. 10(1) - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 2 and 108 - Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and other Maritime Zones Act, 1976, ss. 3 and 5 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 22 - Detention order D/- 23-10-1990 passed against detenu - Validity of - (A) Communication to detenu - S. 9(1), COFEPOSA Act, 1974 - Held, s. 9(1) speaks of only maximum period of making declaration but not maximum period for communicating same to detenu and there is no statutory period, specified u/s. 9(1) for serving declaration to detenu - (B) Expression 'Indian Coastal Waters' in declaration - Definition of - Held, definition of Indian Customs Waters covers not only Indian coastal waters but also much more because customs waters extend 24 nautical miles from coastal baseline which follows that Indian coastal waters are within Indian customs waters and therefore, expression 'Indian Coastal Waters' used in declaration refers to an area within Indian Customs Waters which is highly vulnerable to smuggling and HC rightly held that this typographical mistake has in no way prejudiced detenu in making his representation - (C) Smuggling - Held, apprehended activities of smuggling by detenu have a territorial nexus with State of Maharashtra, indisputably detenu is a resident of Bombay, grounds of detention show that almost all arrangements were made for disposal of smuggled silver ingots in Bombay, two previous attempts to smuggle silver, hence detaining authority legitimately drew his subjective satisfaction that in future also smuggling activities of detenu may take place within State of Maharashtra - (D) Continued Detention - Validity of - Held, u/s. 9(1) Central Govt. or its officer specially empowered may make declaration if satisfied that any one of conditions enumerated in (a) to (c) of s. 9(1) is attracted; continued detention in pursuance of declaration has to be read along with original order detention; in present case, first respondent on being satisfied that detenu is likely to smuggle goods into and through Indian Customs Waters contiguous to State of Karnataka which is highly vulnerable to smuggling which satisfies requirements u/s. 9(1)(a) has passed this order, hence it cannot be said to passed without application of mind - (E) Whether order of detention can be passed against person in custody? - Held, yes - Order for detention can be validly passed against a person in custody - For that purpose it is necessary that grounds of detention must show that detaining authority was aware of fact that detenu is already in detention and there were compelling reasons justifying such detention despite fact that detenu is already in detention - Further held, though full text of remand order was not placed before detaining authority, substance of same was placed; non-placing of remand order before detaining authority has in no way affected either subjective satisfaction of authority or detenu's right to make a detailed representation - Appeal dismissed.
