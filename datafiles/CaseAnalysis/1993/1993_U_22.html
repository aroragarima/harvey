R.K. Jain v Union Of India And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

14 May 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, K. Ramaswamy, M.M. Punchhi

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1160; (1993) 4 SCC 119; (1993) SCC (L&S) 1128; AIR 1993 SC 1769; AIR 1993 SCW 1899; 1993 (25) ATC 464; 1993 (47) ECR 1; 1993 (65) E.L.T. 305; JT 1993 (3) SC 297; 1993(2) SCALE 843; [1993] 3 S.C.R. 802; 1993 (3) SLR 376

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Customs; Indirect Tax

Summary: Administrative - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - CEGAT Members (Recruitment anti Conditions of Service) Rules, 1997 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 217, 323A, 323B - Public interest litigation - Malfunctioning of Tribunal - Appointment of president - Eligibility - Petitioner addressed a letter (which was treated as PIL) to then Chief Justice of India, complaining that as the Customs, Excise and Gold Control Appellate Tribunal (CEGAT) was without a President for the last over 6 months the functioning of the Tribunal was adversely affected - There was malfunctioning of Tribunal - SC appointed respondent No. 3 as Tribunal's president which was questioned by other members - Serious allegations were made against respondent No. 3 and his competence to hold the post was questioned - It was contended that the appointment was made in violation of the Rules - Whether respondent No. 3 was competent to hold the post of president of CEGAT and Whether SC could interfere with the appointment of president - Held, on the question of appointment of respondent No. 3 as the President of the CEGAT, Rules empower the Central Govt. to appoint any member as the President of the CEGAT - It was true that u/r. 10(4) of the Rules, a serving judge and under the proviso thereto, a retired judge, could also be appointed a Member and President simultaneously - In the case of a serving judge his age of superannuation was fixed at 2 years but in the case of a retired judge he might be appointed for it period of 3 years at the most - Insofar as a serving HC Judge was concerned, he holds office until he attains the age of 62 years, vide art. 217 of Constitution - R. 10(4) of the Rules needs a suitable change to make it sufficiently attractive for sitting HC Judges to accept appointment as the President of the CEGAT - To instill the confidence of the litigating publican the CEGAT - Govt. should make a sincere effort to appoint a sitting Judge of the HC was a President of the CEGAT in consultation of the Chief Justice of India and it a sitting Judge was not available the choice should fall on a retired Judge as far as possible - It was averred that respondent No. 3 had been in the past proposed for appointment as a Judge of a HC but his appointment did not materialise due to certain adverse reports - SC could not sit in judgment over the choice of the person made by the Central Govt. for appointment as a President if the person chosen was qualified and eligible for appointment under the Rules - Therefore SC could not interfere with the appointment of respondent No. 3 on the ground that his track record was poor or because of adverse reports on which account his appointment as a HC Judge had not materialized - Allegations made by petitioner in regard to the working of the CEGAT were rave and the authorities could ill afford to turn a Nelson's eve to those allegations made by a person who was fairly well conversant with the internal working of the Tribunal - Refusal to inquire into such grave allegations, some of which were capable of verification, could only betray indifference and lack of a sense of urgency to tone up the working of the tribunal - Time was ripe for taking stock of the working of the various Tribunals set up in the country after the insertion of arts. 323A and 323B of the Constitution - After the incorporation of these two articles, Acts have been enacted where under Tribunals were constituted for dispensation of justice - Sufficient time was passed and experience gained in these last few years for taking stock of the situation with a view to finding out if they have serve the purpose and objectives for which they were constituted - An intensive and extensive study need to be undertaken by the Law Commission in regard to the Constitution of Tribunals under various statutes with a view to ensuring their independence so that the public confidence in such Tribunals may increase and the quality of their performance may improve - Petitions disposed of.
