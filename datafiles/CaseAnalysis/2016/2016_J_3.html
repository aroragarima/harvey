J. K. Lakshmi Cement Limited v Commercial Tax Officer, Pali

Supreme Court of India

16 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 654; AIR 2016 SC 5571; JT 2016 (12) SC 485; 2016(8) SCALE 809; [2016] 5 S.C.R. 261

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Assessment Year, Levy, Companies Act, 1956, Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, Tax Payable, Exemption Notification, Rate Of Tax, Concessional Rate, Quantum, Rate

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, s. 8(5) - Companies Act, 1956 - Notification - Partial exemption - Retrospective application - Appellant/company was engaged in the business of manufacturing and selling Grey Portland Cement - State Govt. issued notification u/s. 8(5) of the 1956 Act dt. 06-05-1986 allowing partial exemptions from sales tax payable in respect of inter-State sales - Re-assessment notice was issued to appellant for disallowing partial exemption on ground that while calculating the benefits under notification dt. 06-05-1986 appellant had not included the figure of sale of levy cement made in the base year i.e. 1984-85 - For a/y 2000-2001, a show cause notice was issued by respondent to appellant seeking to disallow the benefits under notification dt. 06-05-1986 on a purported retrospective application of circular dt. 16-04-2001 - On appeal, Dpty. Commissioner allowed appeal holding that appellant would be entitled to avail such partial exemption in respect of inter-State sales made on which concessional rate of 6% was not availed of by it under notification dt. 21-01-2000 - Aggrieved, Appellate Authority filed an appeal before Board which was allowed - Appellant filed revision petition before HC - Single Judge held that condition no. 3 of Notification No. 21-01-2000 unequivocally states that once assessee avails of the benefit of concessional rate of tax under notification he cannot get partial benefit as envisaged in notification dt. 06-05-1986 - Hence, instant appeal - Whether appellant is entitled to dual benefit of partial exemption under the notification dt. 06-05-1986 and also the lower rate of tax @ 6% under notification dt. 21-01-2000.

Held, the circular dt. 15-04-1994 under the notification dt. 07-03-1994 would equally apply and would guide the interpretation of notification dt. 21-01-2000. It is limpid that circular dt. 15-04-1994, when in force, had referred to the notifications dt. 07-03-1994 as well as 06-05-1986. The circular did not in clear and categorical terms lay down that dual or multiple benefits under the two notifications could be availed of by the same dealer. Circulars are binding on the authorities administering the enactment but cannot alter the provision of the enactment, etc. to the detriment of the assessee. Thus, a circular should not be adverse and cause prejudice to assessee. SC hold that notification dt. 21-01-2000 is ambiguous or susceptible to two views of interpretations. The language being plain and clear, it does not admit of two different interpretations. The circular dt. 15-04-1994 was ambiguous and therefore, as long as it was in operation and applicable possibly doctrine of contemporanea exposition could be taken aid of for its applicability. The circular having been withdrawn, contention of contemporanea exposition does not commend acceptation and has to be repelled and SC do so. Therefore, SC hold that it would certainly not apply to the notification dt. 21-01-2000. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Circulars issued under tax enactments can tone down rigour of law for an authority which wields power for its own advantage is given right to forego advantage when required and considered necessary.
