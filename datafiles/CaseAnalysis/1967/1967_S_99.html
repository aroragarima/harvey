Krishna Chettiar and Others v State of Madras

Supreme Court of India

05 September 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, Vishishtha Bhargava, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 76; 1968 (21) STC 258

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Sales Tax

Summary: Sales Tax - Madras General Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 186 and 332 - Special Deputy Commercial Tax Officer, Salem (PW1) in company of Asst. Commercial Tax Officer made a surprise visit to business premises of M/s. Vasantha Rice Mill situated at Salem to inspect accounts and to check stocks of foodgrains mill belonged to Krishna Chettiar, appellant - Officers of Sales Tax Department arrived at factory Krishna Chettiar agreed to produce accounts and Asst. Commercial Tax Officer and peons proceeded to sago factory building to take stock of food grains lying in stock - At sago factory building, peons found Ramamurthy putting some account books in a gunny bag and trying to run away by back door of factory - Seeing this, two Commercial Tax Officers gave chase and Special Deputy Commercial Tax Officer carried gunny bag and proceeded to main office in order to examine books - When Krishna Chettiar came near sago factory building and attempted to snatch it from him - Krishna Chettiar called for help from men from threshing-floor and with assistance of Ramamurthy and others, he succeeded in snatching gunny bag containing account books and handed them over to Ramamurthy, who ran away with them - High Court set aside acquittal recorded by Magistrate - (A) Whether search and seizure of account books were valid? - (B) Whether appellants were within their rights in resisting both? - Held, there is no requirement of recording of reasons at that stage, because all that officer has to do is to go and ask dealer to produce his account books for his inspection - Two officers of Commercial Tax Department went to premises of mill and asked to see account books and to check stocks and Krishna Chettiar readily agreed - There was no question at that stage of any search of any premises which would attract provision in CrPC or case on which Magistrate relied present case involved only an inspection of accounts and registers under first and second sub-sections and therefore officers were well within their rights in seeking that inspection without proceeding to record reasons in writing - It was only when Ramamurthy was seen running away with account books that third sub-section came into play - Judge in HC has rightly said, it was not to be expected that tax officers would sit down to record their reasons in writing while Ramamurthy made away with books - They had first to get hold of books and could record their reasons in writing later when actual seizure was to be made - Search and seizure were validly done - Appellants were not within their rights in resisting them - Appeals dismissed.
