Vinita Saxena v Pankaj Pandit

Supreme Court of India

21 March 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

AR. Lakshmanan, Ruma Pal

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 154; (2006) 3 SCC 778; AIR 2006 SC 1662; 2006 (63) ALR 518; 2006 (4) Bom.C.R. 810; 2006 (2) CTC 328; 2006 (128) DLT 387; 2006 (1) JCC 577; 2006 (3) JhrCR(SC) 114; JT 2006 (3) SC 587; 2006 (2) MLJ(SC) 383; 2006 (143) PLR 251; 2006 (2) RCR(Civil) 302; 2006(3) SCALE 367; 2006 (3) SCJ 163; [2006] 3 S.C.R. 116

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Family & Personal; Women & Children

Keywords: Wilful Neglect, Insanity

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, ss. 13(1)(i-a) and (ii) - Constitution of India, 1950 - Marriage Laws (Amendment) Act, 1976 - Dissolution of marriage u/s. 13(l)(i-a) and (iii) H M A, 1955, on grounds of mental and physical cruelty and insanity - Dismissed by High Court on appeal - Held, assessment of degree of mental disorder for dissolution of marriage - Degree must be such that spouse seeking relief cannot reasonably be expected to live with other - All mental abnormalities are not recognized as grounds for grant of decree - Burden of proof of existence of requisite degree of mental disorder is on spouse basing claim on that state of facts - Been established beyond doubt by Medical doctors who had deposed as witnesses and brought original medical record of respondent that respondent is suffering from mental disorder - Grant of divorce on plea of mental insanity/mental disorder is different than cruelty - Appellant, had proved beyond doubt that respondent suffered from mental disorder and that appellant suffered cruelty by and at behest of respondent - Mental cruelty to amount to cruelty, must be such willful treatment of party which caused suffering in body or mind either as an actual fact or by way of apprehension in such a manner as to render continued living together of spouses harmful or injurious having regard to circumstances of case - It must be proved that one partner in marriage however mindless of consequences has behaved in a way which other spouse could not in circumstances be called upon to endure, and that misconduct has caused injury to health or a reasonable apprehension of such injury - Where parties are young and mental disorder is of such a type that sexual act and procreation of children is not possible it may furnish a good ground for nullifying marriage because to beget children from a Hindu wedlock is one of principal aim of Hindu Marriage - Facts and circumstances of case as well as all aspects pertain to humanity and life would give sufficient cogent reasons to allow appeal - Appeal allowed.
