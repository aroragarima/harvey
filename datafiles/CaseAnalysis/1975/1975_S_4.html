S. Iqbal Singh v S. Gurdas Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 September 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

A. Alagiriswami, P.K. Goswami, N.L. Untwalia

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 384; (1976) 3 SCC 284; AIR 1976 SC 27; [1976] 1 S.C.R. 884

Case Digest

Subject: Municipalities & Local Governments

Summary: Election - Representation of People Act, 1951, s. 123(1) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 171-B - Corrupt practice of bribery - Election to Parliament - Respondent No. 1 was declared elected - Election petition - Alleged many irregularities that took place on date of polling - (A) Whether respondent No. 1 is guilty of corrupt practices specified? - Grant of gun licenses to voters - Word 'gratification' should be deemed to refer only to cases where a gift is made of something which gives a material advantage to recipient - Where a licence gives a material advantage to licensee grant of such licenses amounts to a gratification - A bargain for purposes of this section does not mean that candidate or his agent makes an offer and voter accepts it in sense that he promises to vote - It is enough if candidate or his agent makes gift or promise on that condition - Held, it cannot be said that person in authority making that promise and holding out that he would carry out many remedial measures to benefit people was resorting to bribery or bargaining for votes - More active at election time than other times; that cannot be said to amount to corruption - A gun licence gives no material advantage to its recipient - But word 'gratification' as used in s. 123(1) does not refer to such gratifications any more than in s. 171-B of IPC, 1860 - Arms licence is a licence for regulatory purposes - Its possession gives no material advantage to its possessor - Grant of gun licenses to voters would not amount to bribery - There is no evidence regarding bargaining for votes by promise of gun licenses - Money was not distributed among voters directly but was given to Panchayat and public at large - There was no corrupt practice - (B) Whether 15,000 ballot-papers were invalid and were wrongly polled and counted? - Held, there is absolutely nothing on record to show how figure 15,000 was arrived at - Allegation in petition was that 15,000 invalid votes were counted in favour of returned candidate but in evidence as well as arguments it was only claimed that there were 15,000 invalid ballot papers which were counted - There is nothing to show how many of those 15,000 went to appellant and how many to 1st respondent - Appellant has not succeeded in establishing allegations - Appeal dismissed.
