Guramma Bhratar Chanbasappa Deshmukh And Another v Malappa

Supreme Court of India

19 August 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Raghubar Dayal, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 424; 1964 MPLJ 133

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Joint Family Property, Natural Son, Posthumous Son

Summary: (a) Hindu Law - Adoption - Adoption by senior widow soon after husband's death - Subsequent birth of son to junior widow does not invalidate adoption of son already adopted.On the death of the last owner on 8-1-1944 leaving three widows and two widowed daughters from a predeceased wife the senior widow adopted a son on 30-1-1944. Later the junior widow gave birth to a posthumous son on 4-10-1944. It was contended that since the son was in the womb of the junior widow at the time of the adoption by the senior widow, the adoption was invalid, relying on the analogy of the right of the son to challenge validity of alienation made by the father while he was in the womb.

Held, that the adoption was valid. The existence of a son in embryo does not invalidate an adoption. There is an essential distinction between an alienation, partition and inheritance on the one hand and adoption on the other. The son's right to set aside an alienation hinges on his secular right to secure his share in the property belonging to the family, as he has a right by birth in the joint family property and transactions effected by the father in excess of his power when he was in the embryo are voidable at his instance. But in the case of adoption, it secures mainly spiritual benefit to the father and the power to adopt is conferred on him to achieve that object. The doctrine evolved only for a secular purpose would be inappropriate to a case of adoption.

(b) Hindu Law - Alienation - Manager's power is circumscribed - Alienation voidable at instance of existing member or member in the womb and born subseuentl unless alienation was for family purpose.

A coparcener whether he is natural born or a son adopted into the family, acquires an interest by birth or adoption, as the case may be, in the ancestral property of the family. A managing member of the family has power to alienate for value joint family property either for family necessity or for the benefit of the estate. If another member was in existence or in the womb of his mother at the time of the alienation, the power of the manager is circumscribed and his alienation becomes voidable at the instance of the existing member or the member who was in the womb but was subsequently born, as the case may be, unless it was made for purposes binding on the members of the family. If another member was conceived in the family or inducted therein by adoption before such consent or ratification, his right to avoid the alienation will not be affected. I L R 1944 All. 612, A I R 1931 Mad. 550 and (1941) I L R 20 Pat. 727, Ref.

(c) Hindu Law - Alienation - Manager - Gift to a stranger is void.

A gift to a stranger of a joint family property by the manager of the family is void and cannot be sustained however much the donor was beholden to such stranger on the ground that it was made out of charity.

(d) Hindu Law - Joint family property - Father has power to make a gift to daughter to reasonable extent - Fact that two gift deeds were made in favour of daughter does not invalidate the gift.

Under Hindu law the father or his representative can make a valid gift, out of family property by way of reasonable provision for the maintenance of his daughter, regard being had to the financial and other relevant circumstances of the family. By custom or convenience such gifts are made at the time of marriage, but the right of the father or his representative to make such a gift is not confined to the marriage occasion. It is a normal obligation and it continues to subsist till it is discharged either during the life-time of the father or thereafter. It is not possible to lay down a hard and fast rule, prescribing the quantitative limits of such a gift as that would depend on the facts of each case and it can only be decided by Courts, regard being had to the overall picture of the extent of the family estate, the number of daughters to be provided for and other similar circumstances. It cannot be said that such gift must be made by only one document or only at single point of time. If the reasonableness of the gift is not disputed, the fact that two gift deeds were executed instead of one, will not make the gift anytheless a valid one.

(e) Hindu Law - Lingayats are governed by law applicable to Shudras. ILR 1943 Bom. 706, Ref.

(f) Hindu Law - Partition - Shudras - Adopted son takes one-fourth of the share of after-born natural son in Bombay. (1892) I L R 17 Bom. 100 and I L R 49 Bom. 762, Approved. Madras and Bengal view not applied.
