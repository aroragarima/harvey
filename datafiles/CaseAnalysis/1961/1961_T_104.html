Â The Chief Inspector Of Mines And Another v Lala Karam Chand Thapar Etc.

Supreme Court of India

10 February 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 532; AIR 1961 SC 838; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 9

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Mines Act, 1923, Colliery, Companies Clauses Act, 1845, Vindhya Pradesh Ordinance, 1949, Patents, Design and Trade Marks Act, 1888, Mines Creche Rules and Coal Mines Pithead Bath Rules, 1946, Indian Coal Mines Regulations, 1926

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Constitution - Indian Coal Mines Regulations, 1926, regn. 48 - Mines Act, 1923, s. 29 - Mines Act, 1952, ss. 73, 74 and 76 - General Clauses Act, 1897, s. 24 - Accident occurred in colliery on 5.02.1955 as a result of which 52 persons lost their lives - The court of enquiry submitted its report that the accident was due to negligence and non-observance of some of the provisions of the Regulations, 1926 - Criminal proceedings were instituted against 14 persons including the manager and the agent of the colliery, all the directors of the company which was the owner of the colliery and the directors of the managing agents of that company - Sub-Divisional Magistrate took cognizance of the offences, and issued processes against all the 14 persons - Appellant filed applications for quashing the criminal proceedings submitting that the regulations for the alleged violation of which the complaints were lodged had ceased to have any legal existence long before the date of the alleged violation by the repeal of the Mines Act, 1923, under which they had admittedly been made - Directors of the managing agents also raised in their applications the point that since the managing agents were not the owners of the colliery the directors of the managing agents should not be prosecuted - HC rejected the appellant's contention that the Regulations framed u/s. 29 of the Mines Act, 1923, ceased to have legal existence after the repeal of that Act - However, it accepted the contention of the managing agents' directors that they were not liable to prosecution - HC also held that all the directors of the company which owned the colliery could not be prosecuted and only one to be chosen by the complainant out of all the directors could be proceeded against - Hence, present appeals - (A) Whether the Mines Regulations, 1926, framed as they were u/s. 29 of the Mines Act, 1923, survived the repeal of the Mines Act, 1923, by the Mines Act, 1952? - Held, yes - Where the Mines Act, 1923, was repealed, and was reenacted with modifications as the Mines Act, 1952, s. 29 of the 1923 Act empowering the Central Government to make regulations consistent with the Act for specified purposes was reenacted in the 1952 Act as s. 57 regulations were made in 1926 u/s. 29 of the 1923 Act, but at the relevant date, in 1955, no regulations had been made u/s. 57 of the 1952 Act, so that in 1955 the Mines Regulations, 1926, had not been superseded by any regulations made under there enacted provisions of s. 57 of the 1952 Act - Therefore if s. 24 of the General Clauses Act is operative the Mines Regulations, 1926, were in force at the relevant date in-1955, and shall be deemed to have been made u/s. 57 of the 1952 Act - (B) Whether all the directors of the company which owned the colliery could be prosecuted? - Held, s. 76 of the Mines Act provides that where the owner of the mine is a firm or other association of individuals, any one of the partners or members thereof may be punished; where the owner is a private company, any one of the shareholders may be prosecuted and punished and where the owner is a public company not 'any one of the shareholders' but any one of the directors may be prosecuted and punished - Appeals disposed of.
