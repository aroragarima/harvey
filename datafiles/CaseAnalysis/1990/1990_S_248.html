Kishore Chand v State of Himachal Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

29 August 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, P.B. Sawant

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 12; (1991) SCC (Cr) 172; (1991) 1 SCC 286; AIR 1990 SC 2140; 1990 (3) Crimes 343; 1990 CRLJ 2289; 1990 CrLR(SC) 608; JT 1990 (3) SC 662; 1990(2) SCALE 369; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 105

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19 and 21 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 201 and 34 - Evidence Act, ss. 25, 26 and 27 - Murder - Circumstantial evidence - Whether conviction of appellant proper and justified in facts of case? - In a case of circumstantial evidence, all the circumstances from which the conclusion of the guilt is to be drawn should be fully and cogently established - All the facts so established should be consistent only with the hypothesis of the guilt of the accused - There must be a chain of evidence so far consistent and complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for a conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and it must be such as to show that within all probability the act must have been done by the accused and the accused alone - Held, no prior intimacy between accused and deceased - TIP not conducted - Confession not made in presence of Magistrate and extra judicial confession made not voluntary - Recovery of weapon doubtful as it was recovered after a long time after arrest - Prosecution has utterly failed to prove circumstances against the appellant and the chain of circumstances was broken at every stage without connecting the accused to the commission of the alleged crime as the prosecution failed to prove as a primary fact all the three circumstances, much less beyond all reasonable doubt bringing home the guilt to the accused, and to prove that the appellant alone committed crime - Appellant entitled to benefit of doubt - Appeal allowed.
