Â Member, Board of Revenue v Arthur Paul Benthall

Supreme Court of India

04 October 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Syed Jaffer Imam, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 82; AIR 1956 SC 35; 1956 (1) MLJ 43; 1956 (1) MLJ(SC) 43; 1956 SCJ 56; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 842

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Board of Revenue Act, 1913 - Indian Stamp Act, 1899, ss. 31, 56 (2) and 57 - Bengal Act III of 1922 - Respondent applied to Collector of Calcutta u/s. 31 of Stamp Act for adjudication of duty payable on a power-of-attorney which he proposed to execute - By that power, he empowered three persons jointly and severally to act for him in his individual capacity and also as executor, administrator, trustee, managing agent, liquidator and all other capacities - Collector referred matter u/s. 56(2) of Act to decision of Chief Controlling Revenue Authority, who eventually referred it u/s. 57 to High Court of Calcutta stating his own opinion that stamp duty was payable on power 'for as many respective capacities as the principal executes power' - HC held that different capacities of executant did not constitute distinct matters for purposes of s. 5 of Act - Petitioner contends that instrument in question comprises distinct matters, and must be stamped in accordance with s. 5 - Whether instrument falls u/s. 5? - Held, it is unable to agree with respondent that when a person executes a power-of -attorney in respect of all matters in which he could act, it should be held, as a matter of law and without regard to contents of instrument, to comprise a single matter - Whether it relates to a single matter or to distinct matters will depend on a number of factors such as who are parties thereto, what are the subject-matters on which it operates and so forth - Question whether a power- of - attorney relates to distinct matters is one that will have to be decided on a consideration of terms of instrument and nature and extent of authority conferred thereby - If one person holding properties in two different capacities, each unconnected with other, executes a power in respect of both of them, instrument should logically be held to comprise distinct matters - That will be in consonance with generally accepted notion of what are distinct matters, and that certainly was view which respondent himself took of matter when he expressly recited in power that he executed it both in his individual capacity and in his other capacities - Appeal allowed.
