Darbara Singh v State Of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

12 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 290; (2012) 10 SCC 476; AIR 2013 SC 840; 2012 CRLJ 4757; JT 2012 (8) SC 530; 2012 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3223; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 362; 2012(8) SCALE 649; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 541

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Prosecution alleged that upon instigation by 'X', the appellant hit deceased on the head with a Kirpan, while 'Y', co-accused hit him on the chest with a Kirpan, as a result of which deceased died instantly - Trial court convicted the appellant and 'Y' for the offence punishable u/s. 302 of IPC while 'X' was acquitted - HC dismissed appeal filed against said order - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant submitted that there was nothing on record to show that the appellant had a common intention with 'Y' to kill the deceased and therefore the appellant could not have been convicted as such - Whether order of trial court as affirmed by the HC could be upheld - Held, PW11, who conducted the post-mortem opined that the cause of death was haemorrhage and shock as a result of injury to vital organs i.e. lung and heart, which was sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course of nature - So far as the issue of motive was concerned, it was a settled legal proposition that motive has great significance in a case involving circumstantial evidence, but where direct evidence was available, which was worth relying upon, motive loses its significance - In the instant case, firstly, there was nothing on record to reveal the identity of the person who was convicted for rape as alleged by the appellant, there was also nothing to reveal the status of his relationship with the appellant and further, there was nothing on record to determine the identity of that girl or her relationship to the 'Y' - More so, the conviction took place 20 years prior to the incident - Therefore, the case put forward by appellant was clearly a false story - Even the submission regarding common intention does not tilt the balance in favour of the appellant - The manner in which injury no.1 has been caused clearly suggests that both the accused persons acted in furtherance of a common intention - Impugned order of the trial court as affirmed by the HC was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
