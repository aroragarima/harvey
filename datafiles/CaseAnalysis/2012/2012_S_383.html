V. K. Sasikala v State represented by Superintendent

Supreme Court of India

27 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 425; (2012) 9 SCC 771; AIR 2013 SC 613; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 5183; 2013 CRLJ 177; JT 2012 (9) SC 609; 2013 (3) KarLJ 83; 2012 (4) MLJ(Crl) 355; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 244; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 641

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Deem

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, s. 13 (1) and (2) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120B - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Food adulteration - Charged - Appellant's prayer for inspection of documents - Denied - Appellant allegedly committed offence of food adulteration - Appellant was charged u/ss. 120B of IPC and s. 13(2) r/w s. 13(1) (e) of Act - Pending Trial, appellant wanted to inspect certain unmarked and unexhibited documents - Trail Court, however, rejected the application of appellant - Appellant filed same application before HC which was also rejected - Hence instant appeal - Whether prayer of appellant for inspection of documents was sustainable - Held, issue that emerged would touch upon the larger doctrine of free and fair trial that was painstakingly built up by the courts on purposive interpretation of art. 21 of Constitution - It was not the stage of making of the request - Efflux of time that had occurred or the prior conduct of the accused that was material - What was of significance was if in given situation the accused comes to the court contending that some papers forwarded to the Court by the investigating agency were not exhibited by the prosecution as the same favours the accused the court should concede right to in the accused to have access to the said documents, if so claimed - That, according to SC, was the core issue in the case which should be answered affirmatively - In this regard, SC said that it found it difficult to agree with the view taken by the HC that the accused should be made to await the conclusion of the trial to test the plea of prejudice that he might have raised - Such plea should be answered at the earliest and certainly before the conclusion of the trial, even though it might be raised by the accused belately - That was how the scales of justice in Criminal Jurisprudence were to be balanced - There was yet another possible dimension of the case - It was the specific contention of the accused in both the applications for certified copies of the unmarked documents and for inspection that it was in the course of the examination of the accused u/s. 313 Cr.P.C. that perception had developed that the accused might be giving incomplete/ incorrect answers in response to the questions put to her by the Court and that she needed copies of the documents or at least opportunity of inspection of the same to enable her to provide effective answers and to appropriately prepare her defence - Any debate or discussion with regard to the purport and object of the examination of accused u/s. 313 Cr.P.C. was wholly unnecessary as the law in this regard was fairly well settled by long line of the decisions of SC - Examination of accused u/s. 313 CrPC not only provided the accused opportunity to explain the incriminating circumstances appearing against him in the prosecution evidence but such examination also permitted him to put forward his own version, if he so chooses, with regard to his involvement or otherwise in the crime alleged against him - Examination of accused u/s. 313 CrPC did have fair nexus with the defence that he might choose to bring, if the need arises - Any failure on the part of the accused to put forward his version of the case in his examination u/s. 313 CrPC might have the effect of curtailing his rights in the event the accused chooses to take up specific defence and examine defence witnesses - Besides, the answers given by the accused in his examination, if incorrect or incomplete, might also jeopardises him as such incorrect or incomplete answers might have the effect of strengthening the prosecution case against the accused - While the anxiety to bring the trial to its earliest conclusion had to be shared it was fundamental that in the process none of the well entrenched principles of law that have been laboriously built by illuminating judicial precedents was sacrificed or compromised - In no circumstance, the cause of justice could be made to suffer, though, undoubtedly, it was highly desirable that the finality of any trial was achieved in the quickest possible time - To balance the need to bring the prosecution in instant case to its earliest conclusion and at the same time to protect and preserve the right of the accused to fair trial - Thus, appeal of appellant was allowed with directions - Appeal disposed of.
