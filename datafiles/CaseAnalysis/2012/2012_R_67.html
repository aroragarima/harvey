Â Re: Special Reference No.1 Of 2012 v Na

Supreme Court of India

27 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, J. S. KHEHAR, S.H. Kapadia, Dipak Misra, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 321; (2012) 10 SCC 1; 2012 (4) CompLJ 225; JT 2012 (9) SC 457; 2012 (7) MLJ(SC) 532; 2012(9) SCALE 310; [2012] 9 S.C.R. 311

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Telecom

Keywords: Migration, Special Courts, Constitutionality, Inter-State Water Disputes Act, 1956, Karnataka Cauvery Basin Irrigation Protection Ordinance, 1991

Summary: (A) Telecom - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1) and 39(b) - Presidential Reference - Maintainability - Centre for Public Litigation (CPIL) and other private petitioners filed a petitions before SC, contending that granting of Unified Access Service Licenses (UAS Licenses) to private respondents (Telecom operators), through procedure adopted by DoT (Department of Telecom) was arbitrary, illegal and in violation Constitution and sought to quash such allocation of spectrum and licenses, as alienation, transfer or distribution of natural resources/national assets otherwise than by following fair and transparent method consistent with fundamentals of equality enshrined in the Constitution - SC by its order dt.2-2-2012 held that licenses granted to private respondents on or after 10-1-2008 and subsequent allocation of spectrum to licensees were illegal and hence quashed - Hence, in exercise of powers conferred u/art. 143(1) of Constitution, President of India made instant Reference, inter- alia, with questions that for disposal of all natural resources across all sectors and in all circumstances was by the conduct of auctions and that the effect of order dt.2-2-2012 of SC was to be given retrospective - Whether the instant reference was maintainable - Held, fact that in instant Reference the word 'doubt' was used and it was a fact that art. 143(1) did not use the term 'doubt' - No specific format was in any of Schedules of Constitution as to how a reference was to be drawn - Use of word 'doubt' in a Reference was not a constitutional command or mandate and was not necessary in a Reference to be maintainable u/art. 143(1) of Constitution - Merely because earlier a review was filed and withdrawn and in the recital the narration pertained to said case, the same would not be an embargo or impediment for exercise of discretion to answer the Reference - SC was neither required to go into the truth or otherwise of facts of recitals nor could it go into the question of bona fides or otherwise of authority making a Reference - SC would decline to answer a Reference if it was improper, inadvisable and undesirable; or questions formulated have purely socio-economic or political reasons, which had no relation whatsoever with any of provisions of Constitution or otherwise were of no constitutional significance or there was authoritative pronouncement of SC which was already decided the question referred - Instant Reference should require SC to say something different to what was enunciated in 2G Case, 2012 Indlaw SC 29 as a proposition of law, could not strike at the root of maintainability of Reference - Reference was maintainable, notwithstanding its effect on ratio of 2G Case, as long as decision in that case qua lis inter parties was left unaffected - Reference answered.

(B) Telecom - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1) and 39(b) - Natural resources - Disposal of - Auctions - Constitutionality - Whether auction was only permissible method for disposal of all natural resources across all sectors and in all circumstances - Held, no court would ever implicitly, indirectly, or by inference, hold a range of laws as ultra vires the Constitution, without allowing every law to be tested on its merits - One of most profound tenets of constitutionalism was presumption of constitutionality assigned to each legislation enacted - 2G Case, 2012 Indlaw SC 29, did not even consider a plethora of laws and judgments that prescribe methods, other than auction, for dispensation of natural resources - Observations in 2G case, could not apply beyond the specific case of spectrum, which according to the law declared in 2G Case, was to be alienated only by auction and no other method - Auction as a method of disposal of natural resources could not be declared as a constitutional mandate u/art. 14 of Constitution, as it would be contrary to scheme of art. 14 - Art. 14 of Constitution might imply positive and negative rights for an individual, but with respect to State, it was only couched in negative terms - Art. 14 of Constitution was an injunction to State against taking certain type of actions rather than commanding it to take particular steps - Reading mandate of auction into its scheme would thus, be completely contrary to intent of art. 14 of Constitution - Constitutional mandate was an absolute principle that had to be applied in all situations; it could not be applied in some and not tested in others - Absolute principle was then applied on a case by case basis to see which actions fulfilled the requirements of Constitutional principle and which did not - Strength of constitutional adjudication lay in case to case adjudication and auction could not be elevated to a constitutional mandate - Reading auction as a constitutional mandate would be impermissible because such an approach should distort another constitutional principle embodied in art. 39(b) of Constitution - Auctions might be best way of maximizing revenue but revenue maximization might not always be best way to sub-serve public good - 'Common good' was sole guiding factor u/art. 39(b) of Constitution for distribution of natural resources - It was touchstone of testing whether any policy sub-serves 'common good' and if it did, irrespective of means adopted, it was clearly in accordance with principle enshrined in art. 39(b) of Constitution - Reference answered.

(C) Telecom - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1) and 39(b) - Plethora of laws - SC decisions - Judicial review - Whether a broad proposition of law that only the route of auctions could be resorted to for disposal of natural resources did not run contrary to several judgments of SC - Held, 'law declared' in a judgment, which was binding upon Courts, was the ratio decidendi of judgment - It was the essence of a decision and principle upon which, case was decided, which had to be ascertained in relation to subject-matter of decision - 2G Case, 2012 Indlaw SC 29, did not even consider a plethora of laws and judgments that prescribe methods, other than auction, for dispensation of natural resources - Observations in 2G case could not apply beyond specific case of spectrum, which according to law declared in 2G case, was to be alienated only by auction and no other method - Reference answered.

(D) Telecom - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1) and 39(b) - Policy decisions - Interference by SC - Scope - Whether there was permissible scope for interference by Courts with policy making by Govt. including methods for disposal of natural resources - Held, methodology pertaining to disposal of natural resources was clearly an economic policy - It entailed intricate economic choices and Court lacked the necessary expertise to make them - It could not be for SC to evaluate the efficacy of auction vis-Ã -vis other methods of disposal of natural resources - Court could not mandate one method to be followed in all facts and circumstances - Thus, auction, an economic choice of disposal of natural resources, was not a constitutional mandate - Court could test the legality and constitutionality of those methods - Courts could not and would not compare which policy was fairer than other, but, if a policy or law was patently unfair to extent that it fell foul of fairness requirement of art. 14 of Constitution, Court would strike it down - Auction despite being a more preferable method of alienation/allotment of natural resources, could not be held to be a constitutional requirement or limitation for alienation of all natural resources and therefore, every method other than auction could not be struck down as ultra-vires the Constitutional mandate - Auctions were not the only permissible method for disposal of all natural resources across all sectors and in all circumstances - Reference answered.

Jagdish Singh Khehar, J:

Telecom - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1) and 39(b) - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, s. 11A - Maximization of revenue - Reciprocal consideration - Whether there were circumstances in which natural resources ought to be disposed of only by auction - Held, 'auction' expressed in instant opinion, might be read as a means to maximize revenue returns, irrespective of whether means adopted should technically and correctly be described as tender, tender-cum- auction, or auction - In executing public contracts in its trading activity State should be guided by relevant principles, and not by extraneous or irrelevant consideration and should be based on reasonableness and rationality as well as non-arbitrariness - State while entering into a contractual relationship, was bound to maintain standards and any departure from standards would be invalid unless the same was supported by good reasons - While awarding a contact or a license, executive should adopt a transparent and fair method - Executive should ensure, that all eligible persons get a fair opportunity to compete - For awarding contracts or licenses, executive should adopt a rational method, so as to ensure that claims of worthy applicants were not scuttled - On subject of natural resources like spectrum, it was bounden duty of State to ensure adoption of a non-discriminatory method which would result in protection of national/public interest - Thus, State was duty bound to adopt the method of auction by giving wide publication while alienating natural resources, so as to ensure that all eligible persons could participate in process - A criteria or procedure had to be adopted so that choice among those falling in same category was based on reason, fair play and non-arbitrariness - When natural resources were made available by State to private persons for commercial exploitation exclusively for their individual gains, State's endeavour should be towards maximization of revenue returns - That alone would ensure, that fundamental right enshrined in art. 14 of Constitution and directive principle contained in art. 39(b) of Constitution, were extended to citizens of country - Legality and constitutionality of policy was one matter, and manner of its implementation quite another - Even at implementation stage a forthright and legitimate policy, might take shape of an illegitimate stratagem - If legislative policy had no nexus to any legitimate objective, or it transgresses the mandate of distribution of material resources to 'best subserve the common good', it might well be unfair, unreasonable or discriminatory No part of natural resource could be dissipated as a matter of largess, charity, donation or endowment, for private exploitation - Each bit of natural resource expended should bring back a reciprocal consideration - Reference answered.
