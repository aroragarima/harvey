State of Karnataka v Registrar General, High Court of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

10 August 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, R.P. Sethi

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 502; (2000) 7 SCC 333; (2000) SCC (Cr) 1359; AIR 2000 SC 2626; AIR 2000 SCW 2794; 2000 (3) CCR 123; 2000 (3) Crimes 172; 2000 CRLJ 3958; JT 2000 (8) SC 619; 2000 (3) RCR(Criminal) 626; 2000(5) SCALE 504; 2000 (3) SCJ 201; 2000 (6) SLT 123; [2000] Supp2 S.C.R. 384; 2000 (5) Supreme 553

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Legality, Directions of HC

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 307 - Directions of HC - Legality - Seven persons were prosecuted in a Sessions court for various offences, the serious-most among which was the offence u/s. 307 IPC - Sessions Judge acquitted all the accused - State filed petition for leave to appeal against the said order of acquittal - DB refused leave and made a departure from the precedents and issued unusual direction to State Public Prosecutor - HC directed the State Public Prosecutor to forward a copy of this order to the Secretary to Government (Home) as also to the Honorable Home Minister both of whom shall acknowledge the receipt of the same and shall report back to HC within a period of two months as to what precisely is the reaction of the Government to the observations of HC - Hence instant petition.

Held, judicial disposition is definitely different from a paper presented for seminar discussion. Nor can it be equated with a dissertation. Judicial decorum requires that judgments and orders should confine to the facts and legal points involved in the particular cases which Judges deal with. May be, sometimes Judges would, perhaps wittingly or even unwittingly go outside the contours of the litigation, but even such overlappings should be within bounds of propriety and sobriety. But there is no justification for traversing so far beyond the convass as was done by the HC in instant case or to cover areas which are grossly extraneous to the subject matter of the case. If the subordinate courts are also to be tempted and encouraged to follow suit by travelling far outside the scope of the lis the consequences would be far too many. Demoralization of departments would badly erode the already impaired efficiency of our forces. It is time to remind ourselves once again that judgment should confine to the scope of the case. It would have been very appropriate if Judges of DB who rendered the impugned order would have reminded themselves of the above equation administered by the apex court more than three decades ago. For the aforesaid reasons, directions issued to the State Public Prosecutor as well as to the Home Minister and Home Secretary of the State is set aside. Appeal disposed of.
