Kalyani (Dead) By Lrs v Narayanan and Others

Supreme Court of India

27 February 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, V.D. Tulzapurkar, A.P. Sen

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 531; (1980) Supp SCC 298; AIR 1980 SC 1173; [1980] 2 S.C.R. 1130

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Survivorship

Summary: (A) Family & Personal - Constitution of India, 1950 - Succession - Joint Family Property - Inheritance - Family Arrangement - There was a joint Hindu family consisting of several members - There were certain properties valuing certain amount - Owner of the property was ill - He had prepared a will for his family for distribution of his properties - This was a registered deed - There was a certain dispute which took place between the family members after certain death took place - Suit was principally resisted by defendant no. 1 as per his written statement - Partition of properties took place - Will and deed of partition was not effective - Plaintiffs suit was dismissed - Appeal was filed by the plaintiff - Whether under the Mitakshara Law the parties were governed by customary law, and, in the absence of any rule of customary law on the point in question, by Mitakshara Law property could be divided, albeit by a family settlement, between two artificial units of a joint family, one comprising the sons of a father by his first wife, the first wife and his step mother, and the other comprising his son by his second wife and the second wife so as to constitute each unit into a coparcenary with rights of survivorship between its members - Held, a partition branchwise was unknown to Mitakshara law and was wholly untenable - So long as a family remained an undivided family, two or more members of it, whether they be members of different branches or of one and the same branch of the family, can have no legal existence as a separate independent unit, but all the members of a branch, or of a sub-branch, can form a distinct and separate corporate unit within the larger corporate family and hold property as such - Such property would be joint family property of the members of the branch inter se, but would be separate property of that branch in relation to the larger family - The principle of joint tenancy was unknown to Hindu law except in the case of the joint property of an undivided Hindu family governed by the Mitakshara law - Since the execution of Ext. P-1 after the death of the father, the son of the second wife took his share of the property left the family there was a disruption of the joint family and the sons by the fathers first wife held the property, which remained for them after the son of second wife obtained his share, not as joint tenants but as tenants-in-common, the plaintiff would be entitled to the share to which her deceased husband Raman was entitled - Husband of plaintiff had 1/4 share in A schedule properties which the plaintiff would be entitled and therefore, there would be a preliminary decree in her favour to that effect - Plaintiff's claim to a share in properties set out in schedules B and C annexed to the plaint had been concurrently negatived by both the courts on the finding that they are the properties of defendant no. 1 and his wife and were not accretions to the property which devolved from the second son of the deceased father - Appeal partly allowed.

(B) Family & Personal - Constitution of India, 1950 - Succession - Joint Family Property - Inheritance - Family Arrangement - Whether the use of the word 'tavazhi' (in any case a misnomer) in describing the two units in the will, Ext. P-1 left by the father and held to be the basis of the family settlement, is sufficient in the circumstances, to establish an intention that the members of each unit were to take the property as coparceners and not as tenants-in- common, the grouping into units being only for convenient enjoyment - Held, Ext. P-1 itself specified the share of each member separately - There was no concept known to Hindu law that there could be a branch of a family wife- wise - If a Hindu father had two wives and he had three male children by the first wife and two by the second, each wife constituting a branch with her children of the family was a concept foreign to Hindu law - Therefore, tavazhi wife-wise stated in Ext. P-1 had to be ignored and the contention that there was a partition amongst wife-wise branches as represented by each wife is equally untenable - Ext. P-1 did bring about a specification of shares and once such shares were defined by the father who had the power to define and vesting the same there was a disruption of joint family - Therefore, the four sons of the first wife held the property as tenants in common - Appeal partly allowed.
