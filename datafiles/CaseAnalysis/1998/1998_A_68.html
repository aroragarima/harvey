M/S Hyderabad Vanaspathi Limited v Andhra Pradesh State Electricity Board And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

01 April 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Srinivasan, S.C. Agrawal, S. Saghir Ahmad

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 604; (1998) 4 SCC 470; AIR 1998 SC 1715; JT 1998 (3) SC 84; 1998(2) SCALE 603; [1998] 2 S.C.R. 620; 1998 (4) SLT 182; 1998 (3) Supreme 454

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Electricity & Energy

Summary: Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 , arts.14, 20(1)- Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910, s.39 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.379 - Agreements - Power supply - Assessment - Promissory estoppels - Appellant was a public limited company engaged in manufacture entered into two agreements with Electricity Board - Power supply was immediately disconnected and provisional assessment of loss was made at Rs. 61,28,535 Prosecution was launched u/s.379 of IPC r/w s.39 of 1910 Act - Appellant filed a suit for a declaration that it was not liable to pay any amount as penal damages and prayed for a direction for refund of amount of Rs. 22.50 lakh collected by Board during pendency of assessment proceedings and for perpetual injunction restraining defendants from disconnecting power supply - Trial court detained that dismissed suit - HC apprehended that terms and conditions of supply on basis of which agreements were entered between appellant and Board - Order was challenged by appellant in appeal - Hence, instant appeal - Whether disconnection of power supply was violative of art.14 of Constitution - Held, condition was not arbitrary or unreasonable, but within powers of Board - It does not offend any of art. of Constitution - Prima facie conclusion of power-theft reached by authorities, it was not necessary to give further hearing to consumer and action taken by Board disconnecting supply was not violative of arts.20 (1) and 14 of Constitution - Immediate disconnection of supply was not violative of art.14 of Constitution and principles of Natural Justice would not apply - Principle 'Nemo Judex in Cause Sua' would not apply in case as officers have no personal it's with consumers - Principle of promissory estoppel applied on the facts and circumstances and by entering into standard agreement containing provision for revision of "rate schedule" from time to time - Consumer had not given up his claim for rebate for a period of three years as per promise detained out by Board - Appeal dismissed.
