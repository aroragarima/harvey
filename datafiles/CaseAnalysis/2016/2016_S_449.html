Mumtaz@ Muntyaz and another v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

01 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Uday Umesh Lalit, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 429; AIR 2016 SC 3151; 2016 (4) JCC 2417; JT 2016 (6) SC 232; 2016 (4) MLJ(Crl) 414; 2016 (3) RCR(Criminal) 552; [2016] 3 S.C.R. 434; 2016 (3) WLN(SC) 162

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Dying Declaration, Juvenile, FIR, Endorsement, Kerosene, Age, Seizure Memo, Dead Body, Birth, Juvenile Justice Act, 1986, Provocation, Juvenile Justice (Care And Protection Of Children) Act, 2000, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Investigating Officer, Inquiry, Seizure, Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015 - Juvenile Justice Act, 1986 - Dying declaration - Determination of Juvenility - Charge sheet was filed against appellants and other co-accused for causing death of deceased by setting fire upon him and upon perusal of evidence, Trial Court found appellants guilty of charges punishable u/s. 302 read with s. 34 of IPC and imposed punishment but acquitted other co-accused of all charges - Aggrieved by conviction and sentence, appellants filed appeal before HC wherein order of conviction and sentence passed by Trial Court was affirmed relying upon eye-witness account and dying declaration - Hence, instant appeals - Whether impugned orders are sustainable.

Held, dying declaration bears appropriate endorsement of doctor on duty which endorsement was proved by PW8. There is nothing in cross examination of either PW5 or PW8 or in dying declaration which could raise any doubt and hence, evidence in that behalf is trustworthy and so, dying declaration is reliable. Hence, there are no reasons to interfere with findings of Trial Court regarding conviction and sentence of appellant-M and so, appeal preferred by said appellant is dismissed. However, appellant-D, on date of occurrence was more than 16 years of age but less than 18 of years of age. As per 1986 Act, which was in force at that time, he was not juvenile and so Trial Court rightly tried and convicted said appellant. However, during pendency of appeal, 1986 Act was repealed and 2000 Act came into force which raised age of juvenility from 16 to 18 years and as per s. 20 of 2000 Act, determination of juvenility is required to be done in all pending matters in accordance with s. 2(1) of 2000 Act. Hence, sentence of life imprisonment passed against appellant-D is set aside and matter is remitted to Jurisdictional Juvenile Justice Board for determining the appropriate quantum of fine that is to be levied on appellant-D and amount of compensation that is to be awarded to family of deceased. Appeals disposed of.
