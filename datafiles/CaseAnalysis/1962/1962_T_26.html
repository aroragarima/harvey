Supreme Court of India

18 October 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K.C. Das Gupta, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 390; AIR 1963 SC 874; 1963 (6) FLR 103; 1962 (2) LLJ 760; 1964 (1) SCJ 140; [1963] Supp1 S.C.R. 625

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Arbitration Act

Summary: Labour & Industry Law - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136(1), 133(1) and 134(1) - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 10 A - (A) How does 136 differ from art. 133 and 134 of Constitution? - Held, art. 133(1) and 134(1) provide for appeals to Supreme Court against judgments, decrees or final orders passed by High Court, no such limitation is prescribed by art. 136 - All Courts and all Tribunals in territory of India except those in cl. (2) are subject to appellate jurisdiction of Supreme Court under art. 136 (1) - Whereas appellate jurisdiction of SC u/arts. 133(1) and 134(1) can be invoked only against final orders; no such limitation is imposed by art. 136(1) - All causes and matters that are brought for adjudication before Courts or tribunals are covered by art. 136 (1) - In exercising its powers under this Article, SC in its discretion refuses to entertain applications for special leave where it appears to court that interference with orders sought to be appealed against may not be necessary in interest of justice - (B) When can art. 136 be invoked?- Held that two conditions must be satisfied; (1) act complained against must have character of a judicial or a quasi-judicial act as distinguished from a mere executive or administrative act, and (2) authority whose act is complained against must be a court or a tribunal - Unless both conditions are satisfied, art. 136 (1) cannot be invoked - (C) Whether there can be an appeal from an award of arbitrator? - Further held, decisions of arbitrators to whom industrial disputes are voluntarily referred under s 10-A, Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 are, no doubt, quasi-judicial decisions and amount to a determination or order under art. 136(1) - But an arbitrator acting under s.10-A is not a 'Tribunal' under art. 136 even though some of 'the trappings of a Court' are present in his case - As one of essential conditions for invoking art. 136(1) is not satisfied, special leave to appeal to SC from decision of an arbitrator acting under S. 10-A, Industrial Disputes Act, cannot be granted - (D) When can an order be called as administrative order and judicial order?- Further held, courts of law established by State decide cases brought before them judicially and decisions thus recorded by them fall under category of judicial decisions - Administrative or executive bodies decisions fall clearly under category of administrative or executive orders - Even Judges have, in certain matters, to act administratively, while administrative or executive authorities may have to act quasi-judicially in dealing with some matters entrusted to their jurisdiction - Where an authority is required to act judicially either by an express provision of statute under which it acts or by necessary implication of said statute, decisions of such an authority generally amount to quasi-judicial decisions - (E) What is difference between a tribunal and a court? - Further held, expression "a Court" in technical sense is a tribunal constituted by State as a part of ordinary hierarchy of courts which are invested with State's inherent judicial powers - Tribunal as distinguished from court, exercises judicial powers and decides matters brought before it judicially or quasi-judicially, but it does not constitute a court in technical sense - (F) Whether arbitrator can be considered as a tribunal? - Further held, fact that arbitrator under s. 10 A is not exactly in same position as a probate arbitrator, does not mean that he is a tribunal under art. 136 - He is not invested with States inherent judicial power - He is appointed by parties and power to decide dispute between parties who appoint him is derived by him from agreement of parties and from no other source - Hence, from decision of an arbitrator under s 10 A special leave to appeal to SC under art. 136 (1) cannot be granted From mere fact that a writ under art. 226 may lie against award pronounced by an arbitrator acting under s. l0 A it cannot be concluded that such an arbitrator is a 'tribunal within art. 136 (1) because art. 226 is in a sense wider than art. 136, power conferred under art. 226 not being conditioned or limited by requirement that writs can be issued only against an order of a court or a tribunal - Appeals dismissed.
