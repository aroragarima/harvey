State of W. B. and Others v Karan Singh Binayak and Others

Supreme Court of India

20 March 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.K. Sabharwal, U.C. Banerjee

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1954; (2002) 4 SCC 188; AIR 2002 SC 1543; JT 2002 (3) SC 324; 2002(3) SCALE 108; 2002 (2) SCJ 540; [2002] 2 S.C.R. 620; 2002 (2) SLT 729; 2002 (2) Supreme 537

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - West Bengal Estates Acquisition Act, 1953, ss. 2(i), 4, 5, 6, 44 and 57A - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulations) Act, 1976 - Bengal Municipal Act, 1932, s. 548(2) - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 151 - Whether, as intermediary, erstwhile owners were entitled to retain land u/s. 6(1)(b) and it does not vest in State Govt. u/s. 4(1) of Act? - S. 6(1)(b) permits intermediary to retain land when it is appertaining to building and structure owned by intermediary and it does not contemplate that when building and structure is leased out, owner will not be entitled to retain land appurtenant to such building and structure which was leased with land; in sub-cl. (b) of sub-s (1) of s. 6, khas possession has not been mentioned whereas it is so in certain other clauses of s. 6(1), where it was intended that actual possession should be with intermediary, it was said so specifically - Held, s. 6(1)(b) only mean that where building and structure is not owned by intermediary or any person holding under him by leave or licence, he would not be entitled to retain land comprised in or appertaining to such building or structure, in other words, it means that when building and structure is owned by intermediary even though tenanted, he would be entitled to land comprised in or appertaining thereto; further there is neither any finding nor any material to suggest that a mill stood on land on date of vesting, hence, s. 6(1)(g), proviso to s. 6(2) and s. 6(3) have no applicability; it is a case of composite lease and s. 6(1)(b) has thus been rightly applied by High Court - Whether proceedings under ULC Act or preparation of record-of-rights were ultra vires and acts without jurisdiction and, therefore, those proceedings would not operate as a bar in appellants' invoking inherent jurisdiction u/s. 151 of CPC by virtue of conferment of such power u/s. 57A of Act? - Inherent powers cannot be used to reopen settled matters and cannot be resorted to when there are specific provisions in Act to deal with situation - Further held, it would be an abuse to allow reopening of settled matter after nearly four decades in purported exercise of inherent powers; if land had vested in State u/ss. 4 and 5 of Act on issue of notification in year 1954 as contended, ULC Act will have no applicability as s. 19 of ULC Act provides that Chap. II of Act, subject to provisions of s. 10(2) shall not apply to any vacant land held by any State Govt. - Appeal dismissed.
