G. Krishnareddy v Sajjappa (D) By Lrs. And Anr.

Supreme Court of India

18 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 448; (2011) 13 SCC 226; AIR 2011 SC 2762; 2011 (6) ALD(SC) 95; 2012(1) ALL MR 493; JT 2011 (11) SC 187; 2011 (6) KarLJ 269; 2011 (4) RCR(Civil) 751; 2011 (4) RLW 3511; 2011(7) SCALE 712; [2011] 9 S.C.R. 136

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Karnataka Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prohibition of Transfer of Certain Lands) Act, 1978 - Valid purchase - Claim of adverse possession - Sustainability - Original grantee was granted the suit land by the State on 1957 with a condition prohibiting any alienation for a period of 15 years - Original grantee sold the suit land to appellant's father on 1968 - 1978 Act came into force subsequently and original grantee filed application under the provisions of the Act for resumption of suit land from appellant's father - Assistant Commissioner allowed application, which was confirmed by Deputy Commissioner - However, HC, in appellant's writ petition, remanded the matter to Deputy Commissioner - Assistant Commissioner after conducting an enquiry held that the purchaser was in possession of the land for more than 12 years that was further confirmed in appeal by the Deputy Commissioner - Respondents, heirs of original grantee, challenged Deputy Commissioner's order before HC - Single Judge held that since the purchaser had taken the stand that by purchasing the said land under a valid sale deed he had been enjoying the cultivation and possession in his own right as owner thereof, therefore, he was precluded from setting up the inconsistent plea of adverse possession either as against the State or the grantee - Appellant's writ appeal thereafter had also been dismissed - (A) Whether the appellant's plea of adverse possession was sustainable - Held, predecessor-in-interest of the appellant claimed title over the suit land by virtue of purchase and at no stage he had put up any hostile claim to the property - Appellant's plea was of ownership by right of purchase and therefore a lawful right to enjoy the property - Single Judge while allowing the writ petition filed by the respondent had made reference to the said position and held that the plea of adverse possession was not available to the predecessor-in- interest of the appellant in law and in view of such legal position the authorities below erred in accepting the plea of adverse possession in respect of the granted land - Hence, findings of the HC was justifiable - (B) Whether in the instant case the period of limitation would be 12 years or 30 years - Held, grant given to the original grantee indicated that nowhere in the said grant it had been clearly and specifically stated that it had been an absolute transfer of the right in title and possession by the State Government to the concerned person - Grantee also indicated that it was only a transfer of the possession of the land by way of allotment and in none of the clauses of the grant it was stated that it was a conveyance of the title over such land by the State Government - Cls. 1 of the grant gave authority to the grantee to clear the land and to bring it to cultivable stage - It further provided that the grantee could enjoy the property for 15 years - Not only the grant was only for a limited period but it was also for cultivation, therefore, it was a grant for possession by way of cultivation for a limited period and it could not be said that by the said grant the transferee had acquired absolute title to the land in question from the State Government - Hence, the period of limitation which would have been applicable in the instant case would be 30 years - No merit in appeal - Appeal dismissed.
