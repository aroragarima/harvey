Kayjay Industries (P) Ltd v Asnew Drums (P) Ltd. & Ors

Supreme Court of India

20 March 1974

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, D.G. Palekar, P.N. Bhagwati

Where Reported

1974 Indlaw SC 63; (1974) 2 SCC 213; AIR 1974 SC 1331; [1974] 3 S.C.R. 678

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 o.XXI, r. 90 - State Financial Corporation Act, 1951 s. 31 - Mortgage -Auction sale - Frequent adjournments - Decree holder/respondent no.4 had lent Rs. 10 lakhs to respondent no.1 by mortgaging its land, factory building, plant and machinery - Respondent nos. 2 and 3 were guarantors for said loan - Decree holder had issued notice to respondent no.1u/s. 31 of the Act, for his default in repayment as no reply was received decree holder filed suit against respondent nos. 1, 2 and 3 u/s. 31 of the Act before Trial court for attachment and sale of properties - Trial court decreed suit in favour of decree holder and receiver was appointed for sale through court auction - Proclamation for sale was made and appellant was highest bidder - Judgement debtor/appellant had postponed sale on one pretext or other and after frequent adjournments it was finally auctioned for lesser price - Judgement debtor had challenged said sale and Executing court had refused to set aside u/o. XXI, r. 90 of CPC - Appeal was filed in HC against said order which reversed decision of Executing court and set aside sale - Hence, instant appeal by auction purchaser.

Held, Trial court had exercised a conscientious and lively discretion in concluding the sale at Rs. 11 .5 lakhs. If the market value was over 17 lakhs, it is unfortunate that a lesser price was fetched and mere inadequacy of price cannot demolish every court sale. Further, the court tried its best, time after time, to raise the price, well-known industrialists in the public and private sectors knew about it and turned up and offers reached a stationary level. Therefore, judgment debtor himself, by his litigious exercises, would have contributed to the possible buyers being afraid of hurdles ahead and after all, producing around Rs. 11.5 lakhs openly to buy an industry is not easy even for an apparently affluent businessmen. Thus, sale proceedings had been pending too long and respondent no.1 could not, even when given the opportunity, produce buyers by private negotiation and even a valuer's report was not produced by him Hence, Trial court had committed no material irregularity in the conduct of the sale in accepting the highest offer of the appellant and no merit in the application to set aside the sale. Appeal allowed.
