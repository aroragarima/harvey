State of Punjab v Naib Din

Supreme Court of India

28 September 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, S.N. Variava

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 100; (2001) 8 SCC 578; (2002) SCC (Cr) 33; AIR 2001 SC 3955; 2001 (4) CCR 69; 2001 CRLJ 4656; 2001 (2) JCC 224; JT 2001 (8) SC 189; 2001 (4) RCR(Criminal) 465; 2001(6) SCALE 532; 2001 (3) SCJ 692; 2001 (6) SLT 773; [2001] Supp3 S.C.R. 396; 2001 (7) Supreme 317

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conviction, Sustainability, Opium Act, 1878, Possession of opium

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Opium Act, 1878, s. 9 - Possession of opium - Conviction - Sustainability - A case was registered against accused respondent u/s. 9 of the Act - Trial Court passed conviction order - On appeal before Appellate Court the same was dismissed - Respondent filed revision before HC and the same was disposed of - Hence instant appeal.

Held, respondent failed to show that there was any failure of justice on account of omission to put a question concerning such formal evidence when he was examined. No objection was raised in Trial Court on ground of such omission. No ground was taken up in Appellate Court on such ground. If any Appellate Court comes across that Trial Court had not put any question to an accused even if it is of a vital nature, such omission alone should not result in setting aside conviction and sentence as an inevitable consequence. Effort should be made to undo or correct lapse. If it is not possible to correct it by any means Court should then consider impact of lapse on overall aspect of case. After keeping that particular item of evidence aside, if remaining evidence is sufficient to bring home guilt of respondent, the lapse does not matter much, and can be sidelined justifiably. But if lapse is so vital as would affect entire case, appellate or Revisional Court can endeavor to see whether it could be rectified. Some dilution of rigour of provision can be made even in light of a contention raised by respondent that non-questioning him on a vital circumstance by Trial Court has caused prejudice to him. The explanation offered by counsel of respondent at appellate stage was held to be a sufficient substitute for answers given by respondent himself. Impugned order passed by HC is set aside. Appeal disposed of.
