M/S. National Seeds Corporation Ltd. v M. Madhusudhan Reddy And Another

Supreme Court of India

16 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 506; (2012) 2 SCC 506; 2012 (3) ALD(SC) 136; 2012 (91) ALR 503; 2012 (2) AWC 2138; 2012 (1) CPJ(Sc) 1; JT 2012 (1) SC 330; 2012 (4) MahLJ 118; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 166; 2012 (1) RCR(Civil) 838; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 1065

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection; Agriculture & Agri. Products

Keywords: Seeds, Appropriate Laboratory, Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act, 2011, Consumer Protection (Amendment) Act, Consumer Protection Bill

Summary: (A) Consumer Protection - Agriculture & Agri. Products - Seeds Act, 1966, ss. 10, 19, 21 - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, ss. 2(d), 13(1)(c) - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 8 - Complaint - Jurisdiction - Alternate remedy - Challenged - Respondents (agriculturists) filed complaints u/s. 12 of 1986 Act before District Consumer Forum alleging that they had suffered loss due to failure of crops/less yield because the seeds sold/supplied by appellant (Corporation) were defective - District Forum allowed complaints and awarded compensation to respondents - Aggrieved appellant filed appeals and revisions before State Forum, which were dismissed - Aggrieved appellant filed appeal before National Commission - Commission by impugned order held that expert report was rightly relied upon by District Forum concluding that crop had failed due to use of defective seeds - Hence, instant appeals - Appellant contended that District Forums did not have jurisdiction to entertain complaints filed by respondents and that under 1966 Act there was a remedy for respondents to file an application u/s. 10 of 1966 Act - Further contended that appellant entered into agreements with growers, with an arbitration clause and that growers could not be treated as 'consumer' within meaning of s. 2(d) of 1986 Act, as appellants purchased seeds for commercial purpose - Whether 1966 Act was a special Act vis-Ã -vis 1986 Act and District Forums could not have entertained complaints filed by respondents as they could remedy under 1966 Act - Held, though 1966 Act was a special legislation enacted for ensuring that there was no compromise with quality of seeds sold to farmers and others and provisions were made for imposition of substantive punishment on a person found guilty of violating provisions relating quality of seeds, legislature had not put in place any adjudicatory mechanism for compensating the farmers/growers of seeds and other similarly situated persons who might suffer loss of crop or who might get insufficient yield due to use of defective seeds sold/supplied by appellant or any other authorised person - No one could dispute that agriculturists and horticulturists were the largest consumers of seeds - Farmers suffer loss of crop due to various reasons, one of which was use of defective/sub-standard seeds - 1966 Act was totally silent on issue of payment of compensation for loss of crop on account of use of defective seeds supplied by appellant and others who might obtain certificate u/s. 9 of 1966 Act - A farmer who suffer loss of crop due to defective seeds could approach Seed Inspector and make a request for prosecution of person from whom he purchased seeds - If found guilty, such person could be imprisoned, but that could not redeem the loss suffered by farmer - There was nothing in 1966 and Rules thereunder which might give an indication that provisions of 1986 Act were not available to farmers who were otherwise covered by wide definition of 'consumer' u/s. 2(d) of 1986 Act - Any attempt to exclude farmers from the ambit of 1986 Act by implication would make that Act vulnerable to an attack of unconstitutionality on the ground of discrimination and there was no reason why provisions of 1986 Act should be so interpreted - By virtue of explanation which was added w.e.f. 18.6.1993 by Consumer Protection (Amendment) Act 50 of 1993, it was clarified that expression 'commercial purpose' used in ss. 2(d)(i) did not include use by a consumer of goods bought and used by him for purpose of earning his livelihood by means of self-employment - Since farmers/growers purchased seeds by paying a price to appellant, they would certainly fall within the ambit of s. 2(d)(i) of 1986 Act and there was no reason to deny them the remedies which were available to other consumers of goods and services - Maharashtra Hybrid Seeds Company Ltd. v. Alavalapati Chandra Reddy, 1998 Indlaw SC 826, followed - Appeals dismissed.

(B)Consumer Protection - Agriculture & Agri. Products - Seeds Act, 1966, ss. 10, 19, 21 -Consumer Protection Act, 1986, ss. 2(d), 13(1)(c) - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 8 - Arbitration - Consumer compliant - Maintainability - Whether growers of seeds were not entitled to file complaint under 1986 Act and only remedy available to them for alleged breach of terms of agreement was to apply for arbitration - Held, remedy available to an aggrieved farmer/grower to lodge a complaint with concerned Seed Inspector for prosecution of seller/supplier of seed could not but be treated as illusory and he could not be denied relief under1986 Act on the ground of availability of an alternative remedy - Remedy of arbitration was an optional remedy - Farmer could either seek reference to an arbitrator or file a complaint under 1986 Act - If grower opts for remedy of arbitration, then it might be possible to say that he could not, subsequently, file complaint under 1986 Act - However, if farmer chosen to file a complaint in first instance before competent Consumer Forum, then he could not be denied relief by invoking s. 8 of 1996 Act - S. 3 of 1986 Act made clear that remedy available in that Act was in addition to and not in derogation of provisions of any other law for the time being in force - Agreements entered into between appellant and growers clearly postulated supply of foundation seeds by appellant with an assurance that crop would be purchased by it - It was neither pleaded case of appellant nor any evidence was produced before any of Consumer Forums that growers had freedom to sell seeds in open market or to any person other than appellant - Thus, it was not possible that growers had purchased seeds for resale or for any commercial purpose and they were excluded from definition of term 'consumer' - Growers had agreed to produce seeds on behalf of appellant for purpose of earning their livelihood by using their skills and labour - Maharashtra Hybrid Seeds Company Ltd. v. Alavalapati Chandra Reddy, 1998 Indlaw SC 826, followed - Appeals dismissed.
