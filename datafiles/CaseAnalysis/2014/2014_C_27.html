State of Rajasthan v Chandgi Ram and others

Supreme Court of India

09 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 599; (2014) 14 SCC 596; 2014 CRLJ 4571; 2014 (4) JCC 2940; JT 2014 (14) SC 443; 2014 (4) MLJ(Crl) 611; 2014(10) SCALE 352

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.302/34 - Murder - Acquittal - Sustainability - Respondents allegedly committed offence of murder - Respondents were convicted by Trial Court u/s.302/34 IPC - HC set aside the conviction of respondents - Hence instant appeal -

Held, post-mortem Doctor/PW-7, who confirmed the injuries found on the body of the deceased as per the post-mortem report (Exhibit P-10), disclosed that there were as many as 14 injuries of which the head injury was fatal. The said version of the doctor also confirmed the injuries sustained by the deceased on his head, as well as, other vital parts of his body. Therefore, a cumulative consideration of the evidence amply established the crime in which the respondents were involved, resulted in the killing of the deceased. Also according to the statement of the IO/PW-13, during the course of investigation all the weapons described by the eyewitnesses, which had blood stains on them, were recovered from the possession of the Respondents-accused. It can also be inferred from the post-mortem report (Exhibit P-10) of PW-7, the medical officer that the various injuries caused on the deceased were from the weapons recovered at the instance of the accused. Therefore, these findings are strong factors in establishing the culpability of the Respondents-accused in committing the murder. Reasoning of the DB of HC in concluding that the offence was not made out, it was mainly on the ground that there was delay in the lodging of the FIR and the conduct of the witnesses as spoken to buy them did not inspire confidence. When the HC had interfered with the conviction imposed by the Trial Court, it ought to have examined the evidence meticulously and expressed cogent and convincing reasons as to why the detailed consideration of the evidence did not inspire confidence in order to interfere with the conclusion of the Trial Court. HC had miserably failed to carry out the said exercise and without assigning reasons, much less convincing reasons, has chosen to interfere with the conviction imposed by the Trial Court in a light hearted manner. Eye witnesses' account of the concerned witnesses was all convincing and was corroborative in every minute aspect of the occurrence. Eye witnesses' version was natural and there was nothing to suspect their version in narrating the occurrence. Appeal allowed.
