Union of India and another v Delhi High Court Bar Association and others

Supreme Court of India

14 March 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Kirpal, Y.K. Sabharwal, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 163; (2002) 4 SCC 275; AIR 2002 SC 1479; AIR 2002 SCW 1347; 2002 (5) ALD(SC) 99; 2002 (2) AllCJ 1247; 2002 (47) ALR 324; 2002 (2) BC 194; 2002 (3) CLR 306; [2002] 110 Comp Cas 141; 2002 (2) CompLJ 231; 2002 (2) CTC 106; 2002 (96) DLT 726; JT 2002 (3) SC 131; 2002 (2) MLJ(SC) 122; 2002(2) SCALE 668; 2002 (2) SCJ 449; [2002] 2 S.C.R. 450; 2002 (2) SLT 556; 2002 (2) Supreme 435; 2002 (2) UPLBEC 1617

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Recovery Officer, Shall Not Be Bound

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Recovery of Debts Due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993, ss.17, 18, 19, 22, 25, 28(1), 28(2), 31, 34(1) - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 14, 23A , 323A, 323B - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 19 r. 1 - Debts Recovery Tribunal (Procedure) Rules, 1993 - Establishment of Tribunal - Bar of jurisdiction - Constitutional validity - Procedure for recovery of debts due to banks and financial institutions which was being followed resulted in significant portion of funds being blocked - In order to remedy locking up of huge funds, Parliament enacted the Act - Validity of the Act was successfully challenged before HC - HC held that though Tribunal could be constituted by Parliament even though it was not within purview of arts. 323A and 323B of the Constitution, impugned Act was unconstitutional as it eroded independence of judiciary and was hit by art. 14 of the Constitution - HC came to conclusion that though Parliament had legislative competence to enact law, but as it had abrogated/negated power of judicial review, which had violated basic feature of the Constitution, the Act was void - Some of provisions of the Act were struck off - Hence, instant appeals - Whether Parliament has competence to enact law for establishing Banking Tribunals.

Held, while arts. 323A and 323B of the Constitution specifically enable the legislatures to enact laws for the establishment of Tribunals, in relation to the matters specified therein, the power of the Parliament to enact a law constituting a Tribunal, like the Banking Tribunal, which is not covered by any of the matters specified in arts. 323A or 323B of the Constitution is not taken away. These arts., however, cannot be interpreted to mean that it prohibits the legislature from establishing Tribunals not covered by these arts., as long as there is legislative competence under an appropriate entry in the Seventh Schedule. There can be little doubt that under Entry 45 of List I, it is the Parliament alone which can enact a law with regard to the conduct of business by the banks. The very purpose of establishing the Tribunal being to expedite the disposal of the applications filed by the banks and financial institutions for realisation of money, the Tribunal and the Appellate Tribunals are required to deal with the applications in an expeditious manner. It is precisely for this reason that s. 22(1) of the Act stipulates that the Tribunal and the Appellate Tribunal shall not be bound by the procedure laid down by the CPC. The bifurcation of original jurisdiction between the Delhi High Court and the subordinate Courts is a matter, which cannot have any bearing on the validity of the establishment of the Tribunal. HC had held that Ss. 25 and 28 are arbitrary and unreasonable, being without any guidelines or control. The Act is a valid piece of legislation. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - Recovery of dues is essential function of any banking institution, in exercise of its legislative power relating to banking, Parliament can provide mechanism by which monies due to Banks and Financial Institutions can be recovered.
