Kalidindi Venkata Subbaraju and Others v Chintalapati Subbaraju and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 November 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Shelat, S.M. Sikri, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 412; AIR 1968 SC 947; 1968 (2) MLJ(SC) 75; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 292

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 s. 32(5) - Immovable property - Will - Succession - Compromise - Minor - Sound state of mind - Validity of will - Burden of proof - Reversioners - Deceased left a will bequeathing all his properties to his mother absolutely - Uncle of deceased's father (uncle) filed a suit for a declaration that the will was not valid as deceased was a minor at the time of execution of the will and was not in a sound disposing state of mind - Suit ended in a compromise decree by which the uncle admitted that deceased was a major when he died, that he was in a sound disposing state of mind and that the will therefore was genuine and valid - Under the compromise decree he received certain part of the land and the rest of the property was retained by deceased's mother - By settlement deeds, the mother settled part of the land received under the decree in favour of her daughters, the mothers of appellants - Properties were possessed of and enjoyed first by the said two daughters and later by appellants - After the mother died, appellants obtained a deed of surrender from daughters - Appellants filed the suit contending that they were the nearest reversioners of deceased, being the sons of his sisters, that the said compromise decree was collusive, that the deceased did not execute the will that even if he did he was not a major nor of sound disposing state of mind when he executed it and that therefore the said will was not valid - Whether respondents had discharged the burden of proof that the will was validly executed by deceased and he was a major at the time of executing it - Held, there was ample evidence to conclude that deceased executed the will and was at the time in a sound disposing state of mind - Though respondents did not produce the original will but produced only its certified copy, there was evidence from which the HC could conclude that the will was duly executed by deceased and attested by the witnesses who had affixed their signatures thereto - There was evidence that the mother had produced the will soon after its execution in suit filed by the uncle, that she had based her claim to deceased's property by virtue of the will and that her conduct in dealing with the property was that of an absolute owner based on her claim under the will - Also there were the settlement deeds executed by her in favour of her daughters - And the fact of the terms of the will were natural and rational, in that it was consistent with deceased's anxiety that in the absence of any male heir to him the properties should go to his mother to enable her to make due provision for his sisters instead of dying intestate and the properties thereon going to his uncle and his heirs under the law - Moreover, there was also the evidence of a DW testifying to the execution of the will by the deceased and to his having attested the original will along with other witnesses - His evidence also was that deceased was then in a sound disposing state of mind - Both the trial court and the HC accepted the evidence of the DW as a person who had attested the will - Thus, the effect of the certified copy of the will having been thus rightly admitted was as if the contents of the will were before the court and the court could proceed to construe those contents - HC was quite competent in construing the contents of the will and in holding that the terms of the said will were natural and rational and proved that deceased was in a sound disposing state of mind - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Land & Property - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 s. 32(5) - Immovable property - Will - Succession - Compromise - Minor - Sound state of mind - Validity of will - Burden of proof - Whether the conclusion of the HC and the trial court that deceased was 19 years of age at the time he executed the will was justified - Held, statement made by deceased in his will that he was 19 years of age at the time of its execution was admissible u/s. 32(5) of the 1872 Act and was rightly relied upon by both the trial court and the HC in establishing that the deceased was a major and was competent to make the will - Further there was evidence of the DW testifying to the execution of the will by deceased and to his having attested the original will along with other witnesses - Both the uncle who was then the only reversioner under the law and the mother conducted themselves on the footing that the said will was competently made and by virtue of that will the mother had become the absolute owner of the properties left by deceased - Similarly, the three daughters, and the appellants themselves accepted the statements made by the mother in favour of her daughters and took possession of and enjoyed the lands in suit - Neither the said daughters nor the appellants until the present suit was filed ever raised any contention regarding the validity of the said will - Authority of the mother to settle the said properties treating herself as the absolute owner of those properties was never challenged by the appellants - Such a conduct was only consistent with the fact that it was understood amongst the members of the family that deceased was a major at the time of the execution of the will and the will was validly made - Thus, the statement of deceased admissible u/s. 32(5) coupled with the evidence of DW as also the evidence as to the conduct of the parties before the court showed there was ample evidence on which the trial Court and the HC could rightly found their conclusion that the will was made at the time when deceased was a major - Appeal dismissed.
