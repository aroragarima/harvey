Laliteshwar Prasad Singh and others v S. P. Srivastava (Dead) Through Lrs.

Supreme Court of India

15 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R. Banumathi, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 896; (2017) 2 SCC 415; 2017 (121) ALR 222; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 137; JT 2016 (12) SC 178; 2017 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 635; 2017 (1) MLJ 207; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 460; 2017 (135) RD 259; 2016(12) SCALE 901

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Specific Relief Act, 1963, Hindu Succession Act, 1956, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, ss. 15, 15(1)(b), 15(2) - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 34 - Suit Property - Declaration of Title Challenged - Appellant/defendant purchased suit property from respondent no.2/ defendant no.2 - Respondent no.1/plaintiff filed suit for declaration of title with respect to suit properties - Trial Court dismissed suit of plaintiff for want of failure to prove title to suit property - HC in appeal by plaintiff reversed order of Trial Court and decreed suit on ground that property devolved on 'X' agnate of plaintiff - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, it is well settled that the First Appellate Court shall state the points for determination, the decision thereon and the reasons for decision. However, it is equally well settled that mere omission to frame point/points for determination does not vitiate the judgment of the first appellate court provided that the first appellate court records its reasons based on evidence adduced by both the parties. An appellate court is the final court of facts. The judgment of the appellate court must therefore reflect court's application of mind and record its findings supported by reasons. When appellate court agrees with the views of the trial court on evidence, it need not restate effect of evidence or reiterate reasons given by trial court; expression of general agreement with reasons given by trial court would ordinarily suffice. When the first appellate court reverses the findings of the trial court, it must record the findings in clear terms explaining how the reasoning's of the trial court is erroneous. In instant case, the HC has neither framed the points for determination nor discussed the evidence adduced by the defendants. The HC seemed to have only considered two aspects (i)genealogical table produced by the plaintiff; (ii) documentary evidence adduced by the plaintiff. The HC ought to have given an opportunity to the appellants to adduce additional evidence and make their submission. Hence, the impugned judgment of the HC is set aside and the matter is remitted back to the HC for fresh consideration. Order accordingly.
