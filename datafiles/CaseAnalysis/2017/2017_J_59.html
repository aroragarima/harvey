Justice K. S. Puttaswamy (Retired) and another v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, Jagdish Singh Khehar, J. Chelameswar, S.A. Bobde, R.K. Agrawal, Rohinton Fali Nariman, Abhay Manohar Sapre, Sanjay Kishan Kaul, S. Abdul Nazeer

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 641; 2017 (5) ALL MR 686; 2017 (3) CLT(SC) 281; JT 2017 (9) SC 141; 2017 (6) MLJ 267

Subject: Constitution; Human Rights; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Arbitrariness, Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Right To Freedom, Right To Property, Freedom Of Movement, Indian Companies Act, 1913, Precedent, Companies Act, 1956, Contract, Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, Indian Post Office Act, 1898, Indian Telegraph Act, 1885, Limitation, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Medical Termination Of Pregnancy Act, 1971, Indian Stamp Act, 1899, Review, Freedom Of Speech, Personal Liberty, Oath, Narcotic Drugs And Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, Conservation Of Foreign Exchange And Prevention Of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, Fairness, Reasonable Restriction, Speech And Expression, State Action, Equal Protection, Procedure Established By Law, Torts, Morality, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, Punjab Police Rules, Punjab Excise Act, 1914, Punjab Security of Land Tenures Act, 1953, Passports Act, 1967, Reasonableness, Indian Easements Act, 1882, Right To Information Act, 2005, Religion, Human Rights Act, 1998, Deprivation, Constitutionality, Libel, Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, Maharashtra Control Of Organised Crime Act, 1999, Protection Of Human Rights Act, 1993, Information Technology Act, 2000, Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Police Regulations, Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007, U.P. Summary: Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, J., R.K. Agrawal, J. and S. Abdul Nazeer, J.

(A) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950 - Privacy - Constitutional protection - Petitioners filed instant writ petition challenging for constitutional interpretation and if privacy was to be construed as a protected constitutional value, it would redefine in significant ways concepts of liberty and entitlements that flow out of its protection - Whether privacy was a constitutionally protected value.

Held, observation that privacy was not a right guaranteed by Constitution was not reflective of the correct position. Decisions rendered by SC subsequent to Kharak Singh v. State of U.P. & Others , 1962 Indlaw SC 577, upholding the right to privacy would be read subject to the above principles. Informational privacy was a facet of the right to privacy. The dangers to privacy in an age of information could originate not only from state but from non-state actors as well. SC commend to UOI the need to examine and put into place a robust regime for data protection. The creation of such a regime requires careful and sensitive balance between individual interests and legitimate concerns of State. The legitimate aims of State would include for instance protecting national security, preventing and investigating crime, encouraging innovation and spread of knowledge, and preventing the dissipation of social welfare benefits. Those were matters of policy to be considered by UOI while designing a carefully structured regime for the protection of data. Since, UOI has informed SC that it has constituted a Committee chaired by former Judge of SC for that purpose, the matter should be dealt with appropriately by UOI having due regard to what was set out in judgment. Reference answered.

J. Chelameswar, J.

(B) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Right of privacy - Limitation - Whether SUBJECTS who are amenable to the laws of country have a Fundamental Right of Privacy against State.

Held, no legal right could be absolute and every right has limitations. Such aspect of the matter was conceded at the bar. Therefore, even a fundamental right to privacy has limitations. The limitations are to be identified on case to case basis depending upon the nature of the privacy interest claimed. There are different standards of review to test infractions of fundamental rights. While the concept of reasonableness overarches Part III of Constitution, it operates differently across Articles (even if only slightly differently across some of them). If a privacy claim specifically flows only from one of the expressly enumerated provisions u/art. 19 of Constitution then the standard of review would be as expressly provided u/art. 19 of Constitution. However, the possibility of a privacy claim being entirely traceable to rights other than art. 21 of Constitution was bleak. Thus, it is critical that such standard be adopted with some clarity as to when and in what types of privacy claims it is to be used. Only in privacy claims which deserve the strictest scrutiny is the standard of compelling State interest to be used. As for others, the just, fair and reasonable standard u/art. 21 of Constitution would apply. When the compelling State interest standard was to be employed should depend upon the context of concrete cases. However, such discussion sets the ground rules within which a limitation for the right of privacy is to be found. Reference answered.

S. A. Bobde, J.

(C) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Fundamental rights - Applicability - What was at stake is the amplitude of fundamental rights including that precious and inalienable right u/art. 21 of Constitution.

Held, privacy violations would usually have to answer to tests in addition to the one applicable to art. 21 of Constitution. An inalienable constitutional right to privacy inheres in Part III of Constitution. The right to privacy is inextricably bound up with all exercises of human liberty both as it is specifically enumerated across Part III, and as it is guaranteed in the residue u/art. 21 of Constitution. It is distributed across the various articles in Part III and mutatis mutandis, takes the form of whichever of their enjoyment its violation curtails. Any interference with privacy by an entity covered by art. 12 of Constitution description of 'state' should satisfy the tests applicable to whichever one or more of Part III freedoms the interference affects. Reference answered.

R.F. Nariman, J.

(D) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Human rights - Inalienable - Whether does a fundamental right to privacy reside primarily in art. 21 of Constitution read with certain other fundamental rights.

Held, art. 21 of Constitution would not be the sole repository of those human rights but only reflect the fact that they were âinalienableâ; that they inhere in every human being by virtue of the person being a human being. Secondly, international covenants expressly state that those rights are 'inalienable' as they inhere in persons because they are human beings. Right of privacy is an inalienable human right which inheres in every person by virtue of the fact that he or she is a human being. Inalienable fundamental right to privacy resides in art. 21 of Constitution and other fundamental freedoms contained in Part III of Constitution to the extent that they indicate to the contrary stand overruled. Reference answered.

Abhay Manohar Sapre, J.

(E) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Privacy right - Infringement - Whether "right to privacy" was a fundamental right under Part III of Constitution.

Held, âright to privacyâ is a part of fundamental right of a citizen guaranteed under Part III of Constitution. However, it is not an absolute right but was subject to certain reasonable restrictions, which State was entitled to impose on the basis of social, moral and compelling public interest in accordance with law. Further, the âright to privacyâ has multiple facets and the same has to go through a process of case-to-case development as and when any citizen raises his grievance complaining of infringement of his alleged right in accordance with law. Reference answered.

Sanjay Kishan Kaul, J.

(F) Constitution - Human Rights - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 21 - Data protection - State interests - Justifiability - Whether there was any justification for making all truthful information available to public affecting their right to privacy.

Held, formulation of data protection was a complex exercise which needs to be undertaken by State after a careful balancing of privacy concerns and legitimate State interests, including public benefit arising from scientific and historical research based on data collected and processed. The right of privacy was a fundamental right. It was a right which protects the inner sphere of individual from interference from both State and non-State actors and allows the individuals to make autonomous life choices. However, technology has made it possible to enter a citizen's house without knocking at his/her door and that was equally possible both by State and non-State actors. Let the right of privacy, an inherent right, be unequivocally fundamental right embedded in part-III of Constitution however, subject to the restrictions specified, relatable to that part. Reference answered.

Ratio - Right to privacy is a part of fundamental right of a citizen guaranteed under Part III of Constitution.
