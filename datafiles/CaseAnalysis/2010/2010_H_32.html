Man Kaur (Dead) By Lrs. v Hartar Singh Sangha

Supreme Court of India

05 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 824; (2010) 10 SCC 512; 2010 (6) AWC 6025; 2010 (6) CTC 652; JT 2010 (10) SC 565; 2011 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2108; 2010 (7) MLJ(SC) 1059; 2011 (2) MPLJ 1; 2011 (1) RCR(Civil) 189; 2010(10) SCALE 523; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 515; 2010 (3) UC 2069

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Contract & Commercial - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 16(c) - Suit for specific performance of an agreement of sale - Agreement of sale dt. 20.10.1978 entered between plaintiff (Non-Resident Indian) represented by his attorney holder and defendant represented by her husband and attorney holder - Plaintiff filed a suit for specific performance of the said agreement of sale against the defendant submitting that he was always ready and willing to perform his part of the contract and get the sale deed registered by paying the balance consideration and that in spite of a notice dt. 5.3.1980 calling upon the defendant to complete the sale, the defendant had failed to execute the sale deed - Plaintiff further submitted that in the alternative, if he was found not entitled to specific performance, then for a decree of recovery of Rs.21,500/- (that is Rs.11500/- paid to defendant's attorney holder and Rs.10000/- as liquidated damages) with costs - Defendant resisted said suit contending that defendant's husband, in spite of the expiry of the last date fixed for sale, went to Chandigarh and met the plaintiff and plaintiff informed him that he could not arrange the entire funds for making full payment and therefore could not proceed with the sale - Defendant also contended that as plaintiff was not ready and willing to perform the contract by paying the balance of the sale price and get the sale completed, he was not entitled to specific performance and that in view of the breach committed by the plaintiff, the earnest money amount paid by him stood forfeited - Trial Court decreed said suit - HC dismissed appeal filed against said order - Hence, present appeal - (A) Compliance of s. 16(c) of Specific Relief Act, 1963 - Held, to succeed in a suit for specific performance, the plaintiff has to prove (i) that a valid agreement of sale was entered by the defendant in his favour (ii) that the defendant committed breach of the contract and (iii) that he was always ready and willing to perform his part of the obligations in terms of the contract - In present case, plaintiff neither signed the agreement of sale nor signed the plaint nor gave evidence, in particular, about his readiness and willingness - The plaint was signed by plaintiff's attorney holder (PW1) in whose favour plaintiff had executed the power of attorney and who had no personal knowledge of the transaction - Therefore, it has to be held that though there were necessary averments in the plaint about the readiness and willingness of the plaintiff, and though PW1 and PW2 gave evidence about his readiness and willingness, the suit has to fail for failure to comply with s. 16(c) of the Specific Relief Act, as there was no acceptable or valid evidence of such readiness and willingness of plaintiff to perform his part of the obligations in terms of the contract - (B) Payment of damages - Held, the agreement does not specifically provide for specific performance - Nor does it bar specific performance - It provides for payment of damages in the event of breach by either party - The provision for damages in the agreement is not intended to provide the vendor an option of paying money in lieu of specific performance - Therefore, plaintiff will be entitled to seek specific performance (even in the absence of a specific provision therefor) subject to his proving breach by the defendant and that he was ready and willing to perform his obligation under the contract, in terms of the contract - (C) Forfeiture of earnest money - Held, in present case, the evidence clearly showed that defendant's attorney holder had entrusted the work of securing the clearances to the property dealer, who was acting on behalf of plaintiff - This was within the knowledge of the attorney holder of plaintiff at the relevant point of time - Moreover, PW 2 also admitted in his evidence that he was to get the NOC and ULC clearance but he sent a telegram to defendant's attorney holder at the instance of plaintiff, asking him to come to Chandigarh on 7.6.1979 and execute the sale deed - Material on record shows that the plaintiff committed breach - Therefore, the earnest money stood forfeited and plaintiff is not entitled for refund of the earnest money - Appeals allowed
