Mohammed Abdulla Khan v Prakash K.

Supreme Court of India

04 December 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, S. Abdul Nazeer

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 937; 2017(14) SCALE 23

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Presumption, Vicarious Liability, Defamation, Books, Reason To Believe, Believe, Press and Registration of Books Act, 1867, Imputation, Distinct Offence, Distinct, Lord Campbell's Act, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Libel Act, 1843

Summary: Criminal â Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 499,500,501,502 â Quashing of proceedings â Vicarious liability of owner of newspaper â Alleging that news item containing allegations against appellant published in newspaper owned by respondent were highly defamatory in nature - Appellant lodged report with PS against respondent and another person who was Editor â No action was taken by police and later, appellant filed private complaint with court and court took cognizance of matter for offences punishable u/ss. 500,501 and 502 of IPC â Aggrieved, respondent filed Revision before Trial Court but the same was dismissed - Respondent filed petition before HC invoking s.482 of CrPC and by order dt.23-11-2016, HC allowed said petition and quashed proceedings insofar as they pertaining to respondent â Hence, instant appeal â Whether proceedings against respondent are liable to be quashed.

Held, whether the content of appellantâs complaint constitutes an offence punishable under any one or all or some of the sections of ss. 500,501,502 of IPC was not examined by HC for quashing the complaint against respondent. Further, whether there is sufficient evidence to establish the guilt of the respondent for any one of the said offences is a matter that could be examined only after recording evidence at the time of trial but that could never be a subject matter of a proceeding u/s. 482 of CrPC. Further, HC concluded that prosecution of respondent would lead to miscarriage of justice without any discussion and without disclosing ay principle which forms the basis of the said conclusion. Further, extent of applicability of the principle of vicarious liability in criminal law particularly in the context of the offences relating to defamation are neither discussed by HC in the impugned judgment nor argued before SC as respondent had not appeared either in person or through counsel. As such, issue is not examined in detail but the issue requires a serious examination in an appropriate case because the owner of a newspaper employs people to print, publish and sell the newspaper to a make a financial gain out of the said activity. Further, where defamatory matter is printed and sold or offered for sale, whether the owner thereof could be heard to say that he could not be made vicariously liable for the defamatory material carried by his newspaper etc. requires a critical examination. Further, each case requires a careful scrutiny of various questions but neither prosecutions nor power u/s.482 of CrPC could be either conducted or exercised casually as done in instant case. Hence, judgment of HC is not sustainable and so, same is set aside. Trial Court to proceed with the case in accordance with law. Appeal allowed.
