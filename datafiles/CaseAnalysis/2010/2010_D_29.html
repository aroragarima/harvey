R.S.R.T.C. and others v Deen Dayal Sharma

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, R.V. Raveendran

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 338; (2010) 6 SCC 697; AIR 2010 SC 2662; 2010(4) ALL MR 426; 2010 (3) ESC 309; 2010 (126) FLR 407; 2010 (3) LLJ 15; 2010 (4) MPLJ 274; 2010(5) SCALE 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Service

Summary: Constitution - Service - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Road Transport Corporations Act, 1950 - Rajasthan State Road Transport Corporation Employees Service Regulations, 1965 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Rajasthan State Road Transport Workers and Workshop Employees Standing Orders, 1965 - Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1946 - Jurisdiction of Civil court in matter of industrial dispute - Respondent filed a civil suit against the appellants praying therein that the order of dismissal passed against him be declared unlawful, illegal, void and ineffective being contrary to the Standing Orders as no departmental enquiry was held - Appellants challenged the jurisdiction of civil court and submitted that the dispute being an industrial dispute, it can only be resolved by the Industrial Tribunal - Trial Judge overruled the objection raised by the appellants and ordered reinstatement of respondent and other financial benefits to him - Appeal and second appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - Hence, present appeal - Held, relied on Bal Mukund Bairwa, 2009 INDLAW SC 2092, wherein it was held that if the infringement of the Standing Orders is alleged, the civil court's jurisdiction may be held to be barred but if the suit is based on the violation of principles of common law or constitutional provisions or on other grounds, the civil court's jurisdiction may not be held to be barred - Nature of right sought to be enforced is decisive in determining whether the jurisdiction of civil court is excluded or not - In the present case, the respondent asserted his right that the departmental enquiry as contemplated under the Standing Orders, ought to have been held before issuing the order of dismissal and in absence thereof such order was liable to be quashed - Such right, if available, could have been enforced by the respondent only by raising an industrial dispute and not in the civil suit - It has to be held that civil court had no jurisdiction to entertain and try the suit filed by the respondent - Appeal allowed.
