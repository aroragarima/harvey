Shradha Devi v Krishna Chandra Pant And Others

Supreme Court of India

26 October 1982

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, A.P. Sen

Where Reported

1982 Indlaw SC 209; (1982) 3 SCC 389B; AIR 1982 SC 1569; 1983 CRLR 534B; 1982(2) SCALE 964; [1983] 1 S.C.R. 681; 1982 UJ 920

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Council Of States, Relief of scrutiny

Summary: Election - Practice & Procedure - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss.56 and 81 - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, r.76 - Election - Declared unsuccessful - Raised objection against counting of ballot papers - Sustainability - Unsuccessful candidate for election to council of States (Rajya Sabha) at the election held on 28-3-1979, was the appellant - After the poll was closed according to the time prescribed by the Election Commission u/s. 56 of Act, Returning Officer/PW4 commenced counting of votes - As the election was to be in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote, PW4 as required by r.76 of Rules proceeded to ascertain the quota - In all 421 members exercised the franchise - Eleven ballot papers were rejected by PW4 as invalid - Accordingly the quota was worked out at the value of 3417 - Respondents 2 to 11 were declared elected as each of them secured the value of ballot papers greater than the quota in the course of counting - As the counting proceeded further, the contest was between the election petitioner (appellant) and respondent No.1 and respondent No.1 was declared elected in the 14th count - Once all the 11 vacancies were filled in, counting was closed - Petitioner filed election petition u/s. 81 of Act in HC - Petition was for scrutiny and recount on the allegation of miscount and directed against respondent No.1 because he was declared elected to the last vacancy - Single Judge rejected the petition substantially holding that the petitioner had failed to prove that all eleven rejected ballot papers were not shown to the counting agents - It was held that petitioner failed to prove such error in counting which would enable her to seek relief of scrutiny and recount - In reaching conclusion, Single Judge of HC had completely misdirected himself as to the nature of proof required for a relief of scrutiny and recount on the allegation of miscount - Single Judge first took up the allegations of errors in counting, more particularly directed to the allegation of improper rejection of valid votes which would materially affect the result as set out in petition, and then through the help of the Joint Registrar excluded the nine ballot papers without giving inspection and only took into consideration two ballot papers which answered the error as complained of and then proceeded to hold that even if these two ballot papers rejected as invalid were taken into account and the value of the votes computed, the result would not be materially affected and, therefore, rejected the election petition - Whether Single Judge of HC rightly rejected petition for scrutiny and recount of ballet papers - Held, Singe Judge of HC did not examine the other 9 ballot papers on the erroneous view that only two were correlated to the averments in the plaint - There was specific averment in petition that the marks were not such as to lead to identity of the elector and that the ballot papers could not be rejected as invalid under rule 73 (2) (d) of Rules - That allegation was wholly substantiated by a casual look at the remaining nine ballot papers - Error was apparent - Once the error was established the scrutiny and recount had to be ordered as a prima facie case of miscount was made out and, therefore, the decision of the Single Judge of HC was liable to be set aside - At one stage SC was inclined to examine the validity of each ballot paper - But as the HC did not undertake that exercise it would not be proper for SC to undertake the same for the first time - Position of law was made very clear, namely, that once an error was established it was not necessary that the pleadings should show error in respect of each individual invalid ballot paper, and prima facie proof of error resulting in miscount having been established, a scrutiny and recount had to be ordered - And the scrutiny of invalid ballot papers should precede the recount - It was further made clear that where voting was in accordance with the proportional representation by the single transferable vote a ballot paper could be valid in part - And it should be remembered that every mark or writing would not result in invalidation of the vote - Mark or identification should be such as to unerringly reveal the identity of the voter and the evidence of prior arrangement connecting the mark should be made available - There was no such evidence - Therefore, the ballot papers could not have been rejected on the ground mentioned in r. 73 (2) (d) of Rules, such marks being in instant case some erasures or a bracket - Appeal allowed.
