M. S. Sandhu and another Etc. v State of Punjab and others Etc.

Supreme Court of India

07 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, Surinder Singh Nijjar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 347; (2014) 6 SCC 514; AIR 2014 SC 2394; JT 2014 (8) SC 132; 2014(7) SCALE 12

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Punjab Police Service Rules, 1959, Punjab Civil Services General Conditions of Service Rules, 1994, Punjab Judicial Service Rules, 1963

Summary: Service - Punjab Police Service Rules, 1959, rr.3,4,6,8 and10 - Constitution of India, 1950, art.141 - Dispute between promotes and direct recruits - For claiming Seniority - Dispute before SC was related to seniority in the cadre of Deputy Superintendent of Police (DSP) between the direct recruits and promotees - HC, vide impugned judgment had allowed the Civil Writ Petition and quashed the seniority list of State Govt. thereby directing the State Govt. to prepare the seniority list in accordance with the judgment of SC in one case - Paramjit Singh Sandhu And Ors. Etc. v Ram Rakha And Ors. Etc. 1979 Indlaw SC 250, relied on - Whether judgment of SC in was rightly followed by the HC - Paramjit Singh Sandhu And Ors. Etc. v Ram Rakha And Ors. Etc. 1979 Indlaw SC 250, relied on -

Held, case of SC was concerned with same 1959 Rules pertaining to the officers of the very cadre, namely DSP Cadre - It was interpreting the rule in the context which had arisen before it - Court was of the opinion that to save r.10 of Rules from the vice of the arbitrariness and to avert the situation of striking down the same, it would be appropriate to interpret the said r.10 of Rules to mean that rule of quota should apply not only at the time of appointment but at the time of confirmation also and confirmation was to be done on the basis of vacancies - Appellants' argument was that the principle was totally discarded by the subsequent Constitution Bench judgment wherein it was held that the rule of quota applicable at the stage of appointment/recruitment would have no applicability at the time of confirmation - B. S. Yadav And Others Etc. v State Of Haryana And Others Etc. 1980 Indlaw SC 220, relied on - Thus, on the one hand, SC had the judgment in the case of Paramjit Singh which pertained to the very Rules and the ratio of the judgment was applied by HC and on the other hand, SC had the law laid down by the Constitution Bench in B.S.Yadav wherein the provisions of some other rules came up for interpretation - According to SC, approach of HC to follow the dicta in Paramjit Singh was most appropriate which pertained to the same Service and same Rules - That was the mandate of art. 141 of the Constitution - HC could depart only in a situation where it found that the said judgment was subsequently overruled, specifically or impliedly or it is per- incurium - Service rules, in a particular case might specifically provide the co-existence of quota and rota - There might also be a situation where service rules should be interpreted as such - That was a very important comment made by the Constitution Bench after taking note of the ratio in Paramjit Singh's case - It was specifically noted how the Court on harmonious reading of rr.3,4,6,8 and 10 of Rules had come to the conclusion that quota rule was operative both at the time of initial appointment and at the time of confirmation - After taking note of the ratio on the harmonious interpretation of the Rules in question, rather than stating that such an interpretation was impermissible or wrongly given, the Constitution Bench clarified that there might be circumstances where such an interpretation would be permissible and validity of the Rules would be tested in the total setting of facts - That was precisely done by the Bench in Paramjit Singh's case - Only conclusion which could be drawn from the reading of judgment was that the harmonious reading of Rules done in that case was in fact approved, and by no stretch of reasoning, can it be inferred that it was overruled - It needed to be highlighted at instant stage that having regard to the overall circumstances and the factual position which prevailed while deciding Paramjit Singh's case, the Court held that in order to save Rule 10 from the vice of arbitrariness, the only interpretation which could be given was to hold that the quota rule would apply both at the time of recruitment and at the time of confirmation - Detailed reasons were given justifying the said line of action taken by the Court and that portion of the judgment had already been extracted - In the beginning, not only that, even when the Review Petition was filed the Court made it clear that there was no ambiguity in the judgment - It was also made clear that what the Court meant was that quota should be co-related to the vacancies which were to be filled in - Who retired and from what source he was recruited might not be very relevant because retirement from service might not follow the quota rule - Roster had to be introduced which was to continue while giving confirmation - Introduction of roster only postulated ascertainment of available number of vacancies and proceeding to make recruitment keeping in view of the quota - It would be appropriate to point out that the argument of the appellants that the operation of the rotational principle in quota would lead to inequitable results was refuted by the respondents who submitted that by applying the principle as directed by SC, the State Govt. had drawn out a seniority, the perusal of which showed that against the existing strength of the service of 450 there were 406 promotee officers as against 360 vacancies following to their share @ 80% and 44 direct recruits as against 90 vacancies to their share @ 20% - Further appointment to the promotion to the IPS cadre was made solely on the basis of merit and the ratio of officers which made it to the IPS from the State Service could not be taken as an indicator of any discrimination resulting by virtue of Rules - SC find some substance in the argument - Somewhat similar argument, as taken by the appellants before SC, was commented upon by SC in one case - Maharashtra Vikrikar Karamchari Sangathan v. State of Maharashtra 2000 Indlaw SC 525, relied on - Operation of the Rules might result in harsh consequences as far as appellants were concerned - But on the vagaries of such outcomes, the Court could not keep on interpreting a rule differently - It was more especially when the promotees being in excess of their quota have enjoyed the fortuitous appointment beyond their quota of vacancies - Likewise, appellant's argument that the quota rule had broken down would not cut much ice - First of all, such an argument was not even raised/pleaded, nor any material was placed on record which showed that the adherence to the quota rule as possible leading to break down of the quota - Private respondents had made fervent plea that as a matter of record whenever State Govt. had endeavoured to make direct recruitments, vacancies had been duly filled with adequate number of candidates - Therefore, for want of any material no definite findings could be recorded on that aspect, more so, such a case was not pleaded before the HC - May be, because of such situation recurring time and again either in favour of the promotees or in favour of the direct recruits, the Govt. had remedied the situation by amending the rules thereby bringing the rule of continuous length of service for determining the seniority - It was always open to the Govt. to take such steps for the benefit of all in the service and to ensure that the result was equitable - However, in the instant case, SC did not find fault with the judgment of the HC - Appeals dismissed.
