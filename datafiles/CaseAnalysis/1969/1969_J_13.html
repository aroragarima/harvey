Jain Bros. & Others v The Union Of India & Others

Supreme Court of India

18 November 1969

Case Analysis

Bench

A.N. Grover, C.A. Vaidyialingam, A.N. Ray, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1969 Indlaw SC 230; (1969) 3 SCC 311; AIR 1970 SC 778; [1970] 77 ITR 107; [1970] 3 S.C.R. 253

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Registered Firm, Unregistered Firm, Payment of Bonus Act, 1965, Finance Act, 1956, Finance Act, 1958

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Income Tax Act, 1922, s. 23(5) - Income Tax Act, 1961 - A/y 1960-61 - Double Taxation - A notice u/s. 22(2) of Act, 1922 was served on the appellant-a registered firm, calling upon it to submit a return of the income for the a/y 1960-61 - A return was filed but not within time - Income tax Officer (ITO) completed the assessment on 23.11.1964, computing the total income of the firm at Rs. 4,75,368/- - In view of the amendment made by the Finance Act of 1956 in. s. 23(5) of the 1922 Act the tax payable by the firm as also the amount to be included in the income of each partner was determined - ITO also passed an order under cl. (a) of s. 271 (i) of 1961 Act imposing a penalty for non-compliance with the notice u/s. 22(2) of 1922 Act - Appellant challenged said order before Appellate Asst. Commissioner - Although those proceedings were still pending, another writ petition was filed challenging the validity and the constitutionality of s. 23(5) of the 1922 Act and ss. 297(2)(g) and 271(2) of the Act of 1961 - HC dismissed said writ petition - Appellant filed appeal against said order contending that s. 23(5) of the 1922 Act was invalid for the reason that the same income in the hands of both the firm and the partners could not be simultaneously subjected to tax - Whether submissions of the appellant could be accepted and impugned order of the HC could be set aside? - Held, after the amendment of 1956, the appellant-firm did not cease to be an assessee on the contrary it was recognised as a separate entity and was subjected to tax as such - There can be double taxation if the legislature has distinctly enacted it - It is only when there are general words of taxation and they have to be interpreted they cannot be so interpreted as to tax the subject twice over to the same tax - The Constitution does not contain any prohibition against double taxation even if it be assumed that such a taxation is involved in the case of a firm and its partners after the amendment of s. 23(5) of 1922 Act by the Act of 1956 nor is there any other enactment which interdicts such taxation - Even if s. 23(5) of 1922 Act provides for the machinery for collection and recovery of tax, once the legislature has in clear terms, indicated that the income of the firm can be taxed as also the income in the hands of the partners, the distinction between a charging and a machinery section is of no consequence - Appeal dismissed.
