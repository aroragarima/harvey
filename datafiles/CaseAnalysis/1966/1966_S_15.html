Sitaram Motilal Kalal v Santanuprasad Jaishankar Bhatt

Supreme Court of India

08 February 1966

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Mohammad Hidayatullah, R.S. Bachawat

Where Reported

1966 Indlaw SC 367; 1966 ACJ 89; AIR 1966 SC 1697; 1966 (68) BomLR 489; 1966 MahLJ 881; 1966 MPLJ 913; [1966] 3 S.C.R. 527

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation

Keywords: Quantum Of Damages, Principal And Agent

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Liability of owner of vehicles - Respondent sued three persons for damages for personal injuries which led to amputation of one of his legs in a motorcar accident - Vehicle belonged to appellant who had entrusted it to second respondent for being plied as a taxi - At time of accident, it was driven by third respondent to whom it had been handed over by second respondent for purpose of taking a driving test to obtain a driver's licence - Trial Judge decreed a part of claim against second and third respondents, but held that owner of vehicle was not liable - On appeal to High Court, owner of vehicle was also held responsible and decree of court below was also enhanced - Present appeal - Owner of vehicle had handed it over to second respondent to ply it on hire as a taxi - For master's liability to arise, act must be a wrongful act authorised by master or a wrongful and unauthorised mode of doing some act authorised by master - Driver of a car taking car on master's business makes him vicariously liable if he commits an accident, But it is equally well-settled that if servant, at time of accident, is not acting within course of his employment but is doing something for himself master is not liable - Vehicle was proved to be driven by an unauthorised person and on his own business de facto driver was not driver or agent of owner but one who had obtained car for his own business not even from master but from a servant of master - Held, prima facie, owner would not be liable in such circumstances - There is no proof that second respondent was authorised to coach cleaner so that cleaner might become a driver and drive taxi - HC differed from Trial Judge by relying upon inadmissible evidence - Once inadmissible evidence is rightly excluded, it is quite clear that this was an act done not on owner's business but either on business of third respondent or that of third and second respondents together - It has not been proved to have been even impliedly authorised by owner or to come within any of extensions of doctrine of scope of employment - Appeal allowed.
