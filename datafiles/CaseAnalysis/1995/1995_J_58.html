C. Ravichandran Iyer v Justice A.M. Bhattacharjee and Others

Supreme Court of India

05 September 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, B.L. Hansaria

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 711; (1995) 5 SCC 457; (1995) SCC (Cr) 953; AIR 1995 SCW 3768; JT 1995 (6) SC 339; 1995 (2) LLN 615; 1995(5) SCALE 142B; 1995 (7) SLR 161; [1995] Supp3 S.C.R. 319; 1995 (3) UPLBEC 1723

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges

Keywords: Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Advocates Act, 1961 - Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 - Whether a Bar Council or Bar Association is entitled to pass resolution demanding a Judge to resign, what is its effect on independence of judiciary and whether it is constitutionally permissible? - Held, where complaint relates to Judge of High Court, Chief Justice of that HC, after verification, and if necessary, after confidential enquiry from his independent source, should satisfy himself about truth of imputation made by Bar Association through its office bearers against Judge and consult Chief Justice of India, where deemed necessary, by placing all information with him - When Chief Justice of India is seized of matter, to avoid embarrassment to him and to allow fairness in procedure to be adopted in furtherance thereof, Bar should suspend all further actions to enable Chief Justice of India to appropriately deal with matter - This is necessary because any action he may take must not only be just but must also appear to be just to all concerned, i.e., it must not even appear to have been taken under pressure from any quarter - Chief Justice of India, on receipt of information from Chief Justice of HC, after being satisfied about correctness and truth touching conduct of Judge, may tender such advice either directly or may initiate such action, as is deemed necessary or warranted under given facts and circumstances - If circumstances permit, it may be salutary to take Judge into confidence before initiating action - On decision being taken by Chief Justice of India, matter should rest at that - This procedure would not only facilitate nibbing in bud conduct of a Judge leading to loss of public confidence in courts and sustain public faith in efficacy of rule of law and respect for judiciary, but would also avoid needless embarrassment of contempt proceedings against office bearers of Bar Association and group libel against all concerned - Independence of judiciary and stream of public justice would remain pure and unsullied - Bar Association could remain a useful arm of judiciary and in case of sagging reputation of particular Judge, Bar Association could take up matter with Chief Justice of HC and await his response for action taken there under for a reasonable period - In case allegations are against Chief Justice of a HC, Bar should bring them directly to notice of Chief Justice of India - On receipt of such complaint, Chief Justice of India would in same way act as stated above qua complaint against a Judge of HC, and Bar would await for a reasonable period response of Chief Justice of India - It would thus be seen that yawning gap between proved misbehavior and bad conduct in consistent with high office on part of a non cooperating Judge/Chief Justice of a HC could be disciplined by self-regulation through inhouse procedure - This inhouse procedure would fill in constitutional gap and would yield salutary effect - Recourse to this procedure was not taken in case at hand may be because of absence of legal sanction to such a procedure - Petition disposed of.
