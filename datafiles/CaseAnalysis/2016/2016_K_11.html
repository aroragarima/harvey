Commissioner of Commercial Taxes, Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala v K. T. C. Automobiles

Supreme Court of India

29 January 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Shiva Kirti Singh, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 80; (2016) 4 SCC 82; 2016 (1) ACC 720; AIR 2016 SC 805; JT 2016 (1) SC 499; 2016 (1) KLT 710; 2016 (1) MLJ 687; 2016 (2) RCR(Civil) 204; 2016(1) SCALE 702; [2016] 1 S.C.R. 994; [2016] 88 VST 257

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Sale of Goods Act, 1930, Central Motor Vehicles Rules, 1989, Requirement, Relevant Provisions, Pondicherry Sales Tax Act

Summary: Sales Tax - Kerala General Sales Tax Act, 1963 - Motor Vehicles Act, 1988 - Central Motor Vehicles Rules, 1989 - Sale and registration of motor vehicles - IB issued show cause notice proposing to levy penalty u/s.45A of 1963 Act on ground that respondent had wrongly shown 263 number of cars as sold from its Mahe Branch, wrongly arranged for registration under 1988 Act at Mahe and wrongly collected and remitted tax for those transactions under the provisions of Pondicherry Sales Tax Act - Further, by showing sales at Mahe, even though sales were concluded at Kozhikode, Kerala, respondent failed to maintain true and complete accounts as an assessee under 1963 Act and had evaded payment of tax to tune of Rs.86 lakh and odd during relevant period - Later, IB passed order imposing penalty of Rs.86 lakh - Aggrieved, respondent filed appeal before Deputy Commissioner and the same was allowed - Later, Commissioner initiated suo-motu proceeding u/s. 37 of 1963 Act and passed final order setting aside appellate order and restoring original order of penalty passed by IB - Aggrieved by suo-motu order, respondent filed appeal before HC and by impugned order, HC allowed the same - Hence, instant appeal - Whether vehicles in question had been sold at Kozhikode in Kerala or at Mahe.

Held, motor vehicles in question could come into the category of ascertained goods and could get appropriate to the contract of sale at the registration office at Mahe where admittedly all were registered in accordance with 1988 Act and Rules. Further, under 1988 Act and Rules, the seller or manufacturer is bound to transport the motor vehicles to the office of registering authority and only when it reaches there safe and sound, in accordance with the statutory provisions governing motor vehicles it could be said to be in deliverable state and only then the property in such a motor vehicle could pass to the buyer once he has been given notice that the motor vehicle is fit and ready for his lawful possession and registration. Hence, allegations and facts noted by IB does not lead to conclusive inference that sales under controversy had taken place at Kozhikode, Kerala, though created some doubts. Hence, judgment of HC is affirmed. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Motor vehicle remains in the category of unascertained or future goods till its appropriation to the contract of sale by the seller is occasioned by handing over its possession at or near the office of registration authority in a deliverable and registrable state.
