Â Rabia Bai v Custodian-General of Evacueeproperty

Supreme Court of India

12 January 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 226; AIR 1961 SC 1002; 1962 (1) SCJ 430; [1961] 3 S.C.R. 448

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Appellant, Rabia Bai, who is a citizen of India came to know in 1949 that certain premises in Madras, was put up for sale - Said premises belonged to one Mohamad Gani Jan Mohamad who had left for Pakistan in 1947 and had settled there - Said Gani came to Madras in April, 1949, and arranged for sale, and as a result of negotiations between him and appellant's husband sale formalities were duly completed - Appellant applied for confirmation of this sale deed and her application has been rejected - On 11-01-1951, Asst. Custodian of Evacuee Property, Madras City, had declared property of vendor to be evacuee property since he was of opinion that vendor's case fell within four corners of definition of 'an evacuee' u/s. 2(d)(ii) of Act - Order refusing to confirm transaction was passed on 31-07-1951 - In revision, Custodian- General was of opinion that appellant's case fell u/s. 40(4)(a) of Act and dismissed matter - Whether transaction attracts provisions of s. 40(4)(a)? - Held, it is clear that if a transaction is affected by absence of good faith either in vendor or vendee its confirmation may properly be rejected u/s. 40(4)(a), in other words, good faith is required both in vendor and vendee - Fact that consideration was paid by appellant and that she was acting in good faith may perhaps be relevant in determining character of her conduct in regard to transaction, but it would not be relevant or material in determining character of conduct of vendor in relation to transfer - Therefore, if a vendor sold his property not for any necessity or for any other legitimate purpose but solely with object of converting it into cash and removing it to Pakistan, that clearly was intended to defeat provisions of Act which he knew would soon be extended to Madras, and so it would be difficult to hold that he was acting honestly within meaning of s. 40(4)(a) of Act - Appeal dismissed.
