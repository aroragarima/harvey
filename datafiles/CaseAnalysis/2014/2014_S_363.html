Godrej and Boyce Manufacturing Company Limited and another v State of Maharashtra and others

Supreme Court of India

30 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, R.M. Lodha, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 64; (2014) 3 SCC 430; AIR 2014 SC 1446; 2014 (103) ALR 724; 2015 (3) Bom.C.R. 138; JT 2014 (2) SC 326; 2014 (123) RD 595; 2014(1) SCALE 707; [2014] 2 S.C.R. 203

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Timber, Allocation, Consent Decree, Trees, Sales Tax Act, 1954, Maharashtra Private Forests (Acquisition) Act, 1975, Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950, Bihar Taxation on Passengers and Goods (Carried by Public Service Motor Vehicles) Act, 1961, Employees State Insurance Scheme, Salsette Estates (Land Revenue Exemption Abolition) Act, 1951, Maharashtra Private Forests (Acquisition) (Amendment) Act, 1978, Indian Forest (Maharashtra Unification and Amendment) Act, 1960, Government of India (Adaptation of Indian Laws) Order, 1937, Indian Forest (Bombay Amendment) Act, 1955, Indian Forest (Bombay Amendment) Act, 1948, Cultivation of waste land, Private land, Appropriation of land, Taking over of possession

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Salsette Estates (Land Revenue Exemption Abolition) Act, 1951, s. 4 - Indian Forest Act, 1927, ss. 35(1), 35(3) - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976, ss. 6, 20 - Maharashtra Private Forests (Acquisition) Act, 1975, ss. 2(f)(iii), 2(c-i), 5 - Cultivation of waste land - Allocation - Private land - Appropriation of land - Taking over of possession - Justifiability - Legality - Appellant (company) acquired land question in respondent State by a registered deed of conveyance dated 30-7-1948 from the successor in interest of 'FCB', who, in turn, had been given a perpetual lease/kowl for the land by the then Govt. of the State on 7-7-1835 - Under the 1951 Act, provided that waste lands granted under a perpetual lease/Kowl not appropriated or brought under cultivation before 14-8-1951 should vest in and be the property of the State - According to State (respondent), disputed land was not appropriated or brought under cultivation before 14-8-1951 and, thus, it vested in or was property of the State by virtue of s. 4 of 1951 Act - Aggrieved appellant filed a suit before HC praying, inter alia, for a declaration that it was the owner of disputed land in question and that provisions of 1951 Act had no application to disputed land and, that disputed land had been appropriated by appellant before 14-8-1951 for its industrial undertaking - HC passed consent decree that except certain land, all the lands were appropriated or brought under cultivation before 14-8-1951 and are the property of appellant - Thus, land was not a forest land and 1927 Act did not apply to waste land - Thereafter, appellant applied for and was granted permission, on various dates, by Municipal Corporation to construct residential buildings on the disputed land - Appellant constructed 4 such buildings on the basis of permissions granted from time to time and those building were occupied for residential purposes by its staff - Appellant was granted exemption u/s. 20 of 1976 Act to construct multi-storeyed buildings on disputed land - Thereafter, notice u/s. 35(3) of the 1927 Act (as amended) and published in Bombay Govt. Gazette of 6-9-1956 in respect of disputed land - However, an ex parte mutation of revenue records by State to show that the disputed land was 'affected' by the provisions of 1975 Act (Private Forest Act) and said notice under 1927 Act was not served on appellant - Aggrieved appellant filed petitions before HC - HC by impugned judgment dismissed all petitions holding that mere issuance of a notice u/s. 35(3) of 1927 Act without any notification being published in official gazette within the meaning of s. 35(1) of 1927 Act would not mean that the land was excluded from the purview of 1975 Act enacted by respondent State - Hence, instant appeals - Whether disputed land was at all a forest within the meaning of s. 2(c-i) of 1975 Act - Held, it was quite clear from a reading of Janu Chandra Waghmare and Others v. State of Maharashtra and Others, 1977 Indlaw MUM 269, that the 'means and includes' definition of forest in s. 2(c-i) of 1975 Act did not detract or take away from the primary meaning of the word 'forest' - SC was in agreement with that view - In instant case, admitted position, as per the consent decree dt.8-1-1962 was that the disputed land was not a waste land nor was it a forest - Disputed lands were built upon, from time to time, either for industrial purposes or for commercial purposes or for residential purposes - Under the circumstances, by no stretch of imagination could it be said that any of these disputed lands are 'forest' within the primary meaning of that word, or even within the extended meaning given in s. 2(c-i) of 1975 Act - A notice u/s. 35(3) of 1927 was intended to give an opportunity to the owner of a forest to show cause why, inter alia, a regulatory or a prohibitory measure be not made in respect of that forest - It was important to note that such a notice pre-supposes the existence of a forest - The owner of forest was expected to file objections within a reasonable time as specified in the notice and was also given an opportunity to lead evidence in support of the objections - After those basic requirements were met, the owner of the forest was entitled to a hearing on the objections - That entire procedure obviously could not be followed by the State and the owner of the forest unless the owner was served with the notice - Thus, service of a notice issued u/s. 35(3) of 1927 was inherent in the very language used in provision and the very purpose of the provision - Show cause notice was issued to appellant in 1957 (and assuming it was served) but no decision was taken thereon till 1975 that was for about 18 years - That was an unusually long period and undoubtedly much more than a reasonable time had elapsed for enabling the State to take a decision on the show cause notice - Thus, show cause notice should, for all intents and purposes be treated as having become a dead letter and the seed planted by State yielded nothing - Failure of State to take any decision on show cause notice for several decades (assuming it was served on appellant) was indicative of its desire to not act on it - That opinion was fortified by a series of events that have taken place between 1957 and 2006, beginning with consent decree of dt.8-1-1962 in appellant's suit, whereby the disputed land was recognized as not being forest land; permission to construct a large number of buildings (both residential and otherwise) as per the Development Plans of 1967 and then of 1991; exemptions granted by Competent Authority under 1976 Act leading to appellant making unhindered but permissible constructions; and finally, the absence of any attempt by the State to take possession of the 'forest land' u/s. 5 of 1975 Act for a couple of decades - Fact remained that possession of disputed land was not taken over or attempted to be taken over for decades and the issue was never raised when it should have been - To raise it now after a lapse of so many decades was unfair to appellant, the other appellants, the institutions, the State and residents of the tenements that have been constructed in the meanwhile - S. 2(f)(iii) of 1975 Act was not intended to apply to notices that had passed their shelf-life and that only 'pipeline notices' issued in reasonably close proximity to the coming into force of 1975 Act were 'live' and could be acted upon - It was the complete inaction of State, rather its active consent that has resulted in several citizens being placed in a precarious position where they were now told that their investment was actually in unauthorized constructions which were liable to be demolished any time even after several decades - There was no reason why these citizens should be the only victims of such a fate and State be held not responsible for that state of affairs; nor was there any reason why under such circumstances SC should not come to the aid of victims of culpable failure of the State to implement and enforce the law for several decades - Impugned judgment and order of HC was set aside in all of them and the notices impugned in petitions in HC were quashed - Appeals allowed.
