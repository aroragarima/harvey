Gandi Doddabasappa@gandhi Basavaraj v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

28 February 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Khanwilkar, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 182; (2017) 5 SCC 415; AIR 2017 SC 1208; 2017 ALL MR (Cri) 2196; 2017 CRLJ 2028; JT 2017 (3) SC 241; 2017 (4) KarLJ 545; 2017 (2) MLJ(Crl) 74; 2017 (2) RCR(Criminal) 200; 2017(3) SCALE 236

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Show Cause Notice, Weapon, Circumstantial Evidence, FIR, Marriage, Description, Proof, Enhancement, Challenge, Spot, Seal, Version, Blood, Chain, Doctor, Single Witness, Discrepancy, Clothes, Number Of Witnesses, Distance, Panchnama, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Seizure, Suspicion

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 313, 386, 397, 401 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 300, 302, 304 - Culpable homicide - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant/accused allegedly committed murder of deceased/daughter by cutting her neck with sickle - Trial Court acquitted accused on ground that mere intent on part of accused to commit the crime was not sufficient to record a finding of guilt - On appeal, HC allowed appeal and convicted appellant for an offence punishable u/s. 304, Part I IPC and sentenced him to undergo RI for 10 years - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the approach of Trial Court or HC with reference to evaluation of evidence of PW18 was correct.

Held, in instant case evidence of PW18 was corroborated by other circumstances and prosecution evidence. That leaves no manner of doubt that accused not only had strong motive to kill his daughter but was responsible for doing so and excludes the probability of someone else being responsible for death of deceased. However, view taken by HC about the efficacy of evidence of PW18, keeping in mind other proved circumstances and evidence of other prosecution witnesses, appears to be the only possible view and was correct approach in the matter. In view of the external injuries on the body of deceased, Trial Court opined that it was a case of homicidal death but gave benefit of doubt to appellant on the finding that there was no substantive evidence to indicate his complicity in commission of offence. In that sense, there was no challenge to the finding reached by Trial Court that the death of deceased was homicidal. Therefore, it was a case of culpable homicide amounting to murder. HC found that the fatal injuries suffered by deceased were established from the contents of postmortem report proved by doctor/PW 21. Further, PW 21 opined that injury found on the body of deceased could be attributed to the sickle recovered from the scene of offence. The use of sickle during the assault, fortifies the conclusion reached by Courts below about homicidal death and including that injuries resulted in instant death of deceased. Thus, finding of guilt recorded against accused by HC was unexceptionable and does not warrant any interference. Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 313, 386, 397, 401 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 300, 302, 304 - Death sentence - Exception - Validity - Whether the conviction recorded by HC u/s. 304 Part I IPC could be sustained.

Held, none of the exceptions in s. 300 IPC was attracted in instant case. It would necessarily follow that accused committed murder of his daughter who was in the advanced stage of pregnancy and for which he was liable to be punished with either imprisonment for life or death u/s. 302 IPC alone. SC do not find it a fit case to impose death penalty. Thus, appellant was convicted for offence punishable u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced to undergo imprisonment for life. Appeal dismissed.
