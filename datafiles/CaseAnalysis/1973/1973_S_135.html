Bhim Singh Rup Singh v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

28 November 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.V. Chandrachud, M. Hameedullah Beg

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 373; (1974) SCC (Cr) 238; (1974) 3 SCC 762; AIR 1974 SC 286; 1974 CRLJ 337; 1974 CRLR 47; 1974 MPLJ 283; 1974 UJ 115

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Conviction

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) allegedly murdered of his brother-in-law (deceased) - Trial Court acquitted the appellant - On appeal, HC by impugned judgment convicted appellant u/s. 302 of IPC and sentenced him to undergo life imprisonment - Hence, instant appeal - Whether HC Court was justified in interfering with order of acquittal.

Held, the view taken by the HC is not supported by any evidence on the record and indeed, as the very finding recorded by the HC shows, it was attempting to visualise 'what must have happened'. IO does not say in his evidence that he made any notes after interrogating PW5 at the scene of offence. It must therefore follow either that PW5's statement was recorded in the shop but that statement was suppressed or in the alternative that his statement was recorded for the first time a few hours later at the police station. Before he was taken to the police station he had met a host of relatives of the deceased and his own evidence shows that he had some familiarity with them. There were disputes between Narayan and the members of his family on the one hand and the appellant on the other. On the record of the case is a complaint (Ex.15) made by, the wife of deceased against the appellant and a complaint (Ex.20) lodged by the appellant against deceased at the police station. The HC has itself recorded a finding that the appellant bore a grudge against deceased. Trial Court had good reasons for discarding the evidence of the alleged eye-witnesses. The reasons given by the HC for taking a contrary view of that evidence are certainly not of the nature and character as would justify interference with the order of acquittal recorded by the Trial Court. Impugned conviction and sentence was set aside. Appeal allowed.
