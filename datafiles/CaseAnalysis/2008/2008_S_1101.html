State of Meghalaya and Others v Mecken Singh N. Marak

Supreme Court of India

09 May 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Panchal, Altamas Kabir

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2285; (2008) 7 SCC 580; AIR 2008 SC 2862; 2008 (118) FLR 372; JT 2008 (11) SC 119; 2008 (3) LLN 644; 2008(9) SCALE 326; [2008] 8 S.C.R. 89; 2008 (6) SLR 461; 2009 (1) UPLBEC 243

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Summary: Constitution - Service - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Police -Disobedience - Misconduct - Disciplinary Action - Jurisdiction of HC - Interference - Respondent-Sub inspector was removed from his services after departmental inquiry and proved charges of disobedience, misconduct, loss of service revolver and ammunitions - Single judge upheld order of competent authority removing respondent from services - On appeal DB set aside Single judge order and matter was remanded to appellate authority, namely, Inspector General of Police to consider and inflict appropriate punishment, short of removal from service, commensurate with gravity of proven misconduct - Hence, instant appeal - Whether competent authority was justified in removing respondent from service and whether DB was right in remitting matter to Appellate authority for passing appropriate order of punishment short of removal - Held, Court or Tribunal while dealing with quantum of punishment had to record reasons as to why it was felt that punishment was not commensurate with proved charges - Jurisdiction of HC, to interfere with quantum of punishment was limited and could not be exercised without sufficient reasons - HC, although had jurisdiction in appropriate case, to consider question in regard to quantum of punishment, but it had limited role to play - It was well settled that HCs, in exercise of powers u/ art.226, did not interfere with quantum of punishment unless there exist sufficient reasons therefore - In impugned order of HC no reasons had been indicated as to why punishment was considered disproportionate - Failure to give reasons amounts to denial of justice and mere statement that it was disproportionate would not suffice - When statute gives discretion to administrator to take decision, scope of judicial review would remain limited - Proved charges clearly established that respondent, who was police officer failed to discharge his duties with utmost integrity, honesty, devotion and diligence and his acts were prejudicial to exchequer and society - Further, even in cases where punishment imposed by disciplinary authority was found to be shocking to conscience of Court, normally disciplinary authority or appellate authority should be directed to reconsider question of imposition of penalty - HC in case, had not only interfered with punishment imposed by disciplinary authority in a routine manner but overstepped its jurisdiction by directing appellate authority to impose any other punishment short of removal - By fettering discretion of appellate authority to impose appropriate punishment for serious misconducts committed by respondent, HC totally misdirected itself while exercising jurisdiction u/art. 226 - Thus, conclusion of DB could not be regarded as proper at all - Appeal allowed.
