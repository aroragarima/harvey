Â State of Uttar Pradesh v Kaushaliya and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 October 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Raghubar Dayal, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 99; AIR 1964 SC 416; 1964 CRLJ 304; [1964] 4 S.C.R. 1002

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure; Socio-Economic; Women & Children

Keywords: Woman, Prostitution

Summary: Constitution of India, 1953 arts. 14, 19 (1) (d) (e) and 132 (1) - Suppression Of Immoral Traffic In Women And Girls Act, 1956, s. 20 - Issue of vires of s. 20 of 1956 Act - High Court held that s. 20 of Act abridged fundamental rights of respondents u/arts. 14 and 19(1)(d)(e)? - Whether s. 20 of Act 1956 offends arts. 14 and 19(1)(d)(e)? - Differences between a woman who is a prostitute and one who is not certainly justify their being placed in different classes - So, there are obvious differences between a prostitute who is a public nuisance and one who is not - Object of Act, is not only to suppress immoral traffic in women and girls, but also to improve public morals by removing prostitutes from busy public places in vicinity of religious and educational institutions - Differences between these two- Classes of prostitutes have a rational relation to object sought to be achieved by Act - S. 20, in order to prevent moral decadence in a busy locality, seeks to restrict movements of second category of prostitutes and to deport such of them as peculiar methods of their operation in area may demand judicial decisions arising under Act and under analogous Acts were cited at Bar - Held, question whether a particular provisions offends art. 14 or not depends upon provisions of Act wherein that section appears - Decisions of other Acts do not afford any guidance to decide Vires of s. 20 of Act - Shama Bai v State of U P Indlaw (A I R 1959 All 57 relied on - For reasons stated Court do not agree with this view - Court therefore, hold that s. 20 of Act does not infringe art. 14 - Further Court held, provisions of s. 20 of Act are reasonable restrictions imposed in public interest within meaning of s. 19(5) and, therefore, do not infringe fundamental rights of respondents under arts. 19(1)(d) and (e) thereof - Appeals allowed.
