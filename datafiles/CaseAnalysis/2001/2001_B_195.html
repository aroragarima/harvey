State of Madhya Pradesh v Bhooraji and Others

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20738; (2001) 7 SCC 679; (2001) SCC (Cr) 1373; AIR 2001 SC 3372; 2001 (3) CCR 202; 2001 (4) Crimes 190; 2002 (1) CRJ 248; 2001 CRLJ 4228; JT 2001 (7) SC 55; 2001 (4) RCR(Criminal) 40; 2001(5) SCALE 423; 2001 (4) SCJ 457; 2001 (6) SLT 92; [2001] Supp2 S.C.R. 128; 2001 (6) Supreme 311; 2001 (2) UC 458B

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Human Rights

Keywords: Charge - Sheet, Cognizable offence

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 190, 465 and 462 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 148 - Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989, s. 3(2) - Trial Proceedings - Whether High Court necessarily should have quashed trial proceedings to be repeated again only on account of declaration of legal position made by Supreme Court concerning procedural aspect about cases involving offences under SC/ST Act? - Held, a de novo trial should be last resort and that too only when such a course becomes so desperately indispensable - It should be limited to extreme exigency to avert 'a failure of justice' - Any omission or even illegality in procedure which does not affect core of case is not a ground for ordering a de novo trial - This is because Appellate Court has plenary powers for re-evaluating or re-appraising evidence and even to take additional evidence by Appellate Court itself or to direct such additional evidence to be collected by Trial Court - But to replay whole laborious exercise after erasing bulky records relating to earlier proceedings, by bringing down all persons to court once again for repeating whole depositions would be a sheer waste of time, energy and costs unless there is miscarriage of justice otherwise - S. 465 makes it clear that error, omission or irregularity in proceedings held before or during trial or in any enquiry were reckoned by legislature as possible occurrences in criminal courts - Yet, legislature disfavoured axing down proceedings or to direct repetition of whole proceedings afresh - The legislation imposed a prohibition that unless such error, omission or irregularity has occasioned 'a failure of justice', Superior Court shall not quash proceedings merely on ground of such error, omission or irregularity - HC ought to have ascertained whether there was a really a failure of justice before directing re-trial - Matter remitted back to HC to decide appeal afresh in accordance with observations made - impugned order set aside - Appeal disposed of.
