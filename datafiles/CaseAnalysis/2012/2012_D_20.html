Desiya Murpokku Dravida Kazhagam & Anr v The Election Commission Of India

Supreme Court of India

18 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Surinder Singh Nijjar, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 507; (2012) 7 SCC 340; 2012 (6) AWC 5772; 2012 (4) CLT(SC) 13; JT 2012 (4) SC 301; 2012 (2) RCR(Civil) 897; [2012] 3 S.C.R. 1084

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Election; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: House Of The People, Electorate, Allotment Of Symbols, Reserved Symbol, Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order, 1968

Summary: Constitution - Election - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 324 - Representation of People Act, 1951, s. 29A - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, r. 5 and 10 - Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order, 1968, cl. 6, 6B - Election symbols - Amendments - Challenged - Petitioner (Political party) and another filed instant petition challenging amendment of 1968 Order (Symbols Order) - Respondent Election commission to amend 1968 Order issued notification dt.1.12.2000 and substituted cl. 6 with cl. 6A(i), (ii) and cl. 6B - Respondent refused recognition of petitioner as State party, though it secured valid votes in Assembly election - Petitioners contended that despite having secured a larger percentage of votes than was required, it was denied recognition, since they failed to return 2 member of Legislative Assembly - Hence, instant petitions - Whether amendment of Symbols Order by Election Commission was justified - Held, admittedly petitioners were unrecognised political parties, they did not have a reserved symbol for exclusive allotment to candidates setup by those parties at elections and during pendency of petitions, said Order came to be amended again by Notification dt.16.9.2011 - In Conference dt.30.7.1957, there was a general agreement among all participants on various items; relevant in context was that; same symbol would be used throughout India for all candidates of a party, both for parliamentary and assembly elections' - As consensus arrived at said Conference, respondent Commission gave 'recognition' to 14 political parties as National/Multi State parties and allotted to each of them a specific symbol - Such a recognition was accorded in exercise of general power of superintendence conferred on respondent u/art. 324 of Constitution r/w r. 5(1)4 of 1961 Rules - After first General Elections, respondent decided to withdraw recognition of those political parties whose poll performance was poor - Parties, which polled a minimum of 3% of votes at first General Elections, were allowed to retain their recognition and recognition accorded earlier to other parties was withdrawn - Said percentage was raised to 4 after 3rd General Elections, till 1967 - Substance of provisions of allotment of Symbols Order was that, no political party was entitled for allotment or use of an election symbol permanently - Allotment of an exclusive election symbol was available to a political party only so long as it was recognised by Commission - Securing recognition and its continuance depends upon performance of the political party at every succeeding general election - Thus, newly formed political parties were not entitled, as a matter of right, for exclusive allotment of a common election symbol for benefit of all candidates set up by them at any election - Such candidates were required to choose 1 of free symbols notified by Commission - Such allotment of a free symbol depends upon various factors, such as, existence of a prior claim - Thus, all candidates set up by a political party need not get same symbol at a general election - A political party was nothing but an association of individuals pursuing certain shared beliefs - Art. 19(1)(c) of Constitution confered a fundamental right on all citizens to form associations or associate with organisations of their choice - Evolution of law related to criteria for a political party to be recognized as a State Party clearly indicated that Election Commission, in its wisdom, was of view that in order to be recognized as a political party, such party should have achieved a certain bench-mark in State politics - Nothing new was brought out in submissions of petitioners which could make to take a different view from what was decided earlier - Contention regarding political parties winning a larger number of seats while polling a lesser percentage of votes, sounds attractive, but has to be discarded - Voter's right had to be balanced with ground realities of conducting a State-wide poll - Election Commission has kept said balance in mind while setting bench-marks to be achieved by a political party in order to be recognized as a State Party and become eligible to be given a common election symbol - There was variance between views expressed by Constitution Bench in the PUCL case and amendments effected by Election Commission to the Order, by its Notification dt.1.12.2000 - People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL) & another and Association For Democratic Reforms v. Union of India and another, 2003 Indlaw SC 290, referred to - Order accordingly.

J. Chelameswar J.

Held, to ensure conduct of periodic elections to various legislative bodies, Election Commission was established by Constitution - It was endowed with such powers necessary to enable the same to function as an independent constitutional entity to discharge Constitutional obligations entrusted to it untrammelled by authority of Executive - That entire scheme of a representative democracy enshrined in Constitution was for the purpose of achieving Constitutional goal of establishing a 'Democratic Republic' adumbrated in preamble to Constitution - Subject to fulfilment of various conditions stipulated in Constitution or by an appropriate law made in that behalf, every citizen of country has a Constitutional right both to elect and also be elected to any one of Legislative Bodies created by Constitution - Thus, all citizens have a fundamental right to associate for advancement of political beliefs and opinions held by them and could either form or join a political party of their choice - Political parties were, no doubt, not citizens, but their members were generally citizens - Thus, any restriction imposed on political parties would directly affect fundamental rights of its members - There was a statutory compulsion (post 1996) on the part of Election Commission to recognise or not to recognise a political party as it was only on the basis of recognition by Election Commission, rights or obligations created under 1951 Act come into play - There was still no constitutional compulsion in that regard - Once a qualified voter decided to contest an election under provisions of 1951 Act, whether such a voter was sponsored by a political party or not, whether such a political party was recognised by Election Commission or not, there was no way under law, as it existed today, to prevent him from contesting - Election Commission was bound to allot a pictorial symbols to each such candidate - Enjoyment of fundamental rights guaranteed by Constitution could not be made dependent upon popularity of a person or an idea held by person - If it were to be otherwise, it would be very antithesis of liberty and freedom - Constitutional guarantees were meant to protect unpopular, minorities and their rights - Denying benefit of a symbol to candidates of a political party, whose performance did not meet standards set up by Election Commission, would disable such political party from effectively contesting election, thereby, negating right of an association to effectively pursue its political briefs - Object sought to be achieved by Election Commission by the Order was to avoid confusion in minds of voters at the time of voting - Such a result was said to be achieved by Election Commission by denying recognition to political party with insignificant following, thereby, denying them benefit of reservation of an exclusive symbol to its candidates - Simply denying recognition to a political party with insignificant voter-support, it was not understandable how the perceived voter confusion could be avoided - There was snothing either in Constitution or in 1951 Act or any other law, which prohibit an unrecognised political party from setting up candidates at an election - Refusal of recognition by Election Commission, unrecognised or derecognised political parties or independent candidates without any party support could still contest election - Candidates set up by an unregistered political party could contest an election as registration u/s. 29A of 1951 Act was not mandatory for a political party, except that registration begets certain advantages specified in 1951 Act to a political party - Election Commission was bound to allot a symbol to any of candidates belonging to any one of said categories - There was no rational nexus between classification of recognised and unrecognised political parties and professed purpose sought to be achieved by such classification was likely to preserve political status quo -Symbols Order was violative of art. 14 of Constitution - Order accordingly.
