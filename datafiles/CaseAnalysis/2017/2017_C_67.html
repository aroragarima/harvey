Chrisomar Corporation v Mjr Steels Private Limited

Supreme Court of India

14 September 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Sanjay Kishan Kaul

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 809

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Maintenance, Perpetual Injunction, Common Law, Jurisdiction, Contract, Perpetual, Performance, Indian Contract Act, 1872, Settlement, Promisee, English, Promise, Admiralty Court Act, 1861, Necessaries, Colonial Courts Of Admiralty Act, 1890, Admiralty Court Act, 1840, Administration of Justice Act, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Attachment, Constitution of India, 1950, Supreme Court of Judicature (Consolidation) Act, 1925, Admiralty (Jurisdiction and Settlement of Maritime Claims) Act, 2017, U.K. Civil Procedure Code

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Maritime claim - Modification of contract - Validity - Appellant/plaintiff filed admiralty suit before HC seeking for an arrest of respondents/vessel as the necessaries supplied to vessel would amount to maritime claim and lien on vessel - Single Judge held that plaintiffs were entitled to recover their dues and further held that respondent no.1's claim that ownership had changed and that they had become owners of the vessel was not conclusively proved - On appeal, DB held that claim made in the suit was held to have been abandoned when settlement was acted upon and reversed judgment of Single Judge - Hence, instant appeal - Whether, on the date of arrest respondent no. 1 happened to be the owner of vessel as found by HC.

Held, it was clear that there was no new contract which comes into being. The original terms continue to be part of the modified contract except to the extent that they are inconsistent with the modifications made. The sum due under original contract continued to be due and payable under settlement agreement. The fact that interest and legal costs were added would not amount in itself to superseding the original contract, as those relate to payments under the original contract and put promisee in the same position as if the contract had originally been performed. SC observed that original agreement was not superseded but was only sought to be enforced, the manner of performance being different. Such being the case, HC's conclusion that there was a novatio of original agreement on the facts of instant case was incorrect. Respondent's own admission made in the plaint that monies were not yet received under the Letter of Credit and that an injunction should be granted restraining defendant no.1 from receiving such money and against plaintiff's bank from making any such payment to defendant no.1. Thus, it was clear that HC was not correct in its view that it was proved by respondent no.1 that sale had taken place in April, 2000 by third party to respondent no.1 by which respondent no.1 became the owner of vessel. Respondent no. 1 has failed to prove that there was a change of ownership of the vessel in its favour on the date of arrest. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside and restore the decree of Trial Court. Appeal allowed.
