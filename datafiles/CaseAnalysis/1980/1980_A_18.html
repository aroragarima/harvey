Â Ambika Prasad Mishra Etc v State of Uttar Pradesh and Others

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, Y.V. Chandrachud, P.N. Bhagwati, V.D. Tulzapurkar, A.P. Sen

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 188; (1980) 3 SCC 719; AIR 1980 SC 1762; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1159; 1980 UJ 844

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Women

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Uttar Pradesh Imposition Of Ceiling On Land Holdings Act, 1960 - Limitation on agricultural land holdings - Justifiability - Central fact of our country's progress had made land distribution and its inalienable ally, ceiling on land holding, cynosure of legislative attention - When litigative confrontation with large holders had imperiled implementation of this vital developmental strategy, Parliament, in exercise of its constituent power, had sought to pre-empt effectively and protect impregnably such statutory measures by enacting art. 31A of Constitution - SC repelled challenges to land reform laws as violative of fundamental rights but constant struggle between agrarian reform legislation and never-say-die litigation had led to situation where every such enactment had been inevitably accompanied by countless petitions assailing its vires despite art. 31A, not to speak of more extensive Chinese walls like arts. 31B, 31C and 31D of Constitution - Hence instant appeals - Whether parties could take advantage of this handicap and audaciously challenge every date of coming into force of every provision - Held, a proceeding in respect of declaration of rights or interest in any land was deemed not to be such proceeding - That was clear legislative intent behind Explanation - Ordinarily an Explanation was intended to explain scope of main section and was not expected to enlarge or narrow down its scope but where legislative intent clearly and unambiguously indicates an intention to do so, effect must be given to legislative intent notwithstanding fact that legislature named that provision as an Explanation - A feeble submission was made that there was time-wise arbitrariness vitiating statute in that various provisions in Act were brought into force on random dates without any rhyme or reason, thus violating, from temporally angle, art. 14 - It was true that neither legislature nor Govt. as its delegate could fix fanciful dates for effectuation of provisions affecting rights of citizens - A larger latitude was allowed to State to notify date on which particular provision might come into effect - Parties could not take advantage of this handicap and audaciously challenge every date of coming into force of every provision as capriciously picked out - Although mere election manifesto could not be basis for fixation of date, here significance was deeper in that it was virtually announcement of political government of its pledge to people that agrarian policy would be revised accordingly - Other dates do not create any problem being rationally related to date of preceding ordinance or date of introduction of bill - Details were not necessary except to encumber this judgment - SC emphasized that brief of State when meeting constitutional challenges on ground of arbitrariness must be complete coverage, including an explanation for date of enforcement of provision impugned - Court and counsel could not dig up materials to explain fossil dates when long years later an enterprising litigant chooses to challenge - A few other minor infirmities were faintly mentioned but not argued at all or seriously, such as, for instance, contention that s. 38B of the Act which understandably excludes res judicata was challenged as violative of basic structure of Constitution and otherwise exceeds legislative competence - There was no need to dilate on every little point articulated by one or other of numerous advocates who justified their writ petitions or civil appeals by formal expression of futile submissions - Appeals dismissed.
