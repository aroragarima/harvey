Surendra Singh and Others v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

16 November 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Vivian Bose, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 85; AIR 1954 SC 194; 1954 CRLJ 475; 1954 (1) MLJ(SC) 50; 1954 SCJ 12; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 330

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal Procedure Code 1898

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 99 and 108 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 225 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 366, 369, 424 and 537 - Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 225 - All appellants prosecuted for murder - Appeal to High Court of Allahabad - Judge transferred - Died before 'judgment' was delivered - Whether this 'judgment' could be validly delivered after death of one of two Judges who heard appeal? - Held, Allahabad HC framed its Rules in 1952 - Rules provide for four different situations - (1) for judgments which are pronounced at once as soon as case has been heard; (2) for those which are pronounced on some future date (3) for judgments which are oral, and (4) for those which are written - Judgment is final decision of court intimated to parties and to world at large by formal 'pronouncement' or 'delivery' in open court - Decision pronounced or intimated must be a declaration of mind of court as it is at time of pronouncement - Final operative act is that which is formally declared in open court with intention of making it operative decision of court - Up to moment judgment is delivered, Judges have right to change their mind - Therefore, however judgment is nothing but a draft till formally delivered as judgment court - Judges who delivers judgment, or causes it to be delivered by a brother Judge, must be in existence as a member of court at moment of delivered so that he can - No need for him to be physically present in court but he must be in existence as a member of court and be in a position to stop delivery and effect an alternation should there be any last minute change of mind on his part - One cannot assume that Judge would not have changed his mind if he is no longer in a position to do so - Mere signing of draft does not necessary indicate a closed mind - Appeal allowed.
