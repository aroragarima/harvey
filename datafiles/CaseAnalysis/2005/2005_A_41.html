Air India Limited and Others v Vishal Capoor and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 September 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

Ruma Pal, AR. Lakshmanan, C.K. Thakker

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1249; (2005) 13 SCC 42; 2006 (1) Bom.C.R. 731; 2005 (107) FLR 855; JT 2005 (12) SC 170; 2005 (4) LLN 304; 2005(7) SCALE 619; 2005 (6) SLR 63; [2005] Supp3 S.C.R. 670

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 10, 18(3) and 33 - Seniority of pilots - First appellant provided international air transport services, operated by pilots-in-command (PIC) and co-pilots - Respondent nos. 7 to 12 (Adhikari group) submitted that they all were ex- Indian Air Force or Navy pilots and holders of ALTP licences with 500 flying hours as PIC were entitled to seniority over the writ petitioners because they had obtained an ALTP prior to the writ petitioners - Respondent no. 1 to 6 (Writ petitioners) who were holders of CPL licences raised a demand asking for seniority from the date of their joining the first appellant - In 1998 a settlement was arrived at between the Indian Pilots' Guild and the first appellant wherein writ petitioners were placed at serial Nos. 173 to 178 and the 'Adhikari Group' against serial Nos. 205 to 218 - Adhikari Group' filed a writ petition before the HC challenging Clause 7(C) of the 1998 settlement who dismissed the writ petition holding that the 1998 settlement was binding upon all workmen in view of the express provisions of s. 18(3) of 1947 Act - HC held that negotiations were in progress between the Guild and the first appellant and that if the petitioner pilots would be aggrieved by any settlement which may be arrived at they could raise a dispute before the Conciliation Officer - Hence, present appeal - Whether order of HC's could be upheld? - Held, disputed question of fact will normally arise when a petitioner puts forward a case on facts which are controverted by the respondents - This is naturally so, as it cannot be expected that the petitioner will of his, her or its own say that the facts forming the basis of the claim are disputed - Although it may happen that the Court on a scrutiny of the nature of the claim made in the petition may come to a conclusion that the factual issues raised are ex facie controversial and decline, in limine, to exercise jurisdiction u/a. 226 of Constitution, nevertheless the controversy usually surfaces after the respondents have had an opportunity of giving their version of the matter - That was what happened in the present case - Writ petitioners rested their case on clause 7(C) of the 1998 settlement whereas Adhikari Group pleaded that the settlement was vitiated by fraud - Obviously, the burden of proving this would be on the respondents - No court or tribunal has tested the allegations made by the Adhikari group on merits till today - HC shut out the allegations altogether for two reasons - The first reason was that it would amount to entertaining a separate cause of action - Said conclusion was erroneous as it was based on a confusion between onus of proof and cause of action - Disputes between the 'Adhikari group' and the CPL holders formulated - Appropriate Government directed to refer the disputes for adjudication by the appropriate Industrial Tribunal - Appeals allowed.
