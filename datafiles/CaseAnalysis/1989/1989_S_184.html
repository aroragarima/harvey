Â Express Hotels Private Limited v State of Gujarat and Another

Supreme Court of India

02 May 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

M.N. Venkatachaliah, R.S. Pathak, S. Natarajan, S. Ranganathan, S. Mukharji

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 435; (1989) 3 SCC 677; AIR 1989 SC 1949; 1990 (3) CompLJ 275; 1990 (26) ECR 317; [1989] 178 ITR 151; JT 1989 (3) SC 72; 1989(1) SCALE 1200; [1989] 2 S.C.R. 893; 1989 (74) STC 157

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax

Keywords: Karnataka Tax on Luxuries Act, 1979, Tamil Nadu Tax On Luxuries In Hotels and Lodging Houses Act, 1981, Gujarat Tax on Luxuries Act 1977, West Bengal Entertainments and Luxuries (Hotels and Restaurants) Tax Act, 1972

Summary: Indirect Tax - Government Of India Act, 1935 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Gujarat Tax on Luxuries Act 1977 - Karnataka Tax on Luxuries Act, 1979 - Tamil Nadu Tax on Luxuries in Hotels and Lodging Houses Act, 1981 - West Bengal Entertainments and Luxuries (Hotels and Restaurants) Tax Act, 1972 - Luxuries Tax - Legality - 1977 Act seeks to levy tax at certain percentages of lodging charges recovered by proprietors of hotels and lodging houses from persons lodging therein treating lodging accommodation for which charges of Rs. 35 or more per day per person as taxable luxury - Scheme of 1972 Act was slightly different in regard to scope of charge to be given effect to under that Act - Levy there was not confined to lodging charges recovered from persons lodging in hotels, but on basis of provision for luxury and not, as in case of other legislation, as lodging charges actually paid by lodgers - Hence instant appeals were filed for Constitutional validity of legislations of different States viz., Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka and West Bengal, imposing tax on 'luxuries' under Entry 62 of List II of VII Schedule to Constitution of India was challenged - Petitioners contended that that tax laws were not outside purview of Part XIII of Constitution and that present tax on lodgings and accommodations in hotels was violative of freedom of 'trade, commerce and inter-course' and offends art. 301, 'that business undoubtedly was commerce but was something more, it was intercourse' the word 'intercourse' specifically occurs in art. 301 intending to give largest connotation to concept of commerce - Whether quality or standards of lodging accommodation in hotels could be called luxurious by contemporary standards by reason of higher standards of charges payable for accommodation - Held, legislature had chosen to identify luxury by statutory standards prescribed by it - According to legislative assumption, price does become evidence of special quality on basis of which 'luxuries' could be distinguished and that some special quality was attributable to goods and services through means of price - Quality and price, in legislative assessment, could be assumed to have logical inter-relationship - This could not be held to suffer from vice of irrationality - Taxes could and do sometimes, regard to their effect and impact on free flow of trade, constitute restrictions on freedom u/art. 301, but restriction must stem from provisions of law imposing tax which could be said to have direct and immediate effect of restricting free flow of 'trade, commerce and intercourse' - It was not all taxes that had this effect - Freedom u/art. 301 was by all reckoning, great freedom, one of utmost significance to economic unity of nation - Underlying need for and recognition of freedom of inter-State trade, commerce and intercourse - In present case it was been pointed out how tax on 'luxuries' enjoyed by person in hotel was either discriminatory or had direct and immediate effect of impeding freedom of intercourse - There was no substance in contention - Appeals dismissed.
