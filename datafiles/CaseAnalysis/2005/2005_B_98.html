Orissa Agro Industries Corporation Ltd. And Ors v Bharati Industries And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

08 November 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, C.K. Thakker

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1257; (2005) 12 SCC 725; AIR 2006 SC 198; 2006 (62) ALR 495; 2005 (6) AWC 5908; 2006 (1) BC 362; JT 2005 (10) SC 19; 2006 (1) RCR(Civil) 89; 2005(9) SCALE 128; [2005] Supp5 S.C.R. 55

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Maintainability of writ petition - Breach of contract - Appellant-Corporation for disposal of its unserviceable machineries/equipments and other scrap materials called for tenders and after negotiation the offer of respondent no. 1 was accepted - After depositing the required amount, according to the respondent no. 1 when he went to take delivery of the materials he found that many valuable and/or important parts were missing - Respondent no. 1 informed appellant-corporation and gave details of articles which were found missing - However, as no positive response was received, writ petition was filed - HC held that since there were disputed facts which cannot be adjudicated in a writ petition - However, HC directed the appellant-corporation to pay to the respondent no. 1 a sum of Rs. 8.5 lakh and writ petition was allowed - Hence, present appeal - Whether HC should not entertain the writ petition? - Held, where a complicated question of fact is involved and the matter requires thorough proof on factual aspects, the HC should not entertain the writ petition - Further, whether or not the HC should exercise jurisdiction u/art. 226 of the Constitution would largely depend upon the nature of dispute and if the dispute cannot be resolved without going into the factual controversy, the HC should not entertain the writ petition - Hence, no writ order can be issued u/art. 226 to compel the authorities to remedy a breach of contract; pure and simple - It is more so when factual disputes are involved - In the present case, writ petition was primarily founded on allegation of breach of contract - Question whether the action of the opposite party in the writ petition amounted to breach of contractual obligation ultimately depends on facts and would require material evidence to be scrutinized and in such a case writ jurisdiction should not be exercised - Further, HC itself observed that disputed questions of fact were involved and yet went on to give directions as if it was adjudicating the money claim in a suit; this course was clearly impermissible - Appeal allowed.
