B.K. Muniraju v State of Karnataka and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 February 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 1089; (2008) 4 SCC 451; AIR 2008 SC 1438; JT 2008 (3) SC 284; 2008 (3) KarLJ 369; 2008 (2) MLJ(SC) 1181; 2008(2) SCALE 515; [2008] 2 S.C.R. 992

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Mysore Land Revenue Rules, 1938

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 227 and 136 - Karnataka Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prohibition of Transfer of Certain Lands) Act, 1978, s. 4(1) - Mysore Land Revenue Rules, 1938, rr. 43(1),(5) and (8) - Karnataka Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prohibition of Transfer of Certain Lands) Act, 1978, s. 4 - (A) Whether land in question was 'granted land' or 'purchased for a price' by grand-father of appellant? - Document in question which is filed as Annexure-P3, has been styled or titled as 'Certificate of Grant' - In order to know real nature of document, one has to look into recitals of document and not title of document - Intention is to be gathered from recitals in deed, conduct of parties and evidence on record - With a view to ascertain nature of transaction, document has to be read as a whole - A sentence or term used may not be determinative of real nature of transaction - Held, though document, according to appellant 'Certificate of Grant', perusal of clauses therein, clearly shows that land was sold in a public auction and grandfather of appellant purchased same - In addition to recitals, darkhast registered extract also shows that land in question was sold for a price - Merely because document has been styled or titled as 'Certificate of Grant', it cannot be construed that land was a 'granted land' attracting provisions of Act and Rules - (B) Jurisdiction of High Court for interference in a factual decision arrived at by authorities - It is settled law that a writ of certiorari can only be issued in exercise of extraordinary jurisdiction which is different from appellate jurisdiction - Held, it is clear that whether it is a writ of certiorari or exercise of supervisory jurisdiction, none of available to correct mere errors of fact or of law unless following requirements are satisfied, (i) error is manifest and apparent on face of proceedings such as when it is based on clear ignorance or utter disregard of provisions of law and (ii) a grave injustice or gross failure of justice has occasioned thereby - (C) Whether HC committed any error in dismissing writ petition? - Held, two authorities as well as HC adverted to revenue extract and concluded that it was not a 'granted land' and it was purchased in a public auction on payment of a price - HC rightly refused to quash orders of said authorities and dismissed writ petition - Appeal dismissed.
