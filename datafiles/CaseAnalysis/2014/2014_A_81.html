FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Dr. Subramanian Swamy v Arun Shourie

Supreme Court of India

23 July 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Shiva Kirti Singh, Dipak Misra, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 473; (2014) 12 SCC 344; AIR 2014 SC 3020; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 3376; 2014 (306) E.L.T. 193; JT 2014 (8) SC 369; 2014 (5) KarLJ 161; 2014 (3) MLJ(Crl) 462; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 797; 2014(8) SCALE 679

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Commissions Of Inquiry Act, 1952, Contempt Of Courts Act, 1952, Public Servants (Inquiries) Act, 1850

Summary: (A)Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 175, 178, 179, 180 or 228 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss.2(a), 2(b) and 2(c) - Contempt of Courts Act, 1952, ss.5A, 10 and 10A - Contempt petition - Maintainability - When a sitting SC Judge was appointed as a Commissioner by the Central Govt. under the 1952 Act, did he carry with him all the powers and jurisdiction of SC, in other words, whether the functions which were discharged by SC Judge as a Commissioner were purely statutory functions independent of the jurisdiction vested in the SC -

Held, in order to answer the question, it was appropriate to refer to relevant provisions of the two Acts, namely, the 1971 Act and the 1952 Act - 1971 Act was enacted by the Parliament to define and limit the powers of certain courts in punishing contempts of courts and to regulate their procedure in relation thereto - S. 2(a) of 1971 Act defined 'contempt of court' to mean 'civil contempt' or 'criminal contempt' - Civil contempt was defined in s.2(b) of 1971 Act while s.2(c) of 1971 Act defined criminal contempt - Three expressions, 'court' in clause (i), 'judicial proceeding' in s.2(c)(ii)(iii) of 1971 Act were really important, to answer the first question - Ss. 12 and 15 of 1971 Act were the other two sections which had some bearing - S.12 of 1971 Act prescribed punishment for contempt of court - S.15 of 1971 Act dealt with cognizance of criminal contempt by SC or the HC on its own motion or on a motion made by the Advocate General or any other person with the consent in writing of the Advocate General - Expression 'Advocate General' in s.15(1)(a)(b) of 1971 Act in relation to SC means Attorney General or the Solicitor General - 1952 Act provided for appointment of Commissions of Inquiry and for vesting such Commissions with certain powers - S. 2(a)(i) of 1971 Act defined 'appropriate Government' which means the Central Govt., in relation to a Commission appointed by it to make an inquiry into any matter relatable to any of the entries enumerated in List I or List II or List III in the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution and the State Govt., in relation to a Commission appointed by it to make an inquiry into any matter relatable to any of the entries enumerated in List II or List III in the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution - In relation to the State of Jammu and Kashmir, there was a different provision - Ss. 4 and 5 of 1952 Act dealt with the powers and additional powers of Commission - Under s.4 of 1952 Act, the Commission had powers of a civil court while trying a suit under CPC, in respect of the matters, namely, (a) summoning and enforcing the attendance of any person from any part of India and examining him on oath - (b) requiring the discovery and production of any document - (c) receiving evidence on affidavits - (d) requisitioning any public record or copy thereof from any court or office -

(e) issuing commissions for the examination of witnesses or documents etc. - U/s. 5(4) of 1952 Act, the Commission was deemed to be a civil court and when any offence as was described in ss.175, 178, 179, 180 or 228 of IPC was committed in the presence of the Commission, the Commission might, after recording the facts constituting the offence and the statement of the accused as provided for in CrPC, forward the case to a magistrate having jurisdiction to try the same - U/s. 5(5) of 1952 Act, any proceeding before the Commission was deemed to be a judicial proceeding within the meaning of ss. 193 and 228 of IPC - S.5A of 1952 Act empowered the Commission to utilize the services of certain officers and investigation agencies for conducting investigation pertaining to inquiry - S. 10 of 1952 Act made provision for every member of the Commission and every officer appointed or authorized by the Commission in exercise of functions under the Act was deemed to be a public servant within the meaning of s.21 of the IPC - S.10A of 1952 Act provided for penalty for acts calculated to bring the Commission or any member thereof into disrepute - Provision clothes HC with power to take cognizance of an offence stated in s.10(1) of 1952 Act upon a complaint in writing made by a member of Commission or an officer of the Commission authorized by it in this behalf - U/s.10(5) of 1952 Act, the HC taking cognizance of an offence u/s.10 (1) of 1952 Act was mandated to try the case in accordance with the procedure for the trial of warrant cases instituted otherwise than on a police report before a court of a Magistrate - Moreover, s.10A of the 1952 Act left no matter of doubt that the HC was conferred with the power to take cognizance of the complaint in respect of the acts calculated to bring the Commission or any member thereof into disrepute - S.10A of 1952 Act provided the power of constructive contempt to the Commission by making a reference to HC with a right of appeal to SC - Thus, the question was answered in the negative - Petitions dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 175, 178, 179, 180 or 228 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss.2(a), 2(b) and 2(c) - Contempt of Courts Act, 1952, ss.5A, 10 and 10A - Contempt petition - Maintainability - Whether truth could be pleaded as defence in contempt proceedings -

Held, two Judge Bench in one case before SC had held that the amended section enabled the Court to permit justification by truth as a valid defence in any contempt proceedings if it was satisfied that such defence was in public interest and the request for invoking the defence was bona fide - SC approved the view of the two Judge Bench - Indirect Tax Practitioners Association v R. K. Jain 2010 Indlaw SC 618, relied on - Nothing further needed to be considered with regard to second question since the amendment in contempt law has effectively rendered that question redundant - Petitions dismissed.
