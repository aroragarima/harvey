H.G. Rangangoud v M/S.State Trading Corporation Of India Limited & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

11 November 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 772; (2012) 1 SCC 297; AIR 2012 SC 490; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 3927; 2012 CRLJ 828; 2012 (1) JCC 308; 2012 (5) KarLJ 160; 2011 (4) RCR(Criminal) 862; [2011] 13 S.C.R. 97; 2012 (1) UPLBEC 24

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Mines & Minerals; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Mines & Minerals - Practice & Procedure - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, s. 2(c)(ii) -Judicial process - Interference - Contempt - Government communication - Appellant applied for grant of mining lease for iron ore to Government - Government u/s. 5(1) of 1957 Act, recommended to Central Government for grant of mining lease in favour of appellant - Central Government issued notification u/s. 17 A (1A) of 1957 Act, and reserved iron ore deposits, in question, for exploitation by respondents - Central Government returned proposal of State Government for grant of mining lease to appellant - Appellant filed writ petition before HC seeking to quash notification - Single Judge quashed notification - In pursuant to order of HC, appellant made representation to Government for consideration - Aggrieved by Single Judge order respondents appealed to DB - DB reserved judgment - Court was informed that State Government communicated to Central Government that mining lease was granted in favour of appellant - DB found that granting of lease to appellant, when matter was sub-judice, amounted to interference with due course of judicial process - DB initiated criminal contempt against appellant and respondent u/s. 2(c)(ii) of 1971 Act - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that representation was made prior to filing of writ appeal before DB by respondent - Whether the act done by appellant and recommended by Government, when matter was sub-judice, amounted to interference with due course of judicial process - Held, power to punish for contempt was inherent in courts of record and described as a necessary incident to every court of justice - Power was inalienable attribute of court and inheres in every court of record - Power was given by Constitution to secure public respect and confidence in the judicial process- Rule of law was basic rule of governance of any civilized democratic polity - It was only through courts that rule of law unfolded its contours and established its concept - Secretary to Government while making recommendation also did not voice any opinion on pending writ appeal before DB - Any attempt to influence outcome of matter pending before court to prejudice the parties therein would prejudice or interfere with due course of any judicial proceedings - Mere filing of representation and making recommendation thereon in no way prejudiced or interfered or tended to interfere with due course of any judicial proceeding - Alleged act of respondent and appellant in no way prejudiced or interfered or tended to interfere with the due course of any judicial proceeding - Proceeding initiated against appellant and Secretary to Government was not just and appropriate and an abuse of process of court - DB order was set aside - Appeals allowed.
