Â His Holiness Kesavananda Bharati Sripadgalvaru and Others v State of Kerala and Another

Supreme Court of India

24 April 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

S.M. Sikri, J.M. Shelat, K.S. Hegde, A.N. Grover, A.N. Ray, P. Jaganmohan Reddy, D.G. Palekar, Hans Raj Khanna, Kuttyil Kurien Mathew, M. Hameedullah Beg, S.N. Dwivedi, A.K. Mukherjea, Y.V. Chandrachud

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 537; (1973) 4 SCC 225; AIR 1973 SC 1461; [1973] Supp S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Freedom Of Movement, Speech And Expression, Morality, Existing Laws, Constitutionality, Legislative Procedure, Legislative Lists, Residuary Power Of Legislation, Schedules, Severability, State Laws, Basic Structure

Summary: Sikri, C J

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, 6 Judges held that in view of art. 13(2) Fundamental Rights could not be abridged or taken away - Five Judges held that art. 13(2) was inapplicable to Acts amending Constitution - Further, Golak Nath's case, declared that a Constitutional amendment would be bad if it infringed Art. 13 (2), as this applied not only to ordinary legislation but also to an amendment of Constitution - It did not decide whether art. 13 (2) can be amended u/art. 368 or determine exact meaning of expression "amendment of this Constitution" in Art. 368 - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, in Constitution word "amendment" or '"amend" has been used in various places to mean different things - In some articles, word "amendment" in context has a wide meaning and in another context it has a narrow meaning - Different words have been used to meet different demands - In view of great variation of phrases used all through Constitution it follows that word "amendment" must derive its colour from art. 368 and rest of provisions of Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, art. 368 does not enable Parliament in its constituent capacity to delegate its function of amending Constitution to another legislature or to itself in its ordinary legislative capacity - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, meaning of word "Amendment" that is most appropriate and fits in with whole scheme of Constitution - Expression "amendment of this Constitution" in Art. 368 means any addition or change in any of provisions of Constitution within broad contours of Preamble and Constitution to carry out objectives in Preamble and Directive Principles - Expression "amendment of this Constitution" does not enable Parliament to abrogate or take away fundamental rights or to completely change fundamental features of Constitution so as to destroy its identity - Within these limits Parliament can amend every art. - Further, applied to fundamental rights, it would mean that while fundamental rights cannot be abrogated reasonable abridgments of fundamental rights can be effected in public interest - It is of course for Parliament to decide whether an amendment is necessary - Courts will not be concerned with wisdom of amendment - If this meaning is given it would enable Parliament to adjust fundamental rights in order to secure what Directive Principles direct to be accomplished, while maintaining freedom and dignity of every citizen - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Necessary implication from all provisions of Constitution - Held, fundamental importance of freedom of individual, indeed its inalienability, and importance of economic, social and political justice mentioned in Preamble, importance of directive principles, non-inclusion in Art. 368 of provisions like Arts. 52, 53 and various other provisions to which reference has already been made an irresistible conclusion emerges that it was not intention to use word "amendment" in widest sense - In view of this, a necessary implication arises that there are implied limitations on power of Parliament that expression "amendment of this Constitution" has consequently a limited meaning in our Constitution and not meaning suggested by respondents - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - (A) It was contended that Parliament could validly amend Art. 368 to transfer source of amending power from List I, Entry 97 to Art. 368 - It was further contended that 24th Amendment does enlarge power of Parliament to amend Constitution, if Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493, limited it, and as Art. 368 clearly contemplates amendment of Art. 368 itself, Parliament can confer additional powers of amendment on it - Held, it is not legitimate to interpret art. 368 in this manner - Clause (e) of proviso does not give any different power than what is contained in main art. - Meaning of expression "Amendment of Constitution" does not change when one reads proviso - If meaning is same, Art. 368 can only be amended so as not to change its identity completely - Further, words "notwithstanding anything in Constitution" have effect to get rid of argument that Art. 248 and Entry 97, List I contains power of amendment - Similarly, insertion of words "in exercise of its constituent power" only serves to exclude Art. 248 and Entry 97, List I and emphasize that it is not ordinary legislative power that Parliament is exercising u/art. 368 but legislative power of amending Constitution - (B) It was contended that if Parliament cannot increase its power of amendment clause (d) of Section 3 of 24th Amendment which makes art. 13 inapplicable to an amendment of Constitution would be bad - Held, art. 13(2) as existing previous to 24th Amendment as interpreted by majority in Golak Nath's case, prevented legislatures from taking away or abridging rights conferred by art. 13 - U/art. 368, Parliament can amend every art. of Constitution as long as result is within limits already laid down - Amendment of art. 13 (2) does not go beyond limits laid down because Parliament cannot even utter amendment abrogate or authorise abrogation or taking away of fundamental rights - After amendment now a law which has effect of merely abridging a right while remaining within limits laid down would not be liable to be struck down - 24th Amendment as interpreted is valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Object of amendment is to modify decision given by this Court in Rustom Cavasjee Cooper v. Union of India 1970 Indlaw SC 575, where it was held by ten Judges that Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act violated guarantee of compensation u/Art. 31(2) in that it provided for giving certain amounts determined according to principles which were not relevant in determination of compensation of undertaking of named Banks and by method prescribed amounts so declared could not be regarded as compensation - Held, whereas before amendment, Art. 31(2) required law providing for acquisition to make provision for compensation and after amendment Art. 31(2) requires such a law to provide, for an "amount" - Further, substance of fundamental right to property, u/art. 31, consists of three things : one, property shall be acquired by or under a valid law; secondly, it shall be acquired only for a public purpose and thirdly, person whose property has been acquired shall be given an amount in lieu thereof, which, is not arbitrary, illusory or shocking to judicial conscience or conscience of mankind - Parliament cannot empower legislatures to fix an arbitrary amount or illusory amount or an amount that virtually amounts to confiscation, taking all relevant circumstance of acquisition into consideration - In view of interpretation placed on new art. 31(2) as amended it, cannot be said that Parliament has exceeded its amending power u/art. 368 in enacting new art. 31(2) - S. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment ) Act, 1971, as interpreted, is valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - (A) Meaning of word 'amount' in s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Held, word "amount" here means something to give in lieu of property to be acquired but this amount has to and can be worked out by laying down certain principles - These principles must then have a reasonable relationship to property which is sought to be acquired - If this is so, amount ultimately arrived at by applying principles must have some reasonable relationship with property to be acquired; otherwise principles of Act could hardly be principles within meaning of art. 31(2) - (B) Meaning of expression "amount so fixed" - Held, parliament cannot empower legislatures to fix an arbitrary amount or illusory amount or an amount that virtually amounts to confiscation, taking all relevant circumstance of acquisition into consideration - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Art. 31-C was inserted by Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - (A) It was urged that when an Act of Parliament or a State Legislature delegates a legislative power within permissible limits delegated legislation derives its authority from Act of Parliament - It was suggested that similarly State law would derive authority from Art. 31-C - Held, art. 31C enables States to adopt any policy they like and abrogate arts. 14, 19 and 31 of Constitution at will - It enables State to amend Constitution - It enables a State Legislature to set up complete totalitarianism in State - (B) Whether art. 368 enables Parliament to delegate its function of amending Constitution to another body? - Held, no - Art. 368 does not contemplate any other mode of amendment by Parliament and it does not equally contemplate that Parliament could set up another body to amend Constitution - It is well settled in India that Parliament cannot delegate its essential legislative functions - (C) Whether art. 31C can be read to be an implied amendment of Art. 368, and if so read, is it valid, i.e., within powers of Parliament to amend Art. 368 itself? - Held, art. 31C cannot be read to be an implied amendment of art. 368 because it opens with words "notwithstanding anything contained in Art. 368." - Art. 31C does is that it empowers legislatures, subject to condition laid down in art. 31C itself, to take away or abridge rights conferred by arts. 14, 19 and 31 - At any rate, if it is deemed to be an amendment of art. 368 itself/art. 368 does not enable Parliament to constitute another legislature to amend Constitution, in its exercise of power to amend Art. 368 itself - For aforesaid reasons, s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act 1971 is void as it delegates power to legislatures to amend Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Validity of Part VII- Twenty-Ninth-Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) amended Ninth Schedule of Constitution and inserted Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1969 and Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1971 - Effect of insertion of two Kerala Acts in Ninth Schedule is that provisions of Art. 31-B get attracted - Held, as art. 368 does not enable Parliament to abrogate or take away fundamental rights, it does not enable Parliament to do this by any means, including device of Art. 31-B and Ninth Schedule - This device of art. 31-B and Ninth Schedule is bad in so far as it protects statutes even if they take away fundamental rights - Therefore, it is necessary to declare that Twenty-Ninth Amendment is ineffective to protect impugned Acts if they take away fundamental rights - Further, it would be for Constitution Bench to decide whether impugned Acts take away fundamental rights - Constitution Bench to determine validity of Constitution (Twenty-sixth Amendment) Act, 1971 in accordance with this judgment, and law - Cases remitted to Constitution Bench.

BEG, J.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13(2), 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, decision in Golak Nath's case has a bearing upon scope of power of amendment contained in unamended art. 368 - In said case, majority of Judges held that power of amendment itself and not merely its procedure was contained in art. 368 of Constitution and also held this power of amendment to be wide - Majority view in Golak Nath's case, holding that art. 13 operated as a limitation upon powers of constitutional amendment found in art. 368, was erroneous - Minority view there was correct on this question - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, scope of amendment must necessarily be wide in context of whole Constitution - Necessary implication of word "amendment" or meaning of term itself may exclude a possible complete abrogation of present Constitution although that could be done, step by step, by bodies empowered to amend if they so desired and followed appropriate procedure - There is nothing in cases cited which could enable to put in implied limitations, in a Constitution, on Article 368, containing expressly sovereign law-making power of amendment of every part of it - Petition disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Construing expression "amount" - Held, word 'amount' in art. 31(2), as amended, does not convey idea of any prescribed norm - Fixation of amount or laying down of a principle for determining amount are matters within exclusive power of Parliament or State Legislature concerned - Norms and their satisfaction on question of adequacy of compensation or its reasonableness, are matters within exclusive competence of legislative authorities to determine - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - (A) It was contended that Parliament could validly amend Art. 368 to transfer source of amending power from List I, Entry 97 to Art. 368 - It was further contended that 24th Amendment does enlarge power of Parliament to amend Constitution, if Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493, limited it, and as Art. 368 clearly contemplates amendment of Art. 368 itself, Parliament can confer additional powers of amendment on it - Held, when an amendment is made by an appropriate procedure, amendment becomes a part of principal's own will and intention and action - If principal is and must necessarily be a human authority, bodies of persons authorised to amend u/art. 368 of Constitution would share legislative sovereignty and would constitute "Principal" whose will is expressed in amendment - In sphere of primary fundamental law of Constitution lies also amending power contained in art. 368 of Constitution over which control of judicature is limited to seeing that form and manner of amendment is properly observed - Beyond that, authority of judicial organ over Constituent power vested in Constitutional bodies or organs mentioned in Article 368 of Constitution ceases - Further, fundamental rights, may be viewed as such limits - Power of amendment, in a Constitution, must include power to change these limitations to suit needs of each age and generation - Every kind of law, whether fundamental or ordinary, has to be an attempted adaptation to needs of people at a particular time - (B) It was contended that if Parliament cannot increase its power of amendment clause (d) of Section 3 of 24th Amendment which makes art. 13 inapplicable to an amendment of Constitution would be bad - Held, art. 368 expressly provided either for expansion or diminution of scope of powers of amendment - It cannot therefore, be reasonably contended that power of recreation even of whole Constitution by stages was not already contained in unamended art. 368 - This part of proviso also shows that Constitution-makers contemplated a wide amending power so as to meet challenges of times offered by rapidly changing social political, economic, national, and international conditions and situations - 24th Amendment of Constitution is valid - It would, therefore, follow that 25th and 29th Amendments are also valid - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Held, art. 31C has two parts - First part is directed at removing laws passed for giving effect to policy of State towards securing principles specified in clause (b) or clause (c) of Art. 39 of Constitution from vice of invalidity on ground that any such law "is inconsistent with or takes away or abridges any of rights conferred by Articles 14, 19 and 31 of Constitution" - Second part of art. 31C goes on to provide that, if such a law contains a declaration that it is for giving effect to such policy, it will become immune from judicial review altogether - Concession made on behalf of UOI is quite justifiable - It is that a declaration by itself is not part of law made, but it is something only attached to law even though this annexation is by a purported law - Declaration contemplated by art. 31C is like a certificate given after considering relevancy of principles specified in Art. 39 (b), and (c) of Constitution, and therefore, jurisdiction of Court is not ousted - Courts can still consider and decide whether declaration is really good or a mere presence attached to a colourable piece of legislation or to a law which has no. bearing on or nexus with principles found in art. 39 (b) and (c) of Constitution - Out of two equally acceptable views, even on question of nexus, one in conformity with legislative verdict should prevail - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Distinction between fundamental rights and Directive Principles - Held, basis of a difference between ends or means were really to be attempted, it would be more proper, to view fundamental rights as ends of endeavours of Indian people for which Directive Principles provided guide-lines - Another distinction, which seems to me to be valid and very significant is that, whereas, fundamental rights were "conferred" upon citizens, with corresponding obligations of State, Directive Principles lay down specific duties of State organs - Petitions disposed of.

CHANDRACHUD, J.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - (A) Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, 6 out of 11 learned Judges held in Golak Nath case that Art. 368 prescribed not merely procedure for amendment but conferred power to amend Constitution and that amending power cannot be traced to Residuary Entry 97 of List I, Sch. VII read with Arts. 245, 246 and 248 of Constitution - However, decision of leading majority in Golak Nath case that then Art. 368 of Constitution merely prescribed procedure for amendment of Constitution and that power of amendment had to be traced to Entry 97 of List I, Schedule VII read with art. 245, 246 and 248 is not correct - Decision of leading majority and of Hidayatullah, J. that there is no distinction between an ordinary law and a law amending Constitution is incorrect - Art. 13(2) took in only ordinary laws, not amendments to Constitution effected u/art. 368 - Decision of leading majority and of Hidayatullah, J. that Parliament had no power to amend Constitution so as to abrogate or take away Fundamental Rights is incorrect - (B) If majority decision in Golak Nath case be incorrect, what is extent of inherent or implied limitations, if any, on power of Parliament to amend Constitution by virtue of its power under Art. 368? - Held, there are no inherent limitations on amending power in sense that Amending Body lacks power to make amendments so as to damage or destroy essential features or fundamental principles of Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, Part XX of Constitution is entitled "Amendment of Constitution", not "Procedure for Amendment of Constitution" - Art. 368, which is only art. in Part XX must therefore be held to deal both with procedure and product of that procedure - Marginal note to Art. 368 : "Procedure for Amendment of Constitution" was only a catchword and was in fact partially correct - It did not describe consequence of adoption of procedure because title of part described it clearly - Legislative power under Entry 97, List I, belongs exclusively to Parliament - Power to amend Constitution cannot be located in that Entry because in regard to matters falling within proviso to Art. 368, Parliament does not possess exclusive power to amend Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, word 'amendment' in Art. 368 has a clear and definite import and it connotes a power of widest amplitude to make additions, alterations or variations - Power contained in Art. 368 to amend Constitution is indeed so wide that it expressly confers a power by clause (e) of proviso to amend amending power itself - Art. 368, manifestly, does not impose any express limitations - Power of amendment of Constitution conferred by then Art. 368 was wide and unfettered - It reached every part and provision of Constitution - Further, preamble is a part of Constitution and is not outside reach of amending power u/art. 368 - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 has by s. 2 thereof added a new clause (4) to Art. 13 of Constitution providing that nothing in that art. "shall apply to any amendment of this Constitution made under Art. 368" - Section 3 (a) of Act substitutes a new marginal heading to Art. 368 in place of old - Marginal heading of unamended Art. 368 was : "Procedure for amendment of Constitution." - New heading is : "Power of Parliament to amend Constitution and procedure therefor - Held, old Art. 368 not only prescribed procedure for amendment of Constitution but conferred power of amendment - That position is made clear by s. 3 of 24th Amendment which substitutes by clause (a) a fully expressive marginal heading to Art. 368 - Further, that constitutional amendments are made in exercise of constituent power and not in exercise of ordinary law-making power - That position is reiterated by clause (b) of s. 3 - Clause (c) of s. 3 makes it obligatory for President to give his assent to bill for a constitutional amendment - Finally, clause (d) of s. 3 of 24th Amendment excludes application of Art. 13 to an amendment made u/art. 368 - Constitution (Twenty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1971, thus, merely clarifies what was true law and only declares true legal position as it obtained before that Amendment and must therefore be held valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 brings about significant changes in Art. 31 and introduces a new Art. 31-C - By s. 2 (a) of Amendment Act, 1971, clause (2) of art. 31 is substituted by a new clause which permits compulsory acquisition or requisition of property for a public purpose by authority of law, which provides for acquisition or requisitioning of property "for an amount which may be fixed by such law or which may be determined in accordance with such principles and given in such manner as may be specified in such law" - Held, s. 2 (a) and s. 2 (b) of 25th Amendment are valid - Though Courts have no power to question a law described in Art. 31(2) substituted by s. 2 (a) of Amendment Act, on ground that amount fixed or determined for compulsory acquisition or requisition is not adequate or that whole or any part of such amount is to be given otherwise than in cash, Courts have power to question such a law if (i) amount fixed is illusory; or (ii) if principles, if any, are stated, for determining amount are wholly irrelevant for fixation of amount; or (iii) if power of compulsory acquisition or requisition is exercised for a collateral purpose; or (iv) if law of compulsory acquisition or requisition offends principles of Constitution other than one which is expressly excepted u/art. 31 (2-B) introduced by Section 2 (b) of 25th Amendment Act - namely Art. 19 ( l ) (f); or (v) if law is in nature of a fraud on Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Art. 31-C has been introduced by 25th Amendment in order to achieve purpose set out in art. 39(b) and (c) - Held, language of Art. 31-C makes it clear that only such laws will receive its protection as are for giving effect to policy of State towards securing principles specified in Art. 39(b) or (c) - True nature and character of Art. 31-C is that it identifies a class of legislation and exempts it from operation of Arts. 14, 19 and 31 - Art. 31-C does not delegate power to amend - Clearly this does not exclude jurisdiction of Court to determine whether law is for giving effect to policy of State towards securing principles specified in Art. 39 (b) or (c) - Laws passed under Art. 31-C can, be upheld only, and only if, there is a direct and reasonable nexus between law and Directive Policy of State expressed in Art. 39 (b) or (c) - Therefore, s. 3 of 25th Amendment which introduced Art. 31-C into Constitution is valid - In spite, however, of purported conclusiveness of declaration therein mentioned, Court has power and jurisdiction to ascertain whether law is for giving effect to policy of State towards securing principles specified in Art. 39(b) or (c) - If there is no. direct and reasonable nexus between such a law and provisions of Art. 39 (b) or (c), law will not, as stated in Art. 31-C, receive immunity from a challenge under Arts. 14, 19 or 31 - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Validity of Part VII- Twenty-Ninth-Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) amended Ninth Schedule of Constitution and inserted Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1969 and Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1971 -2035 - Held, 29th Amendment Act is valid. two Kerala Acts mentioned therein, having been included in Ninth Schedule, are entitled to protection of Art. 31-B of Constitution - Order accordingly.

DWIVEDI, J

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, in Golak Nath, Wanchoo J. and two other Judges and Hidayatullah Bachawat and Ramaswami JJ. took view that power to amend Constitution is located in art. 368 - Subba Rao C. J. and four other Judges, on contrary, held that art. 368 does not grant power of amending Constitution and it merely provides for procedure for amendment of Constitution - Agreed with view that amending power resides in original art. 368 - Majority decision in Golaknath is not correct and should be overruled - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, phrase "amendment of this Constitution" is determinative of dominion as well as magnitude of amending power - Words "this Constitution" in phrase embrace entire Constitution, as according to art. 393 "this Constitution" is called "Constitution of India" - Accordingly each and every provision of Constitution including Part III falls within sway of amending power - As Preamble is a part of Constitution, it is liable to amendment u/art. 368 - No provision of Constitution can claim immunity from sway of amending power - Amending power can amend each and every provision of Constitution including Preamble and Part III - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Nature of Amending Power - Held, parliament's additional power to amend certain provisions of Constitution by ordinary law would not obliterate distinction between constituent power and legislative power - Constitution is of a hybrid pattern - It is partly controlled and partly uncontrolled - It is uncontrolled with respect to those provisions of Constitution which may be amended by an ordinary law through legislative procedure; it is controlled with respect to remaining provisions which may be amended only by following procedure prescribed in art. 368 - When any part of Constitution is amended by following legislative procedure, amendment is result of exercise of legislative power; when it is amended through procedure prescribed by art. 368, amendment is result of exercise of constituent power - Amending power conferred by art. 368 is a constituent power and not a legislative power - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, word "amendment" should be so construed as to fructify purpose underlying Art. 368 - Word 'amendment' in Art. 368 is broad enough to authorise varying, repealing or abrogating of each and every provision in Constitution including Part III - Nature, object and history of amending power and context of art. 368 leave little room for doubt that word "amendment" includes power of repealing or abrogating each and every provision of Constitution - Art. 368 permits Parliament to apply not only physician's needle but also surgeon's saw - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Inherent and implied limitations on amending power - Held, art. 368 places no express limits on amending power -It expressly provides for its own amendment - Parliament and more than half of States may jointly repeal art. 368 and thus make fundamental rights immutable if they so desire - It is not permissible to enlarge constructively limitations on amending power - Courts are not free to declare an amendment void because in their opinion it is opposed to spirit supposed to pervade Constitution but not expressed in words - Constitution does not recognise supremacy of SC over Parliament - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, art. 368 provides specifically for a procedure for amending Constitution - Power to amend Constitution is accordingly necessarily implied in Art. 368 - Art. 368 finds place in Part XX of Constitution - Heading of Part XX is "amendment of Constitution" and not "procedure for amendment of Constitution." - Heading will include both power as well as procedure - Word 'only' in art. 368 rules out all other procedures for amendment - So no law can be made for a referendum or a constituent assembly - A referendum or a constituent assembly will reduce Art. 368 to redundance - Referendum was not accepted by framers of Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - Held, it consists of two relevant sections, ss. 2 and 3 - These sections have been drawn in light of various judgments in Golaknath - S. 2 adds clause (4) to art. 13 - As majority decision in Golaknath had taken view that art. 13(2) is a limitation on amending power to take away or abridge fundamental rights, cl. (4) removes that limitation - S. 3 consists of four clauses; Clause (a) substitutes marginal note to unamended Art. 368 ; Clause (b) renumbers unamended Art. 368 as cl. (2) and adds cl. (1) to it - New clause (1) calls amending power as 'constituent power' - It empowers Parliament to amend 'by way of addition, variation or repeal' any provision of Constitution in accordance with prescribed procedure - Except as regards assent of President to Bill, everything else in 24th Amendment was already there in unamended Art. 368 - Amendment is really declaratory in nature - It removes doubts cast on amending power by majority judgment in Golaknath - 24th Amendment is valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - By Twenty-fifth Amendment Act, original clause (2) of Art.31 is recast to some extent by deleting any reference to 'compensation' in cases of compulsory acquisition and requisition for a public purpose but to receive an "amount" which legislature itself may fix or which may be determined in accordance with principles as may be specified by law - Held, all that amendment has done is to negative interpretation put by this court on concept of compensation - Clause (2) recognizes fundamental right to receive an amount in case of compulsory acquisition or requisition and all that it wants to clarify is that fundamental right is not to receive compensation as interpreted by SC but a right to receive an amount in lieu of deprivation which legislature thinks fit - Whether a particular law fixes an amount which is illusory or is otherwise a fraud on power denying fundamental right to receive an amount specifically conferred by clause (2) will depend upon law when made and is tested on basis of clause (2) - Possibility of abuse of a power given by an amendment of constitution is not determinative of validity of amendment - Further, new clause 2B inserted in Art.31 having consequence of excluding application of Art.19 (l) (f) to a law referred to in clause (2) of Art.31 is merely a re-statement of law laid down by this court after constitution came into force - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Art. 31C was inserted - Main clause of art. falls into two parts - First part provides that a law of a particular description shall not be deemed to be void on ground that it affects injuriously somebody's fundamental rights u/arts. 14, 19 and 31 - Second part provides that if such a law contains a particular declaration, courts shall not entertain a particular kind of objection - Approach suggested on behalf of Union that new Art.31C does not have effect attributed to it on behalf of petitioners - It is, submitted, that Art.31C does not prevent judicial review as to whether law referred to therein is of description it maintains it is If on a consideration of its true nature and character court considers that legislation is not one having a nexus with principles contained in Art.39 (b) or (c), it will not be saved under Art.31C - Approach suggested on behalf of Union is correct approach to interpretation of Art.31C - Twenty-fifth Amendment Act valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Validity of Part VII- Twenty-Ninth-Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) amended Ninth Schedule of Constitution and inserted Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1969 and Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1971 - Held, twenty-ninth Amendment is not different from several similar Amendments made previously by which Statutes were added from time to time to ninth schedule and whose validity has been uphold by SC - Twenty-ninth Amendment is, therefore, valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Express or implied limitations in Art.368 itself - Held, no implied limitations can be inferred from Preamble or fundamental rights which, being as much part of a legal document as any other provision of constitution, are subject to equal consideration in the, matter of legal construction - Unqualified manner in which it is given in Art.368 of constitution it is impossible to imply any limitations on power to amend fundamental rights - Since there are no limitations express or implied on amending power, it must he conceded that all Amendments which are in question here must be deemed to be valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Held, art. provides that when special procedure directed by it is successfully followed Constitution stands amended in terms of proposal for amendment made in Bill - As long as art. stood in its present form Parliament could not possibly introduce its own procedure to amend Constitution by calling a constituent assembly, a convention or like - Altogether, it will be seen that grant of power under Art. 368 is plenary, unqualified and without any limitations, except as to special procedure to be followed - Amendment of constitution is not a law made by State and hence Art.13 (2) would not control an amendment of constitution - Order accordingly.

HEGDE AND MUKHERJEA, JJ.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, while interpreting a provision in a statute or Constitution primary duty of court is to find out legislative intent - Ordinarily legislative intent is gathered from language used - If language employed is plain and unambiguous, same must be given effect to irrespective of consequences that may arise - But if language employed is reasonably capable of more meanings then one, then Court will have to call into aid various well settled rules of construction and, in particular, history of legislation-to find out evil that was sought to be remedied and also in some cases underlying purpose of legislation - Though power to amend Constitution u/art. 368 is a very wide power, it does not yet include power to destroy or emasculate basic elements or fundamental features of Constitution - Word "amendment" in art. 368 is not a word of precise import and has not been used in various Articles and parts of Constitution to convey always same precise meaning, it is necessary to take aid of other relevant rules of construction to find out intention of Constitution makers - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Whether there is any implied limitation on amending power u/art. 368? - Held, parliament has no power to abrogate or emasculate basic elements or Fundamental features of Constitution such as sovereignty of India, democratic character of policy, unity of country, essential features of individual freedoms secured to citizens - Nor has Parliament power to revoke mandate to build a Welfare State and egalitarian society - These limitations are only illustrative and not exhaustive - Despite these limitations, however, there can be no question that amending power is a wide power and it reaches every Article and every part of Constitution - Word "amendment" in art. 368 carries with it certain limitation and, further, that power conferred u/art. 368 is subject to certain implied limitations though that power is quite large - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, power to amend Constitution u/art. 368 as it stood before its amendment empowered Parliament by following form and manner laid down in that Article to amend each and every Article and each and every Part of Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - Held, 24th Amendment Act did not enlarge amending power of Parliament - It merely made explicit what was implicit in original Article - 24th amendment has not made any material change in Art. 368 as it stood originally - Scope of Parliament's power to amend Constitution or any part thereof must be held to have remained as it was before 24th Amendment notwithstanding alterations made in phraseology of Art. 368 - 24th Amendment must be held to be valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - (A) Substituted Art. 31(2) - Held, newly substituted Art. 31(2) does not destroy right to property because (i) fixation of "amount" under that Article should have reasonable relationship with value of property acquired or requisitioned; (ii) principles laid down must be relevant for purpose of arriving at "amount" payable in respect of property acquired or requisitioned; (iii) "amount' fixed should not be illusory and (iv) same should not be fixed arbitrarily - (B) Meaning of word 'amount' - Held, word "amount" is a neutral word; standing by itself, it has no norm and is completely colourless - Question whether "amount" in question has been fixed arbitrarily or same is illusory or principles laid down for determination of same are relevant to subject-matter of acquisition or requisition at about time when property in question is acquired or requisitioned are open to judicial review - But it is no more open to court to consider whether "amount" fixed or to be determined on basis of principles laid down is adequate - (C) Clause 2 (b) of 25th Amendment Act - Held, clause 2 (b) of 25th Amendment Act which incorporated Article 31 (2-B) is also valid as it did not damage or destroy any essential features of Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Held, clause (3) of 25th Amendment Act which introduced into Constitution Art. 31-C is invalid for two reasons i.e., (1) it was beyond amending power of Parliament in so far as amendment in question permits destruction of several basic elements or fundamental features of Constitution and (2) it empowers Parliament and State Legislatures to protanto amend certain human freedoms guaranteed to citizens by exercise of their ordinary legislative power Art. 31-C permits destruction of some of basic features of our Constitution and consequently, it is void - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Validity of Part VII- Twenty-Ninth-Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) amended Ninth Schedule of Constitution and inserted Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1969 and Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1971 - Held, 29th Amendment Act is valid but whether Acts which were brought into IXth Schedule by that Amendment or any provision in any of them abrogate any of basic elements or essential features of Constitution will have to be examined when validity of those Acts is gone into - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13(2) - Expression "law" in art. 13(2) - Held, expression "law" in Article 13 (2) even before Article 13 was amended by 24th Amendment Act, did not include amendments to Constitution - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Whether Parliament has power to abridge or take away any of Fundamental Rights prescribed in Part III of Constitution? - Held, article opens by saying "An amendment of this Constitution" which means an amendment of each and every provision and part of Constitution - Nothing in that Article to restrict its scope - Power of amendment implied in that Article can reach each and every Article as well as every part of Constitution - Further, Art. 368 as it originally stood comprehended both power as well as procedure to amend Constitution - Order accordingly.

MATHEW. J

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, decision in Golaknath Case that Parliament had no power to amend Fundamental Rights in such a way as to take away or abridge them was wrong, that power to amend u/art. 368 as it stood before 24th Amendment was plenary in character and extended to all provisions of Constitution - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Construing expression "amendment of this Constitution" - Held, u/art. 368 there was, or is, no power to amend Fundamental Rights and other essential or basic features in such a way as to destroy or damage their essence or core - Nor can art. be amended in such a way as to invest people-legal sovereign with power to do it - Art. 368, as it stood before Amendment, conferred plenary power to amend all provisions of Constitution and 24th Amendment, except in one respect, namely, compulsory character of assent of President to a bill for amendment, is declaratory in character - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - 24th Amendment made certain changes in Art. 368 to make it clear that Parliament, in exercise of its constituent power, hats competence to amend by way of addition, variation or repeal, any of provisions of Constitution in accordance with procedure laid down in art. and that Art. 13(2) world not be a bar to any such amendment - Held, 24th Amendment did not add anything to content of Art. 368 as it stood before amendment, that it is declaratory in character except as regards compulsory nature of assent of President to a bill for amendment and that art. as amended makes it clear that all provisions of Constitution can be amended by way of addition, variation or repeal - Only limitation is that Constitution cannot be repealed or abrogated in exercise of power of amendment without substituting a mechanism by which State is constituted and organized - That limitation flows from language of art. itself - Further, object of amendment was declaratory in character is clear from Statement of Objects and Reasons for Amendment - Amendment has added nothing to content of art. except requirement as to compulsory character of assent of President to bill for amendment - That an Amending Body, in exercise of its power to amend, if power to amend is plenary, can make an amendment in order to make clear what was implicit in art. and to correct a judicial error in interpretation of art. appears to me to be clear - 24th Amendment is valid - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - By 25th Amendment, word 'amount' was substituted for word 'compensation' in clause (2) of art. 31 - Held, 25th Amendment is valid - Word 'amount' in Art. 31(2), as amended, does not convey idea of any norm - Fixation of amount or principle for determining amount is a matter within absolute discretion of Parliament or State legislatures - Court cannot go into question whether amount fixed by law or principle laid down for determining amount is adequate or relevant - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Held, purpose of Art. 31-C is only to give immunity to a law for giving effect to policy of State towards securing Directive Principles u/art. 39(b) and (c) from attack on ground that its provisions violate Arts. 14, 19 and 31 - Expression 'no law' in latter part of Art. 31-C can only mean type of law referred to in first part - Declaration visualized in Art. 31-C that law gives effect to policy of State towards securing principles specified in Art. 39 (b) and (c) of Constitution would not oust jurisdiction of Court to go into question whether law gives effect to policy - Jurisdiction of Parliament or State legislatures to incorporate declaration in a law is conditioned upon circumstance that law is one for giving effect to State policy towards securing aforesaid principles - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) Act - Validity of Part VII- Twenty-Ninth-Amendment - Constitution (Twenty-Ninth Amendment) amended Ninth Schedule of Constitution and inserted Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1969 and Kerala Land Reforms (Amendment ) Act, 1971 - Held, 29th Amendment put two Acts in question, in Ninth Schedule with a view to make provisions thereof immune from attack on ground that Acts or provisions thereof violate any of Fundamental Rights - 29th Amendment is valid - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13(2) - Meaning of word 'Law' - Held, word 'law' in Art. 13(2), in context, could only mean an ordinary law - When Art. 13 (2) said that State shall not make any 'law', meaning of expression 'law' has to be gathered from context - In setting of Art. 13(2. what was prohibited was that Parliament shall not pass a law in pursuance of its powers under Chapter I of Part XI or any other provisions enabling it to pass laws, which were legislative in character - Constitution-makers only wanted to provide against more common invasion of Fundamental Rights by ordinary legislation - Petitions disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Implied or express limitations - Held, there were no express or implied limitations upon power of Parliament to amend Fundamental Right in such a way as to destroy or damage even core or essence of rights and 24th Amendment, by its language, makes it clear beyond doubt - Opening words of amended art. should make it clear that no. invisible radiation from any other provision of Constitution would operate as implied limitation upon power of amendment - Further, amended Art. 368 puts it beyond doubt that power to amend provisions of Constitution is in art. itself, that power includes power to add, vary or repeal any provision of Constitution, that power is a constituent power, that assent of President to a bill for amendment is compulsory and that nothing in Art. 13(2) will apply to an amendment under art. - Petitions disposed of.

RAY, J :

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of Golak Nath's case, 1967 INDLAW SC 1493 - Held, majority view in Golak Nath case was that an amendment of Constitution pursuant to Art. 368 is law within meaning of Art. 13(2), and, therefore, an amendment of Constitution abridging fundamental rights will be void - Majority view indicates that a constituent power was required to amend fundamental rights - Majority view has totally ignored aspect that constituent power is located in Art. 368, and, therefore, amendment under that Article is not a law within meaning of Art. 13(2) - An amendment of Constitution is an exercise of constituent power - Majority view in Golak Nath case is wrong - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 368 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Interpretation of art. 368 - Held, art. 368 confers power on Parliament subject to procedure provided therein for amendment of any provision of Constitution - Word "amendment" must be given its full meaning - This means that by amendment an existing Constitution or law can be changed - This change can take form either of addition to existing provisions, or alteration of existing provisions and their substitution by others or deletion of certain provisions altogether - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Whether power to amend is under any express limitation of Art. 13(2)? - Held, an amendment of Constitution is not an ordinary law made under powers conferred under Chapter I of Part XI of Constitution, and, therefore, it cannot be subject to Art. 13(2) - Power of amendment is safety valve which to a large extent provides for stable growth and makes violent revolution more or less unnecessary - Further, art. 368 provides in clear and unambiguous terms that an amendment bill after compliance with procedure stated therein and upon President giving assent to such bill Constitution shall stand amended in accordance with terms of bill - This constitutional mandate does not admit or provide any scope for any conflict with any other Article of Constitution - This is fundamental law - No other Article of Constitution has limited its scope - Moment President gives his assent to an amendment bill amendment becomes a part of Constitution - There cannot be a law before assent of President - Therefore, validity of any such supposed law cannot arise - An amendment of Constitution becomes a part of fundamental law - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Meaning of word 'law' - Held, either Constitution nor an amendment of Constitution can be or is law within meaning of art. 13 - Law in art. 13 means laws enacted by legislature subject to provision of Constitution - Law in art. 13 (2) does not mean Constitution - Constitution is supreme law - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 13(2) and 368 - Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Validity of 24th Amendment - Constitution, 24th Amendment Act amended Art. 368 - Constitution 24th Amendment Act enacts that Parliament may in exercise of its constituent power amend by way of addition, variation or repeal any provision of this Constitution in accordance with procedure laid down in that Article - Other part of amendment is that nothing in Article 13 shall apply to any amendment under Art. 368 - Held, in Golak Nath case majority view was that Art. 13(2) operated as a limitation on power of amendment - 24th Amendment took note of that decision and removed all doubts by amending Art. 13(2) and providing a new sub-article (4) there and also by amending Art. 368 to effect that Art. 13(2) shall not apply to any amendment of Constitution - 24th Amendment is valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 31(2) - Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 2 - Validity of s. 2 of Constitution (Twenty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Constitution 25th Amendment Act has amended Art. 31(2) and also Art. 31(2A). effect of these two amendments with regard to Arts. 31(2) and 31(2A) is two-fold, (1) no property shall be compulsorily acquired or requisitioned save for a public purpose and save by authority of law which provides for an amount which may be fixed by law or which may be determined in accordance with such principles; (2) Nothing in art. 19(1) (f) shall affect any law as is referred to in Art. 31(2) - It substituted word "amount" for word "compensation" for property acquired or requisitioned - Held, 25th Amendment is valid - Adequacy of amount fixed or principles specified cannot be subject matter of judicial review - Art. 31(2) is self contained and Arts. 31(2) and 19(1)(f) are mutually exclusive - Amendment of fundamental right prior to amendment was and is now after 24th Amendment valid - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 14, 19, 31, 31-B and 31-C - Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, s. 3 - Validity of s. 3 of Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971 - Introduction of Art. 31-C which enacts that notwithstanding anything contained in Art. 13 n. law giving effect to policy of State towards securing principles prescribed in clauses (b) and (c) of Article 39 shall be deemed to be void on ground that it is inconsistent with or takes away or abridges any of rights conferred by Articles 14, 19 and 31; and no law containing a declaration that it is for giving effect to such policy shall be called in question in any Court on ground that it does not give effect to such policy - Art. 31C is inextricably bound up with Arts. 39(b) and (c) because purpose and phraseology in both Articles are essentially identical - Law contemplated in Art. 31-C will operate on ownership and control of material resources of community to be distributed as best to subserve common good - Held, Art. 31C does not delegate or confer any power on State Legislature to amend Constitution - Art. 31C merely remove restrictions of Part III from any legislation giving effect to Directive Principles u/art. 39(b) and (c) - Power of Parliament and of State legislatures to legislate on class of legislation covered by Art. 31C is rendered immune from arts. 14, 19 and 31 - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Whether there are implied and inherent limitations on power of amendment? - Held, there are no implied and inherent limitations on power of amendment - Neither Preamble nor Article 13 (2) is at all a limitation on power of amendment - Order accordingly.

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 31A, 31B and 368 - Power to amend - Held, power to amend is wide and unlimited - Power to amend means power to add, alter or repeal any provision of Constitution - There can be or is no distinction between essential and inessential features of Constitution to raise any impediment to amendment of alleged essential features - Parliament in exercise of constituent power can amend any provision of this Constitution - U/art. 368 power to amend can also be increased - Order accordingly.
