Rohtas Industries Limited v Brijnandan Pandey

Supreme Court of India

11 October 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

S.K. Das, P. Govinda Menon, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 65; AIR 1957 SC 1; 1956 (2) LLJ 444; 1956 SCJ 740; [1956] 1 S.C.R. 800

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Industrial Disputes (Appellate Tribunal) Act, 1950, Industrial Disputes (Amendment And Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1956, Trade Union Act, 1926, misa, Industrial Disputes (Appellate Tribunal) Act

Summary: Constitution - Labour & Industrial Law - Practice & Procedure - Industrial Disputes (Appellate Tribunal) Act, 1950, s. 22 - Indian Penal Code,1860, ss. 295, 295 A, 436 and 437 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Discharge of workmen - Breaking idol of deity(ganesha) in public - Insult to feelings of Hindu community - (A) Whether there is a scope for enquiry u/s. 22 with regard to discharge or dismissal of a workman? - Held, there is a scope for enquiry u/s. 22, which can be availed to come to a conclusion whether there was a prima facie case made out for discharge or dismissal of workman and employer was not actuated by any improper motives or did not resort to any unfair practice - (A1) Who are temporary employees? - Held, under terms of employment, temporary employees could be moved from one work to other and they are not made permanent employees even if they enjoy any of benefits of permanent employees - (A2) Whether a special leave appeal against decision of labour appellate tribunal lies before Supreme Court? - Held, such special powers under art. 136 of Constitution exist where Labour Appellate Tribunal has not directed its mind to real question to be decided on an application under s. 22 - It has passed an order on basis of a somewhat irrelevant finding which has resulted in manifest injustice -(A3) Whether an Industrial Tribunal has power to create new obligations or modify contracts in interests of industrial peace? - Held, an industrial tribunal does not however, have power to create new obligations or modify contracts in interests of industrial peace to protect legitimate trade union activities and to prevent unfair practice or victimization - Industrial tribunal cannot ignore an existing agreement or existing obligation - Courts have power to enforce contracts - Appeal allowed.
