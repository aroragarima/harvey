Â Ramdas Athawale v Union Of India And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

29 March 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

B. SUDARSHAN REDDY, K.G. Balakrishnan, S.H. Kapadia, R.V. Raveendran, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 233; (2010) 4 SCC 1; AIR 2010 SC 1310; 2010(3) ALL MR 417; 2010 (4) Bom.C.R. 831; JT 2010 (3) SC 368; 2010 (3) MLJ(SC) 1333; 2010(3) SCALE 412

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conduct Of Business, House Of The People

Summary: (A) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 85, 87 - First Session of House of People in a year - Validity of the proceedings challenged on the ground that the President had not addressed both Houses of Parliament as envisaged u/art. 87 of the Constitution - Held, Session of House of People in the previous year was adjourned sine die and the resumption of its sitting in the next year is nothing but reconvening of the same Session after its adjournment sine die - Art. 87(1) does not require summoning of every Session of Parliament which was adjourned for its own reasons after commencement of its Session pursuant to the summons of the President - It is only when a House is prorogued and a new Session thereafter is summoned u/art. 85 (2) of the Constitution, the special address by the President as provided for u/art. 87 (1) is required with reference to the new Session so as to inform the Parliament of the cause of its summons - No such special address is needed, if a Sessions is adjourned sine die in the previous year and the sittings of the same Session is resumed in the next year - Petition dismissed.

(B) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 85, 87 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Amendment of arts. 85 and 87 - Effect of - Held, arts. 85 and 87 were amended so as to do away with the summoning of Parliament twice a year and the constitutional requirement of the President's special address at the commencement of each Session - After the amendment, not more than six months are to elapse between the last Session and the first day of the following Session and the House is presently prorogued only once a year and the President addresses both Houses of Parliament only at the commencement of the first new Session of each year - Petition dismissed.

(C) Parliament & Legislature - Adjournment and prorogation of Parliament - Distinction between - Held, an adjournment is an interruption in the course of one and the same Session, whereas a prorogation terminates a Session - Effect of prorogation is to put an end with certain exceptions to all proceedings in Parliament - Petition dismissed.

(D) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business in Lok Sabha, r. 15 - Ruling of the Speaker of House of People - Scope of judicial review - Whether the decision of the Speaker directing resumption of sitting of the House of People which was adjourned sine die is susceptible to judicial review in a proceeding u/art. 32 of the Constitution - Held, powers to regulate Procedure and Conduct of Business of the House of the People vests in the Speaker of the House - By virtue of the powers vested in him, the Speaker, in purported exercise of his power u/r. 15 of the Rules issued notice through the Secretary General of the House of People directing resumption of sittings of the House of People which was adjourned sine die - Whether the resumed sittings was to be treated as the second part of the last Session as directed by the Speaker is essentially a matter relating purely to the procedure of Parliament - Validity of the proceedings and business transacted in the House after resumption of its sittings cannot be tested and gone into by the SC in a proceeding u/art. 32 of the Constitution - No decision of the Speaker can be challenged by a member of the House complaining of mere irregularity in procedure in the conduct of the business - Such decisions of Speaker are not subject to the jurisdiction of any Court and they are immune from challenge - Referred the decisions of SC in In re, Under Article 143, Constitution of India, 1964 INDLAW SC 447 (also known as Keshav Singh's case) and Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain & Anr., 1975 INDLAW SC 433 - Petition dismissed.

(E) Practice & Procedure - Hypothetical question - Adjudication by Court of Law - Held, if an issue raised is purely a hypothetical question and there is no existing lis between the parties, the Court would not decide the matter - Further, SC does not decide matter which are only of academic interest on the facts of a particular case - Petition dismissed.

(F) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Scope of art. 32 - Held, art. 32 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a Constitutional remedy and relates only to the enforcement of the right conferred by Part III of the Constitution and unless a question of enforcement of a fundamental right arises, art. 32 does not apply - Further, no petition u/art. 32 is maintainable, unless it is shown that the petitioner has some fundamental right - Petition dismissed.
