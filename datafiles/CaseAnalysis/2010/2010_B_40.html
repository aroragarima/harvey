Kusum Sharma & Others v Batra Hospital & Medical Research Centre & Others

Supreme Court of India

10 February 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, H.S. Bedi

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 139; (2010) 3 SCC 480; 2010 ACJ 1444; AIR 2010 SC 1050; 2010 (2) ALD(SC) 89; 2010 (79) ALR 921; 2010 (2) AWC 1859; 2010 (2) Bom.C.R. 599; 2010 (1) CCC 132; 2010 (2) CTC 461; 2010 (2) JhrCR(SC) 62; JT 2010 (2) SC 7; 2010 (3) MLJ(SC) 512; 2010 (3) MPLJ 290; 2010 (1) OLR 884; 2010 (2) RCR(Civil) 161; 2010 (1) RLW 722; 2010(2) SCALE 267; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 685; 2010 (2) UC 1156

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection; Criminal; Practice & Procedure; Tort

Keywords: Degree Of Care, Hospitals, Medical Profession, Risk, Negligent

Summary: (A) Consumer Protection - Consumer Protection Act, 1986 - Medical negligence - Deficiency of service - Deceased died due to post-operative complications - Appellants, legal heirs of the deceased, filed complaint before National Commission alleging medical negligence and deficiency of service - Appellants contended that a vital organ of the deceased was injured during the surgery and such injury led to the death - National Commission dismissed the complaint - Held, deceased was operated by 3rd respondent and the tumour removed - Operation was successful and injury as contended by the appellants was superficial and same was repaired - Said injury was clearly recorded in the operation transcript and in the discharge summary of deceased - 3rd respondent who performed the operation had reasonable degree of skill and knowledge, hence he cannot be held guilty of negligence by any stretch of imagination - Further, appellant consented for surgery and 3rd respondent's choice to the mode of surgery was not so palpably erroneous - Therefore, appellants have failed to make out any case of medical negligence against the respondents, hence impugned order of National Commission is upheld - No interference is called for - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Medical negligence - Criminal prosecution - Scope - Held, in order to prosecute a medical professional for negligence under criminal law it must be shown that the accused/doctor did something or failed to do something which in the given facts and circumstances no medical professional in his ordinary senses and prudence would have done or failed to do - Hazard taken by the accused/doctor should be of such a nature that the injury which resulted was most likely imminent - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Practice & Procedure - Negligence - Proof - Criminal and civil proceedings - Distinction between - Held, a mere preponderance of probability is sufficient, and the defendant is not necessarily entitled to the benefit of every reasonable doubt in civil proceedings - However, persuasion of guilt must amount to such a moral certainty as convinces the mind of the Court, as a reasonable man, beyond all reasonable doubt in criminal proceedings - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Consumer Protection - Tort - Medical negligence - SC laid down principles for holding a medical practitioner guilty of medical negligence and the principles must be kept in view while deciding the cases of medical negligence - Held, as long as the doctors have performed their duties and exercised an ordinary degree of professional skill and competence, they cannot be held guilty of medical negligence - Applying the well settled principles enumerated the conclusion becomes irresistible that the appellants have failed to make out any case of medical negligence against the respondents - Appeal dismissed.
