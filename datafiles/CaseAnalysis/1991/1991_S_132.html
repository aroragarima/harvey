Padmaben Shamalbhai Patel v State Of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

18 January 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, M. Fathima Beevi, Veeraswami Ramaswami

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 463; (1991) SCC (Cr) 275; (1991) 1 SCC 744; AIR 1991 SCW 464; 1991 (1) Crimes 349; 1991 CRLR 162; 1991 CrLR(SC) 362; 1993 (2) DMC 473; 1991 (1) GLH 125; 1991 (2) HLR 71; JT 1991 (1) SC 205; 1991(1) SCALE 54; [1991] 1 S.C.R. 88; 1991 (2) ShimLC 14

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 32 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Appellant charged with murder of her brother's wife - Trial Court acquitted her - HC on a re-appreciation of evidence convicted appellant - Present appeal - Whether dying declaration can be used as evidence? - Dying declaration is admissible in evidence on principle of necessity and can form basis for conviction if it is found to be reliable - Based on premise that ordinarily a dying person will not falsely implicate an innocent person - In order that a dying declaration may form sole basis for conviction without need for independent corroboration it must be shown that person making it had opportunity of identifying person implicated and is thoroughly reliable and free from blemish - If, it is found that maker of statement was in a fit state of mind and had voluntarily made statement on basis of personal knowledge without being influenced by others and court on strict scrutiny finds it to be reliable there is no rule of law or even of prudence that such a reliable piece of evidence cannot be acted upon unless it is corroborated - Held, witnesses deposed that deceased was in a fit state of mind and was able to speak - She gave name of appellant in her dying declaration - (B) Whether having regard to her injuries her statements were true? - Held that mere fact that she had suffered 90 per cent burns is no reason to discard testimony of both medical men when they say that she was in a fit state of mind and was able to make dying declaration in question - (C) Whether since dying declarations were not in question, answer form they must be discarded? - Court merely stated that dying declaration should preferably be in question and answer form - Having regard to her condition they could not have questioned her in detail - Held, in these circumstances failure on part of medical men to record her statements in question and answer form can in no manner affect probative value to be attached to their evidence - Appeal dismissed.
