Kamti Devi and another v Poshi Ram

Supreme Court of India

11 May 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, R.P. Sethi

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 8; (2001) 5 SCC 311; (2001) SCC (Cr) 892; AIR 2001 SC 2226; 2001 (4) ALD(SC) 47; 2002 (2) Bom.C.R. 376; 2001 (2) CGLJ 193; JT 2001 (Supp1) SC 87; 2001 (3) MLJ(SC) 72; 2001 (3) RCR(Civil) 587; 2001(4) SCALE 195; 2001 (4) SCJ 147; [2001] 3 S.C.R. 729; 2001 (4) SLT 120; 2001 (4) Supreme 78; 2001 (2) UC 125

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Women & Children

Keywords: Births, Deaths and Marriage registration Act 1886

Summary: Family & Personal - Women & Children - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 112 - Presumption in favour of paternity of child - Conclusive proof of legitimacy - Marriage between appellant and respondent was solemnised in the year 1975 - After, 15 years, son was born - Long period in between was marked by internecine legal battles in which the spouses engaged as against each other - Respondent filed a civil suit for a decree declaring that he was not the father of the child, as he had no access to the appellant during the period when the child would have been begotten - Suit was dismissed - On appeal, first appellate court, after re-evaluating the entire evidence, found that the respondent succeeded in discharging the burden for rebutting the presumption by proving that he had no access to the mother of the child during a very long stretch of time covering the relevant period - First appellate court allowed the appeal and decreed the suit - Finding was upheld by HC - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the party who wants to dislodge the conclusiveness has the burden to show a negative, not merely that he did not have the opportunity to approach his wife but that she too did not have the opportunity of approaching him during the relevant time. The first appellate court, which is the final fact finding court, after evaluating the entire evidence adduced by both sides was satisfied in a full measure that the respondent had no opportunity whatsoever to have liaison with the defendant mother. The finding thus reached by the first appellate court cannot be interfered with in a second appeal as no substantial question of law would have flowed out of such a finding. Appeal dismissed.
