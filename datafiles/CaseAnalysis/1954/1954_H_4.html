Â Harishankar Bagla and Another v State of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

14 May 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 135; AIR 1954 SC 465; 1954 CRLJ 1322; 1954 (2) MLJ(SC) 211; 1954 SCJ 637; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 380

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Constitution - Essential Supplies (temporary Powers) Act, 1946, s. 7 r/w cl. 3 of Order - Cotton Textiles (Control of Movement) Order, 1948, cl. 3 - Constitutionality of ss. 3 and 4 of Act, 1946 on touchstone of art. 19(1)(f)(g) of Constitution of India, 1950 - Whether s. 3 of Act, 1946 ultra vires Legislature on ground of excessive delegation of legislative power? - Appellant and his wife was found in possession of six maunds of cotton allegedly without permission, which they were taking from Bombay to Kanpur - Provisions perused - Requirement of a permit to transport by rail cotton textiles to a certain extent operates as a restriction on rights of a person who is engaged in business of purchase and sale of cotton textiles - Art. 19(5) however permits such restriction to be placed provided they are in public interest - During period of emergency it was necessary to impose control on production, supply and distribution of commodities essential to life of community - It was for this reason that Legislature passed Act, 1946, authorising Central Govt. to make orders from time to time controlling production, supply and distribution of essential commodities - Cl. 3 of Control Order does not deprive a citizen of right to dispose of or transport cotton textiles purchased by him - It requires him to take a permit from Textile Commissioner to enable him to transport them - Held, requirement of a permit in this regard cannot be regarded as an unreasonable restriction on citizen's right under sub-cls. (f) and (g) of art. 19(1) - On point of excessive delegation, precedents referred and deliberated upon - Preamble and body of sections of Act, 1946 sufficiently formulate legislative policy and ambit and character of Act is such that details of that policy can only be worked out by delegating them to a subordinate authority within framework of that policy - Further held, provisions of ss. 3, 4 and 6 of Act, 1946 are constitutional and impugned order is also constitutional - Appeal dismissed.
