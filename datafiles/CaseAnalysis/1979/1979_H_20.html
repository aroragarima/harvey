State Of U.P. & Ors. v Hindustan Aluminium Corpn. Ltd. & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

17 April 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Shingal, D.A. Desai

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 241; (1979) 3 SCC 229; AIR 1979 SC 1459; [1979] 3 S.C.R. 709

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Electricity & Energy

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, Art. 226 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948, Ss. 18, 26 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910, Ss. 22-B, 28, 28(1) - Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951- Agreement - License - Supply of Power - Condition Precedent - Contractual Obligation - Agreement was entered into between appellant/State and respondent/Company for supply of power - Respondent/Company was granted a further license for expansion of its installed capacity - Appellant/State took a decision to reconnect pumping sets which had been disconnected for non-payment of electricity dues - Meanwhile, respondent/Company filed petition, on receipt of letter, that power supply to Company should be cut off completely with immediate effect, which was dismissed - Company filed its second petition for an application of stay, in which HC admitted petition, but rejected application for stay - Respondent filed petition u/art. 226 of Constitution - Appellant/State was aggrieved as HC had interfered with Order, made u/s. 22-B of 1910 Act - Hence instant appeal - Whether interference of HC in Order made u/s. 22-B of 1910 Act and whether state of things contemplated by enactment had ceased to exist - Held, Court rejected application of respondent/Company for grant of exemption u/cl. 10 of the Order - There was nothing to prevent respondent from making a fresh application, if it thinks that there was a real and substantial improvement in generation of energy in appellant/State - So even though application was valid and had wrongly been quashed by HC, Court not thought it necessary to restore it, so that it would not be deemed to form part of cl. 6(a) (i) of Order - High Court therefore erred in taking view that continuance of order was no longer justified - Order made was therefore justified and was a valid Order - Order was not discriminatory and need not be struck down u/art. 14 of Constitution - As stated by HC, respondent was unable to establish that there was no shortage in generation of electricity when impugned Order was made u/s. 22-B of 1910 Act - Court was therefore unable to take view that appellant wantonly disregarded its contractual obligations to respondent - Appeals disposed of.
