Automotive Tyre Manufacturers Association v Designated Authority and others

Supreme Court of India

07 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 21; (2011) 2 SCC 258; 2011 (263) E.L.T. 481; JT 2011 (1) SC 282; 2011(1) SCALE 149; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 198

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Importation, Right To Be Heard, Administrative Power

Summary: Indirect Tax - Customs - Practice & Procedure - Customs Tariff Act, 1975, s. 9 - Customs Tariff (Identification, Assessment & Collection of Anti-Dumping Duty on Dumped Articles & for Determination of Injury) Rules, 1995 - Notification No. 36/2005-Cus. dt. 27.04.2005 - Anti-dumping duty on tyre manufacturers - Appellant, is an association representing domestic tyre manufacturing units, who import Nylon Tyre Cord Fabric (NTCF) from various countries, including China, as one of their basic raw materials for manufacture of tyres - In 2003, the Association of Synthetic Fibre Industry (ASFI), respondent No. 3, filed an application under the 1995 Rules before the Designated Authority (DA) praying for imposition of anti-dumping duty u/s. 9A of the Act, on imports of NTCF from China - DA made recommendations to the Central Government who accepted the findings of the DA, and issued Notification No. 36/2005-Cus levying anti-dumping duty at different rates w.e.f. 26.07.2004 - Appellant challenged the findings of the DA as well as the Customs Notification No. 36/2005-Cus. submitting that the DA had given his findings without hearing the appellants - Tribunal held that the imposition of anti-dumping duty being legislative in character, the principles of natural justice were not applicable to the proceedings before the DA and, therefore, persons affected had no right to be heard before the imposition of duty - Hence, the present appeal - (A) Whether the decision of the DA is legislative, administrative or quasi-judicial in character? - Held, DA determines the rights and obligations of the 'interested parties' by applying objective standards based on the material/information/evidence presented by the exporters, foreign producers and other 'interested parties' by applying the procedure and principles laid down in the 1995 Rules - Rule 5 of the 1995 Rules provides that the DA shall initiate an investigation so as to determine the existence, degree and effect of any alleged dumping upon the receipt of a written application by or on behalf of the domestic industry - Under r. 6(7) of the 1995 Rules, the DA is required to make available the evidence presented to it by one party to other interested parties, participating in the investigation - It is also pertinent to note that r. 12 of the 1995 Rules which deals with the preliminary findings, explicitly provides that such findings shall "contain sufficiently detailed information for the preliminary determinations on dumping and injury and shall refer to the matters of fact and law which have led to arguments being accepted or rejected' - Hence, DA is entrusted with a quasi-judicial function - (B) Whether impugned notification could be quashed on the ground of breach of the principles of natural justice? - Held, the procedure prescribed in the 1995 Rules imposes a duty on the DA to afford to all the parties, who have filed objections and adduced evidence, a personal hearing before taking a final decision in the matter - Even written arguments are no substitute for an oral hearing - In the present case, admittedly, the entire material had been collected by the predecessor of the DA; he had allowed the interested parties and/or their representatives to present the relevant information before him in terms of r. 6(6) of Rules but the final findings in the form of an order were recorded by the successor DA, who had no occasion to hear the appellants - Hence, the final order passed by the new DA offends the basic principle of natural justice - Thus, the impugned notification having been issued on the basis of the final findings of the DA, who failed to follow the principles of natural justice, cannot be sustained - It is quashed accordingly - (C) Whether the appellants will be entitled to the refund of the duty already paid and collected? - Held, in the case of indirect taxes like central excise duties and customs duties, the tax collected by the State without the authority of law, shall not be refunded to the petitioner unless he alleges and establishes that he has himself borne the burden of the said duty and that he has not passed on the burden of duty to a third party - In the present case, the DA had recorded a clear finding to the effect that the Chinese exporters had been underselling below the non- injurious price to the tune of 25-20% during the period of investigation - It is, therefore, manifest that the burden of anti-dumping duty had been absorbed by the exporters - The said finding of fact attained finality in as much as it had not been assailed by any of the interested parties - Hence, the importers viz. ATMA and its constituent members have passed on the burden of the levy to third person, it follows that members of ATMA cannot claim refund of the anti-dumping duty levied in terms of the Notification No.36/2005-Cus - Appeals partly allowed.
