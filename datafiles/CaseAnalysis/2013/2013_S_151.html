Sujit Biswas v State Of Assam

Supreme Court of India

28 May 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 340; (2013) 12 SCC 406; AIR 2013 SC 3817; 2013 CRLJ 3140; 2013 (3) JCC 1887; JT 2013 (8) SC 570; 2013 (2) MLJ(Crl) 871; 2013 (2) OLR 744; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 227; 2013(7) SCALE 546; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 830

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conjectures

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 376(2)(f) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 11 - Rape - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant accused allegedly murdered the deceased by committing rape - Appellant allegedly committed offences punishable u/ss. 376(2)(f) and 302 IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant u/ss. 376(2)(f) and 302 IPC and awarded him the death and death reference was made to HC - Appellant preferred appeal before HC - HC disposed of the appeal and death reference by impugned common judgment commuting death sentence to life imprisonment - Aggrieved, appellant filed instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in passing the impugned order - Held, mere abscondance of accused did not lead to a firm conclusion of his guilty mind - An innocent man might also abscond in order to evade arrest, as in light of such a situation, such an action might be part of natural conduct of accused - Abscondance was in fact relevant evidence, but its evidentiary value depends upon surrounding circumstances, and hence, same should only be taken as a minor item in evidence for sustaining conviction - FIR lodged had disclosed previous statement of informant which could only be used to other corroborate or contradict maker of such statement - However, in event that informant was a person who claims to know facts, and was also closely related to victim, it was expected that he would have certainly mentioned in FIR, all such relevant facts - Omission of important facts affecting probability of case, was a relevant factor u/s. 11 of the Act to judge veracity of case of prosecution - Inference could be drawn against accused only and only if incriminating material stands fully established, and accused was not able to furnish any explanation for same - However, accused had the right to remain silent, as he could not be forced to become a witness against himself - Instant case was required to be examined in light of aforesaid settled legal propositions - However, said circumstance was not put to appellant while he was examined u/s. 313 CrPC by Trial Court, and in view thereof, same could not be taken into consideration - Hence, even by a stretch of imagination, it could not be held that aforementioned circumstances clearly point towards guilt of appellant, and in light of such a fact situation, burden lies not only on accused to prove his innocence, but also upon prosecution, to prove its case beyond all reasonable doubt - Impugned judgments and orders passed by Courts below were set aside - Appeal allowed.

Laws : Criminal; Practice & Procedure

(B)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 3 - Circumstantial evidence - Circumstance not put in s. 313 CrPC examination - Effect - Held, circumstantial evidence was essential to decide whether or not chain of circumstances was complete - No matter how weak evidence of prosecution might be, it was the duty of Court to examine accused, and to seek his explanation as regards incriminating material that had surfaced against him - However, circumstances which were not put to accused in his examination u/s. 313 CrPC, could not be used against him and should be excluded from consideration - Statement could not be treated as evidence within meaning of s. 3 of the Act, as accused could not be cross-examined with reference to such statement - Appeal allowed.
