State of Andhra Pradesh v Cheemalapati Ganeswara Rao and Another

Supreme Court of India

23 April 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

J.R. Madholkar, K. Subba Rao, Raghubar Dayal

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 332; AIR 1963 SC 1850; 1963 CRLJ 671; [1964] 3 S.C.R. 297

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Abuse Of Power

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 -Constitution of India, 1950 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947 - Abuse of power - Chargesheet - Partnership firm - Respondent no. 1 was tried before the Court of Sessions, Visakhapatnam for offences u/ss. 120-B, 409, 477-A and 471 read with s. 467, IPC - While respondent no. 2 was tried for an offence u/s. 120-B and for offences under ss. 409 read with s. 109, 477-A and 471 read with s. 467, IPC - Each of the respondents were convicted for the first two offences, but the respondent no. 1 alone was convicted of the other two offences - Various sentences were passed against them - Respondents preferred appeals before the HC challenging their convictions and sentences - HC allowed the two appeals, acquitted the respondents and dismissed the application for revision preferred by the State - State of Andhra Pradesh had come up before this Court in appeal by obtaining special leave under art. 136 of the Constitution - Whether the acquittal of the respondents were set aside and appeal of the HC was remitted - Held, it would have taken considerable time to adjourn this matter and give the opportunity to the parties to include those documents on record - HC will of course be bound by the finding which we have given on the questions of law agitated before us - HC should consider the evidence and decide for itself whether it is sufficient to bring home all or any of the offences to the respondents - This Court had pointed out in the latter decision that while it must be shown that the approver is a witness of truth, the evidence adduced in a case cannot be considered in compartments and that even for judging the credibility of, the approver the evidence led to corroborate him in material particulars would be relevant for consideration - HC should have bear this in mind for deciding whether the evidence of the approver should be acted upon or not - It would be necessary for it to consider for itself the evidence adduced by the prosecution on the specific charges and then to conclude whether those charges have been established or not - With these observations, the acquittal of the respondents was set aside and remit the appeal to the HC for decision on merits in the light of observations - Appeal allowed.
