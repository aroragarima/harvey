Â Smt. Somavanti and Others v State of Punjab and Others

Supreme Court of India

02 May 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

J.R. Madholkar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, K. Subba Rao, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 405; AIR 1963 SC 151; [1963] 33 Comp Cas 745; 1963 (2) MLJ(SC) 18; [1963] 2 S.C.R. 774

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Oaths Act, 1873, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Possession Of Land

Summary: Constitution - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 2(f), 4, 6(3) and 6 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 4 - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(f) and 31 - (A) Meaning of 'Conclusive proof' and 'Conclusive evidence' - No difference between two expressions - Since evidence means and includes all statements which Court permits or requires to be made, when law says that a particular kind of evidence would be conclusive as to existence of a particular fact it implies that fact can be proved either by that evidence or by some other evidence which Court permits or requires to be advanced - There is thus no difference between effect of expression 'Conclusive evidence' from that of 'Conclusive proof', aim of both being to give finality to establishment of existence of a fact from proof of another - (b)Whether s. 6 of Land Acquisition Act (1 of 1894), is Constitutionally valid? - Held, Act provides that declaration made by State that a particular land is needed for a public purpose shall be conclusive evidence - Constitution is not thereby infringed - Act being a pre-Constitution Law is protected from operation of art. 31(2) by provisions of art. 31(5) (a) and art. 19(1) (f) is not attracted to such a case - (c) Meaning of term 'Public Purpose' - Further held, broadly speaking expression 'Public Purpose' would include a purpose in which general interest of community, as opposed to particular interest of individuals, is directly and vitally concerned - It is because of this that legislature has left it to Govt. to say what is a public purpose and also to declare need of a given land for a public purpose - (d) Extent of conclusiveness of Govt. - Further held, declaration under s. 6 that a particular land is needed for a public purpose or for a company is not to be made by Govt. arbitrarily, but on basis of material placed before it by Collector - On objections - Final to enable Govt. to arrive at its satisfaction - Sub-s. (3) of s. 6 says that such a declaration shall be conclusive evidence that land is needed for a public purpose or for a company - Power of state not made conditional upon existence of a collateral or extraneous fact - Provisions of sub-s. (3) preclude a court from ascertaining whether either of these ingredients of declaration exists - Whether in a particular case purpose for which land is needed is a public purpose or not is for State Govt. to be satisfied about? - If purpose is within legislative competence of State declaration of Govt. will be final - Exception - If there is a colorable exercise of power declaration will be open to challenge at instance of aggrieved party - If it is a private purpose or no purpose at all, action of Govt. would he colorable as not being relatable to power conferred upon it by Act and its declaration will be a nullity - To such a declaration protection of s. 6(3) will not extend - (e) Whether art. 14 of Constitution is violated when State chooses a particular industry and declares it to be public purpose for purposes of acquisition? - Art. 14 of Constitution not violated - It is always open to State to fix priorities amongst utilities of different kinds, bearing in mind needs of State existing facilities and other relevant factors - No question of discrimination would arise merely by reason of fact that Govt. has declared that establishment of a particular industry is for a public purpose - (E) Whether notifications can be made simultaneously? - Further held, a notification under sub-sec (1) of s. 4 is a condition precedent to making of notification under sub-s. (1) of s. 6 - If Govt, therefore, takes a decision to make such a notification - There is no departure from any provision of law even though two notifications are published on same day - Petition dismissed.
