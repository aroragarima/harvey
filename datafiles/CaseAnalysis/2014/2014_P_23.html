Easwari v Parvathi and others

Supreme Court of India

10 July 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 463; AIR 2014 SC 2912; 2014 (6) ALD(SC) 5; 2014(5) ALL MR 894; 2014 (106) ALR 222; 2014 (4) AWC 4233; 2014 (3) CLT(SC) 98; 2014 (4) CTC 583; JT 2014 (8) SC 432; 2014 (3) RCR(Civil) 955; 2014(8) SCALE 499; [2014] 8 S.C.R. 844

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Hindu Marriage (Tamil Nadu Amendment) Act, 1967

Summary: Family & Personal - Land & Property - Share in scheduled property - Determination of -Respondents filed suit as the legal heirs of deceased - Disputes pertained to the properties which were held by deceased and his first wife who pre-deceased him - After their death the respondents were in possession of the said properties - It was alleged that the deceased married the appellant as a result whereof she made a claim over the suit property - Respondents sought declaration and injunction with regard to the properties described as schedule 'A' and schedule 'B' properties - Trial Court passed the decree in favour of the respondents for both the schedule properties - On appeal, Lower Appellate Court confirmed the 'B' schedule property in favour of the respondents but reversed the decree with regard to 'A' Schedule property - Second appeal was filed - HC confirmed the order of the Trial Court and set aside the order passed by the first appellate court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the appellant was the second wife of the deceased and was entitled to have a share in the suit 'A' schedule property -

Held, HC could not be precluded from reversing the order and judgment of the Lower Appellate Court if there was perversity in the decision due to mis-appreciation of evidence - Also, even when both the Trial Court and the lower court have given concurrent findings, there was no absolute ban on the HC in second appeal to interfere with the facts - In the instant case, alleged marriage should be proved only on the basis of legal presumption of long co-habitation which was not present in the instant case - For the proof of marriage, there was no evidence - HC held that the Lower Appellate Court, without proper evidence of marriage of the appellant with deceased, had erroneously come to the conclusion as if the marriage had been conducted properly - From the evidence on record it could not be said that the marriage between deceased and appellant was proved - HC correctly assessed and appreciated the facts in the instant case - Judgment and order passed by the HC affirmed - Appeal dismissed.
