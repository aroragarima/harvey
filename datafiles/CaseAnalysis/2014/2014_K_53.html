Pune Municipal Corporation and another v Kausarbag Co-Operation Housing Society Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

09 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, M.Y. Eqbal

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 683; 2015(2) ALL MR 942; JT 2014 (11) SC 526; 2015 (3) RCR(Civil) 675; 2014(11) SCALE 674; [2014] 10 S.C.R. 843

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1966, ss. 126 and 15 - Entitlement to TDR - Respondent-writ petitioner, a housing society, claimed Transferrable Development Rights (TDR) under the Development Control Regulations (DCR) i.e. N- 2.4 framed under the 1966 Act - Claim was resisted and rejected by the Pune Municipal Corporation and the State of Maharashtra (appellants) on the ground that the land in question was not reserved for a public purpose in the development plan prepared under the Act and being shown as an existing garden therein, the claim to TDR had no legal basis - Writ petition was filed - HC held in favour of respondent-writ petitioner - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, there is a difference between acquisition under the 1894 Act by resort to the provisions of s. 126(1)(c) of the 1966 Act and acquisition dehors the said provision of the 1966 Act. If under DCR N-2.4.17, TDR can be granted in cases of acquisition under the 1966 Act acquisition under the 1894 Act upon invocation of s. 126(1)(c) would be included. However, Govt. Order dt. 3-2-2007, seeks to prohibit the grant of DCR under DCR N-2.4.17 so far as lands in respect of which Award under the 1894 Act had been passed or possession of which has been taken over. This is contrary to the clear intent behind DCR N- 2.4.17. The Govt Order in question, having been issued u/s. 154 of the 1966 Act, therefore, cannot override the DCR N-2.4.17 as the directions u/s. 154 of the 1966 Act would be in the nature of administrative instructions. The DCR governing the grant of TDR though may have gone beyond what is contemplated under the 1966 Act, the State and its authorities cannot be permitted to request the Court to collaterally adjudge the validity of the said norms laid down by the State itself. So long as the DCR holds the field all executive actions must be within the four corners thereof. The rejection of the claim of the respondent Society to TDR under the 1966 Act read with DCR N-2.4.17 is seriously flawed. The order of the HC affirmed. Appeals dismissed.
