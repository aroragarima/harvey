Dharmendra Kirthal v State of Uttar Pradesh and another

Supreme Court of India

02 August 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 482; (2013) 8 SCC 368; AIR 2013 SC 2569; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 3275; JT 2013 (11) SC 523; 2013(9) SCALE 729; [2013] 7 S.C.R. 823

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Special Courts, Constitutionality, Motor Vehicles (Amendment) Act, 1994

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 21, 22(4), 32, 300A - Uttar Pradesh Gangsters and Anti-Social Activities (Prevention) Act, 1986, s. 12 - Speedy trial - Abeyance - Constitutionality - Petitioner, who was undergoing trial before Trial Court had filed instant writ petition challenging Constitutional validity of number of provisions of the Act, 1986 being violative of arts. 14, 21, 22(4) and 300A of Constitution - Petitioner contended that accused who was tried by Special Courts under the Act was treated differently because trial in other Courts were kept in abeyance whereas accused tried by other Courts gets benefit of speedy trial and no justification to treat accused under the Act in such a manner as it violates equal treatment before law u/art. 14 of Constitution - Held, legislature had incorporated such a provision so that an accused did not face trial in two cases simultaneously and a case before Special Court did not linger owing to clash of dates in trial - Further, trial was not hampered as trial in other Courts was to remain in abeyance by legislative command - Thus, question of procrastination of trial did not arise - Therefore, s. 12 of the Act did not frustrate concept of fair and speedy trial which were imperative facets of art. 21 of Constitution - Hence, legislature had in actuality, responded to actual feelings and requirements of collective - Thus, accused under the Act was in a distinct category and differentiation between two, namely, a person arrayed as an accused in respect of offences under other Acts and an accused under the Act was a rational one - It could not be said to be arbitrary - Therefore, contention that s. 12 of the Act invites wrath of art. 14 of Constitution was sans substratum - Therefore, upheld Constitutional validity of s. 12 of the Act as it did not infringe any of facets of arts. 14 and 21 of Constitution - Petition dismissed.
