Devender Pal Singh Bhullar v State of (NCT of Delhi)

Supreme Court of India

12 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

G.S. Singhvi, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 225; (2013) 6 SCC 195; AIR 2013 SC 1975; 2013 CRLJ 2888; JT 2013 (5) SC 598; 2013 (2) KLT 353; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 647; 2013(5) SCALE 575; [2013] 6 S.C.R. 676

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 419, 420, 498, 471 - Passports Act, 1967, s. 12 - Terrorists and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 2, 3, 4 - Terrorism - Clemency petition - Delay in disposal - Commutation of death sentence - Entitled to - Petitioner allegedly detonated a bomb on the cavalcade of a political party and thereby caused the death of 9 persons and injury of 17 persons - Petitioner was charged with offences u/ss. 419, 420, 498 and 471 IPC, s. 12 of the Act and u/ss. 2, 3 and 4 of TADA - Designated Trial Court convicted and sentenced petitioner to death - Appeal and subsequent review to SC were dismissed - Petitioner filed mercy petition before President of India - In the meantime, petitioner filed curative petition before SC, which was also dismissed - President dismissed mercy petition after delay of 8 years - Hence, instant writ petition - Whether 8 years of delay in disposal of mercy petition should be treated as sufficient ground for commutation of death sentence into life imprisonment - Held, instant case was not a fit one for exercise of power of judicial review for quashing the decision taken by President not to commute sentence of death imposed on petitioner - It had been held in various cases that while imposing punishment for murder and similar type of offences, Court was not only entitled, but was duty bound to take into consideration the nature of the crime, motive for commission of the crime, magnitude of the crime and its impact on the society, nature of weapon used for commission of the crime, etc. - If the murder was committed in an extremely brutal or dastardly manner, which gave rise to intense and extreme indignation in community, Court should be fully justified in awarding death penalty.- If the enormity of the crime was such that a large number of innocent people were killed without rhyme or reason, then too, award of extreme penalty of death would be justified - Further, rule enunciated in Sher Singh vs. State of Punjab, 1983 INDLAW SC 382, Triveniben vs. State of Gujarat, 1989 INDLAW SC 43 and some other judgments that long delay could be one of the grounds for commutation of sentence of death into life imprisonment should not be invoked in cases where a person was convicted for offence under TADA or similar statutes - Such cases stood on an altogether different plane and could not be compared with murders committed due to personal animosity or over property and personal disputes - Seriousness of crimes committed by terrorists could be gauged from the fact that many hundred innocent civilians and men in uniform had lost their lives - There was considerable delay in disposal of petition filed by petitioner but, keeping in view the peculiar facts of the case, there was no valid ground to interfere with the ultimate decision taken by President not to commute sentence of death awarded to petitioner into life imprisonment - Further, a substantial portion of delay could well-nigh be attributed to the unending spate of petitions on behalf of the petitioner by various persons - Further, documents adduced to the effect that petitioner had suffered physically and mentally on account of prolonged detention in jail after his conviction and sentence to death, however, the same could not be relied upon for recording a finding that petitioner's mental health had deteriorated to such an extent that sentence awarded to him could not be executed - Petitions dismissed.

(B) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161 - Executive clemency - Judicial review - Scope - Held, Court's power of judicial review of decision taken by the President u/art. 72 or the Governor u/art.161 of the Constitution, was very limited - Court could neither sit in appeal nor exercise power of review, but could interfere if it was found that the decision was taken without application of mind to relevant factors or the same was founded on extraneous or irrelevant considerations or was vitiated due to malafides or patent arbitrariness - However, delay in disposal of petition filed by petitioner u/art. 72 did not justify review of decision taken by President not to entertain his plea for clemency - Petitions dismissed.

(C) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 72, 161 - Power of executive clemency - Nature and scope - Held, power vested in President u/art. 72 and Governor u/art.161 of Constitution was manifestation of prerogative of State - It was an important constitutional responsibility to be discharged by highest executive keeping in view considerations of larger public interest and welfare of the people - While exercising power u/art. 72, President was required to act on the aid and advice of Council of Ministers - Central Government was duty bound to objectively place the case of convict before President with a clear indication about nature and magnitude of crime committed by him, its impact on society and all incriminating and extenuating circumstances - State Government was required to give advice to Governor to enable him to exercise power u/art.161 of Constitution - On receipt of the advice of the Govt., President or Governor, should take a final decision in the matter - However, in appropriate case, President or Governor should take cognizance of relevant facts and then decide whether a case was made out for exercise of power u/art.72 or 161 of Constitution - Petitions dismissed.
