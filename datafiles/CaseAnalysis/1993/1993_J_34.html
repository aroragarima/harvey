Commissioner of Income Tax (Additional), Lucknow v Jeevan Lal Sah

Supreme Court of India

16 September 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

B.P. Jeevan Reddy, S.P. Bharucha

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 628; (1995) Supp4 SCC 247; 1994 (117) CTR 130; [1994] 205 ITR 244; [1994] 73 TAXMAN 182

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Practice & Procedure - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, s. 28(1)(c) - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 148 and 271(1)(c) - Finance Act, 1964 - Whether principles enunciated in case of CIT v Anwar Ali [1970 Indlaw SC 492] continue to be good law even after amendment to s. 271(1)(c) of Income tax Act, 1961, effected by 1964 Finance Act? - By 1964 Finance Act, word 'deliberately' occurring in cl. (c) of s. 271(1) was omitted and Explanation was added - Explanation creates a presumption of law, which is no doubt rebuttable and shifts burden of proof to assessee in situation covered by it - Held, even after Amendment of 1964, penalty proceedings continue to be penal proceedings -Where Explanation has made a difference is, while deciding question of fact, presumption created by it has to be applied which has effect of shifting burden of proof; rule regarding burden of proof enunciated in Anwar Ali case is no longer valid - Question is no longer res integra and decisions in case of CIT v K R Sadayappan [1990 Indlaw SC 478] clearly point out change that has been brought about by introduction of Explanation; where it is a case of undisclosed or unexplained cash deposit or any other concealment, standard is same - Principle enunciated in Anwar Ali case is no longer necessary and cases to which said Explanation is attracted have to be decided in light of law enunciated in Mussadilal Ram Bharose case [1987 Indlaw SC 179] and Sadayappan case [1990 Indlaw SC 478 - Appeals partly allowed.
