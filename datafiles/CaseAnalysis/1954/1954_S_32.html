Supreme Court of India

16 April 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Ghulam Hasan, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 33; AIR 1954 SC 282; 1954 (20) CutLT 250; 1954 (1) MLJ 596; 1954 SCJ 335; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 1005

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Religious Denomination, Madras Hindu Religion Endowments Act

Summary: Constitution - Trusts & Associations - Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, 1951, s. 103 - Constitution of India, 1950 art. 13, 26, 19(1)(f), 25, 3l, 44(2), 26(d), 27, 19(1), 19(5), 26(b), 25(2), 248(1) and 265 - (A) Mahantiship - Rights and duties of mahant - Mahantiship a corporate body - Freedom of religion - Mahantiship is not heritable like ordinary property - Mahant has right to enjoy this property or beneficial interest - So long as mahant entitled to hold office - Mathadhipati is not a corporate body - Mahant is head of a spiritual fraternity - By virtue of his office has to perform duties of a religious teacher - Freedom of religion not confined to religious beliefs only it extends to religious practices as well, subject to restrictions imposed by Constitution itself - (B) Constitution of India, 1950 art. 25 - Religion is a matter of faith with individuals or communities and it is not necessarily theistic - There are well known religions in India like Buddhism and Jainism which do not believe in God or in any Intelligent First Cause - Guarantee under Constitution of India not only protects freedom of religious opinion but it protects acts done in pursuance of a religion and this is made clear by use of expression 'practice of religion' in art. 25 - (c) What constitutes essential part of a religion is primarily to be ascertained with reference to doctrines of that religion itself - Restrictions under arts. 25 and 26 - Fundamental rights and restrictions with respect to matters of religion - Freedom of religion in Constitution of India is not confined to religious beliefs only; it extends to religious practices as well subject to restrictions which Constitution itself has laid down - (D)Extent of religious autonomy - Under art. 26(b), a religious denomination or organization enjoys complete autonomy in matter of deciding as to what rites and ceremonies are essential according to tenets of religion they hold and no outside authority has any jurisdiction to interfere with their decision in such matters - Scale of expenses to be incurred in connection with these religious observances would be a matter of administration of property belonging to religious denomination and can be controlled by secular authorities in accordance with any law laid down by a competent Legislature - It could not be injunction of any religion to destroy institution and its endowments by incurring wasteful expenditure on rites and ceremonies - Under art. 26(d), it is fundamental right of a religious denomination or its representative to administer its properties in accordance with law - Must leave right of administration to religious denomination itself subject to such restrictions and regulations as it might choose to impose - A law which takes away right of administration from hands of a religious denomination altogether and vests it in any other authority would amount to a violation of right guaranteed under cl. (d) of art. 26 and 27 - (E) Right of entry in public temple or religious institution - There can be no such thing as an unregulated and unrestricted right of entry in a public temple or other religious institution, for persons who are not connected with spiritual functions thereof - It is a traditional custom universally observed not to allow access to any outsider to particularly sacred parts of a temple, as for example, place where deity is located - There are also fixed hours of worship and rest for idol when no disturbance of any member of public is allowed - (F) Extent of art. 27 - Specific appropriation of proceeds of any tax in payment of expenses for promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or religious denomination - Distinction between a tax and a fee lies primarily in fact that a tax is levied as a part of a common burden while a fee is a payment for a special benefit or privilege - Appeal dismissed.
