Super Forgings and Steels v Thyabally Rasuljee

Supreme Court of India

01 December 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Venkatachala, K. Ramaswamy

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 600; (1995) 1 SCC 410; JT 1995 (1) SC 51; 1995 (1) MLJ(SC) 59; 1995 (2) RCJ 487; 1995 (1) RCR(Rent) 417; 1995 (1) RentLR 200; 1994(5) SCALE 125; [1994] Supp6 S.C.R. 229

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1960 - Non-residential building - Eviction - Landlord filed a petition against the tenant on the ground available to him u/s. 10(3)(a)(iii) of the 1960 Act before the Rent Controller at Madras for eviction of the tenant from the 'petition non-residential' building - Eviction order was challenged by the tenant before the Appellate Authority and the HC, in appeal and revision, respectively - Both the appeal and revision were dismissed by judgments and orders made in them - Hence, instant appeal - Whether, the orders against the appellant tenant were liable to be set-aside - Held, that s. 10(3)(a)(iii) of the 1960 Act conferred a right which was not an accrued right, on the landlord to take advantage of the ground available there under to evict his/her tenant from a non-residential building - Non-residential building in occupation of landlord, which was 'his own', envisaged in s. 10(3)(a)(iii) of the 1960 Act, was not only that of which he was an absolute owner, but also that of which he was a co-owner - A co-owner of a building who was its landlord was regarded under Rent Control Laws of our country as its owner entitled to obtain possession of such a building from a tenant for his bonafide requirement - In an appeal u/art. 136 of the Constitution, the Court was empowered to take cautious cognizance of events and developments subsequent to the institution of eviction proceeding and to grant, deny, mould the relief sought by the parties, in consonance with justice and fair play - Subsequent developments happened during the pendency of the present appeal wherein the business was taken over deceased respondents family, namely respondents nos. 2 to 10 - Eviction order did not create in the respondents an indefeasible vested right when it had not become final and conclusive - Respondents nos. 2 to 11 were disentitled to obtain eviction order u/s. 10(3)(a)(iii) of the 1960 Act because they were the co-owners of a non-residential building, where they are carrying on that business on which the deceased respondent had sought eviction of the appellant-tenant from another non-residential building of which respondents nos. 2 to 11 had become the co-owners after the demise of their respondent no. 1 - Subsequent developments which arose during the pendency of the present appeal warranted the setting aside of the eviction order which was questioned in appeal - Appeal allowed.
