Â Akadasi Padhan v State of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

05 December 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, K.C. Das Gupta, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 442; AIR 1963 SC 1047; 1964 (2) SCJ 37; [1963] 2 S.C.R. 691; [1963] Supp2 S.C.R. 691

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Environment, Wildlife & Animal

Keywords: Competence Of The Legislature

Summary: Constitution - Trade - Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 19(6) - Orissa Kendu Leaves (Control of Trade) Act, 1961, ss. 3(1)(c) and 18 - Constitution Of India, 1950, art. 19(6) (as amended by Constitution (First Amendment) Act (1951) - (A) Interpretation of art. 19(6) - Held, in attempting to construe art. 19 (6), it must be borne in mind that a literal construction may not be quite appropriate - Task of construing important Constitutional provisions like art. 19 (6) cannot always be accomplished by treating said problem as a mere exercise in grammar - It is essential to bear in mind political or economic philosophy underlying provisions in question, and that would necessarily involve adoption of a liberal and not a literal and mechanical approach to problem - (B) Whether State creating monopoly is justified u/art. 19? - Further held, amendment clearly indicates that State monopoly in respect of any trade or business must be presumed to be reasonable and in interests of general public so far as art. 19(1)(g) is concerned - Art. 19(6)(ii) clearly shows that there is no limit placed on power of State in respect of creation of State monopoly - It is not correct to say that creation of a State monopoly must be justified by showing that restrictions imposed by it are reasonable and are in interests of general public - (C) Whether violation of fundamental right should be indirect or incidental effect of a legislation? - Further held, it should not be - Several rights guaranteed by 7 sub-cls of art. 19(1) are separate and distinct fundamental rights and they can be regulated only if provisions contained in cls. (2) to (6) are respectively satisfied - It is necessary to distinguish between direct purpose of Act and its indirect or incidental effect - If legislation seeks directly to control citizen's right under art. 19(1)(g), its validity has to be tested in light of provisions contained in art. 19(6) - If such a legislation as for instance, a law creating a State monopoly, indirectly or incidentally affects a citizen's right under any other cl. of art. 19(1) as for instance, art. 19(1)(f), that will not introduce any infirmity in Act itself - (D) Whether s. 4 of Orissa Kendu Leaves (Control of Trade) Act falls within expression 'law relating to' u/art. 19(6)? - Further held, having regard to scheme of State monopoly envisaged by act, s. 4 cannot be said to be such an essential part of said monopoly as to fall within expression 'law relating to' under art. 19(6) - Validity of s. 4 must be tested in light of first part of art. 19(6) so far as a persons rights under art. 19(1)(g) are concerned, and under art. 19(5) so far as his rights under art. 19(1)(f) are concerned - Object of fixing prices was to help growers to realise a fair price - Prices are not unduly low or compare unfavourably with prices prevailing in locality in previous years - Restrictions in regard to fixing of price prescribed by s. 4 are reasonable and in interests of general public both under art. 19(5) and art. 19(6) - (E) Whether r. 7(5) of rules framed under s. 18 of Orissa Kendu Leaves (Control of Trade) Act is void? - Further held, it is void - It leaves it to sweet will of officer concerned to fix terms and conditions of agreement on ad hoc basis - (F) Whether extended meaning of word agents in a commercial sense is wholly inapplicable in context of art. 19(6)(ii)? - Further held, it is inapplicable - Agency includes only agents who can be said to carry on monopoly at every stage on behalf of State for its benefit and not for their own benefit at all - Such agents would be entitled to remuneration for their work as agent - Where agreement entered into by a person with Govt. provides that subject to other terms and conditions, all charges and outgoings shall be paid by agent and he shall not be entitled to any compensation whatsoever for any loss that may be sustained - Agent becomes personally liable to bear loss which, under normal rules of agency, principal would have to bear - Agent appointed under this agreement seems to carry on trade substantially on his own account - If he makes any profit after complying with said terms, profits is his, if he incurs any loss owing to circumstances specified in agreement, loss is his - Petition allowed in part.
