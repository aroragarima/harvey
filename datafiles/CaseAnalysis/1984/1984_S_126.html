Lingappa Pochanna Appelwar and Others v State of Maharashtra and Another

Supreme Court of India

04 December 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Sen, E.S. Venkataramiah, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 301; (1985) 1 SCC 479; AIR 1985 SC 389; 1985 (1) CCC 690; 1984(2) SCALE 1022; [1985] 2 S.C.R. 224

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Maharashtra Restoration of Lands to Scheduled Tribes Act, 1974, Maharashtra Co-operative Society Act, 1960

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Constitution - Maharashtra Restoration of Lands to Scheduled Tribes Act, 1974, ss. 3, 3(1), 4 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19(1)(f), 13 - Transfers and alienations - Power to legislate - Legality - Appellant by a registered sale-deed purchased agricultural land from father of respondent No. 2 who was a tribal - Suo motu proceedings were initiated u/s. 3(1) of the Act for restoration of lands to respondent No. 2 - Sub-Divisional Officer held that case falls within s. 3(1) of the Act and directed appellant to hand over possession of an area of 19 acres and restored to respondent No. 2 on payment of Rs. 461.76p. - On appeal, Tribunal dismissed appeal upholding order passed by Sub-Divisional officer - Appellant filed a writ petition before HC challenging orders of Tribunal as well as that of Sub-Divisional officer - Single Judge dismissed writ petition and DB too dismissed Letters Patent Appeal preferred by appellant - Hence, instant appeal - Whether ss.3 and 4 of the Act provided for annulment of transfers made by members of Scheduled Tribes and for restoration of lands to them on certain conditions were ultra vires State Legislature as being beyond the purview of Entry 18 of List II of the Seventh Schedule or were otherwise violative of arts. 14, 19(1)(f) and 31 of Constitution - Held, impugned Act in its true nature and character was a law relating to transfers and alienations of agricultural lands by members of Scheduled Tribes in State to persons not belonging to Scheduled Tribes - Such a law did not fall within Entries 6 and 7 in List III but was within Entry 18 in List II - However, power to legislate could not be denied to State on ground that provisions of ss. 3 (1) and 4 of the Act incidentally trench on existing law or a law made by Parliament - Thus, power of State Legislature to make a law with respect to transfer and alienation of agricultural land under Entry 18 in List II carries with it not only a power to make a law placing restrictions on transfers and alienations of such lands including a prohibition thereof, but also power to make a law to reopen such transfers and alienations - Hence, impugned Act having been placed in ninth Schedule of Constitution, contention that provisions of ss. 3 (1) and 4 of the Act thereof were inconsistent with, or take away, any of fundamental rights conferred by arts. 14, 19(1)(f) or 31 of Constitution, should be rejected at the very threshold because it was protected from any such challenge u/art. 31 B of Constitution - Therefore reasonable nexus for fixation of such date and object sought to be achieved and impugned Act if not violative of art. 14 of Constitution - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 9A, 30 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(g) - Bar Councils Act, 1926, ss. 14(a), 14(b), 14(c) - Legal practitioner - Unconstitutional restriction - Constitutionality - Whether s. 9A of the Act was constitutionally void as it affects (i) fundamental right of an advocate enrolled by State Bar Council to carry on his profession guaranteed by art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution and (ii) right of appellants who were non-tribals being prevented to be represented by a legal practitioner of their choice - Held, problem had to be viewed from two angles first, from viewpoint of legal practitioner, and secondly from that of litigants - Such a right was no doubt conferred by s. 30 of the 1961 Act - But a person enrolled as an advocate under the Act was not ipso facto entitled to a right of audience in all Courts unless s. 30 of the 1961 Act was first brought into force - Such was matter which was still regulated by different statutes and extent of right to practice should depend on terms of those statutes - Thus, right of an advocate brought on rolls to practise was conferred on him by s. l4(1)(a), (b) and (c) of the 1926 Act - Hence, it could not be held that s. 9A of the 1961 Act was not an unconstitutional restriction on advocates to practise their profession - Appeal dismissed.
