Babubhai Bhimabhai Bokhiria and another v State of Gujarat and others

Supreme Court of India

03 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 216; (2014) 5 SCC 568; AIR 2014 SC 2228; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 1901; 2014 CRLJ 2290; 2014 (4) CTC 248; 2014 (3) G.L.R. 2139; 2014 (2) JCC 1224; JT 2014 (4) SC 454; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1439; 2014 (2) MLJ(Crl) 193; 2014 (2) OLR 159; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 915; 2014(4) SCALE 361; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 652

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 201, 34, 120B, 465, 468 and 471 - Arms Act, 1959, s.25 - Evidence Act, 1872, s.32 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s.319 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Petitioner allegedly committed offence of murder - Appellant was convicted by Trial Court u/ss. 302, 201, 34, 120B, 465, 468 and 471 of IPC and s.25 of 1959 Act - HC also concurred with Trial Court - Hence instant petition - Appellant contended that in the course of trial of an offence, when it appeared from the evidence that any person, not being the accused, had committed any offence for which such person could be tried together with the accused facing trial, the court might proceed against such person for the offence which he appears to have committed - Appellant pointed out that the power u/s.319 of CrPC could be exercised when it appeared from the evidence that any person not being the accused, had committed any offence - In appellant's submission, the evidence would obviously mean the evidence admissible in law - Appellant submitted that the note allegedly recovered from the deceased expresses mere apprehension of death and, therefore, it was inadmissible in evidence and did not come within the ambit of s.32 of 1872 Act - Appellant further submitted that the note did not relate to the cause of death nor it described any circumstance that led to deceased's death - It was also pointed out that the note recovered was also not relevant u/s.32 of 1872 Act as it had no proximity with the event of his death, as the same was written over a year ago - Whether the note in question was admissible in evidence or in other words, could be treated as a dying declaration u/s.32 of 1872 Act -

Held, from plain reading of s.32 of 1872 Act it was evident that a statement of a fact by a person who was dead when it related to cause of death was relevant - It was an exception to the rule of hearsay - Any statement made by a person as to the cause of his death or as to any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in his death was relevant in a case in which the cause of death of the person making the statement came into question - Indian law had made a departure from the English law where the statements which directly related to the cause of death were admissible - General expressions suspecting a particular individual not directly related to the occasion of death were not admissible when the cause of death of the deceased came into question - In instant case, except the apprehension expressed by the deceased, the statement made by him did not relate to the cause of his death or to any circumstance of the transaction which resulted in his death - Once SC held so, the note would not satisfy the requirement of s.32 of 1872 Act - Note, therefore, was not admissible in evidence and, thus, could not be considered as such to enable exercise of power u/s.319 of CrPC - Many decisions of SC supported the view which SC had taken, that the note written by the deceased did not relate to the cause of his death or to any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in his death and therefore, was inadmissible in law - of transaction" used in Section 32 of the Act in the case of Pakala Narayanswami v. Emperor, 1939 Indlaw PC 13 and Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra, 1984 Indlaw SC 432, relied on - In instant case, excepting apprehension, there was nothing in the note - No circumstance of any transaction resulting in the death of the deceased was found in the note - Thus, it was evident that no evidence came during the trial which showed even a prima facie complicity of the appellant in the crime - In that view of the matter, the order passed by the Trial court summoning the appellant, as affirmed by the HC, could not be allowed to stand - Appeal allowed.
