M/S. Mandvi Co-Op Bank Ltd. v Nimesh B. Thakore

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Aftab Alam, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 4; (2010) 3 SCC 83; AIR 2010 SC 1402; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 599; 2010 (1) BC 600; 2010 (1) Bom.C.R. 614; 2010 (1) CTC 693; 2010 (1) DRTC 321; 2010 (2) G.L.R. 990; JT 2010 (1) SC 259; 2010 (2) MLJ(Crl) 395; 2010 (3) MPLJ 42; 2010 (1) RCR(Criminal) 681; 2010(1) SCALE 188; [2010] 1 S.C.R. 219; 2010 (1) UC 223

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Banking Public Financial Institutions and Negotiable Instruments Laws (Amendment) Act, 1988, Negotiable Instruments (Amendment) Bill, 2001

Summary: (A) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 143, 144, 145, 145(2) and 147 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Evidence on affidavit - Special provisions for a dishonoured cheque trial - Right of accused u/s. 145(2) of Act - Held, provisions of ss. 143, 144, 145 and 147 of Act expressly depart from and override provisions of CrPC - Ss. 143 to 147 of Act were designed to lay down a much simplified procedure for trial of dishonoured cheque cases - Claim of accused that on being summoned u/s. 145(2) of Act, complainant or any of his witnesses whose evidence is given on affidavit must be made to depose in examination-in-chief all over again plainly appears to be a demand for meaningless duplication, apparently aimed at delaying trial - Order accordingly.(B) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 143 to 147 - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Amended provision - Retrospective or prospective operation - Held, HC had rightly taken the view that the provisions of ss. 143 to 147 of Act do not take away any substantive rights of accused - Those provisions are not substantive but procedural in nature and would, therefore, undoubtedly, apply to cases that were pending on date provisions came into force - Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 145(1) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 315 and 316 - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Right to give evidence on affidavit - HC held that subject to provisions of ss. 315 and 316, CrPC accused can also give his evidence on affidavit - Whether right to give evidence on affidavit as provided to complainant u/s. 145(1) of Act of Act is also available to accused? - Held, on a bare reading of s. 143 of Act it is clear that legislature provided for complainant to give his evidence on affidavit and did not provide for accused to similarly do so - But HC thought that not mentioning accused alongwith complainant in s. 145(1) of Act was merely an omission by legislature that it could fill up without difficulty - There are two errors apparent in reasoning of HC - First, if legislature in their wisdom did not think 'it proper to incorporate a word 'accused' with word 'complainant' in s. 145(1) of Act......', it was not open to HC to fill up self perceived blank - Secondly, HC was in error in drawing an analogy between evidences of complainant and accused in a case of dishonoured cheque - HC overreached itself and took a course that amounts to taking-over legislative functions - Order accordingly.

(D) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 145(2) and 143 to 146 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 165 - Interpretation of s. 145(2) - Issuance of summons - Discretion of Court - Whether a person who had given his evidence on affidavit appears in court, it is also open to accused to insist that before cross-examining him as to facts stated in affidavit he must first depose in examination-in-chief and be required to verbally state what is already said in affidavit? - Held, s. 145(2) of Act says that that court may, at its discretion, call a person giving his evidence on affidavit and examine him as to facts contained therein - But if an application is made either by prosecution or by accused, court must call person giving his evidence on affidavit, again to be examined as to facts contained therein - Scheme of ss.143 to 146 of the Act does not in any way affect judge's powers u/s. 165 of Evidence Act - As a matter of fact, s. 145(2) expressly provides that court may, if it thinks fit, summon and examine any person giving evidence on affidavit - On being summoned on application made by accused deponent of affidavit (complainant or any of his witnesses) can only be subjected to cross-examination as to facts stated in affidavit - Order accordingly.

(E) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 145(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 296(2) - Whether s. 145(2) of Act was identical to s. 296(2), CrPC? - Held, no - Crucial difference between s. 296(2), CrPC and s. 145(2) of Act is that former deals with evidence of a formal nature whereas under latter provision, all evidences including substantive evidence may be given on affidavit - S. 296, CrPC is part of elaborate procedure of a regular trial under CrPC while whole object of s. 145(2) of Act is to design a much simpler and swifter trial procedure departing from elaborate and time consuming trial procedure of CrPC - Hence, notwithstanding apparent verbal similarity between s. 145(2) of Act and s. 296(2), CrPC, it would be completely wrong to interpret true scope and meaning of one in light of other - Order accordingly.

(F) Constitution - Criminal - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 145(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Evidence given on affidavit - Held, evidence given on affidavit must be admissible and it must not include inadmissible materials such as facts not relevant to issue or any hearsay statements - In case complainant's affidavits contain statements that are not admissible in evidence it is always open to accused to point those out to court and court would then surely deal with objections in accordance with law - Order accordingly.
