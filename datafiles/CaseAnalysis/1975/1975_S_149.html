Anant Mills Company Limited and Others v State of Gujarat and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 January 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

Hans Raj Khanna

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 604; (1975) 2 SCC 175; AIR 1975 SC 1234; [1975] 3 S.C.R. 220; 1975 TAXLR 1540

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: (A) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949 - Property tax - Conservancy tax - Validity - Assessment of properties to property tax was made under the 1949 Act - Separate section of the assessment book was prepared by the commissioner of the municipal corporation (Corporation) for each official year in respect of the assessment of property tax on textile mills, factories and university buildings, which were classified as special properties - Rateable value of such properties was determined on a flat rate and took into consideration the plants and machinery situated upon lands and buildings - Rateable value for 1964-65 and 1965-66 was increased - Commissioner also made initial entries in assessment book in respect of special properties for the year 1966-67 - Later, a new s. 152A was inserted in 1949 Act conferring power on the Corporation to assess or reassess property taxes if the original assessment was affected by a decree or order of a court on either of the grounds on which the SC had set aside the assessment for the official years 1964-65, 1965-66 and 1966- 67- Also, r. 21B was inserted in the Taxation Rules permitting the commissioner to make fresh valuation of properties after the expiry of the official year if preparation or completion of the assessment before the expiry of the official year were affected on account of any order of a court - Also, conservancy tax of 9 per cent was fixed by the Corporation for large premises such as mills and factories as against a general rate of 3 per cent - Whether s. 2(1A)(i) of the 1949 Act was violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - Held, s. 2(1A)(i) of the 1949 Act was constitutionally valid and not violative of art. 14 of the Constitution in respect of all the years to which it has been made applicable - There was a presumption of the constitutional validity of a statutory provision - In case any party assailed the validity of any provision on the ground that it was violative of art. 14 of the Constitution, it was for that party to make the necessary averments and adduce material to show discrimination violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - No averments were made in the petitions before the HC that the assessments before the coming into force of Ordinance 6 of 1969 were made by taking into account the rent restriction provisions of the Bombay Rent Act - It was essential for the HC to ascertain and find out the correct factual position before recording a finding that the impugned provision was violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - Validity of s. 2(1A)(i) of the 1949 Act was assailed in the W.P.s filed before the SC and in deciding those W.P.s, SC could not refuse to take into account the material which was placed before it - As that material disclosed that the factual position as it existed before the promulgation of Ordinance 6 of 1969 was that the provisions of the Bombay Rent Act were not taken into account in determining the rateable value, there was no escape from the conclusion that no differential treatment was meted out to pending cases in s. 2(1A)(i) of the 1949 Act - It was plain that the impugned provision could not be held to be violative of art. 14 of the Constitution in the appeals filed against the judgment of the HC and constitutionally valid in the W.P.s - As the HC decided the matter without having the full and complete data before it and as such data was available to the instant court, the contention that the instant court should not take that data into account, was wholly untenable - Therefore there was no material on record as might justify the inference that a differential hostile treatment was meted out in pending cases - The very basis of striking down the impugned provisions on the ground of being violative of art. 14 of the Constitution thus disappeared - Further, classification by treating decided cases as belonging to one category and pending cases as belonging to another category was reasonable and not per se offensive to art. 14 of the Constitution - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution of India, 1950 - Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949 - Property tax - Conservancy tax - Validity - Whether it was sufficient to find out the total expense to be incurred for conservancy service and thereafter to fix different rates for different categories of properties so that the tax raised was sufficient to meet the total expense, or whether as was held by the HC, the different rate of conservancy tax fixed for a particular class of property under the proviso to s. 129(b) of the 1949 Act should have been related to the actual cost involved in supplying conservancy service to that class - Held, the HC was in error in striking down the resolutions passed by the Corporation for the official years 1967-68, 1968-69, 1969-70 and 1970-71 to the extent to which they fixed the rate of conservancy tax at 9 per cent in respect of textile mills and factories because of the absence of sufficient data to show as to what would be the cost of conservancy service for that particular category of properties - S. 129(b) of the 1949 Act took into account the expense required for efficiently maintaining and repairing the municipal drains for finding out the total expenditure for conservancy service - Affidavit filed on behalf of the Corporation showed that the rates of conservancy tax for the different category of properties were fixed after taking into account the total expense for the conservancy service - It was not possible to insist upon arithmetical accuracy in such matters - A broad and general estimate of the cost of conservancy service and the tax receipts after taking into account the relevant factors would satisfy the requirement of law - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution of India, 1950 - Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949 - Property Tax - Conservancy Tax - Validity - Whether the proviso to s. 129(b) of the 1949 Act was violative of art. 14 of the Constitution on account of excessive delegation of legislative power - Held, the Corporation considered the total expense it incurred for the conservancy service before fixing the various rates of conservancy tax - Different rates of conservancy tax have thus to be related to the total cost of conservancy service to be borne by the Corporation - The 'opinion of the Corporation' mentioned in s. 129(b) of the 1949 Act was formed after budget estimates were prepared in accordance with ss. 95, 96 and 100 of the 1949 Act - The entire procedure as laid out in those ss. provided built-in safeguards and laid down adequate guidelines in the matter of taxation - It therefore could not be said that the legislature had not prescribed any guiding principle for the Corporation for determining the rates of conservancy tax - Proviso to s. 129(b) of the 1949 Act did not suffer from the vice of excessive delegation of legislative power - Appeals dismissed.

(D) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Constitution of India, 1950 - Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949 - Property tax - Conservancy tax - Validity - Whether ss. 406(2)(e) and 411(bb) of the 1949 Act were violative of art. 14 of the Constitution and r. 42 of the Taxation Rules was void in so far as it provided that if an appeal was preferred or entertained against the tax, warrant would not be issued for the recovery of the amount of tax - Held, s. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act was not violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - S. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act sought to regulate the exercise of the right of appeal, which was conferred upon a person aggrieved with the demand of tax made from him, and the right of the Corporation to speedy recovery of the tax - It did not have the effect of creating a discrimination as was offensive to the principle of equality enshrined in art. 14 of the Constitution -Right of appeal was conferred upon all persons who were aggrieved against the determination of tax or rateable value - Bar created by s. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act to the entertainment of the appeal by a person who had not deposited the amount of tax due from him and who was not able to show to the appellate judge that the deposit of the amount would cause him undue hardship arose out of his own commission and default - S. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act did not have the effect of making invidious distinction or creating two classes with the object of meting out differential treatment to them - It only spelt out the consequences flowing from the omission and default of a person who despite the fact that the deposit of the amount found due from him would cause him no hardship, declined of his own volition to deposit that amount - The right of appeal was the creature of a statute - Legislature while granting the right of appeal could impose conditions for the exercise of such right - Thus, so far as the constitutional validity of s. 411(bb) of the 1949 Act and r. 42 of the Taxation Rules was concerned, it was the common case of the parties that it hinged upon the validity of s. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act - And that in case the validity of the s. 406(2)(e) of the 1949 Act was upheld, the validity of the other two provisions would have been upheld - Thus, constitutional validity of all the three provisions was upheld - Appeals dismissed.
