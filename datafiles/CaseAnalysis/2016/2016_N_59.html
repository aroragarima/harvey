Industrial Promotion and Investment Corporation of Orissa Limited v New India Assurance Company Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

22 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

L. Nageswara Rao, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 591; 2016 ACJ 2470; AIR 2016 SC 3908; 2016(5) ALL MR 968; 2016 (119) ALR 25; 2016 (3) CLT(SC) 338; 2016 (4) CPJ(Sc) 11; JT 2016 (8) SC 256; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 1

Case Digest

Subject: Insurance; MRTP

Keywords: Contract Of Insurance, Contract, Insurance Policy, Monopolies And Restrictive Trade Practices Act, 1969, Proposal Form, Ambiguity, State Financial Corporations Act, 1951

Summary: MRTP - Insurance - Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices Act, 1969, ss. 12B, 36A - State Financial Corporations Act, 1951, s. 29 - Burglary - Insurance claimed - Validity - Appellant extended a term loan of Rs. 40,74,000/- to private entrepreneur which was not repaid - Appellant u/s. 29 of the 1951 Act, took over the assets of private entrepreneur and insured said assets with respondent no. 1 - Appellant auctioned seized assets, however it was found that some parts of plant and machinery were missing from factory premises - FIR was registered - Appellant lodged a claim with respondent no. 1 for an amount of Rs. 34,40,650/- under the Burglary and House Breaking Policy - Respondent no. 1 repudiated claim on the ground that alleged loss did not come within the purview of insurance policy - Appellant filed compensation application u/s. 12B r/w s. 36A of the 1969 Act which was rejected by Commission - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, there is no error committed by Commission in rejecting claim of appellant. It is clear from the facts of instant case that appellant has made out a case of theft without a forcible entry. The case of appellant is that forcible entry is not required for a claim to be made under the policy. Following the well-accepted principle that a contract of insurance which is like any other commercial contract should be interpreted strictly, SC was of opinion that policy covers loss or damage by burglary or house breaking which have been explained as theft following an actual, forcible and violent entry from the premises. A plain reading of the policy would show that a forcible entry should precede the theft, and unless they are proved, claim cannot be accepted. There is no difference between a contract of insurance and any other contract, and that it should be construed strictly without adding or deleting anything from the terms thereof. On applying said principle, SC has no doubt that a forcible entry is required for a claim to be allowed under the policy for burglary/house breaking. Thus, SC uphold order of Commission. Appeal dismissed.
