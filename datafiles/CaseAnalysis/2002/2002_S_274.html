Bindeshwari Prasad Singh Alias B. P. Singh and Others v State of Bihar (Now Jharkhand) and Another

Supreme Court of India

13 August 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

B.P. Singh, M.B. Shah

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1638; (2002) 6 SCC 650; (2002) SCC (Cr) 1448; AIR 2002 SC 2907; AIR 2002 SCW 3315; 2002 (3) CCR 156; 2002 (3) Crimes 130; 2002 (6) CRJ 193; 2002 CRLJ 3788; 2002 (3) JCC 1691; JT 2002 (6) SC 22; 2002 (4) RCR(Criminal) 61; 2002(5) SCALE 564; 2002 (4) SLT 731; [2002] Supp1 S.C.R. 495; 2002 (5) Supreme 332

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Revisional jurisdiction - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 401(3) - High Court ordered retrial without any apparent failure of justice - Whether order of HC for retrial was justified? - Held, no - In exercise of revisional jurisdiction against an order of acquittal at instance of a private party, court exercises only limited jurisdiction and should not constitute itself into an Appellate Court which has a much wider jurisdiction to go into questions of facts and law, and to convert an order of acquittal into one of conviction - When a retrial is ordered, dice is heavily loaded against accused, and that itself must caution court exercising revisional jurisdiction - Further held, HC was not justified in interfering with order of acquittal in exercise of its revisional jurisdiction at instance of informant - It may be that HC on appreciation of evidence on record may reach a conclusion different from that of Trial Court - But that by itself is no justification for exercise of revisional jurisdiction u/s. 401 of CrPC, 1973 against a judgment of acquittal - Judgment of Trial Court was not perverse - No defect of procedure has been pointed out - No improper acceptance or rejection of evidence nor was there any defect of procedure or illegality in conduct of trial vitiating trial itself - Appeal allowed.
