Kumaran v State of Kerala and another

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Navin Sinha

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 364; (2017) 7 SCC 471; AIR 2017 SC 2433; 2017 ALL MR (Cri) 2641; 2017 (2) BC 586; JT 2017 (5) SC 368; 2017 (2) KLT 821; 2017 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1440; 2017 (2) MLJ(Crl) 562; 2017 (2) RCR(Criminal) 879; 2017(6) SCALE 62

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Accused Person, Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, Single Judge, Proviso, Expenses, Warrants, Special Reasons, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Attachment, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Complainant

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 357, 357(3), 386, 421, 431 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 53, 64, 70 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 138 - Dishonor of Cheque - Compensation - Entitled to - Complainant filed complaint u/s. 138 of the 1881 Act against accused for dishonouring of cheque - Trial Court convicted accused u/s. 138 of the Act and sentenced him for 4 months simple imprisonment directing him to pay compensation - On appeal, Appellate Court upheld conviction of Trial Court - On further appeal, HC held that despite the fact that default sentence was undergone, under the provisions of CrPC compensation was recoverable - Hence, instant appeals - Whether when compensation was ordered as payable for an offence committed u/s. 138 of the Act and in default, a jail sentence was prescribed and undergone, was compensation still recoverable.

Held, compensation under CrPC was always recoverable as a part of fine, and that even after default imprisonment having been undergone, a fine could still be collected in the manner provided by s. 386 CrPC. Courts held that despite the fact that the reach of s. 386(1) CrPC proviso was only qua warrants that issued after default imprisonment was undergone, yet, the principle of the proviso to s. 386(1) CrPC would apply even to warrants issued before default imprisonment was undergone. Therefore, the law till the enactment of CrPC made it clear that s. 386 CrPC and s. 70 IPC read together would lead to the conclusion that fines were recoverable even after default imprisonment was undergone, provided there were special reasons for recovery of the same. A legal fiction was not to be extended beyond the purpose for which it was created or beyond the language of section by which it is created. Further, object of the legal fiction created by s. 431 CrPC was to extend for the purpose of recovery of compensation until such recovery was completed and that would necessarily not only to s. 421 CrPC but also to s. 70 IPC a companion criminal statute. So long as compensation was directed to be paid, albeit u/ss. 357(3), 431 CrPC, s. 70 IPC and s. 421(1) CrPC proviso would make it clear that by a legal fiction, even though a default sentence was suffered, yet, compensation would be recoverable in the manner provided u/s. 421(1) CrPC. However, once the purpose of legal fiction was ascertained, full effect should be given and it should be carried to its logical conclusion. Appeals dismissed.
