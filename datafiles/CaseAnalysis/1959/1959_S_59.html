Â Mahadeo v State of Bombay. (And Connected Petitions)

Supreme Court of India

09 March 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, Sudhi Ranjan Das, S.K. Das, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 248; AIR 1959 SC 735; 1959 SCJ 1021; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 339

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Madhya Pradesh Abolition of Proprietary Rights (Estates, Mahals Alienated Lands) Act, 1951, ss. 3 and 4 - Sale of Goods Act, 1930, s. 2(7) - Constitution of India, 1953, art. 32 - General Clauses Act, 1897, s. 3(26) - Right to use property -Unregistered documents -Govt. alleged to have entered into agreements by which some of proprietors in former State of Madhya Pradesh granted to one or other of petitioners right to take forest produce, mainly tendu leaves, from forests included in Zamindari and Malguzari villages of grantors - Govt. has disclaimed these agreements and auctioned rights afresh - Petitioners state that this is an invasion of their fundamental rights - (A)Whether petitioner can claim for her fundamental rights to use property which was leased by unregistered document? - Document was construed as conveying to petitioner any part or share in proprietary right of grantor - As purely a license granted to her to enter upon land coupled with a grant - Right acquired by her would be either in nature of some profits a prendre or a purely personal right under a contract- Deed required registration being unregistered, it passed no title or interest - Held, petitioner had no fundamental right which she could enforce - Document was not a lease but amounted to a license to enter on land coupled with a grant to cut certain trees on it and carry away wood, in other words, it was profit a prendre - (B) Whether nature of document can be disguised by labeling it something else? - Held that under Transfer of Property Act, s. 8 true nature of a document cannot be disguised by labeling it something else - (C) Whether there is any distinction between lease and profit a prendre under Transfer of Property Act? - Further held, that in a lease, one enjoys property but has no right to take it away - In a profit a prendre one has a licence to enter on land, not for purpose of enjoying it, but for removing something from it, namely, a part of produce of soil - Petition dismissed.
