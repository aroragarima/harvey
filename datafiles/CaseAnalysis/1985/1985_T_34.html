Â Union of India and Another v Tulsiram Patel and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 July 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

D.P. Madon, Y.V. Chandrachud, V.D. Tulzapurkar, R.S. Pathak, M.P. Thakkar

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 401; (1985) 3 SCC 398; (1985) SCC (L&S) 672; AIR 1985 SC 1416; 1985 (2) CCC 945; 1985 (3) CompLJ 45; 1985 (51) FLR 362; 1985 LabIC 1393; 1985 (2) LLJ 206; 1985 (2) LLN 488; 1985(2) SCALE 133; 1985 (2) SLJ 145; 1985 (2) SLR 576; [1985] Supp2 S.C.R. 131

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation

Keywords: Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Central Industrial Security Force Act, 1968, Constitution (Fifteenth Amendment) Act, 1963, Police (Incitement To Disaffection) Act, 1922, Central Industrial Security Force Rules, 1969, Constitution (Fiftieth Amendment) Act, 1984, International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights

Summary: Majority View

(A) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 309, 310 and 311 - Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976 - Central Civil Services Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, 1965 - Railway Servants (Discipline and Appeal; Rule 1968 - Central Industrial Security Force Rules, 1969 - Interpretation of Arts. 309, 310 and 311 of the Constitution and after the amendment of Clause (2) of Art. 311 by the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976, the second proviso to that clause - Art. 311 of the Constitution confers certain safeguards upon persons employed in civil capacities under the Union of India or a State - First safeguard (given by Clause (1) of Art. 311) was that such person could not be dismissed or removed by an authority subordinate to that by which he was appointed - Second safeguard (given by Clause (2) of Art. 311) was that he could not be dismissed, removed or reduced in rank, except after an inquiry in which he was informed of the charges against him and given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in respect of those charges - Second safeguard was, however, not available to him when he was dismissed, removed or reduced in rank in any of the three cases mentioned in the second proviso to Art. 311(2) - (A) Pleasure doctrine - Held, pleasure doctrine embodied in Art. 310(1), the protection afforded to civil servants by Clauses (1) and (2) of Art. 311 and the withdrawal of the protection u/cls. (2) of Art. 311 by the second proviso thereto were all provided in the Constitution on the ground of public policy and in the public interest and were for public good - (B) Exercise of pleasure by President/Governor - Whether pleasure of the President or the Governor u/art. 310(1) was to be exercised by the President or the Governor personally or it could be exercised by a delegate or some other authority empowered under the Constitution or by an Act or Rules made u/art. 309? - Held, pleasure of the President or the Governor was not required to be exercised by either of them personally - U/cls. (1) of that Art., a government servant could not be dismissed or removed by an authority subordinate to that by which he was appointed - Thus, though u/art. 310(1) the tenure of a government servant was at the pleasure of the President or the Governor, the exercise of such pleasure could be either by the President or the Governor acting with the aid and on the advice of the Council of Ministers or by the authority specified in Acts made u/art. 309 or in rules made under such Acts or made under the proviso to Art. 309; and in the case of Clause (c) of the second proviso to Art. 311(2), the inquiry to be dispensed with not on the personal satisfaction of the President or the Governor but on his satisfaction arrived at with the aid and on the advice of the Council of Ministers - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 311 - (A) Interpretation of second Proviso to Art. 311(2) - Held, language of the second proviso was plan and unambiguous - Words in the second proviso 'this clause shall not apply' meant Clause (2) - Resultant effect of these words was that when a situation envisaged in any of the three clauses of the proviso arises and that clause becomes applicable, the safeguard provided to a government servant by Clause (2) was taken away - However, before, any clause of the second proviso could come into play the condition laid down in it must be satisfied - Condition for the application of each of these clauses was different - In the case of Clause (a) a government servant must be guilty of conduct deserving the penalty of dismissal, removal or reduction in rank which conduct had led to him being convicted on a criminal charge - In the case of Clause (b) the disciplinary authority must be satisfied that it is not reasonably practicable to hold an inquiry - In the case of Clause (c) the President or the Governor of a State, as the case may be, must be satisfied that in the interest of the security of the State, it was not expedient to hold an inquiry - Therefore, before denying a government servant his constitutional right to an inquiry, the first consideration would be whether the conduct of the concerned government servant was such as justifies the penalty of dismissal, removal or reduction in rank - Once that conclusion was reached and the condition specified in the relevant clause of the second proviso was satisfied, that proviso becomes applicable and the government servant was not entitled to an inquiry - (B) Extent of denial of opportunity under the Second Proviso - Held, reasonable opportunity envisages by the provision under consideration includes (a) an opportunity to deny his guilt and establish his innocence, which he could only do if he was told what the charges levelled against him were and the allegations on which such charges were based; (b) an opportunity to defend himself by cross-examining the witnesses produced against him and by examining himself or any other witnesses in support of his defence ; and (c) an opportunity to make his representation - (C) Service Rules and the Second Proviso - Held, a government servant who was dismissed, removed or reduced in rank without holding an inquiry because his case falls under one of the three clauses of the second proviso to Art. 311(2) or a provision of the service rules analogous thereto was not wholly without a remedy - He has a remedy by way of an appeal, revision or in some cases also by way of review - Sub-clause (ii) of Clause (c) of the first proviso of r. 25(1) of the Railway Servants Rules expressly provides that in the case of a major penalty where an inquiry has not been held, the revising authority shall itself hold such inquiry or direct such inquiry to be held - (D) Second Proviso - Clause (b) and (c) - Held, while u/cls. (b) the satisfaction was to be of disciplinary authority, u/cls. (c) it was to be of the President or the Governor of a State, as the case may be - Further, while u/cls. (b) the satisfaction had to be with respect to whether it was not reasonably practicable to hold the inquiry, u/cls. (c) it was to be with respect to whether it would not be expedient in the interest of the security of the State to hold the inquiry - While Clause (b) expressly requires that the reason for dispensing with the inquiry should be recorded in writing, Clause (c) did not so require it, either expressly or impliedly - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 309, 310 and 311 - Railway Servants (Discipline and Appeal; Rule 1968, r. 14 - Whether law laid down in Judgment of SC in D.P.O. Southern Railway v. T.R. Challappan 1975 INDLAW SC 386 was correct? - In Challappan's case the Kerala High Court allowed the writ petitions filed before it on the ground that the orders of the Magistrate releasing the concerned railway servants on probation did not amount to imposition of penalty as contemplated by r. 14 of the Railway Servants Rules - Reversing that view SC held that the word 'penalty' in Clause (i) of r. 14 of the Railway Servants Rules did not refer to a sentence awarded by the Court to the accused on his conviction but it merely indicates the nature of the penalty imposable by the disciplinary authority if the delinquent employee had been found guilty of conduct which has led to his conviction on a criminal charge - Held, it was not possible to agree with approach adopted in Challappan's case in considering r. 14 of the Railway Servants Rules in isolation and apart from the second proviso to Art. 311(2), nor with the interpretation placed by it upon the word 'consider' in the last part of r. 14 - Neither R. 14 of the Railways Servants Rules nor a similar rule in other service Rules could be looked at apart from the second proviso to Art. 311(2) - Court in Challappan's case was in error in interpreting R. 14 of the Railway Servants Rules by itself and not in conjunction with the second proviso to Art. 311(2) - It was also not possible to accept the interpretation placed upon the word 'consider' in Challappan's case - According to the view taken in that case, a consideration of the circumstances of the case could not be unilateral but must be after hearing the delinquent government servant - If such were the correct meaning of the word 'consider', it would render this part of R. 14 unconstitutional as restricting the full exclusionary operation of the second proviso - Decision in Challappan's case was, therefore not correct with respect to the interpretation placed by it upon R. 14 of the Railway Servants Rules and particularly upon the word 'consider' occurring in the last part of that rule and in interpreting R. 14 by itself and not in conjunction with the second proviso to art. 311(2) - Challappan's case overruled - Appeals disposed of.

M. P. Thakkar, J.

(D) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 309, 310 and 311 - Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976 - Central Civil Services Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, 1965 - Railway Servants (Discipline and Appeal; Rule 1968 - Central Industrial Security Force Rules, 1969 - Whether law laid down in Judgment of SC in D.P.O. Southern Railway v. T.R. Challappan 1975 INDLAW SC 386 was correct? - Held, 'Challappan' was rightly decided and there was no compulsion to overrule it - Even if the other point of view were to appear to be more 'attractive', it was neither a good nor a sufficient ground to overrule 'Challappan' wherein it was ruled in the context of Rule 14(a)(b) of the Railway Servants (Discipline and Appeal) Rules, 1968, that an employee must at least be heard on the question of quantum of punishment before he was dismissed or removed from service without holding any inquiry - Order accordingly.
